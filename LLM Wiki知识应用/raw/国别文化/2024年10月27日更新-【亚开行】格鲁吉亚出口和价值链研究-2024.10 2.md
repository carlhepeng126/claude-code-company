### EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

_**What policymaking approaches can help to further stimulate Georgian exports?**_ To address this
question, the Georgia Exports and Value Chain Study (the study) uses the examples of four products
with significant export potential. The study assesses the value chains in the exemplified sectors and
offers recommendations for strategic directions regarding overall export promotion elements and
detailed export promotion programs (EPPs) to help formulate essential reforms aimed at boosting
exports. Through the specific actions proposed, the study contributes to informing the future
economic diversification of the country and the competitiveness strategy of the Government of
Georgia.


_**Exports play a key role for Georgia.**_ International trade is an important driver of the country’s wealth
generation and economic expansion. Trade with other countries provides economies like Georgia
access to new technologies and knowledge, thereby promoting innovation. The country’s foreign trade
has expanded significantly in recent years, even in the face of the coronavirus disease (COVID-19)
pandemic. The growth of merchandise exports has gained momentum, resulting in a total export value
of $10.0 billion for goods. However, Georgia’s position in global competitiveness is relatively modest,
and integration into global value chains is still limited. Issues such as low productivity and the challenge
of generating high-quality jobs are significant hurdles. To ensure consistent economic growth and
increase resilience to external economic shocks, it becomes imperative to increase exports of valueadded products by diversifying into high productivity sectors and cultivating innovative capabilities.
This provides an opportunity to harness the advantages of Georgia’s geostrategic location, existing
free trade agreements, and favorable business environment.


_**As examples of trade policy implementation, four sectors and products from Georgia were assessed**_
_**in detail:**_ Pharmaceuticals, apparel, jewelry, and almonds as established export sectors or products
with significant potential. The selection is based on an assessment of export potential, interviews
with stakeholders, and cross-checks with previous analyses. It thus demonstrates a robust qualitative
approach to identifying high-potential sectors. Recycled batteries for electric vehicles are added as a
future opportunity (Figure A).


_**Georgia can learn from successful exporting countries both with regard to export strategies**_
_**and specific EPPs for the economy as a whole.**_ Outperformers in economic complexity based on
trade data include not only highly industrialized economies such as Germany, Sweden, and the
United Kingdom, but also other upper middle-income countries such as Costa Rica or Malaysia.
The study takes these successful examples into account when discussing the way forward and outlines
a strategy, policies, agreements, and regulations to improve export performance. The same applies
to recommendations for key export promotion elements, including awareness and capacity building,
target markets and customers, closing export deals, maintaining relationships, and other relevant
elements. Both the analysis of sectors with high export potential and the overall recommendations
are structured following a sound analytical framework based on previous research and international
best practices (Table A).

##### **Study Research Question**

_**What policymaking approaches can help to further stimulate Georgian exports?**_ To investigate this
research question, the Georgia Exports and Value Chain Study (the study) assesses four exemplified
sectors and/or products with high export performance potential. [1] It adopts a primarily qualitative
approach and conducts an analysis based on more than 25 interviews with important stakeholders.
The study evaluates the value chains and discusses the strengths, weaknesses, opportunities, and
threats within these sectors. It also provides recommendations for strategic directions and export
promotion programs (EPPs) to help design the necessary reforms to increase exports. With the
specific actions proposed, the study provides information for the Government of Georgia’s future
strategy for economic diversification and competitiveness.

##### **Background**


_**Exports are an important stimulus for the creation of wealth and economic growth.**_ Foreign trade
plays a key role in facilitating the efficient allocation of resources and promotes business growth.
By trading with other countries, firms tap into new markets and gain access to a larger customer base.
This can help increase profits and create jobs at home. On average, businesses that export are more
productive and efficient than their counterparts that only operate domestically (Harrison 1994;
De Loecker 2007; Reis and Farole 2013). By opening up to and fostering trade with EPPs, economies
focus on producing what they do best and then trade with other countries to obtain needed goods and
services that are more efficiently produced abroad. Productivity not only affects the competitiveness
of firms in international markets, but also improves domestic profitability and the ability of firms to
cover upfront export costs. Specialization and division of labor can thus result in a more efficient
use of resources and ultimately increase economic growth. Key factors include trade openness and
integration into global value chains, export growth and composition, market reach and share, and
trade partners and the nature of export relationships (Edwards 1993; Hessels and van Stel 2011;
Melitz 2003; Sachs and Warner 1995; Waugh and Ravikumar 2016).

_**Trade fosters knowledge and technology transfer between countries.**_ By trading with other countries,
economies can gain access to new technologies and knowledge. This helps to spur innovation,
particularly in developing countries with limited access to technological advancements. Horizontal
export diversification into new sectors leads to positive externalities, as the newly export-oriented sectors
benefit from contacts with importers and exposure to international competition. Vertical diversification
from primary to manufactured exports is linked to growth, as manufactured exports generally exhibit
stronger spillover effects. Export diversification facilitates structural transformation and is conducive
to development through cross-sectoral technological spillover effects. In addition, trade can attract
foreign direct investment (FDI), which can further stimulate growth and create jobs. Previous research
has shown that there is an inverse relationship between productive capacity and merchandise export
concentration, i.e., economies with high merchandise export concentration index scores have the
weakest productive capacity. Export dependence on a few commodities and vulnerability to negative
external shocks can be reduced by facilitating productive capacities (Anwesha and Acharyya 2007;
Hausmann and Rodrik 2003; Herzer and Nowak-Lehmann 2006; Mania and Rieber 2019; Tadesse and
Ryan 2004; UNCTAD n.d.; Yanikkaya 2003).


_**Exports also play an important role for Georgia.**_ Georgia is a small transitional, lower-middleincome country in the Caucasus. With a population of 3.7 million, the gross domestic product
(GDP) per capita in 2022 amounted to $6,627.7 (at purchasing power parity in current international
United States [US] dollars). The country has a relatively good and improving business environment
and benefits from its strategic geographic position between Central Asia, the Russian Federation,
Europe, and Türkiye. Important sectors are agriculture, minerals, manufacturing, and tourism. Table 1
gives an economic overview.

Introduction 3


_**Georgia’s global competitiveness position is relatively weak and its linkages with global value**_
_**chains are limited.**_ Low productivity and difficulties in creating high-quality jobs are the key challenges
in Georgia. Labor productivity growth represents a positive change in the output volume for a given
volume of hours worked. When more capital is invested in production, the quality of capital increases,
and capital and labor together can be more efficient, resulting in higher levels of productivity. This
means higher multifactor productivity growth. In Georgia, labor productivity continues to lag behind
other European and Central Asian upper-middle-income countries. On average, GDP per hour
worked in Georgia was only $17.33. [2] Many people in rural areas continue to work in low productivity
agriculture. Human capital formation is often weak with poor learning outcomes. The shortage of
skilled labor poses a problem for doing business and firm growth (Zubiashvili and Silagadze 2016).
In recent years, the economy has become relatively less complex, falling to 64th place in the economic
complexity indicator based on trade data (Figure 2). For continued economic growth and improved
resilience to external shocks, it is imperative to export more value-added products by diversifying into
higher productivity sectors and developing innovative capabilities. This creates an opportunity to take
advantage of Georgia’s geostrategic location and existing FTAs.

_**Export potential can be analyzed by using determinants of exports.**_ There is empirical evidence that
several factors can have a significant influence on foreign trade. For example, while the size and growth
of import demand and export supply have a positive effect on exports, international competition and
political and exchange rate risks have a negative effect. These determinants are used in state-ofthe-art approaches to estimate export potential, e.g., in the International Trade Centre (ITC) export
potential assessment, the gravity model, and the decision support model (Aucamp, Steenkamp, and
Bezuidenhout 2023; Cuyvers et al. 1995; Hidalgo et al. 2007; Moser, Nestmann, and Wedow 2008;

_Analytical Approach to Identifying Export Potential_


_**The selection of exemplified products to be comprehensively studied is mainly based on the ITC’s**_
_**assessment of their export potential due to its transparent approach.**_ For an initial identification of
promising products, both the export potential indicator (EPI) and the product diversification indicator
(PDI) of the ITC are applied. As discussed by Decreux and Spies (2016), the assessment of export
potential is conceptually based on the assumption that in a frictionless world, trade flows can be
described as a combination of supply performance, ease of trade, and total demand. While a country’s
capacity to supply existing products is captured by projected market shares, its capacity to diversify
into new products is based on the concept of product space, which creates connections between
products by assessing how often they are found together in countries’ export baskets (Hausmann
and Klinger 2007; Hausmann, Hwang, and Rodrik 2007; Hidalgo et al. 2007). [6] The EPI focuses on
established export sectors with the aim of increasing exports to existing and new markets, identifying
products where an economy has proven to be competitive and the prospects for export success in a
given target market. [7] The left chart in Figure 3 depicts Georgia’s top 25 products with export potential
according to the EPI. The size of each bubble represents the product’s demand- side potential and
the length of the line represents the supply-side potential. The overall score is a combination of these
two factors and market access conditions and bilateral ease of trade. The PDI is used by countries
seeking to diversify and develop new export sectors to identify products that an economy cannot
yet export competitively but which appear feasible. [8] The right chart in Figure 3 shows Georgia’s top
25 products with the highest probability of successful diversification according to the PDI. The size
of each bubble again represents the demand-side potential, while the length of the line represents
the relative proximity between the products already exported with a revealed comparative advantage
and the new product, such proximity increasing the ease to diversify. The overall rank is again a
combination of these two factors and the market access conditions. Looking at export potential,
for example, medicaments for retail sale are the product with the strongest demand potential in the
world. Favorable options for export diversification include almonds (Figure 3).

##### **Public Interventions for Export Promotion**

_Theoretical Background_


_**Export-oriented government strategies, trade policies, and EPPs help economies to leverage positive**_
_**externalities and increase economic growth.**_ The role of governments in the economy has evolved
from marginal to central in recent years. Industrial policy is on the rise (Aiginger and Sieber 2006;
Peneder 2017; Wigger 2019). Labor market challenges in creating quality jobs, the unsatisfactory
rates of productive transformation, and the low growth dynamics following the financial crises have
created a need for proactive government strategies to diversify and upgrade economies. Industrial,
innovation, and trade policies are now increasingly intertwined. Global trade is undergoing a significant
transformation driven by the 4th Industrial Revolution, digitalization, and decarbonization, leading to
changes in trade policy (Aiginger and Rodrik 2020; Chang and Andreoni 2020; Criscuolo et al. 2019;
Dolfsma and Mamica 2020; Jones and Adam 2023; Rodrik 2014).


_Analytical Approach for Public Interventions to Promote Exports_

prevent exporters from entering markets. Problems on the supply side could be related to variables
such as factor inputs and incentive frameworks as well as the lack of regional innovation systems,
clusters, or entrepreneurial ecosystems (Audretsch et al. 2019; Reis and Farole 2012; Qian and Acs
2023; Valaskivi 2016). Economic policymaking, for example for export promotion, also needs to be
embedded in a broader framework to capture all relevant policy aspects or risk negatively influencing

have to deal with complex customs procedures and documentation requirements. Noncompliance
with requirements can lead to delays, penalties, or even the rejection of shipments. Ratification of
conventions can help improve export performance. Countries also have their own technical regulations
and product standards that exporters must comply with. Meeting these requirements often involves
additional testing or compliance procedures, which can be time-consuming and costly for exporters.
Capacity development can help overcome these obstacles (Beine and Coulombe 2007; Gylfason,
Martinez-Zarzoso, and Wijkman 2015; Klasen 2017; Mahmood and Jongwanich 2018).

_Element 3: Awareness and Capacity_


_**EPPs help to raise awareness and develop capacity.**_ Knowledge about the importance of foreign
trade for business development and the mechanics of international trade is important for triggering the
interest of non-exporting firms. Managers, especially in small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs),
often lack knowledge about cultural differences, export practices, documentation requirements, risks
in international business, and financing needs. The same applies to knowledge about geographic
distances, product specifications, or product use. A successful educational policy and informational
support are thus related to the qualification of employees, management training, business seminars, or
conferences. Barriers to export performance regarding capacity also include deficiencies in research,
development, and innovation (R&D&I). As previous studies have shown, there is a positive interplay
between innovation and exports. Government intervention can help mitigate market failures if a system
of interrelated innovation and export promotion tools is successfully implemented (De Fuentes, Niosi,
and Peerally 2021; Freel, Lui, and Rammer 2019; Klasen 2012).


_Element 4: Target Markets and Customers_


_**Supporting firms in their search for target markets and customers is a key objective of the EPP.**_
A lack of knowledge about target markets can lead to missed opportunities. Analyses of foreign markets
through country and sector assessments are not only important components for raising awareness
and building capacity but also for finding promising target markets (Leonidou, Palihawadana, and
Theodosiou 2011; Morgan and Katsikeas 1997; Reid 1981; Verwaal and Donkers 2002). In addition,
weak sector visibility and a lack of a positive image of the sector can lead to problems in finding
customers. Operational support from the government is crucial for promoting the business profile
of exporters and helping firms to grow into new and additional foreign markets [11] (Durmuşoğlu et al.
2012; Seringhaus and Rosson 1994; Wilkinson and Brouthers 2000).


_Element 5: Closing Export Deals_


_**Export promotion also includes public support when closing export deals.**_ A lack of customer
relationships makes it difficult to close export deals. Trade promotion tools can help to overcome this
obstacle. In addition, exporters are often risk-averse and face challenges in price competitiveness. In
many countries, governments therefore offer direct and indirect financial incentives such as export
credits and trade credit insurance. They fill gaps in financing, insurance, or guarantee coverage that
would otherwise result in both export barriers and an insufficient range of offerings. Public interventions
through export credit agencies (ECAs) and export–import banks (EXIMs) provide financial support to
firms with international orientation (Jennekens and Klasen 2023; Klasen et al. 2022; Kurkela 2008;
Rotblatt 2018; Turguttopbas 2013). ECAs and EXIMs have a catalytic effect on export development
(Bird and Rowlands 2004; Morris and Shin 2006). [12]

_Global Perspective_


_**The pharmaceutical sector is influenced by a variety of global and industry-specific trends.**_ Global
demand is increasingly rapidly, which has been further amplified by the urgent need for vaccines and
therapeutics in response to the coronavirus disease (COVID-19) pandemic. This development has
presented additional opportunities for pharmaceutical companies in a market worth $1.4 trillion.
An important achievement of the industry is its ability to simultaneously meet the need for innovative
solutions and the overall needs for basic pharmaceuticals. Several factors contribute to this achievement,
including the emergence of new modalities, pressure to foster innovation, advances in digital
technology, and the redistribution of power among individual players. However, the sector also faces
various challenges, including declining productivity in research, development, and innovation (R&D&I);
escalating capital costs; inflationary pressures; labor market complexities; and global supply chain

sustainability, social responsibility, and governance standards—can serve as both an opportunity and a
constraint for pharmaceutical companies. Firms are therefore compelled to operate with lower costs and
shorter timelines while ensuring the maintenance of quality standards and the promotion of innovation
(Dukart et al. 2022; Nakashima, Kodama, and Sengoku 2023; PwC 2023a).

_Global Perspective_


_**The global almond market is experiencing rising demand in various areas.**_ Global almond production
is growing at a rate of about 3.7% per year in 2010–2019. One trend in recent years is the increasing
popularity of almonds as a healthy snack. Almonds are known for their high protein content, dietary
fiber, and healthy fats, making them a preferred choice for health-conscious consumers. Growing
awareness of the benefits of almonds has led to increased consumption and, consequently, a rise
in almond sales worldwide. In addition, almonds are used in the food industry as a key ingredient
in various products, including confectionery, baked goods, and nut butters. Another significant
development in the global almond market is the increasing adoption of almond milk as an alternative to
dairy products. Almond milk has gained popularity among people who are lactose intolerant or follow
a vegan or plant- based lifestyle. It is used as a substitute for traditional milk. Increasing awareness
of the environmental impact of dairy farming and the associated health benefits have propelled
the demand for almond milk, boosting the almond market. On a global scale, the US dominates
the almond market as the leading producer and exporter. Other countries such as Australia, Spain,
and Iran also contribute to the global supply. The market dynamics and trade patterns for almonds
are influenced by factors such as weather conditions, government policies, and international trade
agreements (ABA 2022; ABC 2022; Louw, Oosthuizen, and Flandorp 2017).

##### **Potential Opportunity: Electric Vehicle** **Battery Recycling**

_Global Perspective_


_**The recycling of electric vehicle (EV) batteries has attracted a great deal of attention in recent years.**_
As the demand for EVs continues to rise, there is growing concern about the environmental impact
of disposing spent batteries. Recycling these batteries has become a crucial aspect of sustainable
energy solutions. Many countries and organizations worldwide have recognized the importance of
developing robust recycling systems for EV batteries. They are investing in R&D&I to develop efficient
and environmentally friendly recycling processes. The focus is on recovering valuable materials such as
lithium, cobalt, nickel, and other metals from spent batteries to reduce reliance on mining and decrease
the overall environmental footprint. In Europe, initiatives such as the European Battery Alliance and
the EU Battery Regulation aim to establish a circular economy for EV batteries. Countries such as the
PRC, Japan, and the US are also taking measures to promote the recycling of EV batteries. They are
developing regulations and policies to promote the collection, reuse, and recycling of batteries, and
to encourage partnerships between government, industry, and research institutions. Technological
advances play a significant role in improving battery recycling processes. Innovative methods such as
hydrometallurgical and pyrometallurgical processes are being explored to effectively extract valuable
materials (Breiter et al. 2023; Luo 2021; Veolia 2022; Zhang et al. 2021).


_Sector Overview_


_**Georgia has a history of manufacturing products for the automotive, aircraft, and locomotive**_
_**industries.**_ After the collapse of the Soviet Union, companies restructured or ceased operations.
In recent years, Georgia has seen a gradual revival of manufacturing. Aero-Structure Technologies
Cyclone, for example, is a new joint venture between the Israeli electronics company Elbit Systems
and the Georgian state-owned Partnership Fund. Furthermore, the automotive sector in Georgia is
a dynamic and promising industry and has established itself as a regional hub for the car trade since
2005. It is estimated that Georgia’s automotive industry generates an average of 31% of total revenues
from car reexport activities annually. The formal Georgian automotive business comprises three major
subsectors: car trade, car parts, and car servicing and repair. With a growing number of local and
international firms establishing their presence, the sector has seen notable development and success
in recent years.


_**There is also considerable investment in the use of EVs and charging networks.**_ For example,
ADB will invest about $7.6 million in Tegeta’s inaugural green bond to finance the deployment of
zero- emission light, road, and non-road EVs and the expansion of Georgia’s EV charging network.
Tegeta is Georgia’s leading automotive products and services company (ADB 2023b). Although EV
battery recycling does not yet play a role in Georgia, there is an opportunity to strive for a leading
role in the EV industry following the automotive sector’s economic vitality and potential expansion in
the country. The recycling of EV batteries could therefore emerge as a promising area related to the
country’s existing automotive sector.

_Value Chains_


_**Business models in other countries range from companies that specialize in specific steps of the**_
_**value chain,**_ such as shredding, to integrated companies that cover the entire process from collecting
end- of-life batteries to refining battery material. Foreign direct investment would show the greatest
potential to grow into the EV battery recycling business in Georgia, as there are currently no value
chains for EV battery recycling in the country. Currently, two common battery recycling technology
pathways are used, with ongoing R&D&I focusing on innovative recycling methods: once end-oflife batteries are received at recycling facilities, they undergo testing, discharging, and disassembly.
The disassembled batteries are then shredded, undergoing thermal treatment to remove impurities
such as plastic, optimize separation of active electrode materials and collector foil, and convert valuable
metals into a reduced form for efficient hydrometallurgical processing. After screening and sorting
steps that utilize the physical properties of the battery components, such as size, magnetism, density,
and conductivity, the process produces multiple material fractions, including the “black mass.” This
is a powder containing materials like nickel, cobalt, lithium, and graphite. Alternatively, a mechanical
pre-treatment can be performed without the use of heat, resulting in a more complex composition of
black mass. One of the two typical processing methods is then used. In hydrometallurgical processing,
the screened black mass undergoes extensive treatment with acids that dissolve the metals. In a series
of steps, including solvent extraction, crystallization, and precipitation, the various metal ions are
separated, which can then be used to produce battery-ready materials like nickel sulfate or lithium
carbonate. In pyrometallurgical recycling, on the other hand, the batteries are smelted directly in a
furnace to extract cobalt, nickel, and copper as an alloy, with the other components usually ending up
as slag. The alloy produced is then further processed using a comparatively simple hydrometallurgical
refining process to extract the raw materials and produce battery metal salts that are ready for the
production of battery precursors. In addition, various new recycling methods are being explored as part
of ongoing R&D&I and commercialization efforts, including direct recycling or recycling of hydroactive
material into cathodes (Breiter et al. 2023; Veolia 2022). Figure 22 provides the EV battery value chain.

Palihawadana, and Theodosiou 2011; Seringhaus and Rosson 1994; Verwaal and Donkers 2002;
Wilkinson and Brouthers 2000). The following paragraphs provide key recommendations for Georgia
and a summary of specific action items.


_**The Government of Georgia could further develop EPPs for target markets and customers.**_
Enterprise Georgia’s export promotion activities include coordinating, organizing, and cofinancing
business-to-business (B2B) exhibitions, supporting local companies in making new trade connections,
acquiring potential partners, and diversifying into export markets. In addition, Enterprise Georgia
promotes exports by planning, organizing, and cofinancing B2B meetings for export-oriented
Georgian companies to find new partners. However, extended activities with an improved and
structured Global Trade Opportunities program could better support businesses in sectors with high
export potential in identifying promising target markets and suitable customers. For example, the
challenging regulatory environment in the pharmaceutical sector demonstrates the need for strong
partnerships between exporters and buyers in target markets that require additional GMP certification.
Enhanced components of a Global Trade Opportunities program may include the development of
comprehensive sectoral market access activities with both technical and financial support for existing
and emerging exporters, specifically (1) providing more up-to-date information on the potential
of various international markets; (2) facilitating extended B2B linkages with potential dealers,
wholesale buyers, and retailers, including arranging virtual meetings; (3) increasing advertising, public
relations agent services and other promotional activities; and (4) facilitating and subsidizing more
regular and extended attendance in prestigious international industry events. Again, support for the
pharmaceutical industry could have a positive impact on other industries. Increased collaboration
with embassies abroad and the expansion of their trade-related roles would also help to identify more
potential business opportunities in target markets that could be matched with and contested by
Georgian exporters. In addition, an easily accessible, extended and up-to-date “Buy from Georgia”
online directory would make it easier for foreign buyers to contact Georgian firms, resulting in greater
trade opportunities. In addition, the Georgian government could develop a new program for target
markets and customers for almond producers, supporting their orientation toward organic, high- value
production and corresponding export market positioning. Box 3 gives the example of Danish embassies

_**The government should consider implementing a public export financing scheme.**_ Public export loan
programs strengthen the competitiveness of exporters by providing them with access to affordable
financing. Georgian exporters often require significant upfront investment to fulfill large orders or
expand into new markets. Access to working capital loans specifically tailored to export activities would
enable exporters to secure the necessary capital and meet customers’ demands. By offering flexible
and competitive financing options to foreign buyers, the program would allow exporters to better
penetrate new markets. An export financing scheme should be developed in partnership with the
National Bank of Georgia, the country’s commercial banks, experienced development organizations,
and foreign ECAs or EXIMs. It could be hosted by Enterprise Georgia, for example.

the National Bank of Georgia, commercial banks and other financial institutions, Enterprise Georgia
could develop a public trade credit insurance program. This would ensure accessibility, awareness,

instruments should be assessed. An international best practice example of a recently established

##### **A Way Forward**


_**The challenges for Georgia’s export sector performance are numerous; the same is true for the**_
_**corresponding recommendations derived in the course of this study.**_ These recommendations
vary in their impact and potential to facilitate and diversify exports. It would be best if they were
all considered, followed, and implemented. However, some of them remain the most critical and
impactful for these objectives and should therefore be addressed first.


_**It is imperative to address the challenges currently faced by Georgian exporters by adopting**_
_**an Export Promotion Strategy 2030.**_ The development of a well-structured export strategy is
crucial for Georgia’s efforts to expand its market presence, increase export revenues, create more
jobs, and improve its overall competitiveness. By successfully implementing the Export Promotion
Strategy 2030 along with the establishment of key performance indicators (KPIs), the Government
of Georgia can create a conducive environment for businesses to thrive on the global stage. This
strategy document could include essential elements such as identifying drivers and strategic
choices, prioritizing policies, and outlining concrete actions to support exports. This comprehensive
approach will lay a solid foundation for an export ecosystem and facilitate specific activities related
to EPPs and other support initiatives.

_**A Team Georgia, led by the Ministry of Economy and Sustainable Development and managed by**_
_**Enterprise Georgia, could be the strategic hub for the implementation of the Export Promotion**_

strategic framework based on a policy that encompasses the entire export ecosystem. The emphasis
should be on priorities and outcomes to improve the competitiveness of Georgian exporters
in various sectors. Examples used in this study could include pharmaceuticals, apparel, jewelry,

Georgia Exports and Value Chain Study


Analyzing how Georgia can diversify its exports, this report shows how it could potentially ramp up its
pharmaceutical, apparel, jewelry, and almond exports, and assesses how an effective export strategy would
drive more resilient economic growth. Based on stakeholder interviews, it considers the trends, value chains,
and opportunities for each of the high potential sectors and explores how Georgia can leverage its location,
favorable business environment, and free-trade agreements. Emphasizing the need for greater innovation
and strong government support, it recommends taking a “Team Georgia” approach that centers on building
an ecosystem to improve export promotion, increase target market access, and support exporters.
