### Background of Discussion

**1. In the third quarter of 2024, Korea's customs basis export value increased by**

**10.4% compared to the same period in 2023, expanding from 10.1% in the**

**previous quarter. However, the real GDP goods export volume decreased by**
**0.6% compared to the previous quarter** **[1)]** **.**


**2. The decrease in GDP goods exports in the third quarter of 2024 compared to**

**the previous quarter can be attributed to temporary factors, such as strikes in**

**the automotive sector. However, it seems that the impact of structural factors**

**was larger than initially anticipated.**


**3. The key drivers influencing Korea's export are intertwined and difficult to**
**separate clearly. However, they can generally be categorized into** **[➊]** **advancements**
**in AI** **[[Figure ]** **1]** **➋intensifying competition with China** **[Figure** **2], and** **➌rising U.S.**
**protectionism** **[Figure** **3].** For example, the export of high-performance
semiconductors, such as HBM used in AI infrastructure, continues to perform
well, but the demand for other general-purpose semiconductors has slowed due
to weak demand and the rising market share of Chinese competitors.
Additionally, exports of chemical products and steel have been significantly
sluggish due to intensifying competition with Chinese products. **Furthermore,**

**under the second term of the Trump administration, the intensification of**

**protectionist policies (Trumpnomics 2.0) could bring unforeseen changes to**

**Korea's trade environment.**


**4. These driving forces will continue to significantly affect Korea’s exports**

**moving forward, but it is difficult to predict their scale and direction at this**
**moment.** Given the high uncertainty surrounding the trade environment, it is
important to assess how these key drivers will impact Korea's export in the
future.

#### **②Non-IT products exports are sluggish due to weak** **demand and China's overproduction.**

**9.** **Non-IT exports have been affected by weak import demand from major**
**export destinations, including China.** Exports of chemical products and steel have
steadily declined in value since 2023. Petroleum products exports, which had
seen year-on-year growth in the first half of 2024, experienced a decline in the
third quarter of 2024. [Figure 9] This sluggishness in non-IT exports is
fundamentally driven by a slowdown in import demand from major countries.
An analysis of the "effective import demand by product" was conducted by
selecting the top 20 export destinations for each product, calculating their
weighted average real import growth rate for 2024, using export shares from
2023. The results show that while demand for IT products increased by 4.8%
year-on-year, demand for non-IT products, such as automobiles (3.2%), chemical
products (3.4%), and steel (3.6%), exhibited relatively low growth rates. [[Figure] [10]]


**10. In particular, exports of chemicals and steel have been significantly impacted**
**by China's low-cost and over-supply.** Exports of petroleum products, such as
gasoline, fell by 35% year-on-year in October 2024 as international oil prices fell
to the $70 per barrel range since September, and refinery maintenance reduced
supply volumes. In the case of steel and chemical exports, the decline in global
prices, driven by weak demand and China's aggressive capacity expansion
leading to overproduction, has sustained the downward trend in exports. [[Figure] [11]]

### Structural Drivers of Korea's Exports

#### **① What is the Future of the Global IT Industry?**

**13. The global semiconductor industry is expected to rapidly shift towards**
**high-performance semiconductors, which is projected to drive an increase in**
**Korea's semiconductor exports.** As global big tech companies expand their
investments in AI infrastructure through 2025, shipments of products equipped
with high-performance semiconductors, such as AI accelerators and servers, are
expected to rise significantly [9)] . Additionally, the growing demand for faster
operating speeds and higher memory capacity to handle increasing computational
loads is expected to drive steady growth in exports of high-performance
products like HBM and NAND flash-based eSSDs by Korean companies. [Figure 13]

While the demand for IT devices such as PCs and mobile phones is expected to
recover slowly, [Figure 14] the recent reduction in DDR4 production by Korean
companies is likely to limit the impact of sluggish low-performance
semiconductor demand over time.


**14. If AI becomes more widely integrated into IT devices such as PCs and**
**smartphones through on-device AI, it will place upward pressure on Korea's**
**semiconductor** **exports.** In 2024, user devices equipped with AI-related
functionalities, including PCs and smartphones, began to enter the market, but
sales have fallen short of expectations. This is one of the main reasons for the
sluggish recovery in IT device demand. However, starting in 2025, new services
like AI agents are expected to launch, and demand for IT devices with built-in
AI features is anticipated to grow significantly. [10)] . In this scenario, demand for
high-performance semiconductors, an area where Korean companies hold a
technological edge, is likely to expand further [11)], positively impacting Korea's
exports.

#### **② How is China’s Role in Korea’s Exports Evolving?**

**16. Since joining the WTO in 2001, China has been Korea’s largest export market**

**and a key partner for trade surpluses, supported by cooperative relations within**

**the global value chain (GVC). However, it has recently transformed into a**
**competitor. Domestically,** China’s focus on a domestic-driven growth strategy [15)]

and advancements in technology have rapidly increased its self-sufficiency rates.


12) China, under its "Made in China 2025" initiative, aims to achieve a 70% semiconductor self-sufficiency rate by

2025 and has already invested 1 trillion yuan (approximately 200 trillion KRW) to this end. As a result, Chinese
semiconductor companies such as YMTC (NAND flash), CXMT (DRAM), and SMIC (foundry) continue to advance
their technologies and expand production capacities. Additionally, in May of this year, China announced its
third "National Semiconductor Industry Development Fund," focused on semiconductor self-sufficiency, with a
capital of 340 billion yuan (approximately 64 trillion KRW).
13) China is expected to further increase its self-sufficiency in general-purpose semiconductors by expanding

production capacity, particularly through CXMT, which is not yet included in the US Entity List.
14) IAccording to IB reports, the technological gap between Korean and Chinese semiconductor companies is

estimated to be 4–5 years for DRAM and approximately 2 years for NAND flash.
15) China has announced strategies such as the “Dual Circulation Strategy” (2020) and the “Domestic Market

Expansion Strategy” (2022). Additionally, it is implementing policies to narrow income gaps between urban
and rural areas and across social classes, strengthen public services such as social security and education
under the “Common Prosperity” policy, promote the advancement of the service industry, and enhance
market competition.


                      - 10 

Furthermore, through its “Belt and Road Initiative”, China is extending its "Red
Supply Chain [16)] " to ASEAN and South American countries, which is expected to
further reduce its reliance on imports from Korea. In fact, while China’s global
imports of intermediate goods grew at an average annual rate of 5.2% between
2017 and 2023, its imports from Korea increased by only 1.6% annually during
the same period.


**17. China is actively fostering advanced industries such as semiconductors, electric**

**vehicles (EVs), batteries, and solar panels as next-generation growth engines. This**

**is expected to intensify competition not only in China’s domestic market but also**
**in the global market.** China has already expanded its influence beyond its
domestic market to the global stage [17)] in Korea's key export categories, including
traditional intermediate goods such as steel, refining, and chemicals. [Figure 16] In
addition to semiconductors, China is actively promoting the so-called "New
Three Industries [18)] "—EVs, batteries, and solar panel—as next-generation growth
engines, further intensifying competition with Korea. Initiatives like “Made in
China 2025” and the “New Whole Nation System” policies, aimed at achieving
self-reliance in core science and technology, have significantly enhanced China's
potential in advanced manufacturing, which is now recognized as being among
the world’s best [19)] . [Table 1] China's technological advancement has also led to a
significant increase in Korea’s imports of intermediate goods from China, with
an average annual growth rate of 8.7% between 2017 and 2023. Since 2023,
Korea’s trade balance with China has shifted to a deficit. [Figure 17] Furthermore,
Korea's domestic market slowdown, combined with an increase in imports of
low-cost Chinese steel and chemical products, has created challenges for related
industries in Korea [20)] .

#### **③ How Will a Second Trump Administration Impact Korea?**


**19. President Trump is expected to swiftly implement an America-first policy**

Among the various related policies, **➊the imposition of high tariffs on China** and
**➋increased trade pressure on major trade surplus countries with the U.S.,**
**including Korea,** are expected to have a significant impact on Korea’s exports.

**China, it is inevitable that there will be negative effects on Korea's exports to**
**China.** The U.S. has already regulated exports to China, including AI chips and
semiconductor manufacturing equipment, to block China’s access to advanced

**22. Considering the recent size of Korea’s trade surplus with the U.S., there is a**

### Implications

**23. Korea’s exports are expected to continue growing, driven by sustained global**
**AI investments, but the growth rate is likely to slow due to China’s rising**
**self-sufficiency, enhanced technological competitiveness, and expanding market**
**share, as well as the strengthening of U.S. protectionism.** Korea’s exports will
likely maintain an upward trend, centered on high-performance semiconductors,
supported by robust infrastructure investments from leading AI companies.
However, the increasing technological capability and self-sufficiency of China in
general-purpose semiconductors and other intermediate goods, along with the
intensification of U.S. protectionist policies, are expected to exert downward
pressure on exports. **As these structural constraints on exports are anticipated to**
**have long-term effects rather than disappearing in the short term, the following**
**efforts are essential to achieve sustained export growth.**


**23. First, efforts must be concentrated on fostering advanced industries, with**
**talent acquisition as the top priority.** Government support is essential for R&D
and facility investments in key strategic industries. In particular, as competition
between countries intensifies [25)] and the importance of industries like
semiconductors and AI core technologies as national strategic assets grows, it is
crucial to expand support further [26)] . Given the critical importance of securing
talent, measures must focus not only on cultivating domestic talent and attracting
global talent but also on providing incentives to prevent brain drain.
Additionally, since these industries require highly advanced manufacturing
facilities, support for facility investments, akin to measures implemented by other
major countries, is also necessary.


**24. Next, it is essential to move beyond a manufacturing-centered industrial**
**strategy and focus on fostering high-value-added services.** Korea’s economic
resources—raw materials, capital, and labor—are significantly limited compared to
major economies. Therefore, it is crucial to recognize that the efficient use of
production factors through a strategy of selection and concentration will
determine the future trajectory of Korea’s economic growth. If we continue to
maintain an economic structure established decades ago without pursuing
fundamental reforms, it is inevitable that the economy will gradually lose its
vitality. In this context, sectors with low productivity and limited potential for
technological advancement require an orderly restructuring, which should serve
as the foundation for cultivating new growth drivers. In particular, considering

**25. Lastly, policy efforts in diplomacy and trade are essential to mitigate trade**
**pressures and expand export markets.** One approach to alleviating U.S. trade
pressure is to shift import sources for crude oil, gas, and agricultural products
to the U.S. [27)] Additionally, as Korean companies actively expand into regions like
India and ASEAN to adapt to supply chain fragmentation, diplomatic support
should be strengthened to facilitate these efforts effectively.

Korean government preemptively increased imports from the U.S., focusing on raw materials like crude oil,
thereby enhancing its negotiating position.
