# Negotiating

## Working Together

Negotiations in Burkina Faso unfold through relationship first, then detail. Early conversations may circle around connection and context rather than terms, particularly when counterparts are assessing whether a partnership will hold over time. A colleague accustomed to front-loading contract discussion may read this as evasion or disinterest, while a Burkinabè counterpart may view an immediate push toward clauses as transactional or dismissive. The groundwork being laid is relational, and it shapes everything that follows.

The process rarely moves in a straight line. A point you considered settled may resurface later, or the conversation may shift to something seemingly unrelated before looping back. This allows both sides to test ideas, surface concerns that weren't obvious earlier, and confirm that nothing has been overlooked. Treating this as backtracking misses the function it serves. Rolling with the rhythm rather than pushing for linear closure opens space for perspectives you might otherwise not hear.

Pace can reflect both cultural preference and tactical positioning. Discussions may slow to allow for consultation with elders, trusted advisors, or those with final authority, or they may signal that your counterpart is weighing whether a better offer is possible. For this reason, presenting your strongest terms at the outset leaves little room to move. Leaving space for concessions that don't compromise your core position aligns with an expectation that negotiation is collaborative adjustment, not a fixed exchange.

Building rapport with those close to decision-making circles moves things from talk to action. Personal goodwill, such as an introduction or support on an unrelated matter, strengthens the partnership and is understood to be reciprocal over time.
