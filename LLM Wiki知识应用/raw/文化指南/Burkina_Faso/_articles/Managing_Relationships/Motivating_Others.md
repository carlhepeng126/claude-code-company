# Motivating Others

## Managing Relationships

Motivation in Burkina Faso grows from collective purpose rather than individual reward. People give their strongest effort when they feel part of a team or project that reflects well on the wider group, and success is measured less by personal achievement than by what it means for the household and community. A manager who frames work as contributing to the organization's reputation, or who recognizes someone's role in a shared accomplishment, taps into a deeper source of energy than individual incentives alone.

Financial compensation matters, but it rarely drives commitment on its own. What sustains engagement is a work environment that feels supportive and human, where contributions are noticed and acknowledged in the moment. A brief word of thanks after a difficult week, or an invitation to take part in a high-profile project, signals belonging and trust. Public recognition, especially in front of the group, strengthens someone's standing and motivates continued dedication.

Strict deadlines and rigid targets, by contrast, can drain momentum. Time in Burkina Faso is understood more fluidly, shaped by family obligations and a sense that outcomes rest ultimately with God. People outside senior roles rarely prioritize overtime over personal or family commitments, and detailed long-term planning is not the norm. A manager who adjusts timelines while keeping the team moving forward will see more sustained effort than one who enforces fixed schedules without flexibility.
