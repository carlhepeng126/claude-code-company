# Business Lunch & Dinner

## Meetings & Presentations

A meal in Bulgaria often begins with shopska salad and rakia, the fruit brandy that acts as more ritual than drink. Even if you prefer water, acknowledging this opening sequence shows you understand the rhythm of the occasion. The brandy itself, usually grape but sometimes plum or peach, appears with a kind of ceremonial consistency. The expectation is to respect the ritual, not to match anyone glass for glass.

What follows tends to unfold slowly. Three-hour meals with multiple courses, cigarettes lit between dishes, and conversations that loop back on themselves serve a deliberate purpose. These dinners function as trust labs, where people watch how you listen, whether you dominate, and how you treat waiting staff. For a colleague from a culture where decisions are made quickly and meals serve efficiency, this can feel indirect or slow. From the Bulgarian side, a visitor who rushes the meal or checks their phone may come across as transactional, signaling that the relationship itself holds less value than the outcome.

Some colleagues follow Orthodox fasting periods with varying degrees of strictness, avoiding meat or dairy on certain days. They may not announce this in advance, but they will notice if there is nothing they can eat. When you host, choose a menu that accommodates meat-eaters, vegetarians, and those observing fasting without drawing attention to it. The practical courtesy lies in offering range, not commentary.

What emerges at the dinner table, over time and across courses, often tells you more about values and reliability than anything said in a formal meeting. Treating these meals as wasted time means missing the assessment that is quietly taking place.
