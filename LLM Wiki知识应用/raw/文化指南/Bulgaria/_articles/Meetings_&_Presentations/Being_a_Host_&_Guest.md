# Being a Host & Guest

## Meetings & Presentations

A full table reflects how a host sees you. Having more food than anyone could eat, several salads, meats, bread, something made from scratch, shows that the guest is worth the trouble. For generations who grew up with empty shops and rationing, being able to offer plenty is a quiet pride. Commenting that there is too much or that food will be wasted can unintentionally touch that history. As a guest, praise the effort and the taste. If the host offers you a package to take home, accept it.

What a host chooses to show you tells a story. In villages, that might mean the garden, preserves put up for winter, animal pens, family photographs. In cities, it might be bookshelves, travel souvenirs, or a recently renovated flat. These are symbols of resilience or achievement, depending on the setting. As a guest, ask about what they share. Showing curiosity is a way of acknowledging the life they are inviting you into.

Seating and toasting follow hierarchy. The most senior or oldest person sits first, makes the opening toast, and starts the meal. The toast before drinking is *naz drave* (good health), offered to each person with eye contact. Leave food or drink on your plate and in your glass when you are full. A host will keep filling both until you do. Portions are large as a default. If you are invited to someone's home, bring wine or spirits for the host and flowers for the hostess in an uneven number, not lilies or chrysanthemums. Offer to remove your shoes at the door and say something appreciative about the home.
