# Delegating & Supervising

## Managing Relationships

Bulgarian organizations are broadly hierarchical, shaped in part by the legacy of Communist-era management practices that favored centralized control and discouraged individual initiative. In traditional companies, managers are expected to direct clearly and subordinates to follow instructions without questioning. Delegation in these settings typically means distributing tasks, not decision-making authority. Passing significant decisions down the line can be read as a manager abdicating responsibility, and employees who have been conditioned to execute rather than shape outcomes may resist taking on that kind of accountability. Some will solve problems independently rather than escalate them, preferring to avoid the appearance of failure or incompetence.

The generational divide can be striking. Younger Bulgarians, especially in IT and technology sectors, are increasingly comfortable with autonomy, flat structures, and taking initiative. They have often studied or worked abroad and expect to contribute beyond execution. A team leader managing across generations may find that an approach effective with a 28-year-old developer in Sofia falls flat with a 55-year-old colleague in a traditional firm. Read the individual and the organizational context, not just the country.

Uncertainty avoidance runs high. Clear rules, defined scope, and explicit process are essential. Ambiguous briefs or open-ended delegation create discomfort and slow execution. When assigning work, be specific about what is required, what the boundaries are, and what decisions the person is expected to make versus escalate. Assume nothing is understood unless it has been stated clearly.

Decision-making tends to be thorough and analytical. What can appear slow to a colleague accustomed to faster iteration reflects careful consideration of each element before moving forward. The output is typically reliable and well-considered, grounded in detail rather than speed.
