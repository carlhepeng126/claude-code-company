# Decision-Making

## Working Together

In many Bulgarian firms, particularly small and mid-sized businesses, final decisions rest with one owner or a very small circle, even when professional managers are in place. Staff usually know who truly holds this authority, regardless of what the organization chart suggests. Understanding this early can prevent a lot of wasted effort. Rather than expecting the person across the table to commit, ask tactfully who ultimately needs to feel comfortable with the decision and tailor your case accordingly, even if you never meet them directly.

Mid-level managers often consult trusted friends, former colleagues, or family members working in the same industry before committing to a decision. This network operates alongside, and sometimes ahead of, internal team discussions. These outside conversations help managers feel secure before they stake their reputation on a course of action. The caution reflects a deeper concern rooted in decades of blame culture across various political and economic systems. Many managers are less worried about missing an opportunity than about being singled out if something goes wrong, which can tip decisions toward the safer option.

If a decision feels slow to form, the delay is often not disorganization but risk avoidance. Offering ways to share the downside, such as pilot programs, staged rollouts, or built-in review points, can help decision-makers move forward. Framing a proposal so the risk doesn't sit with one person alone makes it easier to say yes.
