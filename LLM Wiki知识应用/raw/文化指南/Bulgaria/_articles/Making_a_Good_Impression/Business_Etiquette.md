# Business Etiquette

## Making a Good Impression

Bulgarians often express their realism through mrrrnene, a ritual of shared complaining about everyday frustrations like traffic, bills, or politicians, usually laced with humour. This isn't pessimism; it's a way of signalling that you understand how things really work and that you're not naive. Joining in lightly, without offering solutions or expressing pity, shows you're comfortable with their frankness. If you stay overly positive or lecture about what should be done differently, you may come across as disconnected from the lived reality around you.

Achievement is typically understated. Even successful companies may describe themselves as small or downplay what they've accomplished. This reflects a wariness of boastfulness and a preference for proving yourself through action rather than self-promotion. If you present with too much hype or claim success too loudly, colleagues may hold back, waiting to see if you can actually deliver. Lead with concrete examples and quiet confidence, and let your track record speak without embellishment.

Small gestures like bringing local sweets or paying for coffee are welcomed, but anything lavish or tied directly to a deal can raise discomfort. The country's long history of clientelism and corruption means that gifts which feel transactional, rather than symbolic, trigger suspicion. Keep them modest and clearly personal, never ambiguous.

In many sectors and smaller towns, informal conversations still happen during cigarette breaks. Even if you don't smoke, joining colleagues outside signals openness and puts you where decisions are often quietly shaped.
