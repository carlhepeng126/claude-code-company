# Forms of Address

## Making a Good Impression

Bulgarian has two forms of "you": the formal Vie and the informal ti. The formal pronoun opens most professional relationships, especially with older colleagues or anyone whose seniority or professional standing you're still establishing. Switching to ti is a real marker of closeness, and it happens when the relationship is ready, not when efficiency suggests it. Pushing informality too early reads as presumption, a misjudgment of where you stand. Wait for the Bulgarian counterpart to invite the shift, either explicitly or by using ti first. That invitation tells you the relationship has crossed a threshold.

Titles matter because they reflect effort in a system where education was long understood as a primary route to advancement. A colleague who introduces themselves as "doktor" or "inzhener" (engineer) has invested years in earning that status, and the title anchors their professional identity. Using it signals that you see that investment. Omitting it, especially in early interactions, can feel like an erasure. Start with the surname and the title if one is offered. For someone from a context where first names arrive quickly, this formality can feel slow. For Bulgarians, it's calibration: a way of honoring achievement and maintaining appropriate social distance until the relationship justifies something warmer.

Surnames themselves carry information. Endings like -ov or -eva, or names that sound Turkish or Roma, quietly place people by region, ethnicity, or social history. Bulgarians notice. They may never comment aloud, but the surname becomes part of how someone is categorized: urban or rural, old family or new money, insider or outsider. This matters less in day-to-day work than in how quickly trust forms or which social networks open. You don't need to decode the system yourself, but understanding that it exists helps explain why introductions sometimes carry more weight than the words alone suggest.
