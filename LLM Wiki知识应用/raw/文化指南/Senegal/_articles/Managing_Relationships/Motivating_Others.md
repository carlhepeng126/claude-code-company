# Motivating Others

## Managing Relationships

Many Senegalese professionals respond strongly to work that upholds their dignity and allows them to support others. The concepts of *jom* or *diom* (dignity, courage) and *danké* (solidarity) run deep. Being someone who stands tall, contributes meaningfully, and doesn't abandon their people brings real satisfaction. Work that publicly undermines someone's standing, or isolates them from the chance to help family or community, can quietly drain commitment in ways salary alone won't repair.

The job itself often means something beyond the role. Families and villages take pride when someone works at a respected NGO, bank, ministry, or international company. That affiliation becomes part of how they are known. Recognition that connects a project's success back to a person's region or community, or that frames the work in terms of helping others stand on their feet rather than just meeting targets, tends to resonate more than abstract performance metrics.

Younger staff in particular worry about stagnation. Being stuck in a role with no visible way forward is a common frustration. Clear pathways matter: training opportunities, the possibility of promotion, exposure to new responsibility, even travel. The promise of development has to be credible. Broken promises are remembered and do lasting damage, both to individual motivation and to a manager's reputation.

A colleague from a fast-moving corporate environment might interpret this focus on mobility and visible respect as a lack of urgency around immediate deliverables. From a Senegalese perspective, that same colleague's emphasis on short-term output without attention to belonging or progression may read as transactional and ultimately unsustainable.
