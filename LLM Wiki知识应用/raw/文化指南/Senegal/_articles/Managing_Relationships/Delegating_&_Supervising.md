# Delegating & Supervising

## Managing Relationships

Authority in Senegal draws on a deep cultural template shaped by the Sufi brotherhoods that organize much of religious and social life. The relationship between marabout (spiritual guide) and disciple, built on loyalty, obedience, and personal devotion, creates expectations that extend into the workplace. Supervisors are believed to hold their positions because of greater experience and knowledge, and it would be unusual for them to openly consult those lower in the hierarchy when making decisions. At the same time, managers are expected to show genuine care for their team's wellbeing beyond the purely professional. This means being present for family obligations, listening to personal concerns, and building trust through familiarity before moving to task. Invest time in personal conversation and relationship-building first. A supervisor who moves straight to work without this foundation will be seen as disrespectful and may find that cooperation becomes more difficult to secure.

Give explicit, detailed instructions. Employees will follow direction carefully and expect clarity. Vague briefs create confusion that may not be raised openly, particularly given the indirectness of communication around sensitive topics. If something is unclear or unrealistic, team members are unlikely to challenge it directly. Instead, they may agree to a deadline they believe is aspirational rather than fixed, preferring to say yes to avoid disappointment. Regular progress check-ins are far more effective than relying on a single deadline, and they provide a face-saving way to surface issues before they become problems.

Never correct or criticize an employee in front of others. Public criticism is a serious breach, and any corrective feedback should be handled privately and diplomatically. If the conversation is particularly sensitive, using a trusted intermediary may be more effective than a direct approach. Face is protected through careful, indirect communication, and managers who ignore this will damage trust in ways that are difficult to repair.
