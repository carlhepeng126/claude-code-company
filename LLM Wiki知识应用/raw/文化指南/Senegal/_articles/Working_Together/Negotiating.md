# Negotiating

## Working Together

Conversations may circle around a topic rather than move through it point by point. This reflects a preference for exploring the full context before narrowing toward specifics. Stories, metaphors, and theoretical discussion help both sides understand intent and feel out shared ground. The aim is a solution that both parties can defend to their networks, whether that means brotherhoods, unions, villages, or regional constituencies. Deals are judged not only by commercial outcome but by how visibly they benefit specific communities. A proposal that appears to enrich only Dakar elites risks being seen as politically fragile, so building in local hiring, infrastructure support, or backing for social projects gives the agreement legitimacy beyond the contract itself.

Politeness often shapes how refusals are expressed. Rather than a direct "no," you might hear phrases like "on va voir" (we'll see), "inchallah," or "it's a bit complicated." These responses preserve the relationship while signaling hesitation or resistance. Behind the courtesy, there are often firm red lines. Listen for repeated patterns, shifts in tone, or nonverbal signals that reveal where flexibility ends. A colleague from a culture where decisions are stated explicitly may read this indirectness as evasion or lack of commitment. From a Senegalese perspective, the same colleague's bluntness can feel confrontational and dismissive of the relational foundation that makes any agreement sustainable.

In more delicate negotiations, an intermediary may be invited to help. This person, often a respected elder or skilled communicator playing a role echoing the griot tradition, can phrase concessions and demands in ways that preserve dignity on both sides. Working through such a figure is not a workaround but a form of diplomacy that allows difficult messages to be delivered without rupturing the relationship. Expect some theatrical moments as discussions unfold: raised voices or animated exchanges are often performative, signaling emphasis rather than genuine anger. Haggling is expected and admired when done with humor and respect. The final offer should come from the buyer so the seller retains face, and both parties should leave feeling they have gained something.
