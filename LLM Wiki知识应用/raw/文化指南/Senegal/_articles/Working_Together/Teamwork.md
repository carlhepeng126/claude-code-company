# Teamwork

## Working Together

Senegalese team culture reflects a deep-rooted orientation toward community rather than individual achievement, shaped by early experiences of collective responsibility. In many families, especially those with rural roots, children grow up caring for younger siblings and sharing resources across extended networks. This foundation shows up in the workplace as a natural inclination toward group harmony and mutual support. Teams function best when members feel they belong to something larger than themselves, and when collaboration is built on shared obligation rather than individual performance targets.

Cohesion is often established through informal mechanisms that outsiders may not immediately recognize. Wolof humour, slang, and teasing play a central role in determining who is considered part of the group. Colleagues may test new members with jokes or light provocation, and how you respond signals whether you understand the culture or remain at the edge of it. Accepting the teasing without defensiveness, and showing you can laugh at yourself, builds trust more reliably than formal introductions or credentials. Equally important are the social obligations surrounding life events. Teams expect to mobilize for baptisms, weddings, funerals, and hospitalizations, pooling money and attending ceremonies together. This participation is remembered and shapes how colleagues regard each other over time. If you contribute to these moments, you are seen as a decent colleague; if you treat them as optional, you remain an outsider.

Authority in Senegalese teams does not always align with formal titles. The person who really holds the team together may be the charismatic organizer, the union representative, someone closely connected to a marabout, or a highly competent assistant rather than the designated manager. People turn to these informal leaders first when problems arise, and their endorsement is often needed before a decision gains traction. Identify these figures early, involve them when planning changes, and acknowledge their role openly. A young expatriate manager placed over a team of older Senegalese colleagues may struggle to gain credibility, as age is closely associated with wisdom and experience. Respect for seniority runs deep, and leadership is expected to be energetic, physically present, and personally invested in team members' lives, not simply directive.

Ambition that singles out an individual can create tension. In some teams, members who visibly outperform their colleagues or seek recognition may be seen as disrupting group harmony, and in some cases, quietly undermined. The tall poppy syndrome is real. This does not mean people lack motivation, but rather that career advancement is weighed against family and community obligations, which often take precedence. A manager from a culture where individual achievement is openly rewarded may see this as lack of drive, while Senegalese colleagues may view the same manager's focus on personal targets as self-centered or insensitive to collective well-being.
