# Business Etiquette

## Making a Good Impression

Greetings in Senegal form the foundation of professional interaction, shaped by the cultural principle of teranga (hospitality) and respect for relationships. Colleagues typically exchange extended greetings in Wolof or French before turning to business, asking after family, health, and well-being. These are genuine inquiries, and rushing through them to reach the agenda can come across as impatient or dismissive. Accepting at least a small gesture of hospitality when offered, such as attaya (mint tea) or water, affirms the relationship. Declining too quickly or skipping this exchange altogether may feel cold in a culture where hospitality reflects both personal warmth and professional seriousness. A colleague from a culture where meetings begin with task focus first might interpret these rituals as inefficient preamble, while a Senegalese counterpart may read a swift pivot to business as a lack of respect or genuine interest in the relationship.

Conversations in Senegalese workplaces can be lively and expressive, with overlapping speech and animated gestures, but they rest on a foundation of respect, particularly toward elders and those in authority. The concept of sutura, which centers on dignity and discretion, means that openly shaming or correcting someone in front of others can damage trust and the relationship itself. Criticism is typically softened, delivered privately, or framed indirectly to protect face. Even when debates grow energetic, sarcasm or public put-downs cross a line. What may read as forthright feedback in some settings can feel like a violation here, so adjusting tone and timing matters when giving input or raising concerns.
