# Time Management

## Making a Good Impression

Senegal runs on two clocks. In Dakar's NGOs, start-ups, and banking districts, younger professionals expect punctuality and push back against "temps sénégalais," the local shorthand for meetings that slide and schedules that stretch. Yet outside these circles, and especially in government offices, older family firms, and rural areas, relationships and conversations still outrank the clock. Time bends for people, for prayer, and for the unpredictable realities of infrastructure and season.

When someone suggests sharing attaya, the strong, sweet mint tea brewed in three slow rounds, they are inviting you into a ritual that can structure an hour or more of layered conversation. Treating this like a quick coffee break misreads the intent. The tea itself creates the conditions for trust and connection, and hurrying through it weakens the relationship you came to build. Schedule meetings with this rhythm in mind, and resist the urge to cut social moments short in the name of efficiency.

Senegal's calendar reshapes around major religious events. The Grand Magal of Touba, Gamou, Tabaski, and Korité pull people back to their villages of origin for days or even weeks. Offices empty, transport stalls, and key figures go off-grid. Avoid scheduling critical milestones or decisions during these periods, and build your project timeline with buffer zones around them.

Dakar's traffic, seasonal floods, and construction turn a 20-minute commute into an hour and a half without warning. A day planned around three meetings across the city may already be overambitious. Build realistic schedules that account for physical and seasonal constraints, and confirm timings the morning of, not days ahead.
