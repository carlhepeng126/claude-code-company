# Making Contacts & Scheduling Appointments

## Making a Good Impression

In Senegal, the route to a serious business contact often runs through networks that aren't visible on an org chart. Sufi brotherhoods like the Mouride and Tijani, along with their spiritual leaders known as marabouts, shape trust and access in ways that formal introductions don't always capture. A recommendation from a trusted disciple or a figure linked to these networks can open doors that weeks of emails won't. Religion here isn't separate from professional life; it's woven into how credibility is established and relationships are formed.

Family, neighbourhood and hometown ties also drive how contacts are made. In Dakar and other cities, people rely on "grand frère" or "grande sœur" figures, older mentors who guide younger professionals, alongside schoolmates, cousins and local football club connections. Before committing, people quietly check who you're linked to. If you're unknown, working with someone who has standing in these circles can make the difference. The Senegalese diaspora in France, Italy, Spain and the US often serve as bridges, translating expectations and vouching for outsiders who want to work credibly back home.

When planning visits or setting timelines, check the religious calendar. Events like the Grand Magal of Touba and the Gamou pull huge numbers of people away from offices and into pilgrimage cities for days. Scheduling a major milestone during these periods means you'll be waiting.

Confirm appointments by phone once you're in country, and don't pack your schedule tightly. Meetings may start late or run longer than planned, and Dakar traffic can slow things considerably. Flexibility here shows you understand how things work.
