# Business Lunch & Dinner

## Meetings & Presentations

Sharing a bowl at midday is where relationships solidify in Senegal. The classic pattern is a communal lunch, often with colleagues, extended family, or neighbours gathered around a single dish, and a more family-centred evening meal. A foreign colleague who always eats alone at their desk or returns to the hotel signals distance, not professionalism. Joining the group, even if you can't share the main bowl, builds trust in ways that meetings rarely do. This is where real stories get told.

Regional dishes function as identity markers. Ceebu jën from Saint-Louis, thiebou yapp, yassa, mafé, caldou, or arroz from Casamance each link to a specific place and family tradition. People have emotional ties to their regional version. Serving or praising a dish acknowledges someone's background, not just their taste. For a colleague from a culture where meals are treated as breaks rather than relationship-building moments, this depth of attention to food can feel unfamiliar. From a Senegalese perspective, a foreign partner who never asks about the origin of a dish may come across as indifferent to the people behind it.

Fasting cycles affect meal timing year-round. Beyond Ramadan, voluntary fasts linked to Sufi practices or church commitments are common, often on Mondays or Thursdays. Eating visibly in shared spaces during Ramadan can feel intrusive. An invitation to iftar is a high-trust gesture, and declining without explanation can distance you from the group. If you accept, arrive on time and stay through the meal.
