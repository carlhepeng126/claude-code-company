# Business Meeting Culture

## Meetings & Presentations

A great deal happens before and after the formal meeting. In Senegal, colleagues often sound out a topic in advance through corridor and courtyard conversations, speaking in Wolof or Pulaar to test reactions, build consensus, or ease concerns. The official session then becomes a more public acknowledgement of what has already been felt out. A colleague from a culture where decisions are made in the meeting room may feel they're watching a scripted performance; a Senegalese participant may feel their counterpart is pushing prematurely for decisions that need wider, quieter agreement first. Investing time in these informal moments before and after the agenda is where real opinions surface and alignment is shaped.

Meetings themselves reflect Senegal's griot and oral debate traditions. Even technical discussions often feature long, crafted speeches, proverbs, and call-and-response exchanges. Cutting someone off mid-story cuts off their authority. If you come from a culture where brevity signals respect for others' time, allow room for this spoken performance. Your concise, bullet-point style may need to stretch to fit the rhythm of the room.

Hierarchy is visible in seating and in who opens and closes discussion, but inside that frame, colleagues may joke, tease, and challenge through humor or cousinage, the joking kinship ties that allow indirect critique without confronting status. Read both the formal seating plan and the informal joking patterns to understand who holds decision-making power and who holds influence.

In some sectors, there is a live awareness that unions and youth movements watch what happens in meeting rooms. People may be cautious about decisions that could trigger strikes or street protest. When pitching change, ask quietly how unions or younger staff might see the proposal, and be ready to adjust to avoid decisions that look explosive beyond the room.
