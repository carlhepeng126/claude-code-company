# Business Entertaining

## Meetings & Presentations

Business meals in Bhutan are rare and carefully considered. The culture of doma (betel nut chewing), widespread vegetarianism rooted in Buddhist teachings against harming living beings, and the centrality of family meals at home mean that entertaining colleagues or clients over food is neither routine nor expected. When it does happen, it tends to follow relationship milestones rather than transactional aims, and is more likely in urban settings or with foreign partners than within purely domestic business circles.

Where meals do take place, they unfold slowly and without agenda. Business is seldom discussed, and raising it directly can feel jarring. The gathering itself builds connection. Colleagues from cultures where meals are used to close deals or negotiate terms may find this approach indirect, while Bhutanese counterparts may read an early turn to business as impatient or instrumentalizing. If a topic does emerge, it usually comes late and lightly, introduced by the host.

Alcohol is present in Bhutanese social life, but attitudes vary widely. Ara (locally distilled spirit) may be offered in informal settings, but many abstain for personal or religious reasons. Follow the host's lead and avoid assuming that offering or accepting a drink will be read as warmth. What builds trust is time spent without pressure, and a willingness to let the relationship unfold at its own pace rather than using the meal to accelerate it.
