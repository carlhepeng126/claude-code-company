# Business Etiquette

## Making a Good Impression

In Bhutan, workplace etiquette is shaped by deeply rooted values around hierarchy, respect, and collective harmony. Relationships are built on trust and reputation, and how you present yourself from the outset shapes how colleagues and counterparts will engage with you over time. Formality tends to be higher in government settings or when meeting senior figures, where maintaining proper conduct reflects not just personal courtesy but respect for the role and the institution itself. In newer sectors like tourism or tech startups, interactions may feel more relaxed, though the underlying expectation of respect remains.

Greetings follow a gentle, measured pace. A handshake is common in formal business settings, though it may be lighter or briefer than in cultures where a firm grip is valued. Some Bhutanese, especially women or older people, may prefer a slight bow with palms together at chest level instead of physical contact. Wait for your counterpart to initiate, and mirror what feels comfortable to them. Rushing into familiarity or informality too soon can come across as disrespectful or presumptuous.

Titles and honorifics matter. Address people using their professional title or "sir" and "madam" until invited to do otherwise. Using someone's first name without permission, especially in the presence of others or early in the relationship, risks causing quiet discomfort. Younger professionals may adapt more quickly to first-name exchanges, particularly if they have worked abroad, but it is safer to follow the more formal approach until the tone is set.

Business cards are exchanged respectfully, offered and received with both hands. Take a moment to read the card before putting it away. Treating it casually suggests you place little value on the person or the connection, and in a culture where relationships develop slowly and deliberately, small gestures like this are noticed.
