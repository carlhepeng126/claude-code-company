# Business Etiquette

## Making a Good Impression

Italian business etiquette reflects a culture shaped by the formality of Roman law, Catholic values centered on loyalty and care, and the warmth of regional traditions. Politeness and personal engagement are as important as competence. Etiquette here is less about rigid protocol and more about showing that you value the relationship. Greetings are warm, often accompanied by direct eye contact and sometimes a touch on the arm, especially as trust builds. These gestures reflect emotional engagement with the conversation, not just courtesy. A colleague from a culture where physical distance is maintained may read this closeness as overfamiliarity, while an Italian counterpart might interpret reserve as coldness or disinterest. Acknowledging titles or seniority, particularly in established organizations, shows cultural awareness and respect for hierarchy, even if the atmosphere becomes more relaxed over time.

Italians often use tact and diplomacy to manage sensitive topics, and public criticism is avoided. The concept of *bella figura* (cutting a fine figure) encompasses charm, style, and a desire to preserve harmony. Blaming someone openly for being late or missing a call risks embarrassment, so responses tend to be sympathetic rather than accusatory. If a colleague says an indirect yes or offers a polite excuse instead of refusing outright, they are protecting both parties from discomfort. Address difficult feedback privately and frame it constructively to maintain goodwill.

Small courtesies carry weight. Follow-up messages, greetings, and expressions of thanks help sustain relationships across meetings and projects. Business cards are common and often bilingual; including your degree and full job title signals professionalism, particularly if you need to establish credibility quickly. Gifts in professional settings should be high quality but understated, and are typically exchanged once a relationship has developed, not at the outset.
