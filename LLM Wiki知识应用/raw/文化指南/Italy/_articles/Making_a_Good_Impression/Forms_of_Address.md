# Forms of Address

## Making a Good Impression

In many Italian professional settings, formal address protects dignity and defines the relationship. Using *Lei* (the formal "you") rather than *tu* (the informal form) creates clarity about roles and boundaries, even when the tone remains warm. This formality reflects a culture where hierarchy, expertise, and seniority hold social weight, particularly in workplaces shaped by institutional prestige. A colleague accustomed to first-name introductions and rapid informality may read this distance as coolness. From an Italian perspective, the same informality offered too early can feel presumptuous, as though the relationship is being rushed past a threshold that has not yet been earned.

The shift from *Lei* to *tu* marks a real change in closeness or group membership. It may be offered explicitly by a senior colleague or emerge gradually through repeated interaction, but it signals trust and the loosening of role boundaries. Moving to *tu* before that threshold has been crossed can create discomfort, particularly where age or rank still organizes respect. In settings where a younger professional addresses an older or more senior colleague, maintaining *Lei* until invited to change is often the clearer choice.

Professional titles are used frequently and carry institutional meaning. A university graduate may be addressed as *Dottore* (male) or *Dottoressa* (female), reflecting the cultural value placed on education and credentials. Using these titles in introductions, emails, and meetings acknowledges the legitimacy that qualifications confer. Written communication follows the same expectation: openings such as "Egregio Dottor [surname]" are standard, and formality is maintained even in digital correspondence.

Regional and generational variation exists. Some settings, particularly in the north or in younger, tech-oriented workplaces, may move toward informality more quickly. Others retain formality longer, especially where local tradition or hierarchical structure remains strong. Formal address can coexist with expressive warmth, animated tone, and close conversational energy. Respect in Italy is not necessarily quiet.
