# Dress Code

## Making a Good Impression

Andorrans dress with care in professional settings, and what you wear says something about how you regard the occasion. A clean, polished look shows you take the meeting seriously. Dark suits and understated tailoring are typical in banking, legal firms, and government-facing roles, where formality still anchors first impressions. In retail, tourism, or smaller enterprises, the bar shifts toward smart casual, but rarely toward careless. People notice the effort, not just the outfit.

The small size of the country and the weight of repeat contact make presentation a form of discretion. Andorra's business community is tightly knit, and your reputation travels. Dressing appropriately signals that you understand the social compact: relationships here are built over time, and early impressions stick. A colleague from a culture where dress codes lean more relaxed may read this attention to appearance as overly formal, while an Andorran counterpart might interpret casual dress at a first meeting as a lack of respect for the relationship being built.

Sector and setting shape the standard more than any single national rule. In the financial quarter of Andorra la Vella, you will see sharply tailored suits and leather shoes. In tech startups or co-working spaces in Escaldes-Engordany, business casual is more common, though still intentional. Even in summer, when tourists fill the valleys in hiking gear, locals working in offices or meeting clients keep a clear line between leisure and work.

For dinners or evening events hosted by a colleague, the tone lifts. A well-fitted jacket or a considered dress is expected, not as ceremony but as courtesy. Match the level of care your host brings, and you will sit comfortably at the table.
