# Feedback and Coaching

## Managing Relationships

Andorra's small size and tightly woven social fabric shape how feedback is given and received. In a country where professional and personal networks overlap constantly, maintaining dignity and preserving relationships matters more than direct critique. Feedback tends to be indirect, delivered privately, and framed in ways that allow the other person to reach their own conclusions. A manager might describe a general challenge the team faces rather than call out an individual's mistake, or share how they themselves once handled a similar issue. This approach keeps the relationship intact while still prompting reflection.

Because everyone knows everyone, formal performance reviews often feel unnecessary or even awkward. Coaching happens through conversation, often over coffee or lunch, rather than in scheduled meetings with rating scales. A colleague from a culture where feedback is expected to be direct and frequent may find this style frustratingly vague or slow to address problems. From an Andorran perspective, a blunt assessment can feel disrespectful and risks damaging trust that took years to build. What looks like avoidance is usually an effort to guide someone without shaming them in a place where reputation follows you everywhere.

Public praise is appreciated as long as it doesn't single someone out too sharply or imply hierarchy where relationships are meant to feel more equal. Negative feedback, by contrast, should always be one-to-one and softened with context or suggestion rather than stated as criticism.
