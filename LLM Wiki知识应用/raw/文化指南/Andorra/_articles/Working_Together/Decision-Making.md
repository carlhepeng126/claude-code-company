# Decision-Making

## Working Together

Decisions in Andorra typically involve more people and take more time than organizational charts might suggest. The country's tradition of communal self-governance, rooted in centuries of balancing authority between secular and religious co-princes, has left a lasting preference for consultation and collective input. Even when a senior figure has clear authority, decisions often move through informal networks before they are finalized. This reflects a cultural expectation that major choices should be socially sound, not just operationally correct.

Managers and team leads will often consult trusted colleagues or stakeholders quietly before announcing a course of action. This is less about seeking permission and more about ensuring alignment and avoiding conflict. The goal is not to delay, but to arrive at a decision that holds up relationally as well as practically. If you are waiting for a clear timeline or a quick yes-no answer, recognize that this consultation period is doing real work: it builds legitimacy and reduces the chance of later resistance.

Authority is respected, but it does not replace the need for input. A senior leader may have the final word, but if key voices have not been heard or if the decision feels imposed rather than shaped, implementation can become uneven. For colleagues from cultures where decisions are made quickly and communicated top-down, this can feel slow or indirect. From an Andorran perspective, a decision announced without consultation can seem hasty or disconnected from the realities on the ground.

Flexibility is built into the process. Family obligations, regional ties, or shifts in context can reshape priorities even after a decision has been reached. This adaptability is seen as practical, not inconsistent.
