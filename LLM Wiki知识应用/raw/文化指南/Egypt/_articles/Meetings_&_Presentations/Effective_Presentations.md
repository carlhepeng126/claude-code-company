# Effective Presentations

## Meetings & Presentations

A strong presenter in Egypt combines visible expertise with *heiba* (presence, gravitas). Credentials, data, and institutional standing all build credibility, but so does the way you hold the room. An overly casual or chatty style, especially with older or more conservative audiences, can be read as lack of seriousness. You may be asked to provide background on yourself or your organization before diving into content; this lets people place you in context and decide how much trust to extend.

Even when English is widely understood in the room, people often process decisions emotionally in Arabic, particularly Egyptian Arabic. Key ideas resonate differently when expressed in culturally tuned phrases rather than direct translation. Including Arabic headlines, terms, or bilingual handouts for decision-makers demonstrates respect and helps your message land with weight. Phrases like *karāma* (dignity), *istiqrār* (stability), or references to Egypt's role in the region connect proposals to identity in ways that generic corporate language does not.

Egyptians grow up with schooling that emphasizes both ancient civilization and the country's place in Arab and Islamic history. Presentations that acknowledge Egypt's specific context—population pressure, reliance on the Nile, rural-urban realities—feel grounded rather than imported. Linking your proposal to these identity layers, even briefly, signals you have done more than recycle a standard deck.

Slides here tend to be text-heavy and detailed. Extremely minimalist decks may be admired aesthetically but can leave senior colleagues feeling under-informed, especially when they need to pass materials upward. Provide enough written content that someone can read and decide from the deck alone, while keeping structure and readability intact. Phones may ring, people may step out or arrive partway through; this reflects the rhythm of the day, not disengagement.
