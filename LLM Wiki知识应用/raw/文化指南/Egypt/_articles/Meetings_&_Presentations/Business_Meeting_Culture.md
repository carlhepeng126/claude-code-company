# Business Meeting Culture

## Meetings & Presentations

Meetings in Egypt often orbit around a senior figure, typically a man in his forties to sixties, and the room's visible dynamic reflects that gravity. Juniors and colleagues show *adab* (good manners) by deferring to him in front of others, rarely contradicting him openly. But this front-stage agreement can mislead. Real disagreement, doubt or alternative ideas surface after the formal session ends, in corridor conversations, café chats and WhatsApp threads. If you treat the meeting as the only place where decisions happen, you will miss the second round where views actually emerge. Build time into your process for informal follow-up, and ask your Egyptian partners directly how the discussion landed. Listen for coded phrases like "ya'ni" (meaning "sort of") or "inshallah" (God willing), which can signal hesitation rather than commitment.

Egyptians persuade through stories, jokes and analogies, not just data points. Humour does more than lighten the mood; it often smuggles in sensitive truths that cannot be stated plainly. Decades of state surveillance have shaped how people talk in formal settings, and some topics, especially those touching on politics, security or dissent, are approached obliquely or avoided entirely. People read the room before saying anything that could be misunderstood. If you push for explicit statements on politically sensitive issues in a large meeting, expect vagueness. Move those conversations to smaller, trusted groups instead, and allow narrative space rather than forcing a rigidly linear agenda.

Meeting culture also varies by region, even when everyone is speaking Egyptian Arabic. A ministry room in Cairo feels different from a governorate office in Minya or a tourism agency in Luxor. Upper Egyptians, Alexandrians, Sinai Bedouin and Nubians bring distinct speech rhythms, humour and expectations around hierarchy. Recognizing these differences, rather than assuming a single national style, helps you read the room more accurately and adjust your approach accordingly.
