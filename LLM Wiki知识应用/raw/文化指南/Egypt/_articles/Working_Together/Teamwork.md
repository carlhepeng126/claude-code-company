# Teamwork

## Working Together

Egyptian teams often function like extended families, complete with recognizable roles: the protective older sibling, the wise uncle, the strict parental figure. This familial structure shapes who speaks up, who receives support when things go wrong, and who gets quietly sidelined. Loyalty runs through these relationships, as does *'ayb* (shame), a concept tied to honor and appropriate behavior that influences how feedback is given, how mistakes are handled, and how conflicts are avoided or resolved. A colleague who breaches trust or embarrasses the group may find themselves frozen out without a direct word being spoken. Understanding who fulfills which role in the team often explains more than the formal organization chart ever will.

*Wasta*, the system of connections and influence, also shapes team life. Some colleagues sit within the orbit of a powerful manager or director, while others remain on the periphery regardless of their contribution. This creates a tension between stated values of fairness and the reality of who gets opportunities, promotions, or protection. The result is often quiet cynicism beneath a surface of politeness. A manager from a culture where meritocracy is more openly expected may interpret this as disengagement, while Egyptian colleagues may see blunt merit-based reforms as naive or even threatening if they appear to challenge the social fabric that protects people in uncertain times.

Regional background adds another layer. Teams often mix Cairo-born staff with colleagues from Upper Egypt or the rural Delta. Accents and origins can trigger assumptions, jokes, or stereotypes that reinforce or undermine inclusion. So-called "Sa'idi" jokes about Upper Egyptians, for instance, are common but can signal who is fully heard and who is relegated to ground-level work. Colleagues from rural or southern backgrounds may hold deep expertise but be passed over in discussions unless deliberately brought in. A leader who pays attention to these fault lines and actively amplifies quieter voices will strengthen both trust and performance.
