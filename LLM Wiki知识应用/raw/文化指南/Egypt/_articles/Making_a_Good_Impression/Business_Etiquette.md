# Business Etiquette

## Making a Good Impression

In Egypt, professional decorum rests on the concept of *'ayb* (shame, "not done"), a deeply rooted sense of what preserves dignity and what damages it. Public embarrassment, raising your voice at a colleague, or calling out mistakes in front of others violates this boundary. The injury outlasts the meeting. If you need to address a problem, do it quietly and away from an audience. Focus your critique on the work itself rather than the person. This distinction protects the relationship and keeps the conversation productive.

Hospitality is a moral expectation, not a courtesy. When someone offers you tea, coffee, or juice, accepting is part of showing respect. Refusing everything can seem cold or standoffish. These early moments are not just politeness; they open space for connection. Use the time to ask about family, neighborhood, or football before moving into business. The warmth built here makes the rest of the conversation easier.

Religion shapes the rhythm of many workdays, even among colleagues who do not appear outwardly observant. People may structure their schedules around prayer times, avoid certain foods, or draw a firm line at pork, alcohol, or certain kinds of humor. Scheduling meetings during Friday prayers or organizing events centered on alcohol shows a lack of awareness. Acknowledging these sensitivities without making them a topic of discussion is the more comfortable approach.
