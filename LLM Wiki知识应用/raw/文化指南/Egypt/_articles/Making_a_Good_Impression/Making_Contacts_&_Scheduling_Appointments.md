# Making Contacts & Scheduling Appointments

## Making a Good Impression

Access in Egypt usually depends less on the strength of your organization's name than on the strength of your personal introduction. Many useful doors open through *wasta* (using personal connections to get things done), whether that means a family tie, a school friend, or a friend of a cousin who works in the relevant ministry. This reflects a deeply relational culture where trust is tested through shared people before it is extended to strangers. A cold email to an official address will rarely move things forward as quickly as a warm introduction from someone already respected.

Expect contact-building to happen in informal spaces before it reaches anyone's formal calendar. Coffeehouses (*ahwa*, traditional establishments where people gather over tea or coffee) and shisha cafés are where much of the sounding-out happens. A lot gets tested in these settings, especially among men and increasingly among younger mixed groups, long before a meeting is scheduled. An invitation to join someone in one of these spaces should be treated as part of the professional process, not time off from real work.

Religious and seasonal rhythms quietly reshape availability throughout the year. During Ramadan, working hours shift: days tend to be slower, and social and business life becomes more active in the evenings, often around or after *iftar* (the meal that breaks the daily fast). Meetings may be rescheduled to later hours, and rigid calendar planning will rarely survive the month intact. Religious holidays, weddings, and funerals also reorder priorities in ways that may only become visible at short notice.

When scheduling, combine official channels with phone calls, WhatsApp, and in-person follow-up. Confirm meetings a day or two before, and leave space between appointments. Traffic in Cairo can be severe, and meetings frequently start late or extend beyond their scheduled time.
