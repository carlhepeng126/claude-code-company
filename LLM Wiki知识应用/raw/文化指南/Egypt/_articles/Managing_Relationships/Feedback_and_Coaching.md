# Feedback and Coaching

## Managing Relationships

Personal dignity, or *karāma* (personal honor), shapes how feedback is received and given in Egyptian workplaces. Being criticized in front of others, especially by someone younger or from outside, cuts deep. This is not about thin skin. It reflects a culture where public respect is a measure of worth, and preserving it is a shared responsibility. Feedback that feels like a dressing-down can close the door to real improvement. A manager from a culture where directness is valued might see private, indirect correction as evasive, while an Egyptian colleague may see public critique as disrespectful and unnecessarily harsh.

The tutoring mindset runs through Egyptian professional life. Many Egyptians grew up in a high-stakes exam system supported by private tutors who provided clear guidance, repetition, and worked examples. Abstract feedback conversations or vague developmental goals can feel unhelpful. Coaching works better when it resembles teaching: concrete examples, step-by-step guidance, and chances to try something together. Saying "you need to be more organized" leaves too much unsaid. Walking through a process or demonstrating an approach makes the expectation clear and actionable.

Egyptian humor is famously self-critical, and leaders who can laugh at themselves often create room for challenge without triggering defensiveness. But humor should not bury the message. If feedback is wrapped entirely in jokes, people may not know what actually needs to change. The goal is to keep the tone warm and the message visible.

Giving team members opportunities to present or teach something they excel at restores balance. It signals that feedback is not one-way judgment but part of an exchange where expertise is recognized and dignity is maintained.
