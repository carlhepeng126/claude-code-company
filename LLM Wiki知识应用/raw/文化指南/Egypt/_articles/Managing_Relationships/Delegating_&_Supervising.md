# Delegating & Supervising

## Managing Relationships

Authority in Egypt flows from the top and stays there. This reflects a deeply embedded understanding that hierarchy is morally correct, shaped by thousands of years of pharaonic rule based on *ma'at* (divine cosmic order, where every person had a fixed place and role) and reinforced by the Islamic concept of *wali al-amr* (guardian of authority), which places a religious obligation on leaders to direct and on subordinates to follow. The result in the workplace is that employees expect clear instruction and are unlikely to act outside their defined role without explicit permission. Delegation without tight structure will stall, and the manager who assigns a task without stating precisely how it should be done may find nothing moves forward.

A manager from a culture where initiative is rewarded might read this as passivity or lack of engagement. An Egyptian colleague, in turn, might view that same manager's expectation of independent action as careless, even disrespectful, for failing to provide the guidance their role requires. The consultation you may observe before decisions are made serves a social function. It demonstrates respect and builds buy-in, but rarely changes the outcome. The manager is expected to decide, and the act of asking is distinct from shared decision-making.

Supervision tends to be close, consistent with the extended-family structure of many Egyptian organisations. Feedback, however, moves privately and with care. Public correction risks damaging *sharaf* (honor), which is tied to reputation and social worth, and once lost in front of others is difficult to restore. If performance needs addressing, the conversation happens one-to-one, often softened through relationship and delivered indirectly. *Wasta*, the use of personal connections to get things done, also shapes how work actually moves. Knowing whose relationship with whom can unlock progress across teams or departments often matters more than the formal org chart.
