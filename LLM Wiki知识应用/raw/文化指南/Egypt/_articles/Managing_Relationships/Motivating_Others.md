# Motivating Others

## Managing Relationships

For many Egyptians, the dream is less about innovation for its own sake and more about standing tall: a stable job, a decent flat, children in good schools, aging parents looked after. Security and dignity together drive deep commitment. Linking your project to real improvements in stability, whether through predictable income, health coverage, or education support, speaks to this aspiration far more than abstract talk of growth or disruption. 

People also understand generous behavior and fair treatment through a religious and moral lens. Acts of integrity under pressure, or decisions that protect the team, are often seen as sources of baraka (blessing) or as things that will stand with them before God. Acknowledging this explicitly when someone makes a principled choice, connecting it to both professional and moral value, reinforces motivation in a way that compliance language alone cannot.

Younger professionals in Cairo and Alexandria especially hunger for exposure to the wider world: conferences, joint publications, travel opportunities, strong LinkedIn profiles. Yet many also feel they should not abandon their families to get this. Providing real exposure, distributed fairly rather than only to the most connected, and showing respect for family responsibilities through scheduling and leave decisions, signals that you understand what truly matters. When someone sees that a project offers both professional advancement and room for the obligations that shape their lives, engagement follows naturally.
