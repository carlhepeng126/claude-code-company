# Forms of Address

## Making a Good Impression

Swiss professional relationships begin with visible respect and remain there until trust has been established. Formality functions as a way of not imposing too soon. This reflects a broader cultural instinct that assumes distance is safer than premature warmth, and that courtesy protects both parties until competence and reliability have been quietly demonstrated over time.

This approach plays out directly in how people are addressed. In German-speaking areas, expect *Herr* (Mr.) or *Frau* (Mrs. or Ms.) paired with the surname, and the formal pronoun *Sie*. In French-speaking regions, it's *Monsieur* or *Madame* and *Vous*. In Ticino, *Signore* or *Signora* and *Lei*. Professional titles such as *Doktor* or *Professor* are routinely used and reflect a culture shaped by the vocational education system, where qualifications represent demonstrated mastery and are treated as legitimate markers of standing. Wait to be invited to move to first names. The shift from *Sie* to *du*, or *Vous* to *tu*, is an offer made by the more senior or established person, and it signals that the relationship has moved from transactional to personal. Suggesting it yourself can feel presumptuous.

The language you choose also functions as a form of address. Switzerland's multilingual identity means that selecting which language to use in a meeting or email can be read as a gesture of respect or disregard. Offering the language that best serves the other person, rather than the one you find easiest, acknowledges the social reality of linguistic difference and helps build credibility early on.

In writing, formality should be maintained even in digital channels. Standard openings such as "Sehr geehrter Herr [surname]" in German or "Cher Monsieur [surname]" in French are expected, and closings remain courteous. Confirm the correct spelling and pronunciation of names once, then apply them consistently.
