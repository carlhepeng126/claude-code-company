# Business Etiquette

## Making a Good Impression

Trinidadian business etiquette is shaped by a culture where public participation builds credibility and humour often works as competence, not avoidance. Panyards, Carnival bands, and fête culture act as informal schools of coordination and reliability. People notice who shows up, how they handle social pressure, and whether they contribute to the group's success. This carries over into professional settings, where presence and follow-through can build trust more effectively than credentials alone. If you commit to something, be ready to deliver. Flaking out or making excuses can damage your reputation quickly, because reliability is tested through repeated participation.

Communication tends to be direct, sometimes surprisingly so. A colleague might make a blunt personal observation followed by a joke or a self-deprecating remark to soften the edge. This reflects a cultural respect for resourcefulness and improvisation, rooted in the steelpan story of invention under constraint and Carnival's history as a struggle over who gets to take up space. Keeping a room light through humour helps reduce tension and maintain flow, but it doesn't mean the conversation lacks seriousness. For a colleague from a culture where formality signals professionalism, this directness mixed with levity might feel too casual. From a Trinidadian perspective, an overly formal tone with no warmth might come across as stiff or disengaged. Reading the room and adjusting your tone accordingly will help you fit in without losing credibility.

Context-reading is essential because multi-religious norms shift what feels respectful. With visible Hindu, Muslim, Christian, and Afro-syncretic traditions, expectations around food, alcohol, gender mixing, and sacred time vary widely by setting. Don't assume a single standard applies across all professional environments. Ask if you're unsure, and be prepared to adapt. Gift-giving is not a strong cultural practice in business, but a small, thoughtful token from your own country may be appreciated, especially if it doesn't feel transactional. Avoid anything too valuable, as it can create an uncomfortable sense of obligation.

Business cards are exchanged frequently, though without ritual. Bring a large supply and treat them with respect. Read a colleague's card carefully to understand how to address them, and spell names correctly. Many Trinidadians are devout, so don't be surprised if emails close with "God bless you." Formality around titles and forms of address is expected until you're invited to use first names. If someone has a professional or academic title, use it.
