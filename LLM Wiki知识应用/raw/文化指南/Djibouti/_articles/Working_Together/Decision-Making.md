# Decision-Making

## Working Together

In Djibouti, decisions take shape through networks of trust and proximity to authority. Formal hierarchies are respected, and senior figures hold the final say, but outcomes are shaped well before an announcement is made. Leaders consult selectively with people they know and trust, often from within family, clan, or long-standing professional circles. This builds alignment and reduces the risk of visible disagreement, which can damage reputations and relationships in a society where personal standing and group loyalty run deep.

Timelines are flexible, and decisions may shift in response to changing relationships or new information from trusted sources. What can look like delay to a colleague from a culture where decisions are made quickly and transparently is often careful positioning. A manager from Djibouti working in such an environment may find the speed unsettling, wondering whether sufficient consultation has taken place or whether the decision will hold once social realities come into play.

If you are working with a decision-maker in Djibouti, understand that their consultations may not be visible to you, and that access to these conversations depends on relationship and proximity. Pressing for a faster resolution or documented rationale can be read as disrespectful or naïve. Instead, invest early in building trust with the people who shape decisions behind the scenes, and expect that final clarity will emerge when alignment has been quietly secured.
