# Negotiating

## Working Together

Negotiations in Djibouti work best when framed as the start of a long-term relationship rather than a transaction to be closed quickly. The early stages often focus on understanding each other and establishing confidence that the partnership will hold. This means that diving straight into contract terms can undermine trust before it has time to form. Plan for several rounds of conversation, and expect those early meetings to center on connection as much as on substance.

The discussion may not move in a straight line. A point you thought was settled might resurface later, or the conversation may shift unexpectedly to something that seems unrelated. This circular approach allows both sides to test flexibility and make sure all angles have been considered. It also reflects the consultative nature of decision-making, where key figures or respected elders may need to weigh in before anything is finalized. If you interpret this as indecision or stalling, you risk missing what the process is actually doing: building a shared understanding that can support the agreement over time.

Slowing down can also be tactical. Your counterpart may be waiting to see if you will improve your offer, so presenting your best terms at the outset leaves you with little room to move. Bargaining is expected and seen as a way to reach a fair outcome together. Leave space for concessions that won't weaken your position but will allow the negotiation to unfold naturally. The goal is not just to agree, but to agree in a way that affirms mutual respect and reciprocity.
