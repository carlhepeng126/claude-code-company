# Dress Code

## Making a Good Impression

Professional dress in Djibouti reflects layers of identity that go beyond the workplace. The population is predominantly Muslim, and many business settings blend influences from Somali, Afar, Arab, and French traditions. How someone dresses is often tied to family background, religious observance, and the sector they work in, so expect variation rather than a single uniform style.

Men in government ministries, banks, and formal corporate settings typically wear Western-style suits or dress shirts with trousers. In other sectors, you may see the traditional macawiis (a wraparound sarong-style garment worn by Somali and Djiboutian men) paired with a collared shirt, especially in locally owned businesses or community-facing roles. Women's dress varies widely. Some wear tailored blouses and skirts or trousers in neutral tones. Others wear modest Islamic dress including the hijab, and some favor the dirac, a long, flowing dress common among Somali women, often in vibrant patterns. What unites these choices is modesty and care in presentation, which convey respect for the professional setting and the people in it.

In international organizations or companies with expatriate management, dress codes often tilt toward Western business norms. Even so, colleagues from Djibouti may interpret overly casual dress as a lack of seriousness. A visitor who dresses formally signals engagement with local expectations. A Djiboutian colleague arriving at a European office in business casual, by contrast, may read the relaxed dress of others as informality that doesn't quite match the gravity they associate with business relationships.

Dressing modestly is practical advice across all professional contexts. For women, this means covering shoulders and knees. For men, long trousers are expected in formal settings. These are not rigid rules, but they help avoid unintended messages about respect or professionalism.
