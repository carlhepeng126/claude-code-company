# Sharing Information

## Working Together

Australians lose trust when information arrives wrapped in jargon or corporate language. The preference is for straightforward, plain updates with no spin. Flowery language or euphemism reads as evasive, and credibility drops quickly when people sense they are being managed rather than informed. This directness reflects a wider cultural impatience with pretense and a deep-rooted expectation that colleagues speak plainly, even when the news is uncomfortable. In cross-border work, a manager from a culture where softening language protects relationships may find Australian teams unresponsive or skeptical, while Australians working elsewhere may misread indirectness as a lack of transparency rather than relational care.

Official channels like email, intranets and team meetings carry formal updates, but the informal "bush telegraph" often runs ahead of them. Site gossip, WhatsApp groups and union networks spread news faster, and what people hear on the ground can shape their understanding long before the official version lands. If you assume a memo or presentation has done the job, check what people have actually heard and understood. Relying only on formal communication risks missing the conversation that is already happening.

In safety-critical industries, open reporting of near-misses and incidents is encouraged, though fear of blame can still suppress honest talk. Transparency is improving but remains uneven. Rewarding candor and avoiding punishment of messengers aligns with where Australian safety culture is headed, especially in mining, construction and resources. In Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander contexts, face-to-face communication and community discussion hold more weight than written documents in English. Working effectively with Indigenous partners means investing in in-person time, local interpreters where needed, and space for genuine discussion, rather than relying on printed material alone.
