# Feedback and Coaching

## Managing Relationships

Australian feedback tends to be direct and delivered in plain language, often with humor folded in to soften the edge. A manager might say "You buried the lede there, mate" or "That's not your best work" without much preamble or cushioning. This directness reflects a broader cultural preference for clarity and fairness. People expect to know where they stand, and wrapping criticism in overly formal or elaborate language can feel artificial or even dishonest. The humor serves a purpose: it keeps the tone light and reinforces that the relationship remains intact, even when the feedback itself is pointed.

Highly scripted performance reviews or form-heavy processes can sit awkwardly in this environment. Many Australian workplaces favor ongoing, informal check-ins where issues are raised as they arise rather than saved for a scheduled sit-down. Coaching often happens through working alongside someone, showing them how something is done, and then letting them have a go. This practical, hands-on style means that learning is embedded in doing, not separated into formal training sessions or theory-heavy presentations.

When giving feedback to Australian colleagues, be specific, be fair, and avoid pompous phrasing. They will respect straightforward talk more than carefully staged critique. When receiving feedback, listen for the substance beneath the casual delivery. What sounds like banter may contain real guidance.

Individual praise should be handled with care. Singling someone out too enthusiastically can trigger quiet resentment from others, a reflection of the tall poppy instinct to level excessive displays of individual achievement. Celebrating team wins and shared effort tends to land better and avoids the risk of making one person stand out uncomfortably.
