# Business Meeting Culture

## Meetings & Presentations

Business meetings in Equatorial Guinea reflect a society where Spanish colonial history, oil wealth, and close-knit kinship networks have shaped a formal, status-conscious professional environment. Rank and personal connection both shape who is invited and how the conversation unfolds. Senior figures are expected to attend, and decisions typically rest with those at the top of the hierarchy. If you schedule a meeting with mid-level staff, expect that final approval will still come from above. Invitations should go to counterparts of equivalent seniority, and it helps to clarify the purpose of the meeting in advance so that the right people are in the room.

Meetings often begin with greetings that acknowledge status and allow time for relationship building. This is especially true in first encounters, where formality is the norm. Addressing people by title and showing deference to the most senior person present sets the right tone. The oil and gas sector, which dominates the economy, has introduced some efficiency-driven practices, but even in these settings, rushing through introductions or pushing for immediate decisions can be seen as dismissive. A colleague from a culture where meetings move quickly to outcomes may read this measured pace as inefficiency, while someone from Equatorial Guinea may view a fast-moving agenda as lacking respect for the people involved.

Once business begins, expect the senior person to guide the discussion. Others may defer or remain quieter, not because they lack input, but because speaking out of turn can be seen as overstepping. If you need broader input, consider following up individually after the meeting rather than pressing for contributions in the moment. Silence is common and should be given space rather than filled with further explanations.

Outcomes are rarely confirmed on the spot. Decisions often require consultation outside the room, and timelines may stretch longer than initially suggested. Following up in writing after the meeting is both expected and appreciated, as it allows participants to review and respond without the pressure of an immediate answer.
