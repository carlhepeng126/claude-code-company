# Feedback and Coaching

## Managing Relationships

Feedback in Equatorial Guinea is handled with care for hierarchy and personal dignity. Direct criticism, especially in front of others, risks causing offense and damaging the relationship. Managers tend to offer guidance privately, using indirect language that acknowledges the person's standing while steering them toward improvement. A supervisor might frame a concern as a collective observation or frame the conversation around team goals, allowing the individual to recognize the issue without being singled out. This approach protects face and maintains the respect needed for the working relationship to continue.

Public praise is generally safe and can reinforce team morale, but it should be delivered with awareness of group harmony. Singling someone out too enthusiastically may create discomfort or jealousy among colleagues. A manager from a culture where individual recognition is routine may find this restraint puzzling, while an Equatoguinean employee might view highly individualized praise as awkward or even divisive.

Coaching conversations often extend beyond the workplace. Family input, particularly from elders, can shape how feedback is received and whether action follows. A young professional may consult relatives before accepting a new role or responsibility, particularly if it affects family expectations or status. Understanding that professional decisions are rarely made in isolation helps explain why responses to feedback may take time or involve consultation that isn't immediately visible to the manager.
