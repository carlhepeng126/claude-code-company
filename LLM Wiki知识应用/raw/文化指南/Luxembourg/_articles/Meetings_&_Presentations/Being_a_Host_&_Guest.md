# Being a Host & Guest

## Meetings & Presentations

Good hosting in Luxembourg often shows up as structured ease rather than expressive warmth. In a small, internationally connected environment shaped by overlapping professional and social networks, helping guests feel oriented and comfortable often involves smooth logistics, clear practical support, and a deliberate avoidance of pressure or exposure. Hosts tend to offer navigation help, timely introductions, and language adjustments rather than performative welcome. This reflects Luxembourg's system-oriented culture and the high likelihood of repeat contact. Getting something wrong in public can echo through the network, so courtesy often looks like discretion: not putting guests on the spot, not forcing premature intimacy, and not making errors visible.

Language choice functions as an active form of hospitality. Switching between French, German, and English to reduce friction is a genuine inclusion gesture, grounded in Luxembourg's multilingual reality. Following your host's language lead signals respect and situational awareness. Luxembourgish may be used as a subtle belonging marker among insiders, but the willingness to code-switch outward is itself a hosting act. A guest who responds in the same register, or adapts fluidly without comment, will often be read as socially competent rather than simply polite.

Belonging is often built through participation in shared traditions rather than informal rapport. National rituals like Schueberfouer or Octave model a national habit where showing up to communal moments can build connection faster than trying to accelerate personal closeness. If invited to someone's home, arrive on time with flowers, chocolates, or good wine. Table manners are formal: wait to be seated, follow any table plan, and observe the toast before eating. Business discussion at dinner is typically deferred until coffee. Evening invitations may include spouses and are understood as social rather than transactional.
