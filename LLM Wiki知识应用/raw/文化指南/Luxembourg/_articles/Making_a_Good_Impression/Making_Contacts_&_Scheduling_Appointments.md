# Making Contacts & Scheduling Appointments

## Making a Good Impression

Luxembourg's compact geography and networked society mean that professional circles overlap more than they first appear. Close to half the resident population are foreign nationals, and nearly half of the workforce commutes daily from France, Belgium, and Germany. This creates a quiet awareness that relationships circulate and reputations travel. People tend to approach new contacts with discretion, preferring smooth, non-intrusive first impressions over bold or unsolicited outreach.

A warm introduction from a shared contact can make a real difference. In a place where many people know each other across sectors and borders, a referral signals credibility and context before you've even met. If you don't have a shared connection, a polite, formal email or letter in French or English works well. Explain your company's background, your reason for reaching out, and request a meeting. Give at least two weeks' notice. Avoid Catholic holidays and the main vacation months of July and August. Unsolicited visits are unwelcome; they disrupt carefully planned schedules and suggest a lack of respect for how people organize their time.

When scheduling, be mindful of cross-border commuting patterns. Early morning or late afternoon slots can be more difficult than they look, especially for colleagues traveling from neighboring countries. Reconfirm appointments the day before, and allow extra time for traffic or parking in the city. If you're running late, call ahead. Punctuality reflects seriousness, and lateness at the outset can set the relationship back before it begins.

Language choice also matters. Meetings may take place in French, German, Luxembourgish, or English, and switching between them is common. Even small efforts to meet someone in their preferred language show respect and awareness of the country's multilingual reality.
