# Business Etiquette

## Making a Good Impression

Politeness in Luxembourg is threaded through with procedure. The country's dense institutional life has built a cultural comfort with doing things correctly, documenting clearly, and respecting formal channels. For a colleague used to cutting corners or working around process, this can feel rigid. From a Luxembourg perspective, following the right steps shows courtesy and builds trust, and skipping them reads as careless or dismissive. Clear processes and reliable follow-through build credibility quickly in a system-oriented environment, so clarify next steps in writing and keep commitments visible.

Language choice is a form of etiquette, shaped by the way multilingualism is structured into everyday life. Switching between French, German, and Luxembourgish to fit the setting or person is a common politeness practice. If you're working in English, acknowledge the accommodation by checking in on clarity and avoiding jargon. Treat language flexibility as relational, not just practical.

Luxembourgers are direct but polite. Criticism is delivered diplomatically, with care taken to avoid embarrassment. Once familiarity grows, people speak more openly, but protecting the relationship remains central. This means maintaining a modest tone, avoiding boasting, and keeping emotions measured in professional settings. Body language reflects the same restraint: gestures are contained, physical contact minimal. Grabbing someone's arm or slouching in a meeting will create discomfort. 

If invited to a colleague's home, bring flowers in odd numbers (not chrysanthemums), chocolates, or wine. Small tokens for children are well received. The recipient will usually open the gift right away and thank you directly. In business, keep gifts modest and tasteful.
