# Dress Code

## Making a Good Impression

In Luxembourg, how you present yourself is read less as personal branding and more as situational awareness. The public tone values discretion, quality, and appropriateness over bold display. Neatness and polish signal that you understand the setting and take it seriously. This isn't about fashion risk or standing out. It's about knowing what kind of room you're in and showing you belong there through how you dress.

The capital's institutional and finance districts lean toward internationally standardised formality: dark suits, conservative tailoring, well-made accessories. Outside those districts, in smaller towns and local association life, the social register can shift. Settings where Luxembourgish is the anchor language often come with a more conservative baseline. The difference between Kirchberg's glass towers and a commune gathering can feel sharper than outsiders expect, and outward presentation is part of that shift. A polished suit works in both, but the cultural reading of formality differs.

Quality will be noticed. Labels, cut, and finish matter, though understated rather than ostentatious is the safer line. A colleague from a workplace where dress codes are more relaxed may see this as overly formal or rigid, while someone from Luxembourg meeting a counterpart in casual attire may question their investment in the relationship. Both readings reflect different ideas about what professional respect looks like.

During civic-religious moments like the Octave pilgrimage, modest presentation becomes more deliberate. Around the cathedral and processions, what you wear reflects a broader awareness of context. The principle holds across settings: dress for the occasion, not for yourself.
