# Forms of Address

## Making a Good Impression

Luxembourg's workplace address norms reflect the country's trilingual reality and its institutional formality. Luxembourgish, French, and German are all in daily use, and each language brings its own social register. French tends to appear in formal or institutional settings, German in administrative and educational contexts, and Luxembourgish among locals as a marker of closeness and belonging. The 1984 language law gave Luxembourgish official status as the national language, elevating it symbolically even in contexts where French or German dominate. The choice of language itself can signal the social distance or intimacy of the relationship, so start with a respectful baseline and let the other person's language and register guide you.

Formal address is the norm until explicitly invited to shift. Use surnames with honorifics—Monsieur or Madame in French, or professional titles such as Docteur or Professeur where applicable. Academic qualifications are valued, and titles acknowledge that recognition. Continue using the formal *vous* in French or *Sie* in German until the other person suggests switching to the familiar form. This formality functions as courtesy in a context shaped by EU institutions, finance, and state administration, where a professional baseline of address signals respect rather than distance.

Names themselves can be complex. Many Luxembourgers have French first names and German surnames, or adapt their names to Luxembourgish forms—Charles becomes Charel, Jean-Pierre becomes Jhemp. Some double-barrel surnames to reflect both parents' names, which can combine German, Portuguese, or other origins. Pronouncing a colleague's name correctly matters more than early informality. In a country where nearly half the workforce is foreign-born, people carry address habits from Portugal, France, Belgium, Germany, and beyond. Luxembourg workplaces quietly normalize this variation rather than enforcing a single standard. Match the channel the other person opens, and you're likely on safe ground.
