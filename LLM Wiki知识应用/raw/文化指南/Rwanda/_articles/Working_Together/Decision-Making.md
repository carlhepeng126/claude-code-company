# Decision-Making

## Working Together

Rwanda's approach to decision-making draws from a legacy shaped by both historical governance structures and more recent national priorities. The traditional monarchy relied on clear hierarchies and centralized authority, with decisions flowing from the top through defined levels of chiefs and advisors. That top-down instinct remains visible in modern organizations, particularly in larger corporations and government-linked sectors such as infrastructure, banking, and telecoms. Final authority typically rests with senior leadership, and approval pathways are well defined. At the same time, Rwanda's post-genocide reconstruction introduced cultural tools that emphasize consultation and accountability. Imihigo, a performance contract system rooted in public targets and evaluation, has created a mindset that asks whether a decision is realistic, who owns it, and how it will be measured. That deliverability instinct runs through planning conversations, even when authority itself stays concentrated.

Consultation is valued, but it functions as input rather than shared control. The concept of Umushyikirano, national dialogue framed as thoughtful exchange and questioning, reinforces the idea that decisions gain legitimacy when people feel they have been respectfully heard. In practice, this means managers often seek views from trusted colleagues or technical experts before a choice is finalized, but the decision itself remains with the person or level formally responsible. A colleague from a setting where decisions emerge through team consensus may read this as closed or unilateral. From a Rwandan perspective, a process where no one clearly owns the outcome can feel inefficient and unaccountable.

Unity and social cohesion remain deeply prized after the rupture of 1994. Decisions are not assessed purely on technical merit. Choices that could affect trust, stability, or the dignity of stakeholders are weighed carefully, and framing how an approach supports cohesion can be as relevant as the business case itself. This does not slow every decision, but it does mean that when stakes are sensitive or symbolic, the timeline reflects that seriousness. A proposal that names risks clearly and frames mutual benefit is more credible than one that overpromises or rushes to closure.
