# Sharing Information

## Working Together

Information in Rwanda moves carefully. In a society with high social proximity, especially outside Kigali, reputation travels quickly and what you say to whom can shape how you are seen across a tight social weave. This makes discretion a practical form of self-protection. People layer what they share, starting with what is necessary and deepening as trust and clarity build. Rushing to disclose details early, or pushing for full transparency in a first meeting, can feel invasive rather than efficient. If you are working with a Rwandan colleague who seems guarded in early exchanges, they are not withholding to obstruct progress. They are assessing whether you will treat what they share with respect.

The history of Gacaca courts after 1994, a mass community-based system for establishing truth and accountability, reinforced a national memory that speech can be serious, public, and consequential. This has left a lasting awareness that words, once spoken, can have weight beyond the immediate conversation. Privacy is not evasion. It reflects a wider cultural logic captured in the symbolism of the agaseke peace basket, which represents protection and containment. Safeguarding what is sensitive can be a form of care, not secrecy.

This approach shows up clearly in professional settings. Junior staff often hesitate to share insights openly in mixed forums, especially when senior colleagues are present, not because they lack ideas but because contributing directly can feel like challenging authority. A colleague from a culture where brainstorming sessions invite unfiltered input might read this as passivity. From the Rwandan side, the rapid-fire sharing in such settings can seem reckless or disrespectful of hierarchy. One-on-one conversations, mentorship structures, or smaller working groups create safer channels for knowledge exchange without the exposure risk of large meetings.

Treat confidentiality as a form of dignity. Avoid casual forwarding of sensitive information, speculation, or gossip. Demonstrate that you are a reliable custodian of what people share, and access to deeper insight will follow.
