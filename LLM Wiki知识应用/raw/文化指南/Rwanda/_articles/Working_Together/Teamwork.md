# Teamwork

## Working Together

Teamwork in Rwanda draws heavily on lived traditions of collective responsibility. Practices like umuganda, a monthly nationwide community work day, and ubudehe, a long-standing tradition of mutual support, have shaped a workplace culture where cohesion is built through visible contribution and shared effort. These aren't just symbolic references. They create an expectation that group success comes from everyone participating, not from individual standout moments. A colleague who quietly delivers on commitments without fanfare is often trusted more quickly than someone who promotes their own ideas loudly.

Character influences how teams form and hold together. The ideal of ubupfura, which encompasses integrity, restraint, and fairness, means that how someone behaves in small moments can shape whether they are seen as reliable. Technical skill alone doesn't establish belonging. Colleagues watch for consistency, respect, and whether someone can be counted on without drama. For a team member from a culture where enthusiasm and vocal participation signal engagement, this quieter form of contribution might register as disengagement. From a Rwandan perspective, the reverse can also be true. A colleague who speaks frequently in meetings without follow-through might seem performative rather than dependable.

Dignity is protected carefully in group settings. The concept of agaciro, often translated as self-worth or dignity, creates a sensitivity to anything that risks humiliation. Public blame, sarcasm, or being corrected in a way that corners someone can damage trust and group harmony. Feedback works best when delivered calmly and privately. This isn't about avoiding accountability. It's about keeping the person intact while addressing the issue.

Rwanda's post-genocide unity frameworks, including Ndi Umunyarwanda, have made language and how difference is managed feel consequential in team life. Words are chosen with care, especially around identity or anything that could be divisive. Teams that model disciplined, respectful communication and invite collaborative problem-solving rather than assigning blame tend to build the psychological safety that allows real collaboration to happen.
