# Business Etiquette

## Making a Good Impression

Rwandan business etiquette reflects a deeply rooted social logic: contribution earns belonging, commitments build credibility, and discretion protects relationships. The cultural intuition that you show respect through what you do, rather than what you promise, is grounded in longstanding communal practices like umuganda (community work days) and ubudehe (collective problem-solving). In professional settings, this translates into an expectation that you arrive prepared, follow through reliably, and contribute constructively to shared goals. Credibility builds quickly when responsibility is visible. A colleague from a culture where verbal persuasion or confident assertions are valued might assume their ideas alone will establish authority, while a Rwandan counterpart may be assessing whether those ideas are backed by consistent action. In reverse, a Rwandan professional's quieter presence in a meeting might be misread as disengagement, when in fact they are demonstrating respect through attentive listening and considered contribution.

The governance culture's use of imihigo (performance pledges) has reinforced a broader expectation that commitments are binding. In business contexts, this means you should promise only what you can deliver. Overcommitting to build rapport or speed up a deal can backfire. Colleagues remember what was agreed and expect it to be honored. If circumstances change, address it directly and early, rather than letting the gap speak for itself.

Post-conflict rebuilding has made discretion both an ethical norm and a practical one. Public disagreement, gossip, or anything that risks embarrassment is avoided. Critiques are softened, often delivered in private, and phrased carefully to preserve dignity. A raised concern in a one-on-one conversation after a meeting is more likely than open challenge during it. Silence in a group setting does not mean agreement. It may signal hesitation, discomfort, or the need for further private discussion. Treating what you learn as sensitive unless clearly public builds trust and signals sound judgment.
