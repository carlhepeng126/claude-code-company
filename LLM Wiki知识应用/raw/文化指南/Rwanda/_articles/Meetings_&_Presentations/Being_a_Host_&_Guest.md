# Being a Host & Guest

## Meetings & Presentations

Hospitality in Rwanda reflects a broader moral logic rooted in *ubupfura*, a cultural ideal of good character that includes humility, attentiveness, and self-control. Hosting someone well is understood as a display of dignity, and receiving that care graciously is equally revealing. The focus is less on the scale of what is offered and more on the manner in which it is given and received. Refreshments, even for unannounced guests, are customary and usually include tea, coffee, or soft drinks. Hosts may prepare more than expected, sometimes a full buffet meal for visitors arriving mid-afternoon, because generosity reflects respect and confirms social standing.

As a guest, express gratitude clearly and accept what is offered without hesitation or fuss. Refusing hospitality or appearing indifferent to a host's effort can read as dismissive or socially cold. If you are invited to a home, arriving exactly on time can leave your host unprepared. Calling or texting to say you are on your way is appreciated, though *vuba* (soon) in Rwanda might mean an hour or two rather than ten minutes. Bringing a small gift such as fruit, tea, or sugar is thoughtful but not required. Avoid flowers, which are often associated with funerals. A follow-up message acknowledging the hospitality is always well received.

Discretion is a form of care. What you observe in someone's home or personal setting should not become public conversation. This reflects a wider norm linked to *agaseke*, the lidded basket that symbolizes keeping what matters safe. Hosting and being hosted are not isolated acts but part of a broader expectation that belonging is demonstrated through participation and appropriate conduct, values reinforced through practices like *umuganda* (community work) and *ubudehe* (mutual support). In professional contexts, this means ensuring guests feel socially safe, not put on the spot or exposed to awkward exchanges. Thoughtfulness, not showiness, aligns with the cultural logic of *agaciro*, or dignity.
