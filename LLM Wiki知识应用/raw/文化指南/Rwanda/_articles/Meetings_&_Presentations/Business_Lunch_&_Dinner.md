# Business Lunch & Dinner

## Meetings & Presentations

Business meals in Rwanda serve as careful opportunities to build trust rather than venues for negotiation. The cultural priorities of *agaciro* (dignity) and *ubupfura* (good character) mean that how you carry yourself at table often matters more than what you say. Composed conversation, attentive listening, and avoiding emotional intensity help create the relational steadiness that Rwandans value. A colleague who arrives at lunch ready to close a deal may find the conversation drifts toward family or shared interests first. From a Rwandan perspective, this is the foundation. From a culture where work talk starts immediately, it might feel indirect, but the trust being built is the point of the meal.

Timing your invitation requires awareness of Rwanda's commemorative calendar. Kwibuka begins on April 7 and shapes public tone for roughly 100 days, echoing the period of the 1994 genocide. During this season, celebratory gatherings feel out of step with the national mood. A dinner scheduled in May without awareness of Kwibuka can seem culturally tone-deaf. If you need to meet during this period, keep the tone quieter and follow your counterpart's cues.

Alcohol is not always expected. Many Rwandans drink moderately or not at all, and the host will offer options without pressure. Following their lead keeps the focus where it belongs: on steady relationship-building. Gratitude toward the host and modest, attentive presence signal respect more clearly than grand gestures or picking up the bill uninvited.
