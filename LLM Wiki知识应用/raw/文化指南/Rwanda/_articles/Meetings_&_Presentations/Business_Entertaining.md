# Business Entertaining

## Meetings & Presentations

Business entertaining in Rwanda reflects a broader cultural logic in which offering food, drink, and care affirms the dignity of the guest. This is rooted in the concept of *agaciro*, a Kinyarwanda term meaning dignity or worth. Hospitality is not simply about providing refreshment, but about creating a setting where the other person feels respected and socially safe. A host's attentiveness during a meal or coffee meeting is a form of relationship-building, and the warmth of the setting often holds more meaning than what is discussed. Responding with genuine appreciation and reciprocating the invitation when possible reinforces that you recognize the gesture as more than logistics.

Lunch meetings are common and provide a more relaxed space to strengthen trust without the formality of a boardroom. Dinner is typically reserved for marking progress or celebrating outcomes, and may include spouses or close colleagues. Kigali's café culture also supports coffee meetings, which offer a quieter, less structured alternative. Alcohol may be served, but consumption is moderate and varies by individual practice and religious observance. Follow the host's lead rather than assuming a particular preference.

Conversation during meals is often wide-ranging and not transactional. Business may come up lightly or not at all, and the host usually decides when the tone shifts. A colleague from a culture where meals are used to close deals or review details may find this pace indirect, while a Rwandan host may read an early shift to business topics as impatience or a lack of interest in the relationship itself. The meal is the relationship work.

Keep the atmosphere respectful. Avoid humor or comments that could embarrass, and steer clear of questions about identity or post-genocide history that risk treating the guest as a source of trauma narrative rather than a professional peer.
