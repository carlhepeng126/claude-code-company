# Business Meeting Culture

## Meetings & Presentations

Meetings in Rwanda often function as spaces where dignity and credibility are assessed alongside content. The cultural value of *agaciro* (worth, dignity, self-respect) shapes how people carry themselves and how they read others: tone should be measured, claims grounded, and interpersonal conduct respectful. A colleague accustomed to fast-paced decision-making or assertive debate may find this atmosphere overly cautious, while a Rwandan counterpart may perceive directness or dominance tactics as a failure to protect the sense of worth in the room. The ideal of *ubupfura* (integrity, nobility of character) also matters. Credibility is assessed through behavior as much as competence, so visible follow-through, careful phrasing, and avoiding exaggeration all help establish trust.

The tradition of *umushyikirano* (structured dialogue for exchanging ideas and building accountability) has influenced how seriousness is signaled. Meetings are often framed as opportunities to demonstrate mutual commitment, question each other respectfully, and build shared understanding over time. Presenting clearly, inviting questions, and showing you can be held accountable aligns with this logic. The concept of *imihigo* (performance contracts rooted in public vows) reinforces the idea that promises are social objects: you stake credibility on them. Make commitments carefully, confirm them visibly, and then deliver.

Hierarchy shapes who leads discussions, so addressing senior figures first is expected. Communication tends to be indirect and consensus-seeking, with open confrontation avoided. When tension arises, smaller follow-ups or careful rephrasing often move things forward more effectively than public challenge. Reconfirming meeting details multiple times via email, phone, or WhatsApp is standard practice, as cancellations or delays can happen. In formal settings, minutes may not always be recorded, so sending a written summary of key points and next steps afterward helps maintain clarity and accountability.
