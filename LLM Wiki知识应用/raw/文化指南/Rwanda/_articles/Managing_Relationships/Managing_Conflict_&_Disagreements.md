# Managing Conflict & Disagreements

## Managing Relationships

Disagreement at work in Rwanda is shaped by a national commitment to reconciliation that extends well beyond private relationships. The National Unity and Reconciliation Commission frames conflict itself as something with social ripple effects, which means colleagues often assess whether raising an issue will create wider disruption or damage the fabric of the team. This makes many people reluctant to escalate openly, even when a problem is real. The preference is for structured dialogue, mediation, and internal resolution, with legal proceedings seen as a last resort.

Rwanda's Gacaca courts, which addressed accountability after the genocide through communal truth-telling, have left a lasting imprint on how disputes are imagined. The goal was less about adversarial victory and more about acknowledgement, responsibility, and restoring workable relationships. This approach still influences workplace expectations: disagreements are more likely to be framed as shared challenges to solve than battles to win. When blame is necessary, it should be handled privately and with precise facts, keeping the person's dignity intact throughout.

The cultural values of agaciro (dignity) and ubupfura (integrity and restraint) together encourage conflict styles that avoid public humiliation. This means separating the issue from the person, choosing calm and respectful language, and proposing next steps that allow for reconciliation rather than declaring a winner. A colleague from a culture where direct confrontation is valued for clearing the air may read this as evasion, while a Rwandan colleague may view blunt, public challenges as reckless and disrespectful of the relationship.

Reporting harassment or unfair treatment remains uneven. Many people still prefer to leave a workplace quietly rather than confront a perpetrator, though anti-harassment policies and managerial training are becoming more common in both public and private sectors.
