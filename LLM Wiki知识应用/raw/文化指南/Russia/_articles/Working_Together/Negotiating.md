# Negotiating

## Working Together

In Russian business life, formal agreements are meaningful but rarely final on their own. The concept of *dogovor* (agreement or contract) includes the written terms, but also whether the relationship underneath can handle shifts, delays, and unforeseen circumstances. A signed document matters, but so does whether you've become someone whose reasoning and follow-through are reliable. This dual layer means negotiations often take longer than counterparts from cultures where closure comes quickly might expect, and pressure to finalize early can read as shallow seriousness. A colleague from Russia may read that haste as lack of care, while someone accustomed to swift deal-making may read Russian deliberation as avoidance. Neither is accurate. The slower pace reflects the need to test consistency before trust forms.

That consistency matters partly because of how insider and outsider status shapes the conversation. The *svoi/chuzhoi* divide, often translated as "ours" versus "outsider," organizes how much candor is offered and when real flexibility appears. Early on, you're still *chuzhoi*. That changes through steady reasoning, closed loops, and visible alignment between what you say and what you do. Sudden reframes, shifting priorities, or last-minute pivots can move you backward. When trust begins to build, networks may come into play. The legacy of *blat*, the informal exchange system that once unlocked access in shortage economies, still shapes how relationships open doors. Respect these connections if they exist, but they're context, not a substitute for proving yourself dependable.

Russian negotiators are also risk-sensitive in ways rooted in cultural memory. The saying "initiative is punishable" captures a long-held caution about sticking your neck out when consequences depend on hierarchy or unpredictable shifts. In practice, this means your counterpart may need time to check internally, consult upward, or document trade-offs before agreeing. Don't ask for immediate closure across hierarchy levels. Instead, offer options, make the path to agreement as risk-safe as possible, and allow room for thoughtful review. Calm, grounded language works better than theatrical urgency. Steadiness is read as competence, not passivity.
