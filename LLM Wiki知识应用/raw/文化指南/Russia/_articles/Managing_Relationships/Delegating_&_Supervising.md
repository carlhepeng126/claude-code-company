# Delegating & Supervising

## Managing Relationships

Delegation in Russia typically involves giving precise, detailed instructions with the expectation that they will be followed exactly as given. Discretion and improvisation are rarely encouraged. This approach reflects a deep-rooted preference for decisive, top-down authority that spans from Tsarist rule through the Soviet period, when individual initiative could invite scrutiny and compliance was often the safest course. The result is a working style where employees implement as directed, but no further. Managers who delegate vaguely or expect team members to interpret and adapt on their own often find their instructions produce incomplete or misaligned results.

This style can feel restrictive to managers from cultures where delegation includes empowerment and problem-solving discretion. They may perceive Russian team members as reluctant to take ownership. From a Russian perspective, however, a manager who delegates without clear direction may appear uncertain or unprepared, qualities that undermine credibility. Authority is expected to be visible and assured, and consultation that looks like indecision can weaken a leader's standing.

The workplace also operates through parallel systems: formal hierarchy defines who holds authority, but personal networks determine who can actually make things happen. Access to decision-makers, who are often not the people in the meeting, depends on personal connection. Building these relationships is essential to getting work done, particularly when processes stall or formal channels prove slow.

A generational divide is visible, especially in Moscow's international business sector. Younger professionals, shaped by exposure to global management practices, may respond more comfortably to collaborative styles than colleagues who built their careers under the Soviet system, where non-delegating leadership was the norm.
