# Motivating Others

## Managing Relationships

Motivation in Russia deepens when people feel genuinely inside the circle. The cultural boundary between *svoi* (one of us) and *chuzhoi* (outsider) shapes how commitment is given. Inclusion cannot be announced or promised. It is proven through consistency, fairness, and visible care. A manager who defends the team when it counts, keeps their word, and shows practical support begins to cross that threshold. Once someone feels truly included, loyalty and effort often intensify in ways that formal incentives alone cannot produce.

Stability and clarity can feel like profound respect. In a context marked by long cycles of upheaval, predictability is rarely bland. It is a form of dignity. Clear expectations, reliable follow-through, and calm, steady leadership often energize teams more effectively than high-energy campaigns or shifting priorities. When people know what is expected and trust it will not change without reason, they are freer to focus on the work itself. In contrast, a manager from a culture where quick pivots and rapid iteration are valued might interpret this preference for stability as resistance to change, while Russian colleagues may read constant course corrections as a lack of seriousness or an inability to plan thoughtfully.

Recognition works best when it is specific and rooted in competence. Name the judgment, skill, or rigor you observed. Generic praise can feel shallow. Pride often comes from mastery and from work that is framed as meaningful rather than transactional. Avoid micromanaging. Trust people to apply their expertise, and acknowledge it when they do.

Shared experience builds cohesion without requiring personal disclosure. Informal time together, whether over a meal or outside the office, can strengthen bonds and deepen commitment. This is about being present together, not about vulnerability or self-revelation.
