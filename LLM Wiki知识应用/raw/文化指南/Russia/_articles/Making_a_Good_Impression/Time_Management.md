# Time Management

## Making a Good Impression

Russia's geography shapes time management more visibly than in most countries. Spanning 11 time zones, internal coordination is a constant practical challenge. Teams specify time zones explicitly in meeting invitations, use calendar tools that auto-convert across zones, and rotate meeting times to avoid always favoring one region. What looks like vague scheduling or inconsistent attendance may simply reflect the logistics of coordinating Vladivostok and Moscow.

The calendar also bends around major holidays. New Year and Victory Day in early May create significant travel and attention disruptions. Responsiveness slows noticeably in late December through early January and around the May holiday period. Plan critical milestones outside these windows, and confirm availability well in advance if collaboration depends on these weeks.

Summer introduces a different rhythm. Dacha culture, the practice of spending weekends or longer stretches at a countryside second home, shapes warm-month availability. Colleagues may split their time between city and dacha, making scheduling less predictable. A delay in response is often logistical rather than a sign of disinterest. Offering flexible meeting options or asynchronous updates during summer months keeps momentum without forcing rigid timelines.

Underlying these patterns is a cultural relationship with time that values depth over speed. Russians often avoid committing to hard deadlines, preferring phrases like "we will try" or "perhaps" or "it will be very difficult." This hedging reflects a tendency to avoid personal accountability for failure. Short written confirmations after verbal agreements help reduce ambiguity and keep distributed teams aligned without needing to press for public commitment.
