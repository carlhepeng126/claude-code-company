# Business Meeting Culture

## Meetings & Presentations

Meetings in Russia reflect a culture shaped by strong central institutions, where visible hierarchy determines who speaks first, whose perspective holds weight, and who has the mandate to decide. The most senior person typically opens the meeting and sets the direction. Their role is to inform others of what has been decided or to outline next steps, often after key details have been agreed in private beforehand. Disagreement or new ideas rarely emerge on the spot. If you need a decision, it can help to suggest that junior teams meet separately to develop a recommendation that senior counterparts can then approve together.

Russians often bring a strikingly direct style to meetings, comfortable with frank questions and open critique. This reflects a cultural respect for substance and seriousness rather than aggression. A colleague from a culture where challenge is softened with hedging may read this directness as confrontation, while a Russian colleague may interpret that same hedging as evasiveness or lack of preparation. Responding to blunt questioning with calm evidence and logical detail tends to build credibility, not tension.

Trust deepens as you move from outsider to insider. Warmth and informality may arrive later, once you are seen as reliable and known. Use continuity of contact, follow-through on small commitments, and dependable action to signal that you belong inside the circle. Bring concrete materials, anticipate objections, and show you understand the broader context around your topic. Preparedness reads as respect, and detail outweighs inspirational framing.

Allow more time than scheduled. Meetings may start late or run longer than planned, and side conversations unrelated to the agenda are common. After the meeting, confirm decisions in writing and reconfirm by phone or in person, as outcomes are often revisited once senior authorization is sought.
