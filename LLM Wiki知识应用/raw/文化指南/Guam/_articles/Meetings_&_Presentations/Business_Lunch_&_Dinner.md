# Business Lunch & Dinner

## Meetings & Presentations

Business meals in Guam tend to be relaxed and sociable, reflecting the island's blend of Chamorro, Filipino, American, and broader Pacific influences. Food itself becomes a way to build connection. You'll likely encounter dishes like red rice, kelaguen (a dish of chopped meat or seafood mixed with lemon, coconut, and hot pepper), and finadene (a soy-based dipping sauce with citrus and pepper). These aren't just menu items but expressions of local identity, rooted in Chamorro tradition and adapted through generations of contact with other cultures.

Sharing is common. Meals are often served family-style, with platters placed in the centre for everyone to help themselves. This reflects a broader cultural preference for collective experience over individual separation. Offering a taste of your dish or accepting food from others strengthens rapport. Declining repeatedly, or eating without engaging with others at the table, can feel impersonal.

The tone is generally informal, even in professional settings. First names are used early, and conversation moves easily between work and personal topics. Business discussion may weave through the meal rather than dominate it, and decisions are rarely finalised over food. For colleagues from cultures where meals follow a structured agenda or where personal and professional topics stay separate, this fluidity can feel unfocused. From a Guam perspective, a meal that stays strictly transactional may seem cold or overly rushed.

Pace is unhurried. Meals are meant to be enjoyed, and leaving immediately after finishing can suggest disinterest. Allowing time for conversation after the last course shows respect for the relationship being built.
