# Forms of Address

## Making a Good Impression

In Cameroon's bilingual professional environment, forms of address reflect both inherited colonial structures and deeply rooted respect for age, position, and education. French-speaking regions tend toward more formal conventions, where titles such as Monsieur, Madame, or Professeur are paired with surnames and remain standard until explicit permission is given to do otherwise. English-speaking regions often mirror British formality, with Mr., Mrs., and Dr. used consistently in initial exchanges. Across both linguistic zones, professional titles are taken seriously. A colleague with a doctorate or a senior government role expects that title to be acknowledged, and omitting it can read as either careless or disrespectful. When emailing or addressing a meeting participant, check how they sign their own correspondence and mirror that level of formality.

Regional and generational differences shape how quickly first names enter the conversation. In urban centers like Yaoundé and Douala, younger professionals working in tech or international firms may move to first names after a few meetings, especially if the relationship feels collaborative. In government ministries, state enterprises, or when working with senior figures in rural areas, formality lasts longer and shifts only when the senior person initiates it. Jumping to first names too early can feel presumptuous, particularly if you are younger or newer to the organization.

Written communication follows the spoken norm but adds a layer of ceremony. Emails typically open with a full greeting and close with formal phrases such as Cordialement or Respectueusement in French, or Yours faithfully in English. Even in regular project correspondence, maintaining this structure shows that you recognize the relationship has professional weight. Dropping formality in writing before it has shifted in person can create confusion about where the relationship actually stands.
