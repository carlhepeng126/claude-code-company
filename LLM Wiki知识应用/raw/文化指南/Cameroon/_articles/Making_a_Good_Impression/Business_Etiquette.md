# Business Etiquette

## Making a Good Impression

Cameroon's workplace etiquette reflects its colonial inheritance and its multilingual, multi-ethnic character. French and English are both official languages, and which one opens a meeting often depends on the region and the person you're speaking with. In Francophone areas, French sets the tone for formal business. In Anglophone regions, English does. Switching between the two, or acknowledging both, can signal cultural fluency and respect for the country's linguistic divide. Presenting a business card in both languages is a practical courtesy that shows you've thought about your audience.

Greetings matter, and they tend to be warmer than a quick handshake suggests. Expect questions about your journey, your comfort, or your well-being before any agenda is raised. These exchanges build familiarity and set the relational foundation for what follows. Launching straight into business without this preamble can feel abrupt or transactional, especially to older professionals or those in more traditional sectors. Younger colleagues in urban centers like Douala or Yaoundé may move more quickly, but even then, a few minutes of conversation before the meeting begins is the norm.

Dress conservatively and formally, particularly in government or corporate settings. For men, a suit and tie is standard. For women, tailored clothing that covers shoulders and knees is expected. Appearance is read as a sign of seriousness and professionalism, and showing up underdressed can undermine credibility before a word is exchanged.

Hierarchy shapes how decisions are made and how respect is shown. Defer to seniority in meetings, and allow senior figures to speak first. Interrupting or challenging a senior colleague in front of others risks causing embarrassment and damaging the relationship. If you need to raise a concern, do so privately and tactfully.
