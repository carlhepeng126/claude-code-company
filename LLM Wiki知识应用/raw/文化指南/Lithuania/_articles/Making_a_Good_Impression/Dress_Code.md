# Dress Code

## Making a Good Impression

Professional credibility in Lithuania aligns with neatness and restraint rather than display. Clothing that reads as clean, well-maintained, and contextually appropriate tends to build trust more reliably than bold or overly casual choices. This reflects a broader cultural pattern where understatement often outweighs flash, and where being properly turned out is understood as a form of respect for the setting and the people in it. For first meetings or formal contexts, conservative business attire is a safe baseline: a well-fitted suit in darker tones for men, a tailored suit or modest dress for women. These expectations ease once relationships develop, but early impressions hinge on getting the tone right.

Regional context shapes what "appropriate" looks like. Vilnius tends to be more style-diverse and cosmopolitan, while Kaunas carries a stronger tone of national pride and formality. Smaller towns often lean more conservative and less experimental. These differences are social textures rather than strict rules, but they suggest it's worth calibrating to the local environment rather than assuming one national standard applies everywhere. In practical terms, this means observing what colleagues and clients wear in a given city or sector, and adjusting accordingly.

Season and weather also shape expectations around appropriateness. Long winters make practical preparedness visible, and outerwear, footwear, and overall readiness for the climate can be read as competence and self-respect. Arriving well-prepared for the weather is a small but meaningful signal.

In ritual or memorial contexts, modesty and respect-readiness become more central. Catholic identity remains culturally present, and significant religious or pilgrimage-linked spaces carry their own aesthetic expectations, even for non-religious visitors. In these settings, covered shoulders and understated choices reflect awareness of the occasion rather than personal style.
