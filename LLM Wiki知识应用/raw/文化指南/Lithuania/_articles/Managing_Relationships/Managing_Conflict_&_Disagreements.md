# Managing Conflict & Disagreements

## Managing Relationships

Disagreement in Lithuanian workplaces tends to be handled quietly and through clear structure. Emotional self-control during conflict is often read as maturity, and raising your voice or intensity can make the situation feel unsafe for others, especially in shared or formal settings. This preference comes partly from a cultural instinct to preserve dignity and partly from historical memory of times when public alignment or exposure could be risky. The result is that people typically aim to contain tension rather than air it out in front of the group.

When conflict does emerge, many Lithuanians look for fair process and the right way to handle it. A manager who steps in with a clear plan, or who takes people aside to resolve things privately, is giving everyone a way to resolve the issue without losing face. Forcing someone to defend themselves openly or pushing for immediate resolution can backfire. A pause or a day's distance may be a deliberate cooling move, and it should be respected as such rather than read as avoidance.

If you need to move someone off a project or reverse a decision, offer them a way to explain it to colleagues that preserves their standing. Saying that responsibilities have shifted, or that the directive came from senior leadership, makes the decision easier to accept. A colleague from a culture where direct, in-the-moment resolution is the norm may find this indirect, but a Lithuanian counterpart may see a rushed confrontation as unnecessarily exposing and even unfair.
