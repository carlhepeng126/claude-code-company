# Feedback and Coaching

## Managing Relationships

In Brazil, feedback works best when it preserves dignity and strengthens connection. The tradition of elders mentoring younger people through example, advice and stories rather than harsh critique runs deep across family and community life. That same approach often shows up in the workplace. Guidance is expected to help someone grow, framed with encouragement and delivered in ways that avoid public shaming or damage to the relationship.

Protecting face matters. Direct negative feedback in front of others can feel deeply embarrassing and undermine trust. Managers who frame coaching as supportive rather than judgmental, and who deliver developmental feedback privately, are much more likely to be heard. Balancing positive remarks with areas for improvement helps maintain motivation and keeps the conversation constructive. Asking for the other person's view first can soften the dynamic and make the exchange feel more collaborative than instructive.

The way feedback is given varies across sectors and settings. High-performance corporate environments, particularly in multinational firms or financial hubs, may expect and deliver more direct feedback. In other contexts, tact and cushioning are preferred. Stories, examples and suggestions often land more effectively than blunt assessments, particularly when the relationship is still being built. A manager from a culture where critique is delivered quickly and explicitly may find that their Brazilian colleagues withdraw or seem less engaged after a conversation they assumed was clear. From the Brazilian side, that same directness can feel abrupt or dismissive, especially when it comes without relationship or context.

Trust and genuine care provide the foundation. When coaching comes from someone who has invested in the relationship and clearly has the other person's development in mind, feedback is far easier to receive and act on.
