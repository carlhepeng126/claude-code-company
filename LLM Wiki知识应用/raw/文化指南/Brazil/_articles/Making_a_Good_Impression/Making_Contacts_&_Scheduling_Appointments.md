# Making Contacts & Scheduling Appointments

## Making a Good Impression

In Brazil, professional connections grow through personal networks rooted in a long tradition of relational trust. The country's blended heritage—Indigenous, Portuguese, African, and waves of later migration—has shaped a culture where warmth and personal connection come before purely transactional engagement. People want to know who you are and whether you can be trusted before committing to anything significant. That means the fastest route to a meeting is often through someone who already knows both of you. An introduction from a colleague, a friend from the same alumni network, a church community, or a business association signals that you're worth the time. Cold outreach can work, especially in larger corporations in São Paulo or Rio, but it tends to move more slowly without that initial layer of credibility.

Once contact is made, expect small talk before business talk. This is where rapport is built, and skipping it can make the conversation feel abrupt. A colleague from a culture where efficiency means starting with the agenda right away may interpret this as wasted time, while the Brazilian counterpart may view a quick dive into details as impersonal or even untrustworthy. Both are reading the same moment differently: one values directness, the other sees relationship-building as the foundation for everything that follows. In practice, this means allowing space for questions about your journey, your impressions of the city, or recent local events before you move to the purpose of the meeting.

Scheduling works best when you plan ahead but stay flexible. Book appointments at least two weeks in advance and reconfirm closer to the day, especially if you're coordinating across cities where traffic or last-minute shifts are common. Messaging apps like WhatsApp often get faster replies than email, and it's normal to use multiple channels to stay in touch. Avoid the weeks around Carnival and other major festivals, when offices may be quieter and family or community commitments take priority. In smaller, family-run companies or in regions outside the major financial centers, meetings may start late or stretch beyond the scheduled time. Leaving immediately when the formal discussion ends can feel abrupt; staying for a coffee or a few minutes of conversation shows you value the relationship, not just the transaction.
