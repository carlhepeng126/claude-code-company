# Business Etiquette

## Making a Good Impression

Cuban business etiquette is rooted in a culture that values personal warmth and relationship building before procedure. First impressions are shaped more by how you connect as a person than by how polished your credentials appear. Greetings tend to be warm and unhurried. A handshake is common, often accompanied by steady eye contact and a genuine smile. Among colleagues who know each other, a kiss on the cheek or a light embrace is typical. Skipping this kind of personal acknowledgment in favor of a brisk introduction can come across as cold or transactional.

Formality in address varies by context. Titles matter, especially when meeting senior figures or working within state institutions. Referring to someone as *Licenciado* (a university graduate), *Doctor*, or *Ingeniero* (engineer) shows respect. First names come into play once rapport has been established, but waiting for your Cuban counterpart to suggest the shift is the safer approach.

Dress tends toward smart casual in many professional settings, though this depends on the sector and the occasion. In government or formal business contexts, conservative dress is expected. In creative industries or smaller private ventures, the tone may be more relaxed. What remains consistent is an appreciation for neatness and effort. Appearing too casual can suggest a lack of seriousness about the relationship or the work at hand.

Gift giving is uncommon in formal business settings and should be handled with care. A small, thoughtful gift from your home country may be well received in a more personal context, but avoid anything that could be seen as an attempt to influence or bypass process.
