# Forms of Address

## Making a Good Impression

Titles matter in Cuba. Use "Señor" (Mr.) or "Señora" (Mrs.) followed by the last name in professional settings, or "Licenciado" for university graduates and "Doctor" for those with advanced degrees or medical training. Professional titles reflect educational achievement and status in a society where university education has historically been valued and widely accessible. First names are reserved for established relationships. Waiting for someone to invite informality shows you understand the boundary between professional respect and personal connection.

Workplace greetings follow a pattern: handshakes are standard for first meetings and formal occasions, but among colleagues who know each other, especially women or between men and women, expect a light kiss on the cheek. This greeting is not casual familiarity but a sign that the relationship has moved beyond strictly transactional. If you maintain formal distance when others have shifted to warmth, you may be read as cold or aloof. If you move to informality too quickly, you risk seeming presumptuous.

Written communication, including email, stays formal longer than face-to-face exchanges. Open with the person's title and last name, and close with "Saludos cordiales" or "Atentamente." Even as spoken interactions become warmer, written tone may remain structured. This reflects the enduring formality of official channels in a context where institutional correspondence still follows traditional conventions.
