# Business Etiquette

## Making a Good Impression

Serbian business etiquette reflects a worldview where hospitality is a moral commitment, not decoration. The Slava tradition, recognized by UNESCO, shows how relationships are renewed through ritual gathering in the home, bringing together relatives, neighbors, and friends. This carries into professional life. When Serbians offer coffee or suggest continuing a conversation over food, they're extending an invitation into a deeper circle of trust. Accept these moments with sincerity. Over-formality or treating warmth as procedural small talk can feel socially thin.

Conversation often carries real feeling. The gusle tradition of epic singing reminds us that story, memory, and emotion are socially legitimate ways of processing experience. Serbians are outspoken and expressive, prone to exaggeration to make a story land better, and they use dark humor freely. Discussions may sound heated to someone from a culture where disagreement is softened, but this is how ideas are tested. At the same time, face matters deeply. Direct challenge in front of others can prompt evasion or a reshaped answer to avoid being put down publicly. If you need clarity, follow up privately.

Participation deepens belonging. The kolo chain dance, where people interlink hands and move together, offers a useful metaphor: inclusion is shown through joining in, not standing outside the circle. When a toast is offered or a shared meal begins, engage with the rhythm of the moment rather than hovering at the edges. This is where relationships thicken.

Small gestures follow unspoken rules. A firm handshake signals personal strength; never shake hands while wearing gloves. Serbians stand close and may grab your arm for emphasis. Stepping back will register as distance or mistrust. Firm eye contact is expected and read as sincerity. If you are given a gift, open it on the spot and thank them warmly.
