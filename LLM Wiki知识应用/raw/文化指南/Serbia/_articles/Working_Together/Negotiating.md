# Negotiating

## Working Together

Negotiating in Serbia rarely follows a straight line from offer to agreement. Conversations can circle back to earlier points, shift between business and personal matters, or pause for what seems like a digression but often serves a relational purpose. This reflects a cultural instinct in which persuasion and trust-building are intertwined. The kafana, a traditional gathering place where politics, friendships, and deals have long been discussed over drinks and food, offers a glimpse of this logic: serious agreements can emerge from settings that feel informal or even emotionally charged. What looks like small talk or storytelling may be doing the work of testing character, building rapport, or creating the relational ground that makes a later commitment feel safe.

Time moves differently once negotiation begins. Decisions rest with senior figures, so reaching the person with authority to commit is essential. Once at the table, expect discussion to extend well beyond the technical. Serbian counterparts may use a wide range of tactics to secure better terms: appeals to personal connection, claims of hardship, emotional expression, or anecdotes that shift the mood. This can feel disorienting to a colleague from a culture where negotiation is more linear and emotionally neutral. From the Serbian side, a counterpart who moves quickly to close terms without building relational confidence may seem transactional or untrustworthy, as though they are treating the deal as more valuable than the relationship itself.

Dignity runs through the process. Agreements gain traction when both sides can join the outcome without loss of face. This means leaving room in your opening position, conceding points visibly, and maintaining a tone that allows your counterpart to feel they have shaped the result. Raising your voice or displaying frustration during negotiation does not always indicate breakdown; it may simply be part of the expressive exchange. Stay steady in response, and avoid signaling impatience, which can be read as disrespect or pressure.
