# Motivating Others

## Managing Relationships

In Gambia, people respond most strongly to motivation that recognizes their contribution to something shared. A sense of team, loyalty to a respected leader, and the knowledge that the work uplifts the group tend to generate more sustained energy than individual incentives alone. When someone feels valued as part of a collective effort, and when that effort reflects well on the household or wider network, commitment deepens naturally.

Financial incentives have their place, but they rarely stand on their own. A manager who takes time to acknowledge effort personally, who invites someone to take on a visible responsibility, or who quietly thanks the team after a demanding period, sends a message that resonates more than a formal bonus structure might. Recognition works best when it feels genuine and when it is given in the moment, particularly if it happens in front of others whose opinion matters.

Rigid deadlines and tightly structured targets tend to produce compliance rather than enthusiasm. Gambian professional life moves within a more fluid sense of time, where personal and family obligations take clear precedence and where long-term planning is often seen as contingent on God's will. Expecting someone to work late consistently, or to treat a project timeline as non-negotiable when it conflicts with family needs, will erode goodwill quickly. Managers who can hold the direction steady while adjusting the pace to fit the rhythm of people's lives will see better results than those who push for strict schedule adherence.

What motivates can shift depending on context. Younger professionals, especially in urban settings or international firms, may be drawn to training opportunities, career development, or the chance to build a visible professional reputation. In smaller towns or more traditional settings, stability and the standing that comes with a respected role often mean more than mobility or external recognition.
