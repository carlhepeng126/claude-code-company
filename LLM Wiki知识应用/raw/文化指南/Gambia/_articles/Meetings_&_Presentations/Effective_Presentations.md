# Effective Presentations

## Meetings & Presentations

Presentations in Gambia work best when they feel like an exchange rather than a monologue. Your audience will be listening not only to what you say, but watching how you engage with them. Warmth and approachability open doors that technical precision alone cannot. Even in formal business settings, people respond to speakers who connect rather than lecture. If you come across as distant or overly scripted, you risk being seen as someone who doesn't value the relationship being built in the room.

Pacing tends to be slower than in cultures where speed and conciseness are prized. Gambians often take time to reflect before responding, and silence doesn't signal disengagement. A colleague from a culture where quick exchanges and immediate questions are the norm may read this as hesitation or lack of interest, while a Gambian participant may find rapid questioning pushy or insufficiently thoughtful. Allow space for responses to develop naturally. Pushing for immediate reactions can feel transactional and may close off the very dialogue you're hoping to encourage.

Storytelling strengthens your message. Abstract arguments or data-heavy slides can feel impersonal. Anchor your points in real situations or examples that your audience can recognize. This reflects a broader oral tradition where meaning is conveyed through narrative and context, not just bullet points. Keep supporting materials clear and visual, and avoid overloading slides with text.

Flexibility also matters. People may step out to take calls or join partway through. This isn't disrespect but a reflection of how responsibilities overlap in smaller professional environments. Welcome latecomers warmly and be ready to recap key points without showing frustration. Trust builds through how you handle interruptions, not just how polished your delivery is.
