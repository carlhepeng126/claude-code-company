# Forms of Address

## Making a Good Impression

In Gambian professional settings, forms of address reflect both respect for hierarchy and the country's cultural blending of Islamic, indigenous, and colonial influences. Addressing someone correctly acknowledges their place in a web of social and professional relationships, and getting it wrong can create distance before a working relationship even begins.

Titles are expected, particularly when seniority or professional standing is involved. "Mr." and "Mrs." paired with the surname are standard in formal business contexts, and elders or senior colleagues may be addressed as "Pa" (father) or "Ya" (mother) followed by their first name, a practice rooted in communal respect and widespread across ethnic groups. Professional titles such as "Doctor," "Engineer," or "Director" are used consistently and paired with the surname. In government and corporate settings, omitting a title can read as casual to the point of disrespect, especially when directed at someone older or more senior.

First names come into play once a relationship has been established, but the invitation usually comes from the senior person. In many workplaces, younger or more junior colleagues continue using titles even after years of working together, particularly if the other person holds a position of authority. This endures across urban and rural contexts, though younger professionals in Banjul may shift to first names more readily among peers.

Written communication follows the same logic. Emails and letters open with full titles and surnames, and closings remain formal unless the relationship has explicitly shifted. Dropping formality too soon, even in a follow-up message, can suggest either unfamiliarity with Gambian norms or an attempt to bypass the respect owed to the person's role.
