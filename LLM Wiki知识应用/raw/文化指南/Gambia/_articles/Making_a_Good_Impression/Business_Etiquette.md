# Business Etiquette

## Making a Good Impression

Business interactions in The Gambia begin with greetings that set the tone for everything that follows. People take time to exchange pleasantries, ask about health and family, and often reconnect through shared acquaintances. This early conversation establishes trust and signals that the relationship extends beyond the transaction. Rushing past this stage to get straight to business can feel abrupt and may undermine the rapport you need. If you come from a context where meetings start with agendas and timelines, the warmth and repetition here might seem inefficient. From a Gambian perspective, efficiency without connection risks emptiness. Spending a few minutes on greetings and small talk builds the foundation for collaboration.

Dress expectations reflect formality and respect. Men typically wear suits or smart trousers with long-sleeved shirts. Women dress conservatively, with skirts or trousers that cover the knees and tops that cover the shoulders. In some rural or more traditional settings, local attire such as the *grand boubou* (a flowing robe worn on formal occasions) may be worn. Dressing modestly shows awareness of cultural norms and signals that you take the meeting seriously.

Punctuality is valued in principle, but flexibility is often necessary in practice. Traffic, power outages, and last-minute changes can shift schedules. Arriving on time is the right aim, but don't expect every meeting to start or finish as planned. If your counterpart is late, avoid showing frustration. Build in buffer time where you can and use waiting periods to deepen conversation rather than treat them as disruptions.

Gift-giving is not expected in formal business settings and can be misread early in a relationship. Focus first on building connection. If invited to someone's home, however, a small gift such as sweets or dates is a thoughtful gesture and reflects good manners.
