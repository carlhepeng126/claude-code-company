# Decision-Making

## Working Together

Decisions in Gambia move through layers of consultation that honor both respect and relationship. Senior figures hold real authority, and their approval is often needed before action can proceed. But this doesn't mean decisions are simply handed down. Leaders consult informally with trusted colleagues, family members, or elders before finalizing a course of action. The goal is to ensure the decision fits socially as well as operationally, reducing the risk of conflict or disruption within the group.

This approach reflects a culture where harmony and collective input are valued over speed. A manager may already know the direction they prefer, but they will still sound out key people to build alignment and legitimacy. If you're working with Gambian colleagues, expect decisions to take time, and don't mistake consultation for indecision. Pressing for a quick answer can come across as disrespectful or blind to the social consequences a choice might have. Instead, allow time for the process and make your input available without bypassing senior figures.

Colleagues from cultures where decisions are made quickly and individually may read this consultation as delay or avoidance of accountability. From a Gambian perspective, however, a decision made without proper input risks being socially unsound, and therefore unlikely to hold. What looks like flexibility is often a careful balancing of competing considerations, including family, regional ties, and long-term relationships that extend beyond the workplace.
