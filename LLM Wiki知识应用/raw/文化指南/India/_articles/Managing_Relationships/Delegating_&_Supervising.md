# Delegating & Supervising

## Managing Relationships

In India, the manager's role is unambiguously directive. Clear instruction is expected, not debated. This expectation is rooted in thousands of years of social stratification, from the caste system through Mughal court culture and British colonial hierarchy to the bureaucratic structures that followed independence. While the official caste system is illegal, its shadow persists in how people relate to authority and what kinds of work are considered appropriate to which roles. An Indian employee who is not given explicit direction may feel unmanaged rather than empowered. Collaborative decision-making, common in some global settings, can be read here as indecisiveness or lack of interest. The concept of jugaad, creative improvisation in the face of constraints, shows that Indians are highly resourceful, but this resourcefulness is typically exercised within the authority structure, not as a challenge to it.

Alongside directive leadership, a paternalistic style is also expected. Managers who show genuine interest in their team's personal lives — family, health, circumstances outside work — earn a qualitatively different level of loyalty. The two poles of the Indian management ideal, the strong boss and the caring parent, are often held simultaneously. This means that delegation comes with ongoing supervision and personal connection, not distance.

Feedback and concerns are unlikely to surface in group settings. Employees will rarely flag problems or raise disagreement openly in front of peers or upward in a meeting. Private, one-to-one channels are essential if you want to hear what is actually happening. Maintaining role boundaries also matters: doing work considered beneath your station can erode credibility rather than signal accessibility. What is appropriate varies by sector, generation, and region — a manager in a Mumbai tech startup may operate quite differently from one in a Chennai family business or a Delhi ministry.
