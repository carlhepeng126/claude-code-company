# Feedback and Coaching

## Managing Relationships

Public criticism can end a working relationship in India before you've had a chance to explain yourself. The concept of *izzat* (honor) runs deep, learned early in school and family life, and being corrected harshly in front of colleagues can be remembered for years. When feedback needs to be given, the setting and tone determine whether it strengthens or damages trust. Corrective feedback delivered privately, with respect, and followed by concrete support allows the relationship to stay intact and the work to improve. A colleague who feels their dignity has been preserved will be more open to change than one who has been called out in a meeting.

Polite language around problems often means more than it sounds. Phrases like "we'll try," "there were some challenges," or "it needs some improvement" can signal serious concerns, especially when used by someone who doesn't feel safe being more direct. A colleague from a culture where feedback is explicit and immediate might hear reassurance where an Indian colleague is actually flagging risk. From the other direction, blunt critique can feel unnecessarily harsh to someone expecting feedback to be wrapped in care. Asking follow-up questions in private helps surface what's actually needed without forcing someone to escalate their language publicly.

India's exam culture and *guru* tradition shape how coaching is understood. People are used to being evaluated, ranked, and pushed hard by respected seniors, but they expect that toughness to come with advocacy and protection. A manager who sets high standards but doesn't visibly support their team members in securing opportunities or navigating internal politics may be seen as using them rather than developing them. High expectations paired with concrete help builds the kind of loyalty and effort that formal performance reviews alone cannot.

Feedback frameworks and one-on-one conversations are spreading in multinational companies and some Indian corporates, but comfort with these practices varies. Younger professionals in urban centers may be more familiar with self-assessment and open dialogue, while others may still find it uncomfortable to critique a boss or disclose professional doubts. Inviting feedback on your own approach in low-risk ways, such as asking "what could we do differently next time," signals that the conversation can flow both ways without threatening the hierarchy.
