# Business Etiquette

## Making a Good Impression

Offering chai, coffee or water when someone arrives is nearly universal in Indian business settings. Accepting something, even if just a glass of water, acknowledges the host's welcome and the effort behind it. Refusing everything can feel like closing the door on connection before the conversation even begins. If dietary or health reasons prevent you from accepting, a brief explanation paired with thanks helps preserve the gesture's meaning.

Initial meetings often start with personal questions about where you're from, family, or your impressions of India. This is relational mapping, a way of understanding who you are beyond the agenda. These exchanges build the foundation for a working relationship, and colleagues from contexts where meetings open straight into business may wonder when the discussion will start. From the Indian perspective, colleagues who skip this stage can seem transactional or uninterested in the people they're working with. Engage in the conversation; it's not a delay, it's part of the work.

Hierarchy shapes how meetings unfold. Seating, speaking order, introductions and who closes the discussion are often aligned with seniority. Juniors may stay quiet even when they've done most of the technical preparation. Direct key questions to the senior person while finding ways to gently involve quieter experts, perhaps by inviting them into the conversation through the senior colleague.

It's common for people, especially seniors, to take brief calls or respond to messages during meetings. This reflects multiple parallel priorities rather than disengagement. If you need undivided attention for a critical part of the conversation, say so clearly and without reproach. The request will usually be honored.
