# Business Lunch & Dinner

## Meetings & Presentations

Food choices in India are rarely neutral. Many colleagues are vegetarian or observe specific restrictions rooted in religion, caste, or region: no beef for Hindus, no pork for Muslims, halal meat for some, fish-only diets for others, and fasting on certain days. What someone eats reflects identity, and asking about preferences before choosing a venue or ordering shows respect for those identities. When planning meals, ensure menus clearly mark vegetarian and non-vegetarian options so guests can choose without explanation.

Business dinners typically start late, often between 8 and 9 pm, and extend well beyond the meal itself. Conversation moves fluidly between business topics, personal stories, and local issues. This blend of professional and personal content deepens the relationship, which is central to building trust. Booking back-to-back evening commitments or signaling a need to leave early can come across as transactional and may undermine the connection the meal is meant to create.

In many settings, especially outside major metro centers or with established partners, spouses or children may join business dinners. This inclusion reflects a culture where professional and family life are not rigidly separated. Engaging warmly with family members, particularly elders or children, is appreciated and contributes to the relationship.

Eating with the right hand from shared dishes or thali plates is common practice and entirely appropriate, even in professional settings. If you prefer cutlery, use it quietly without comment. The left hand should not be used for eating. Table manners vary by formality and region, but the underlying expectation is the same: flexibility and respect for the local approach to hospitality.
