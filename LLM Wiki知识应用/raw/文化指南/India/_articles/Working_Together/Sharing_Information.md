# Sharing Information

## Working Together

In organizations where hierarchy is pronounced and internal politics are real, information often functions as strategic capital. Senior leaders may hold back detail to maintain leverage or control outcomes, while juniors ration what they share upward to shield themselves from blame. This caution reflects a workplace reality where being the messenger of bad news can put your reputation at risk, particularly in settings where loyalty and face are closely watched. The result is that status reports may sound more optimistic than circumstances warrant, and phrases like "it's difficult but manageable" can cover ground from minor friction to serious trouble.

Softening unwelcome news is usually about protecting relationships and immediate emotions rather than deliberate evasion. In hierarchical environments, delivering unfiltered bad news can feel disrespectful or destabilizing, so people calibrate their language to the audience and the moment. If you need an accurate read on project status, ask clarifying questions tied to specific indicators rather than relying on tone alone. Agreeing early on what "done" or "on track" actually means helps close the gap between reassurance and reality.

Informal networks run parallel to official channels and often move faster. WhatsApp groups, alumni ties, community links and long-standing friendships create back-channel flows where people cross-check what they hear from management against what peers and trusted contacts report. These networks can be more current and candid than formal updates, so using more than one communication mode and building relationships beyond the org chart will give you a fuller picture. Critical know-how also tends to sit with a few long-tenured staff, vendors or specialists who hold institutional memory in their heads rather than in systems. Involving these people early in planning and recognizing their expertise improves both the quality of collaboration and the flow of information.
