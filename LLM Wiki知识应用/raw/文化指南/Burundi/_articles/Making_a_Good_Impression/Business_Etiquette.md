# Business Etiquette

## Making a Good Impression

In Burundian professional settings, respect flows through small gestures and careful attention to hierarchy. Greetings are never rushed. A handshake is expected, often accompanied by direct eye contact and a warm inquiry about someone's health, family, or journey. With senior figures or elders, it's common to support your right forearm with your left hand while shaking, a mark of deference rooted in the country's deep regard for age and position. Skipping this step, or greeting too casually, can suggest you haven't grasped the relational ground you're standing on.

Conversations begin with relationship-building, and this matters more than moving quickly to business. Asking after family or recent events isn't a formality but a way of establishing who you are in relation to the other person. Trust comes first, then transaction. If you're used to opening with an agenda or timeline, expect the pace here to feel slower. That slowness reflects the priority placed on understanding and connection, which Burundians see as foundational to any credible working relationship.

Directness, especially when it involves disagreement or bad news, is softened to protect dignity. A "yes" might mean "I've heard you" rather than agreement, and refusals are often expressed indirectly through hesitation, vague language, or deferral to others. Listen for what isn't being said outright. Pressing for clarity can backfire if it forces someone into a position where they must say no openly or challenge a senior colleague.

Gift-giving is appropriate once a relationship has been established, but avoid anything too personal or expensive early on. Simple, thoughtful gestures work well: local crafts, sweets, or items from your home country. Always offer and receive with your right hand, or both hands as a sign of respect.
