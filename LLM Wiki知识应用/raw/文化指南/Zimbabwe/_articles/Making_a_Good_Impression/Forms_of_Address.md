# Forms of Address

## Making a Good Impression

In Zimbabwe, how you address someone communicates more than courtesy. It reflects attentiveness to age, professional standing, and the social responsibility these bring. Seniority is understood as a marker of life experience and moral authority, so address shifts visibly when an elder or senior colleague enters a conversation. Begin with formal titles—Mr, Mrs, Dr, or professional qualifications—paired with surnames, and wait for an explicit invitation to move to first names. This applies especially in early meetings, where the formality sets the tone for credibility and willingness to engage.

Respectful address often includes small, visible gestures. Standing when a senior person enters the room, or placing your left hand on your right forearm during a handshake, are ways of acknowledging hierarchy without words. People also lower direct eye contact with superiors, not as disengagement but as deference. A colleague from a setting where sustained eye contact signals confidence may read this as evasiveness, while a Zimbabwean colleague may find unmodulated eye contact from a junior person presumptuous or culturally unaware.

Terms like "Auntie" or "Uncle" are sometimes used for older colleagues outside literal family relationships, turning a formal exchange into something relationally warmer and socially safer. Getting a colleague's name right, especially its correct pronunciation, also holds weight. In a multilingual country where identity has often been flattened by outsiders, careful attention to names becomes a form of recognition.

People move fluidly between formal and informal modes depending on context—workplace, church, rural home, public institution. The choice is relational, not fixed, and reading the setting correctly is part of professional competence. In mixed groups, a respectful baseline reduces the small frictions that accumulate when status cues are misread or ignored.
