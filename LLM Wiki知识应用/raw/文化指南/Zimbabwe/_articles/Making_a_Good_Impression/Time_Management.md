# Time Management

## Making a Good Impression

Time in Zimbabwe bends around bereavement, transport realities, and communal duty. These are not interruptions to a normal schedule. They are the schedule, and they reorganize work life when they arise. A colleague who travels three hours across rural roads for a funeral is not making a personal choice. They are fulfilling a moral obligation that takes precedence over most other commitments. When someone says a family event will delay a project, they are stating a fact that carries social weight across generations and settings.

Economic instability has trained many Zimbabweans to plan with flexibility built in. Years of hyperinflation and unpredictable infrastructure mean people confirm, adjust, and hold backup options as routine practice. This is why follow-up messages and last-minute changes happen more often than colleagues from more stable economies might expect. If you set a single fixed deadline without checking in, you may find yourself waiting without explanation. Build in confirmations and buffers, and treat them as part of the timeline, not a workaround.

Transport shapes punctuality as much as intention does. In cities, *kombis* (shared minibuses) run on their own rhythm, which means arrival times can shift even when someone leaves early. Rural colleagues face longer distances and fewer options. Showing respect for someone's journey, by offering flexibility or acknowledging travel constraints, reduces friction and last-minute cancellations. A meeting rescheduled because of transport is not a failure of commitment. It reflects real constraints that are often beyond individual control.

Urban workplaces may run closer to fixed schedules, especially in formal sectors, but even there, relationship and presence often come before the clock. Finish the conversation before moving to the next appointment. That priority is consistent across settings.
