# Making Contacts & Scheduling Appointments

## Making a Good Impression

Warm introductions change access in Zimbabwe. Trust is often extended through networks rather than credentials alone, and a shared contact who can vouch for you can speed up response times and open doors that cold outreach rarely shifts. For many Zimbabweans, knowing where someone is from and who their people are provides a relational map that shapes how they are seen. This is partly tied to the idea of *kumusha* (one's rural home or origin), which remains an identity anchor even for urban professionals. When making first contact, mention any mutual connections early, and if possible, ask someone trusted to make the introduction directly.

Scheduling works best when treated as coordination within a social system, rather than purely as an availability question. Bereavement obligations, church commitments, and family duties can shift calendars quickly, and whole networks may reorganize around funerals or memorial cycles. These are not peripheral events but core communal structures, and presence at them is a moral expectation. Confirm appointments in writing and follow up by phone the day before. Do not rely on email alone. Build flexibility into your schedule. Meetings may run longer than planned, or a colleague may need to reschedule due to a family obligation that carries real social weight.

The adaptive timing sometimes described as *kukiya-kiya* reflects a lived history of economic instability. People have learned to confirm close to the moment, solve logistical problems through networks, and adjust plans as conditions change. A colleague from a culture where schedules are fixed weeks in advance may read this as unreliable. From a Zimbabwean perspective, someone who cannot adapt when circumstances shift may seem inflexible or unworkable over the long term. Reliability here is often judged by how you respond under changing conditions, not just whether you arrive on time.
