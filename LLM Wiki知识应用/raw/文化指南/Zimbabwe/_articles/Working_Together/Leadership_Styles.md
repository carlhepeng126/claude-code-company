# Leadership Styles

## Working Together

Leadership in Zimbabwe is often read through a moral frame shaped by Chimurenga memory and Great Zimbabwe pride. Authority that appears to serve community uplift rather than personal status tends to hold more credibility. Strength is associated with steadiness and the protection of dignity, not dominance or public humiliation. Leaders who respect seniority structures and recognize local competence earn acceptance more readily than those who speak in ways that sound condescending or assume sophistication is imported. A leader who arrives with solutions already formed, without acknowledging what exists, risks triggering defensiveness rooted in deep historical sensitivity.

In environments shaped by volatility, predictable fairness can feel like care. Calm consistency and follow-through are read as protection, not just competence. A leader who changes course without explanation or who applies rules unevenly may lose trust quickly, even if the intent is adaptive. In practice, this means explaining decisions clearly, honoring commitments, and treating people humanely when pressure rises. Leadership is assessed not only by results but by how relationships are handled: whose dignity is protected, who is respected in front of others, and whether fairness is visible.

Age and experience still carry authority, especially in tradition-inflected settings or rural contexts. A younger leader, particularly a woman, may need to build credibility more deliberately by demonstrating competence, consulting senior colleagues, and avoiding directness that could be read as dismissive. What a colleague from a culture where speed and clarity are prized might see as efficient decision-making may be received in Zimbabwe as bypassing relational accountability. Conversely, a Zimbabwean leader's careful attention to seniority and dignity may be read by that same colleague as slow or overly cautious, when it is actually a form of respect that sustains commitment over time.
