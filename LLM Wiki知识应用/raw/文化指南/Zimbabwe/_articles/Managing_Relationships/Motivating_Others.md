# Motivating Others

## Managing Relationships

Motivation in Zimbabwe is deeply rooted in responsibility that stretches beyond the individual. Supporting parents, siblings, children, and wider kin is not an afterthought but often the central purpose of working. For many, the ability to provide reliably is what defines both success and character. Being dependable enhances family standing and affirms one's identity, particularly within the moral frame of *kumusha* (rural home and ancestral roots). This means that stability and fairness in the workplace often resonate more strongly than individual advancement alone. A manager who offers predictable pay schedules, clear expectations, and consistent follow-through signals care because these things reduce uncertainty for households managing real financial risk.

Recognition can be motivating, but it needs to be handled thoughtfully. Public praise may feel honoring to some, but for others it can feel exposing, especially if it draws envy or unwanted scrutiny. People often weigh whether being singled out is socially safe. Quiet respect can be just as powerful: being trusted with responsibility, receiving sincere private acknowledgment, or being asked to contribute in a meaningful way all register as recognition without the awkwardness of a spotlight. Framing contribution in relational terms, such as "this helps the team" or "this strengthens what we're building together," aligns with *hunhu* or *unhu*, the value placed on decency and social regard.

Many Zimbabwean professionals are part of transnational family networks, coordinating support across borders through WhatsApp groups and managing remittance rhythms alongside their day-to-day work. These obligations are not distractions. They shape what people need from their jobs and why predictability and responsible planning feel morally significant. A colleague from a culture where personal goals are the primary frame for motivation may see these family commitments as complicating factors, while a Zimbabwean colleague may view a purely individualistic approach as disconnected from what gives work meaning.
