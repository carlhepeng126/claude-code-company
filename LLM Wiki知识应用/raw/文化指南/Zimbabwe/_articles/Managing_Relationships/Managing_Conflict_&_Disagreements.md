# Managing Conflict & Disagreements

## Managing Relationships

In Zimbabwe, the relationship itself functions as infrastructure. Disagreements are managed in ways that preserve connection because people's networks overlap across work, family, church, and community. Public confrontation risks a rupture that echoes beyond the workplace, so conflict often moves into private settings where dignity can be protected and the relationship repaired without an audience.

The cultural memory of the *dare* (a traditional council of elders) supports a repair pathway where third parties and quiet counsel are legitimate tools. Conflict is understood as something to contain and resolve without humiliating anyone. If tensions are stuck, involving a trusted intermediary such as a minister or respected senior figure can help mediate without forcing a winner and loser. For a colleague from a culture where direct feedback is expected in the moment, this can feel slow or indirect. From a Zimbabwean perspective, a public challenge may feel reckless because it damages standing in front of others, and that damage can be harder to undo than the original disagreement.

Disagreement may show up as delay, reduced cooperation, or silence rather than open challenge. This is a coherent way to signal that alignment has not been achieved without forcing a confrontation. If you are leading a team, check in gently when silence follows a decision. Questions like "What feels unresolved?" or "What concerns are still there?" invite dissent safely and make it clear that face will be protected.

Some topics carry historical or regional charge, and different communities hold different lived memories. People may tread carefully because the social risk of mis-stepping is real, even when the subject seems routine to an outsider.
