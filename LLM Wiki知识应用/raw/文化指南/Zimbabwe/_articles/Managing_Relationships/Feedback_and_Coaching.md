# Feedback and Coaching

## Managing Relationships

Feedback in Zimbabwe is rarely just about the message itself. It also reveals how the person delivering it understands personhood and respect. The cultural concept of *hunhu* (or *unhu* in Ndebele), which frames character through how you treat others, gives particular weight to how correction is handled. Coaching that preserves dignity reflects moral maturity. Coaching that exposes or humiliates someone, even if factually correct, can suggest the opposite. In tight-knit networks formed through church communities, school groups, or hometown ties, public embarrassment can linger beyond the moment and affect standing in ways that reach well beyond the office.

This is why sensitive feedback is best given in private, and why phrasing tends to be softened rather than blunt. Leading with observation rather than judgment ("I noticed this pattern" instead of "You did this wrong") makes it easier to receive without feeling shamed. Questions can also serve as a form of guidance, allowing the person to reflect rather than defend. Indirectness here is a relational tool, designed to keep the working relationship intact while still naming what needs to change.

Hierarchy and seniority also shape how feedback lands. The same message can be interpreted differently depending on who delivers it and how much social or organizational standing they hold. In more community-rooted settings, correction may feel most legitimate when it comes from someone whose role or age gives them cultural authority to guide. A younger manager offering direct critique to a senior colleague may find the message dismissed or resented, not because it was wrong, but because it felt culturally out of place.

For managers from cultures where candor and speed are valued, this approach can feel overly cautious. From the other direction, a Zimbabwean colleague may read blunt feedback delivered in a group setting as disrespectful or careless, regardless of intent. Balancing clarity with care means being specific about what needs to improve and how to do it, while ensuring the tone and setting support the person's ability to act on it without loss of face.
