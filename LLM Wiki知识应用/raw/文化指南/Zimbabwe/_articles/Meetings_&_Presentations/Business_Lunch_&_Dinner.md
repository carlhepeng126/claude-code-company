# Business Lunch & Dinner

## Meetings & Presentations

Business meals in Zimbabwe serve as relationship infrastructure, not just opportunities to eat while talking shop. Trust is built over shared food, and the care taken in hosting, even under constraint, can say more about a partnership than the agenda discussed. When colleagues invite you to lunch or dinner, they are often extending more than hospitality: they are offering a space where formality loosens slightly and rapport becomes possible.

Sadza, a stiff maize porridge, appears frequently and holds particular meaning. It is comfort food and a marker of home. Offering it to a guest can be a gesture of warmth and belonging, especially when the meal takes place outside of formal hotel settings. You may also encounter dishes rich in peanut butter, roasted or stewed meats, and tropical fruit. In urban hotels, international buffets are common, but even in these settings, the relational tone of the meal often outweighs the menu itself.

When senior figures or elders are present, the atmosphere typically shifts. Warmth may be expressed through attentiveness rather than animated conversation. Colleagues may become more restrained, listening carefully and avoiding questions that could put someone on the spot. If you are hosting and notice this change in tone, follow the lead of those at the table rather than trying to push for more casual exchange. Respect can look like quiet care, and recognizing that rhythm helps preserve dignity.

Practical realities also shape what is feasible. Many people rely on kombis (shared minibuses) to get around, and transport access, family obligations, and safety considerations can make evening meals logistically difficult. If you are arranging a dinner, check timing and location with colleagues rather than assuming availability. In smaller centers especially, feasibility often outweighs formality.
