# Making Contacts & Scheduling Appointments

## Making a Good Impression

Making business contact in Bosnia and Herzegovina begins with understanding that trust is built through people, not process. Decades of conflict and political instability have shaped a professional environment where personal connection comes before institutional reliability. People want to know who stands behind a proposal before engaging with the proposal itself. That means introductions through mutual acquaintances open doors far more effectively than cold emails or LinkedIn requests. A shared colleague or friend who can vouch for your credibility changes the tone of the conversation from cautious to receptive. If you're entering a new market or organization, ask who in your network has ties there and arrange an informal introduction before pursuing a formal meeting.

Generational differences are visible in how initial contact unfolds. Younger professionals, particularly in Sarajevo and international firms, are often comfortable with digital outreach and may move to business topics more quickly. Older colleagues and those outside the capital tend to prefer phone calls over email and expect more time spent on personal exchange before discussing work in detail. In either case, the first conversation is rarely transactional. Expect questions about where you're from, who you know in common, or how you came to work in the region. These aren't digressions; they're how people assess whether the relationship has a foundation worth building on.

When scheduling appointments, give at least one to two weeks' notice and confirm the day before. Meetings may start later than planned or extend well beyond the scheduled time, especially if the conversation is going well. Avoid tightly packed schedules. A colleague who arrives unannounced may join the meeting, or a more senior figure may want to be introduced before decisions move forward. Flexibility in timing shows respect for how decisions actually happen, which is rarely according to a fixed agenda.
