# Business Etiquette

## Making a Good Impression

Business etiquette in Bosnia and Herzegovina is shaped by a history of hospitality and coexistence, where maintaining good relations across ethnic and professional lines has long been necessary for stability. Greetings are warm and often accompanied by direct eye contact and a firm handshake. Conversations typically begin with inquiries about health, family, or recent events. This opening exchange establishes tone and builds trust before moving to business. Skipping this phase can come across as transactional or cold. A colleague from a culture where meetings begin promptly with agendas may find this pacing slow, while a Bosnian counterpart might interpret a rushed start as dismissive or impersonal.

Communication is often indirect when disagreement arises. Open critique, particularly in front of others, risks causing offense or damaging relationships. Hesitations, pauses, or a shift to vague language can signal discomfort or reluctance. Asking clarifying questions privately, rather than pressing for a clear answer in the moment, allows room for honest exchange without public loss of face. This indirectness reflects the value placed on group harmony and respect for hierarchy, especially in more traditional or public-sector settings.

Dress codes tend toward formality in initial meetings. Men typically wear suits, while women opt for conservative business attire. Once rapport is established, dress may relax slightly, but maintaining a polished appearance continues to reflect professionalism and respect. In smaller towns or older companies, formality may remain consistent throughout the relationship.

Gift-giving is not expected in professional contexts but is appreciated in more personal or long-term partnerships. Small, thoughtful items such as quality chocolates or regional products from your home country are well received. Gifts should be presented and received with both hands or the right hand only.
