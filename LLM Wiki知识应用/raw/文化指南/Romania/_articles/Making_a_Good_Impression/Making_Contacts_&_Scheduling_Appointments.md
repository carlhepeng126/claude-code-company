# Making Contacts & Scheduling Appointments

## Making a Good Impression

Cold outreach rarely works in Romania. Trust tends to be earned through consistency and time rather than assumed upfront, a pattern rooted in late-communist experience when public and private realities were often kept separate. People want to know who they're dealing with before they commit. A warm introduction from a mutual contact or professional intermediary can shorten this testing phase considerably. Trade missions, industry events, or introductions through chambers of commerce provide the kind of social proof that helps establish legitimacy early on. Even with a strong introduction, expect the relationship to deepen in layers rather than all at once.

Face-to-face contact still holds weight, especially when the stakes are high. While email and phone calls can set things in motion, decisions and commitments are more likely to follow in-person meetings where people can read tone, intent, and reliability. Plan for several conversations before any agreement is finalized. For colleagues from cultures where deals close quickly after one or two exchanges, this can feel slow. From a Romanian perspective, that pace reflects caution born from experience, and rushing it can register as superficial or untrustworthy. Accepting invitations to coffee or informal meals is part of how people decide whether you're serious and respectful.

When scheduling, book meetings at least a week or two ahead and confirm the day before. Keep your calendar flexible. Meetings may start late, run longer than expected, or be joined by others not originally planned. Summer, Easter, and Christmas can be difficult for business as many operations wind down. Regional differences exist but are often subtle in professional settings. Younger professionals in Bucharest or other cities may move more quickly to business topics, but even there, building rapport before launching into details is rarely a misstep.
