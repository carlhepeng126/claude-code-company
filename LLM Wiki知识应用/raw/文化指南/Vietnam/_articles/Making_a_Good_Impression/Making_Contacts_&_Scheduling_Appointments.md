# Making Contacts & Scheduling Appointments

## Making a Good Impression

Connection in Vietnam is often maintained through ritualised attention. Tết (Lunar New Year) reunites families across regions, while *giỗ* (death anniversaries marked at household ancestor altars) create a repeating cycle of gathering and remembrance. These practices shape how professional relationships are read. A first contact that goes well but isn't followed up may fade faster than in settings where networking feels more transactional. Keeping the thread alive through small gestures, remembered details, or a follow-up message after a gap shows you understand how trust is built over time.

An introduction places you inside a social map. Alumni ties, hometown links, or mutual contacts reduce uncertainty by giving your counterpart a sense of where you belong and who vouches for you. This isn't gatekeeping. It's a practical way to build confidence when hierarchy, family background, and shared reference points still shape how people assess credibility. If you don't have a local introduction, explain clearly how you came to select this company or person. That context helps your counterpart locate you, even without a direct connection.

Regional differences show up quickly. Hanoi, the central cities, and Ho Chi Minh City each carry distinct rhythms in how people initiate contact, what counts as polite directness, and how fast things move. Accent, humor, and food identity often reveal where someone is from, and that "home" remains part of how they narrate themselves, even in urban professional life. Check the local pace before assuming informality or speed will be welcome.

Book meetings a few weeks ahead and reconfirm closer to the date. Expect the first meeting to focus on context and rapport rather than immediate business. Punctuality is expected, so arrive on time. Keep early correspondence warm and respectful rather than brief or blunt.
