# Time Management

## Making a Good Impression

Scheduling in Vietnam operates on two calendars at once. The institutional calendar organizes meetings, projects, and deadlines. The family calendar, particularly the cycle of *giỗ* (death anniversaries honoring ancestors) and the lead-up to Tết (Lunar New Year), governs availability in ways that often override formal plans. These are not competing systems but parallel ones, and family obligations tied to lineage and reunion can be emotionally non-negotiable. Tết functions like a cultural reset: people return home, repair relationships, and begin the year fresh. This means that time commitments around Tết are real planning boundaries, and major decisions or deliverables scheduled for this period will likely meet slower responsiveness or need rescheduling.

Outside the major cities of Hà Nội and TP.HCM, time is filtered through what is feasible locally. Travel distances, infrastructure realities, and weather can shift what "urgent" means in practice. Even in urban centers, final confirmations often happen closer to the moment via messaging platforms like Zalo, which serves as a key coordination channel. A plan made by email may firm up only through a quick message the day before or morning of. This does not reflect disorganization but a preference for real-time adjustment as circumstances clarify. Colleagues from cultures where a confirmed calendar entry is considered final may read this as unreliable, while Vietnamese colleagues may view rigid insistence on advance confirmation as inflexible or out of touch with practical realities.

To work effectively, treat Tết as a hard boundary and plan around it early. Use buffer thinking outside major cities, and confirm closer to the time in a light, friendly way rather than with long explanations. Offering time windows instead of demanding a single precise slot allows structure without forcing false certainty. Learn which channel your counterpart uses for last-mile coordination and use it appropriately. If plans shift, focus on locking the next option smoothly rather than treating rescheduling as a failure of commitment.
