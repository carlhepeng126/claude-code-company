# Forms of Address

## Making a Good Impression

Vietnamese uses kinship-like terms to place age and relationship even in professional settings. Instead of defaulting to job titles or surnames, people often address one another as *anh* (older brother), *chị* (older sister), *em* (younger sibling), *cô* (aunt), *chú* (uncle), or *bác* (older uncle or aunt), depending on perceived age and seniority. These pronouns locate you in a social geometry where respect is encoded through how you position yourself relative to the other person. Getting it wrong can feel like misreading the relationship, not just choosing the wrong word.

The legacy of Confucian norms means politeness often shows up as role-appropriate speech: restraint, careful tone, and sensitivity to seniority. Respect comes through pacing and deference more than through explicit praise or formality of title. Being too casual too soon can read as socially unskilled, especially with senior colleagues or clients. In written communication, opening with "Kính gửi" (respectfully sent to) followed by the appropriate pronoun or title reflects this same awareness.

Names follow the structure of family name, middle name, then given name. Most people are addressed by their given name, prefaced with the appropriate pronoun. So Nguyễn Văn Hải would likely be addressed as "anh Hải" by a younger colleague or "em Hải" by someone senior. If you are unsure of relative age, choose the pronoun for an older person to signal respect.

Regional and organizational contexts shift what feels proper. Northern, central, and southern styles differ, and state-linked, family-run, and foreign-invested firms each have their own subcultures of formality. Watch how colleagues address one another and mirror that pattern, adjusting as relationships develop.
