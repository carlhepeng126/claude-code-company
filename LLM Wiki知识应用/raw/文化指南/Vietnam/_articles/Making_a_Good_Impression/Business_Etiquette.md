# Business Etiquette

## Making a Good Impression

Etiquette in Vietnam reflects a deep socialisation in respect, often learned first in the home. Many families maintain household altars for ancestors, where rituals of gratitude and remembrance teach children to be attentive and to handle what others hold sacred with care. Even among those who have moved away from religious practice, these early lessons in careful behaviour often persist. The tone that emerges is not simply "good manners" but something closer to moral attentiveness, where how you act around others becomes a reflection of who you are. A colleague from a culture where directness is valued above formality may experience this as excessive politeness. From the Vietnamese side, bluntness in mixed-status settings can read as a lack of social skill or relational care, not as clarity.

Protecting dignity, your own and others', shapes how interactions unfold. Critique is often softened or moved to a private setting so that working relationships remain intact. Public confrontation disrupts the group and embarrasses the person being corrected, which can damage trust more than the issue being raised. When you hear "I'll think about it" or "I'll do my best," these often signal reluctance or disagreement without forcing a direct refusal. Open follow-up in a one-to-one conversation gives space for the real answer to surface without the pressure of an audience.

Vietnamese workplaces can feel informal on the surface: sidewalk café meetings, warm teasing, quick check-ins. But beneath that ease, quiet rules about seniority, deference, and status still apply. Watch who speaks first, who summarises, and who gets deferred to. Those cues reveal the invisible structure. If you begin a meeting or join a conversation, open with a relational bridge before moving to task content. Acknowledge effort, ask a brief question about someone's wellbeing, or name a shared goal. That small opener shows you understand that relationships underpin the work, not the other way around.
