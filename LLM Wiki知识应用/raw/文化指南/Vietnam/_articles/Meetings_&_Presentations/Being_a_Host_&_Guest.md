# Being a Host & Guest

## Meetings & Presentations

In many Vietnamese households, the home functions as moral and ancestral space. Ancestor altars keep lineage ties visible, and behaviour around these areas is often calibrated with quiet deference, especially when elders are present. Being a good guest here means being observant rather than performative. Watch where people sit, whether shoes are removed, how they greet senior family members, and how they move around sacred spaces. These cues shift between urban apartments and multi-generational rural homes, so the safest approach is always to follow rather than assume.

Invitations to gatherings such as *giỗ* (ancestral memorial ceremonies involving incense, offerings, and shared meals) often hold deeper relational weight than they appear to. These occasions reassert family continuity and belonging, and being invited can signal a level of trust and inclusion that goes beyond casual hospitality. Accept graciously, bring a modest gift that reflects attention rather than price, and treat the occasion with the seriousness it holds for the host.

Seating at meals follows the same hierarchy as in meetings, with senior guests placed centrally and facing one another. Wait for the host to say *xin mời* (please eat) before beginning. The host will typically serve you, and it is expected that you accept second and third helpings, even if small. If you genuinely cannot continue, signal this early by complimenting the food warmly and mentioning you are full, so declining the third round feels natural rather than abrupt.

Gifts should feel chosen, not expensive. Fruit, sweets, or incense work well. If you know the family enjoys alcohol and the relationship is established, wine or spirits are also appropriate. After the visit, follow up with a brief message of thanks or a remembered detail. Maintaining the relational thread afterward often means more than elaborate gratitude in the moment.
