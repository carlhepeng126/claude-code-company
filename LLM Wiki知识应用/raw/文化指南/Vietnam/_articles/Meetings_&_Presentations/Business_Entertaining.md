# Business Entertaining

## Meetings & Presentations

Shared meals are central to building trust in Vietnamese professional life. Food itself is a medium for warmth and inclusion, and the act of eating together often does more relational work than the conversation around it. Regional cuisines can hold pride of place, and hosting is typically understood through attentiveness rather than expense. A thoughtfully chosen street food venue or café can carry as much meaning as a formal restaurant, and intimacy is rarely tied to price. When someone invites you to a meal, the invitation is the gesture, not the setting.

*Nhậu* (drinking gatherings) can be significant in some circles, particularly among older male colleagues, but comfort levels vary widely by generation, gender, and company culture. Younger professionals and women may participate less enthusiastically, and some abstain entirely for religious or personal reasons. Assuming everyone will drink, or that bonding depends on it, can misread the room. If drinking culture is present, joining in lightly without pressuring others keeps the tone inclusive.

Business meals are rarely the place to advance deals. Early conversation stays warm and broad, and your counterpart will usually signal when, if at all, work topics are welcome. Pushing too soon can seem transactional. The meal is the relationship work. Small gestures matter more than grand ones: ordering to include everyone's preferences, noticing who might feel less comfortable, ensuring the seating works. Quiet attentiveness is often more effective than high energy or performance.

When hosting, invite the delegation as a group rather than singling out one person. The person who extends the invitation is expected to pay, and reciprocating on a future occasion is appreciated. If you need to decline something, do so gently and offer an alternative that preserves the social thread.
