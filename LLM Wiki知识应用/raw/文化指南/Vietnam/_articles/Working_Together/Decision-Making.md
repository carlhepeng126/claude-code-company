# Decision-Making

## Working Together

In Vietnam, the decision pathway is rarely as simple as the org chart suggests. Formal hierarchy matters, but influence often sits quietly with people who have longer tenure, deeper relationships, or the trust of key stakeholders. A manager may hold formal authority but still consult behind the scenes with those whose backing is needed for a decision to move forward without resistance. Understanding who must endorse a proposal, who can quietly block it, and who shapes opinion privately makes the difference between a proposal that stalls and one that gains traction.

Decisions are also shaped by a strong cultural preference for preserving group cohesion and face, especially across levels of seniority. Open disagreement in meetings is rare. Instead, concerns surface through indirect signals, follow-up conversations, or careful silence. The goal is alignment without rupture. This means that formal discussion may feel polite and inconclusive, with real debate happening in smaller channels afterward. What can look to a colleague from a culture where decisions are made quickly like unnecessary repetition is actually the work of building agreement without forcing anyone into a public yes or no that might create discomfort or exposure.

Many Vietnamese organizations, particularly those linked to state institutions, use structured approval processes that emphasize documentation, summary, and procedural legitimacy. Even in private-sector settings, how a decision is formalized can be as important as the content of the decision itself. These steps are not bureaucratic delays but part of how organizations create accountability and institutional cover. A decision that bypasses these steps may be seen as risky or incomplete, no matter how sound the reasoning.

Feasibility often outweighs elegance. Proposals are filtered through questions of real-world implementability: can it be done under current constraints, without creating friction or loss of face for those involved, and with the resources actually available. This reflects both practical realism shaped by rapid economic change and a cultural instinct to avoid outcomes that destabilize relationships or expose people to blame. Present options as workable pathways with clear roles, constraints acknowledged, and risks already thought through.
