# Feedback and Coaching

## Managing Relationships

Giving feedback in Vietnam means attending to dignity first, especially when hierarchy is involved. A harsh public correction can feel like social harm rather than useful clarity, and it can make people cautious or withdrawn. The same feedback delivered privately and with calm restraint tends to be received as professional care. This approach reflects a broader moral grammar shaped by filial piety and ancestor-oriented obligation, where how you treat others becomes a character signal. Correction that preserves the person is read as maturity, not just method.

Vietnam's socialist institutional culture includes a historic language of criticism and self-criticism, known as *phê bình* (self-critique). Even when modern workplaces don't use formal rituals, the cultural memory of public critique lingers. Delivery method can invoke that history, making people wary of exposure. Feedback works best when it focuses on what to do differently rather than who someone is, and when it's framed as dignified guidance rather than confrontation.

Role and seniority also shape how feedback lands. The same counsel can be received as helpful guidance from one person and as disrespect from another, depending on age, position, and relational legitimacy. Offering support alongside coaching—time, resources, a follow-up check-in—makes improvement feel accompanied rather than isolating.

Generational and sector differences add variation. Younger urban teams may tolerate more directness than elder-heavy or state-shaped environments, but even there, public shaming remains costly. Test the tone, then adjust to the micro-culture in front of you.
