# Business Etiquette

## Making a Good Impression

Professional interactions on the Åland Islands reflect a Swedish-speaking Finnish culture that values restraint, equality, and quiet consideration. Warmth is present, but it's expressed through reliability and respect for boundaries rather than immediate closeness. When meeting someone for the first time, a handshake and calm eye contact are enough. Extended small talk is uncommon. Instead, conversations move quickly toward substance. Oversharing personal details early on can feel premature, so let relationships develop at the pace your counterpart sets rather than pushing for familiarity.

Colleagues from cultures where rapport is built through detailed personal questions or storytelling may find Ålandic exchanges brief or even distant. From the other direction, an Ålandic professional might read extensive personal questioning as intrusive, wondering why the work itself isn't enough to anchor the relationship. Neither approach lacks warmth; each simply defines it differently.

Formality is understated. Titles aren't emphasized, and hierarchies tend to be flat, so addressing people by first name is typical once introduced. Still, this ease shouldn't be mistaken for casualness about process or commitments. Punctuality is expected, meetings follow agreed agendas, and promises are taken seriously. Saying "yes" means you will deliver, so hedging or vague assurances are read as unreliable.

Gift-giving in professional settings is rare and can feel awkward if it suggests imbalance. If visiting someone's home, a small, practical token such as flowers or chocolates is fine, but nothing elaborate. Business cards are exchanged without ceremony when useful, usually at the start or end of a meeting. There's no ritual attached, just an efficient transfer of information.
