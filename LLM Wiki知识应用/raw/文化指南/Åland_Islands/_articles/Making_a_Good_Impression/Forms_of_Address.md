# Forms of Address

## Making a Good Impression

Åland maintains strongly egalitarian social norms shaped by its Swedish cultural heritage and small island population. Formal titles are rarely used in day-to-day professional life. People typically address each other by first name from the outset, even when meeting for the first time or speaking to someone more senior. This reflects a broader cultural preference for flat hierarchies and personal directness. Using someone's title or last name without invitation can feel oddly formal or distancing, as though you are holding the person at arm's length rather than engaging as equals.

That said, this informality exists within clear expectations around politeness and modesty. First-name use does not mean casual behaviour. Meetings still begin with a firm handshake, eye contact, and a brief greeting. Tone remains respectful, and interrupting or overstating your position would be noticed. For colleagues accustomed to environments where titles signal respect and where first-name use must be earned, the immediate informality can feel uncomfortably forward or unclear about hierarchy. From an Ålandic perspective, insisting on titles or formal address in return may come across as unnecessarily stiff or status-conscious, misreading equality for disrespect.

In written communication, emails and letters open simply with the recipient's first name or a brief "Hej" (hello in Swedish). Lengthy formal openings are uncommon. Keep your tone warm but direct, and avoid over-elaboration in closings.
