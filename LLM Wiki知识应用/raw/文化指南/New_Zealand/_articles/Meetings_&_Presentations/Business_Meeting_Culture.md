# Business Meeting Culture

## Meetings & Presentations

Meetings in New Zealand often feel more casual than formal. People may arrive in jeans, share a joke during introductions, or keep the tone conversational throughout. But this relaxed style should not be mistaken for a lack of rigor. Expectations around follow-through and delivery are real. The informality reflects a broader cultural discomfort with status displays and hierarchy, not a looseness about outcomes. Come prepared with data, case studies, and clear reasoning, but present them in a way that invites discussion rather than announcing them from above.

One of the strongest norms in New Zealand meetings is the belief that everyone should have a say. People notice when someone dominates the room or when quieter participants are overlooked. This reflects the country's egalitarian impulse, where rank does not automatically grant the right to talk more or decide alone. If you are leading a meeting, make space for others to contribute, and if you are participating, expect your input to be invited even if you are junior. A colleague from a more hierarchical culture may find this disorienting at first, reading the open exchange as a lack of clear leadership. From the New Zealand side, silence from senior figures in those same meetings can come across as withholding or controlling.

Disagreement in New Zealand meetings is rarely voiced head-on. Instead, watch for silence, hesitant phrasing like "I'm not sure about that," or someone pulling back from a discussion. These are meaningful signals. Direct confrontation or visible frustration is uncommon, and when it does occur, it stands out sharply. If you sense resistance, follow up one-on-one after the meeting rather than pressing the point in the room.

In Māori-led contexts, the meeting may open and close with karakia (prayer), mihi (acknowledgments), or waiata (song). Speakers often follow a clear protocol, and the gathering is designed to honor relationships and mana (authority and dignity) as much as to resolve the agenda. These elements are foundational, not ceremonial. Trying to skip or shorten them to move things along will undermine trust and signal a lack of understanding of what the meeting is actually for.
