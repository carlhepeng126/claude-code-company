# Being a Host & Guest

## Meetings & Presentations

Being invited to someone's home in New Zealand, particularly outside the main cities, goes beyond professional courtesy. It signals a shift from work contact to personal trust, a willingness to show the real family context rather than just the office persona. If you receive this kind of invitation, treat it seriously. Canceling lightly will be noticed, and not in your favor.

A good guest contributes. The phrase "bring a plate" means exactly that: turn up with food to share, not an empty plate. More broadly, offering to help with setup, clearing dishes, or giving someone a ride home reflects an instinct to "muck in" that New Zealanders value. Standing back while others do the work reads as disconnected, even entitled. Ask "What can I bring?" and mean it, and look for small practical ways to pitch in before and after the meal.

If you are invited to a marae for an overnight hui, you are entering a relational deep dive. Shared sleeping spaces, communal chores, late-night conversation, and clear tikanga about where you sit, sleep, and speak are all part of the experience. Follow instructions carefully and be ready to do everyday tasks like washing dishes or sweeping. The willingness to join in, rather than observe from the sidelines, is what registers respect.

For colleagues from cultures where guests are expected to remain more formal or passive, this expectation to contribute can feel unclear. From the New Zealand side, a guest who waits to be served without offering help may come across as holding themselves apart.
