# Business Entertaining

## Meetings & Presentations

Business relationships in New Zealand are often built over coffee or a casual café meal rather than elaborate dinners. The instinct to "shout you a coffee" — covering the bill as a friendly gesture — reflects a culture that values low-key connection over visible displays of wealth or status. Suggesting high-end venues or expensive outings can feel uncomfortable and out of step. Good conversation and genuine interest build more trust than the setting itself. If you're proposing an informal coffee or lunch to continue a discussion, you're already speaking the local language.

Sport plays a significant role in relationship building, especially outside the main centers. An invitation to a rugby match, a local club game, or a sponsors' tent may seem informal, but these settings often hold more relational weight than a formal business dinner. Taking part or showing genuine interest when sport comes up can open doors that a restaurant reservation might not. Supporting or sponsoring grassroots events is also noticed and valued in a way that purely transactional gestures are not.

While after-work drinks and wine with dinner remain common, there's a growing shift toward more health-conscious and sober-curious norms. Many New Zealanders now actively limit or avoid alcohol, and a colleague who declines a drink isn't making a statement — they're simply exercising a personal choice that's increasingly normalized. If you're organizing an event, make sure non-alcoholic options are visible and treated as equals, not afterthoughts, and never press someone to drink.

Hospitality in New Zealand tends to be reciprocal. If a local colleague offers to host, accept and engage fully. Refusing or treating their gesture as less formal than your own can undermine the relationship you're trying to build.
