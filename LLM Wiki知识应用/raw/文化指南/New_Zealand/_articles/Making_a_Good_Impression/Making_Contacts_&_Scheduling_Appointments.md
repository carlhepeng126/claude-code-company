# Making Contacts & Scheduling Appointments

## Making a Good Impression

In New Zealand, connections are often made through networks that operate quietly in the background. School and university cohorts, sports clubs, professional bodies, and iwi or hapū organizations form the main channels through which reputations travel and introductions happen. This is less about formal patronage and more about overlapping communities where people already know each other's work. Before committing to a meeting, someone may check quietly through these circles to confirm you're credible and consistent. If you want to meet a senior executive or department head, you don't usually need to navigate layers of bureaucracy. Most people are accessible directly, though a warm introduction through a shared network will always help.

For work involving Māori or Pasifika communities, going straight to a corporate headquarters or central government office can miss the people who actually hold local authority. Iwi organizations, marae committees, church leaders, and community trusts are the entry points that matter. Approaching these contacts directly, and with humility, shows you understand how decisions are actually made and whose voices count.

In smaller towns and regional centers, reputations move through local clubs, churches, marae, and community projects. In Auckland and Wellington, networks tend to be more professional, running through LinkedIn, sector events, and word of mouth, but the same principle applies: people want to know who you are before they invest time.

Meetings should be scheduled at least a few weeks ahead and confirmed in writing. Dropping in unannounced is seen as presumptuous. Keep your delegation small. Many meetings happen one-to-one, and bringing a large team can feel heavy-handed. Plan for meetings to last around an hour, and avoid booking visits during the summer holiday period from mid-December through January, when many people take extended leave.
