# Teamwork

## Working Together

Teams in New Zealand emphasize collective achievement over individual spotlight. The cultural instinct is to say "we did this" rather than "I led this," even when one person drove the work. This reflects a deeper egalitarian reflex shaped partly by Māori *whānau* (extended family) structures and early settler cooperation. Publicly celebrating a single star performer can feel uncomfortable to the group. If you're leading a project, frame your updates around team contributions and acknowledge a range of people, not just the most visible one. This doesn't mean individual effort goes unnoticed, but recognition works best when it reinforces the group rather than isolating someone from it.

Practical contribution earns respect. The colleague who helps set up chairs before a meeting or fixes the projector when it fails gains real standing, regardless of seniority. This hands-on ethic means that coordinating from a distance without joining the work can read as detached or superior. A manager from a culture where delegation signals authority might assume that stepping back shows trust, while New Zealand colleagues might see it as opting out. From the other direction, a New Zealand manager who joins the setup work might seem unprofessional to someone expecting clearer role boundaries. Both interpretations miss the local logic: contributing practically shows you're part of the team, not above it.

Teams often include Māori, Pasifika, Pākehā, Asian, and migrant members, each bringing different comfort levels around speaking up, humor, or challenging authority. Māori or Pasifika staff may carry informal cultural work—opening meetings with *karakia* (prayer or blessing), translating, or bridging with community—that isn't always recognized or shared fairly. If this work stays invisible or falls to the same people repeatedly, it becomes an unspoken burden. Create multiple pathways for contribution—written input, pair work, full-group discussion—so different styles can be heard, and make sure cultural labor is acknowledged as a real contribution to the team's function.
