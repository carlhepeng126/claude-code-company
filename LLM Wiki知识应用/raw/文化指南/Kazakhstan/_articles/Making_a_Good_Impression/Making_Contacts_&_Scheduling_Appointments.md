# Making Contacts & Scheduling Appointments

## Making a Good Impression

Making contact in Kazakhstan begins with placement. Even in fully urban professional circles, people often locate one another through hometown, region, or family roots, sometimes drawing on older mental maps of lineage that extend back across the steppe. This is less about identity politics and more about quickly finding shared ground and assessing how you fit into a wider human geography of familiarity and obligation. It creates a foundation for trust before any business detail is discussed.

A warm introduction makes a real difference. Being brought in by someone already known transfers credibility in a society where reputations historically moved through relational chains. Who vouches for you can hold as much weight as what you bring to the table. Cold outreach, by contrast, leaves too much uncertainty on both sides. If you're working through a shared contact, expect the first meeting to focus on personal connection, not logistics. Let that conversation happen. It sets the tone and reduces the risk your counterpart is taking by agreeing to meet.

Kazakhstan's bilingual professional life adds another layer. Kazakh and Russian often carry different social textures, and switching between them can shift the emotional register of a conversation. Let your counterpart set the language and tone. Early hospitality, a coffee or tea invitation, is rarely just social. It functions as a trust test: can you accept inclusion gracefully, show respect for effort and seniority, and respond without overreaching? Treat these moments as part of the business process, not a delay before it.

Schedule appointments well in advance and confirm again closer to the date. Avoid tight back-to-back slots. Meetings often start later than planned or continue into a meal, and decisions rarely happen quickly. Multiple meetings are expected, with senior figures making final calls.
