# Business Etiquette

## Making a Good Impression

Business cards are expected in Kazakhstan and matter more than you might think. They should be printed in Russian on one side and English on the other, and exchanged at the start of a meeting, not midway through. Take a moment to look at what you've been given before putting it away. The detail communicates attention, and attention communicates respect. This isn't formality for show. Kazakhstan inherited a Soviet comfort with clear frameworks and credentials, and the business card is part of that structure. It confirms who you are and what you represent before the conversation deepens.

Greetings are warm but calibrated. Handshakes on meeting and leaving are standard across professional settings. Kissing or bear hugs are reserved for close friends, not colleagues. Body language can be expressive, especially during disagreement, with gestures that fill the space. This doesn't mean conflict. It reflects a cultural ease with direct debate paired with underlying warmth. Just don't shake hands across a doorway. It's considered bad luck, a superstition shared with Russia, and avoiding it shows awareness.

Hospitality follows a rhythm shaped by dastarkhan logic, the social institution of the table. If you're invited to eat, expect repeated offers and generous portions. Refusal and acceptance have their own dance. This is how warmth and status are communicated, and it extends beyond meals into broader relationship maintenance. Toi culture, the tradition of large life-cycle gatherings, reinforces this pattern. Relationships are not built once. They are renewed through repeated gestures, and this expectation shapes how people read your behavior in professional settings.

Respect for elders is structural, not symbolic. In many social and business contexts, older participants quietly set the tone, even when a younger person formally leads. Who speaks when, who is served first, and what counts as proper are often organized around age and seniority. If you need to raise an issue, especially one involving critique, do it in a way that preserves dignity. The surface may look brisk and procedural, but the social underlayer still governs how disagreement and feedback are received.
