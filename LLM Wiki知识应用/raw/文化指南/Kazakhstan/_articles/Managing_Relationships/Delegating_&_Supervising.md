# Delegating & Supervising

## Managing Relationships

Delegation in Kazakhstan often functions differently than formal structures suggest. Three cultural layers shape how tasks are assigned and followed up. Nomadic traditions emphasize collective decision-making and deference to seniority. Soviet-era institutional habits entrenched top-down control and compliance without initiative. Post-independence modernization has introduced more participative practices, especially in multinationals and the tech sector, but the earlier patterns persist. A supervisor may delegate clearly and assign authority, yet staff may still wait for explicit direction at each stage or seek personal approval before acting. This reflects a legacy in which showing initiative could bring severe consequences for mistakes, making inaction safer than independent judgment.

Tribal affiliation, rooted in the three *zhuz* (ancient confederations that still structure informal identity), can quietly influence who defers to whom. A team member may take guidance more readily from a colleague of the same *zhuz* than from a formally appointed supervisor, even when that supervisor has greater technical expertise. These loyalties are rarely stated but remain real. Building trust across *zhuz* lines requires sustained personal investment, not just reliance on organizational charts.

Public agreement in meetings does not always indicate private commitment. The Soviet-era habit of outward compliance while holding back genuine opinion remains common, particularly among older staff and in public-sector settings. One-on-one follow-ups after group sessions often reveal reservations that were not voiced openly. Problems may also be reframed or downplayed to avoid causing a superior to lose face, which means issues can go unresolved longer than expected.

Effective delegation in this context means providing clear directives, explicit authority to act, and visible follow-up support. Open-ended mandates that assume autonomous initiative tend to stall. Framing tasks with defined steps and regular check-ins produces better results and reduces the risk that staff will default to asking for personal favors rather than moving forward on their own.
