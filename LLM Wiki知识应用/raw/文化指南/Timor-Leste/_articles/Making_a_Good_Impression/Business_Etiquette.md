# Business Etiquette

## Making a Good Impression

In Timor-Leste, professional relationships begin with openness and courtesy. Greetings are warm, often accompanied by a handshake and direct eye contact. Taking time to exchange pleasantries before diving into business is expected, especially with senior figures or those you're meeting for the first time. Rushing this exchange can come across as transactional or dismissive. Asking after someone's family or commenting on shared connections signals respect and helps build the trust that underpins collaboration here.

Hierarchy shapes interaction. Senior colleagues and elders are addressed formally and given space to speak first. Deference is expressed through tone, posture, and listening without interruption. Younger professionals or those newer to an organization typically wait to be invited into the conversation. This respect for seniority reflects both cultural values and the lived experience of a generation that fought for independence. If you're working with a mixed-age team, be mindful that contributions from older colleagues often hold particular weight, even if they're not the most senior by title.

Dress tends toward the conservative. Men wear long trousers and collared shirts, often with a jacket for formal settings. Women dress modestly, with skirts or trousers that cover the knees and tops that cover the shoulders. In rural areas or smaller offices, expectations can be more relaxed, but erring on the side of formality is rarely a misstep. How you present yourself signals how seriously you take the relationship.

Punctuality is valued in principle but approached with flexibility in practice. Traffic, family obligations, or logistical challenges can delay meetings, and this is generally understood. If you're kept waiting, it's rarely meant as a slight. That said, arriving on time yourself demonstrates professionalism and respect, particularly when meeting government officials or international partners.
