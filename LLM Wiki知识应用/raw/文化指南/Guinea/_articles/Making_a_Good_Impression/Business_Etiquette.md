# Business Etiquette

## Making a Good Impression

Greetings in Guinea carry relationship-building weight that goes well beyond the words exchanged. People ask about family, health, and recent events as a way of signaling respect and establishing connection before any business topic arises. Rushing past this stage, or treating pleasantries as a formality, can feel disconnected. Warmth and patience in these opening moments help set the tone for what follows.

Guinean professionals often soften criticism and rarely say no in direct terms. This goes back in part to a history of denunciations during the Sékou Touré era, when public speech could have serious consequences. That legacy still shapes caution in groups today. People may respond with "maybe" or "we will look into it" when the real answer is no, or use humour and silence to steer away from confrontation. Reading between the lines becomes a necessary skill. A colleague from a culture where direct refusal is common may find this vague or evasive, while a Guinean counterpart may view explicit disagreement as unnecessarily blunt and even disrespectful. Both are working to protect something: one values clarity, the other face and group harmony.

Daily life in Conakry is shaped by intermittent power and water supply. A colleague who arrives late or tired may have spent the night without electricity or queuing for water that morning. Acknowledging these realities when setting meeting times or deadlines, rather than focusing only on productivity, signals that you understand the full context people are working in.

Guinea's reputation for djembe and balafon traditions is a genuine source of pride. Mentioning Guinea-specific cultural contributions carries more resonance than generic references to "African culture." Between colleagues from different ethnic groups, you may also hear playful ethnic banter rooted in *cousinage à plaisanterie* (joking cousinship), a traditional relationship between groups like the Fulani, Malinké, Susu, and Forest peoples. This is an insider practice and a sign of closeness, and best left to Guineans to initiate.
