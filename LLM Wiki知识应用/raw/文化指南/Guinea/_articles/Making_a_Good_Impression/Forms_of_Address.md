# Forms of Address

## Making a Good Impression

In official and professional contexts, Guinean colleagues combine French formality with kinship warmth. Initial address follows the pattern Monsieur or Madame plus a professional title such as Directeur or Ministre. In everyday conversation, however, people shift to affectionate kinship terms like grand frère, tonton, or vieux for older men, and equivalent terms for women. This blend of formal status and family-like language reflects a culture where hierarchy is real but relationships soften its edges. In practice, this means beginning with the full formal title and then watching how colleagues around you address the same person. If the team uses tonton, follow that cue rather than insisting on the surname.

Religious titles like El Hadj, Hadja, or Alfa appear frequently in business contexts and should never be dropped. These honorifics mark people who have made the pilgrimage to Mecca or who are recognized Islamic scholars. They represent moral authority and community leadership, and omitting them in a contract signature line or email salutation can undercut someone's standing in ways that outlast the meeting. If an imam or marabout is part of a project discussion, their title often serves as the basis for community consent, not ceremony.

Regional differences in politeness also shape how people expect to be addressed. Among Fulani communities in Fouta, soft speech and visible deference to elders are expected. In Mandé settings, jelis (griots) and lineage heads receive particular respect. In Forest Guinea, elders and people with certain ritual roles command special forms of address. A colleague from a culture where first names are standard from the outset may read this layered system as unnecessarily formal, while Guinean professionals may read the immediate shift to first names as dismissive of age, education, or spiritual standing. When in doubt, use the more respectful form and wait for the invitation to shift.
