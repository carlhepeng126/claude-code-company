# Time Management

## Making a Good Impression

Unreliable electricity shapes the working day in ways outsiders rarely anticipate. Power cuts mean nights without fans or light, disrupted sleep, and mornings when phones have not charged. A team that appears low-energy at eight in the morning may be coming off a night-long blackout. Asking about "le courant" before a critical session gives you the information you need to adjust timing or expectations, rather than reading fatigue as lack of commitment.

Regional geography adds another layer of complexity. In Fouta Djallon, fog and rain slow traffic on mountain roads; in Haute Guinée, heat and dust wear people down; in Basse Guinée, mining trucks and flooded roads turn short drives into hours-long journeys; in Forest Guinea, steep hills and mud change route timing entirely. Map distances mean little. Planning logistics with local travel times by season, not just proximity, means asking how long it really takes in July compared to January, and scheduling accordingly.

Daily prayers and Friday noon prayer are real anchors, and demonstrations, curfews, or sudden political announcements can halt movement and business entirely. Avoid scheduling major workshops across Friday prayer or when the streets are tense. Guineans prioritize safety and information in moments of uncertainty, and flexibility in those windows is read as awareness, not accommodation.

Attaya breaks, three rounds of sweet green tea, mark slower, social periods where people also handle calls to officials, relatives, and clients. They function as both pause and informal work. Accepting attaya time as part of the rhythm gives you an opportunity to sense-check deadlines and constraints informally, rather than treating it as lost productivity.
