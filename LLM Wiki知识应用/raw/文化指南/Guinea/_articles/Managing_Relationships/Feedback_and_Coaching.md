# Feedback and Coaching

## Managing Relationships

Feedback in Guinea is shaped by memories of Camp Boiro and the political imprisonments of the Sékou Touré era, when being singled out publicly often meant denunciation and danger. Family stories of that time still inform workplace behaviour today. Being corrected in front of others triggers anxiety that goes well beyond professional embarrassment, particularly when the feedback highlights individual error. Managers who understand this deliver critical feedback in private, using calm language and behavioural specifics that protect the person's standing within the group.

Regional and ethnic hierarchies add another layer. Publicly correcting a younger colleague from Forest Guinea or another historically marginalized region in front of older coastal or savanna elites can feel like replaying old power structures. Sensitivity to these fault lines means framing feedback in ways that avoid confirming stereotypes or reinforcing "your people are like this" narratives that have long divided the country.

Senior Guineans often deliver coaching through proverbs, anecdotes and half-praise, especially in Mandé settings influenced by griot storytelling traditions. This indirect method allows correction to happen without open confrontation, giving the recipient room to reflect and adjust without losing face. Managers working in Guinea should use stories and examples to frame coaching, and invite local seniors to express lessons in their own styles rather than imposing a foreign feedback template.

Written French appraisals may be skimmed or politely acknowledged without genuine uptake. Real understanding often happens when a trusted colleague re-explains the feedback in Pulaar, Susu, Malinké or a Forest language, adding cultural nuance that formal documentation misses. Always pair written feedback with a verbal conversation, and check that key points have been truly understood rather than simply noted.
