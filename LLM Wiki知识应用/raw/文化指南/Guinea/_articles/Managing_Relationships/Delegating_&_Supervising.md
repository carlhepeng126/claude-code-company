# Delegating & Supervising

## Managing Relationships

Delegation in Guinea unfolds within a landscape shaped by Islamic principles of leadership and decades of unreliable formal institutions. Authority comes with an expectation to provide and protect, not just direct. Managers are seen as responsible for the wellbeing of their teams in ways that extend beyond task completion. This means that time spent understanding team members as people — their family pressures, their networks, their circumstances — is foundational to functioning authority, not a gesture of goodwill. Delegating work without having built that relational foundation can leave instructions feeling hollow or unclear, even when they are technically specific.

Guinea's major ethnic groups — Fulani, Malinke, Soussou, and others — each bring distinct traditions around hierarchy and community. In ethnically mixed teams, the formal reporting structure may not reflect where deference actually sits or how information moves. Pre-existing alliances and tensions between communities can shape who speaks up, who stays quiet, and whose voice holds informal influence. A manager working across these lines needs to observe group interactions carefully and be alert to dynamics that won't appear on any org chart.

Raising concerns upward can feel socially risky. Flagging a problem may suggest that the manager's direction was unclear, or that the employee is disrupting the group's equilibrium. Don't wait for issues to surface spontaneously. Build regular, private check-ins into your rhythm, and demonstrate through consistent behaviour — not just stated policy — that candour won't be penalized. Younger, university-educated professionals, especially those in the mining and minerals sector, often expect more autonomy and flatter structures. They are more likely to take initiative and welcome delegated authority. In more traditional settings, however, employees may have little decision-making power and will look to you for clear, direct instruction on what is expected and why.
