# Managing Suppliers & Customers

## Managing Relationships

In mining zones and infrastructure corridors, suppliers and customers occupy space thick with competing expectations. They must meet corporate requirements and state regulations while managing pressure from local communities that expect jobs, compensation and visible benefit from extractive activity. A supplier who missteps in one of these relationships will find the story quickly retold, often shaping how other communities or cooperatives respond down the line. Designing these partnerships purely around cost or delivery speed misses the political sensitivity embedded in the work.

Women traders and small wholesalers in markets from Conakry to Labé hold detailed knowledge of stock flows, credit chains and consumer sentiment that formal reporting often overlooks. They remember who paid late during the last fuel shortage or currency swing, and that memory shapes future credit terms and stock allocation. Treating these informal and semi-formal actors as peripheral risks missing the people who actually control last-mile distribution. A relationship built on transparency and consistent payment can unlock supply routes that contracts alone will not secure.

Sourcing decisions often favor suppliers from a buyer's home region or ethnic network, especially where legal enforcement of contracts is weak and reputation offers more protection than paperwork. This loyalty is not favoritism; it reflects a rational response to environments where trust must be built relationally rather than institutionally. Understanding this means recognizing that switching suppliers for a marginal cost saving may fracture a web of accountability you cannot easily rebuild. A colleague used to supplier diversity programs enforced by compliance teams may read this as nepotism, while a Guinean counterpart may see formal tendering without relationship as reckless in a context where redress is slow and penalties rarely enforced.
