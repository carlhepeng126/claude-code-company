# Effective Presentations

## Meetings & Presentations

Many participants can read French, but they think in Susu, Pulaar, Malinké or a Forest language. A concept that looks clear on a slide may need a story or a rephrasing to make sense. If you're presenting to a mixed group, build in time for a colleague to recap key points in the relevant lingua franca, or check understanding in a way that doesn't single anyone out. What reads as clarity in French may not land with the same weight when people are translating it back into the language they use to weigh decisions.

Communities and staff have sat through many short-lived projects. Buzzwords about capacity-building and empowerment are often met with polite faces and inward scepticism. If you want engagement, explain concretely what will be different this time—how governance will work, where funding comes from, what happens after the pilot phase—rather than relying on promises that sound like every initiative that came before. People are listening for proof of follow-through, not aspiration.

Stories work better than global case studies. A women's rice group near Kindia or a youth association in Labé will resonate more than a success story from another continent. Use Guinea-based examples and images so your audience feels the work is about them, not adapted from a generic template. Guinea is used to being studied but not always recognised, and that distinction shows up in how people respond.

Presentations draw on a rich oral tradition. Anecdote, detail, and a sense of history matter more than slick visuals. Power cuts are common, so have printed notes or be ready to speak without slides. Greet senior members of the audience at the start, and let silences breathe—they often reflect respect or reflection, not confusion.
