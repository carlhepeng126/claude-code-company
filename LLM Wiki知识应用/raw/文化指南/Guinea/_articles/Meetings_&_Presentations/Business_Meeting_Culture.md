# Business Meeting Culture

## Meetings & Presentations

In Guinea's capital and mining areas, meetings follow corporate rhythms: agendas printed in French, projectors set up, and badges worn. Travel a short distance to a village and the format shifts entirely. Meetings often take place in the chief's courtyard or under a mango tree, with elders seated on benches and younger participants on mats or standing. The physical arrangement reveals who holds authority, and the session unfolds through oral storytelling, proverbs, and translation into local languages rather than dense written slides. This contrast is not about one setting being more or less formal, it is about two different logics for how decisions are shared and validated.

Adapting to the setting means more than changing your materials. In a Conakry office, you can work from a prepared document. In a village hall, people expect examples they can picture, oral explanations, and time to discuss points aloud. If your presentation relies on handouts or technical jargon, it will not land. Keep explanations conversational and build in time for interpretation and response.

Formal meetings in Guinea often follow a consensus-seeking structure in which no one is expected to lose face. Important topics get discussed until a satisfactory outcome emerges, and the agenda may shift to reflect what requires the most attention. Decision-making sits at the top, so ensure senior figures are present if you want commitments. Junior colleagues may appear to have authority, but they are rarely empowered to sign off on anything meaningful.

Real concerns often surface only after the formal session ends. People may stay measured in front of the group, then speak frankly by the tea kettle, at the gate, or walking back to the road. This is where you hear "we agreed because we had to, but here's the real problem." Do not rush off once the meeting closes. Plan for the conversation that follows, and watch who people glance at before speaking or who summarizes at the end. That person is your informal chair, even if the title belongs to someone else.
