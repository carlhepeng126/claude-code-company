# Leadership Styles

## Working Together

In Guinea, leaders are expected to act as protectors and patrons, ensuring staff salaries arrive, shielding teams from arbitrary treatment by state actors or company security, and stepping in during personal emergencies. This expectation grows from decades of political fragility and institutional weakness, where formal systems rarely guarantee safety or fairness. A leader who cannot or will not intervene when staff face trouble loses credibility quickly, regardless of their technical competence or strategic vision.

Moral authority comes through physical presence and visible generosity, especially in difficult conditions. A leader who stays in Conakry, avoids challenging field sites, or withdraws when situations become awkward will struggle to maintain trust. Going to the hard places yourself, sharing small advantages, and standing alongside your team when things go wrong build the kind of respect that sustains leadership over time. If you lead only from compounds or hotels, expect your influence to weaken.

Frequent coups and government reshuffles mean leaders are judged not just on their management style but on their ability to keep teams and projects safe through political turbulence. The skill to navigate shifting regimes without tying yourself too publicly to one camp reassures staff that their livelihoods won't collapse with the next transition. A leader who misjudges political winds or becomes too closely aligned with a particular faction puts the whole team at risk, and that risk is felt personally and immediately.
