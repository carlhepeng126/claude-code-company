# Sharing Information

## Working Together

Formal announcements in Guinea often arrive as French memos, but real understanding happens face to face. Policies drafted at head office can feel distant until someone explains them over tea in Susu, Pulaar, Malinké or a Forest language. Key nuances vanish if you rely only on the written layer. This dual-channel approach reflects both colonial inheritance and practical reality: French remains the administrative language, yet most daily exchange happens in local languages where trust and relationship already exist. If you need a message to reach everyone accurately, pair the official document with spoken debriefs delivered by trusted insiders.

Hierarchies concentrate information at the top. Senior colleagues share knowledge with peers but rarely cascade it downward in writing. Team members gossip among themselves but do not usually expect formal updates about company policy or high-level decisions. This vertical structure reflects concentrated authority and a legacy of systems where sharing too broadly felt risky. If you come from a culture where transparency across levels is standard, the silence lower down can feel like miscommunication. From a Guinean colleague's view, repeatedly asking for written updates from above can seem like a challenge to hierarchy rather than a reasonable request.

Political volatility shapes caution about the written word. Histories of repression mean some staff are wary of putting strong opinions on record. They may prefer off-record conversation, particularly on sensitive topics. At the same time, people rely heavily on radio, WhatsApp and market chatter to track coups, protests, fuel prices and mine closures. Rumour functions as a rapid-warning system in an unstable environment. Monitor informal channels to see how your messages are being reinterpreted, and address damaging misunderstandings quickly. Offer small-group settings where people can ask questions or raise concerns they would never email.
