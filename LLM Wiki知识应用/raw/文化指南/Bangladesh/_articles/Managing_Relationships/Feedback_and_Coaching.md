# Feedback and Coaching

## Managing Relationships

Feedback in Bangladesh is shaped by respect for hierarchy and a concern for social harmony. Managers are expected to guide, but direct criticism risks causing embarrassment and damaging the relationship. Public correction, even mild, can threaten a person's honour and standing within the group. As a result, negative feedback is almost always given in private and framed carefully. A manager might speak in general terms, use suggestions rather than instructions, or raise a concern indirectly to allow the person to save face while understanding the message.

Praise, by contrast, can be given more openly, though it should still be measured. Overstating individual achievement in front of peers can create discomfort or imply comparison. Acknowledging contribution in a way that includes the team or reflects well on the group tends to be more culturally comfortable. Singling someone out repeatedly may feel isolating rather than affirming.

Coaching often happens through example and observation rather than structured conversation. Younger or less senior colleagues watch how things are done and learn by modeling behaviour, asking questions when appropriate, and responding to guidance that is implied as much as stated. Feedback may arrive through a senior colleague or intermediary rather than directly from the manager, particularly if the issue is sensitive. This allows the message to be delivered without confrontation and gives the recipient room to adjust without losing dignity.

For colleagues from cultures where feedback is expected to be direct and immediate, this indirectness can feel unclear or slow. From a Bangladeshi perspective, feedback that is too blunt can feel disrespectful and overlook the relational foundation that makes guidance effective.
