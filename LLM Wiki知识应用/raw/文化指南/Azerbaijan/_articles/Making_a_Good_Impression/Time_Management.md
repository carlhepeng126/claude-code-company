# Time Management

## Making a Good Impression

Azerbaijan blends two scheduling rhythms that coexist rather than cancel each other out. Many offices still keep a Soviet-era structure: fixed hours, a clear start to the day, predictable breaks. Yet everyday life weaves through a layer of family obligations that cannot be scheduled away. A sick parent, a cousin's wedding, or a funeral gathering pulls people in directions that trump the calendar. These are not optional events in Azerbaijani social logic; they are the relationships that define who you are. Declining them would damage ties that matter far more than most work deadlines. When someone's schedule shifts unexpectedly, it often reflects this double pull, not carelessness.

Hierarchy also reshapes the day in ways outsiders might not anticipate. A sudden request from a ministry, a senior manager, or an important client can rearrange everyone's priorities within an hour. Juniors are expected to drop what they are doing when a call comes from above, and saying no is rarely an option. A colleague from a culture where individuals control their own task lists may find this disorienting, while an Azerbaijani counterpart working abroad may struggle with environments where a director's urgent request does not automatically take precedence. Understanding this means building more buffer into joint timelines and asking what other demands your counterparts are balancing, rather than assuming a meeting time, once agreed, will stay fixed.

Officially, the weekend runs Saturday to Sunday. Socially, family events still cluster around Friday evenings, and during Ramadan, Qurban Bayram (Eid al-Adha, the Feast of Sacrifice), and Novruz (the spring equinox festival marking the new year), schedules bend around fasting, preparation, and visiting. Scheduling a workshop or deadline immediately after a major holiday, or when key team members have large family gatherings, will often result in quiet absence or distracted presence. When a meeting does shift, asking "what changed for you?" rather than expressing frustration will usually reveal constraints you will need to plan around in future. Modeling reliability yourself—starting on time, sending materials when promised—creates a steady reference point in an environment where other forces frequently reshape the day.
