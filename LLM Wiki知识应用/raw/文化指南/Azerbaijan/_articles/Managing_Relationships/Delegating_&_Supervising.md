# Delegating & Supervising

## Managing Relationships

In Azerbaijan, managers are expected to direct clearly and take full responsibility for outcomes. Authority comes from the position itself, and consulting subordinates too openly on decisions can undermine how a manager is perceived. This reflects a workplace culture where hierarchy is both structural and visible. Deference shows up in physical gestures such as standing when a senior colleague enters the room or opening doors, behaviours that acknowledge seniority as something to be recognized outwardly. If you want input from your team, frame it as drawing on their expertise rather than seeking consensus. The distinction preserves authority while still making space for contribution.

The Soviet legacy continues to shape how people respond to delegation. Institutional plans rarely delivered results during that period, and the resulting cynicism fostered a default of compliance over initiative. Many older workers still operate within that inherited caution, where taking responsibility means risking severe blame for any mistake. Younger professionals, especially those with international exposure, tend to be more open to collaborative approaches, but even they work within a broadly top-down environment. Gauge individuals rather than applying a single style across the team.

Personal connection deeply influences how people perform. Managers who invest in knowing their team beyond the role itself, understanding family situations and individual concerns, earn a different quality of commitment than those who manage purely through structure. Relationship builds willingness in ways that process alone does not.

Inertia can slow progress even when people are willing. Consistent follow-through and clear accountability help, but expect that things may take longer than formal timelines suggest. This comes from decades of institutional disconnect, where what was planned and what happened rarely matched.
