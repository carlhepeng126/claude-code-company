# Sharing Information

## Working Together

Information sharing in Azerbaijan is shaped by decades of political sensitivity, first under Soviet rule and later in a tightly controlled media environment. People learned early that what you say, where you say it, and who might hear it could bring risk. That caution persists in the workplace. A colleague might hesitate to share concerns in a formal email or meeting even when transparency would move a project forward, because written channels feel exposed and permanent.

Language adds another layer. Someone may describe the same situation differently in Azerbaijani, Russian, and English, adjusting tone and detail based on assumptions about the audience. This isn't duplicity. Each language comes with its own expectations about formality, risk, and what can safely be made explicit. A point framed carefully in English might be shared more openly in Azerbaijani among trusted colleagues.

Informal channels often run faster and deeper than official ones. Telegram chats, word-of-mouth networks, and family connections spread news quickly and are frequently trusted more than formal reports. A colleague from a culture where information is systematically documented and distributed company-wide may feel blindsided when key updates arrive through back channels. From an Azerbaijani perspective, relying only on formal announcements can seem naive or disconnected from how things actually work.

If you want honest input, create low-risk ways to receive it. Off-the-record conversations, encrypted messaging, or feedback through a trusted intermediary can surface concerns that would never appear in a group email. Cross-check meaning with bilingual colleagues, especially when tone shifts across languages. Treat informal feedback as a parallel source of insight, and assume that anything written down may travel further than you intended.
