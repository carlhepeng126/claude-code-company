# Negotiating

## Working Together

Negotiators in Azerbaijan often read proposals through several lenses at once. The person across the table may be weighing not only commercial terms but also how the deal positions the country, whether it creates local employment, how it might be viewed by family or colleagues touched by war or displacement, and where it fits among competing interests from Russia, Turkey, the EU, and Iran. This multi-angled view reflects decades of balancing powerful neighbors and recent history marked by conflict and economic upheaval. Being seen to have sold the country short, even in a purely commercial context, can weigh heavily. Build your proposal to show clearly how it respects sovereignty and brings concrete benefit, and avoid framing that suggests rescue or dependency.

Expect the process to unfold across multiple meetings, and build room into your opening position. Azerbaijani counterparts will assume your first number is inflated and will work to secure visible concessions. This bargaining is routine, rooted in daily life, and the ability to negotiate downward is valued. Present your strongest offer too early and you remove the room needed for this exchange. At the same time, recognize that money alone rarely closes the deal. Symbolic elements such as where activities are based, how branding is shared, or whether scholarships or regional inclusion feature in the package can be as central to agreement as price. These are not afterthoughts. They form part of what makes a proposal acceptable to those who must justify it internally.

Decision-making is concentrated at the top, so confirm you are speaking with someone who has authority or direct access to it. Middle-ranking managers may attend meetings but lack the power to commit. If interest is low, expect indirect signals rather than a flat refusal. Vague excuses about scheduling, expressions of thanks with no follow-up meeting proposed, or polite but non-committal language all indicate the conversation has reached its limit. Allowing face to be preserved on both sides keeps the door open for future engagement.
