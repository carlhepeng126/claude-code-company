# Business Lunch & Dinner

## Meetings & Presentations

Business meals in Mexico are opportunities to deepen trust through shared time, not to close deals. The conversation often begins with personal topics, humour, and storytelling, gradually building rapport that can support later professional work. Conversations during meals are relational, and for someone from a culture where business lunches are short and purpose-driven, this may feel inefficient. From a Mexican perspective, a colleague who stays focused on outcomes over food may seem transactional and unwilling to invest in the relationship.

Food connects deeply to identity and regional pride. You might be offered dishes like *mole* (a complex sauce made with chocolate, chilis, and spices), *tamales* (steamed corn dough filled with meat or vegetables, wrapped in corn husks), *pozole* (a hearty hominy-based soup), or *carne asada* (grilled beef). Trying what's served shows respect for the effort and culture behind it. Meals unfold slowly, with conversation valued as much as the food itself. Leaving quickly once the last dish is cleared can feel abrupt.

Before the meal, ask about dietary preferences and restrictions. Catholics may observe Friday abstinence during Lent, and not everyone drinks alcohol. Offering options, especially non-alcoholic beverages like *aguas frescas* (fresh fruit drinks), reflects awareness rather than assumption. Alcohol is typically accompanied by food, not consumed alone.
