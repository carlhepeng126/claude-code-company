# Teamwork

## Working Together

Collaboration in Mexico draws on Indigenous communal traditions, Catholic moral values, and modern corporate structures. Individual contributions are genuinely valued when they serve a collective goal. Teams expect clear leadership, and the leader's role goes beyond task allocation. A team leader is expected to guide, protect, and support members personally and professionally, particularly during difficult moments. Hierarchy shapes participation, so quieter contributions may reflect respect for seniority rather than lack of engagement. In more traditional settings or outside major urban centers, team members may hesitate to contradict colleagues openly, especially if doing so risks disrupting relational harmony or exposing someone to criticism.

Regional differences show up clearly. Northern teams, particularly in industries with strong ties to U.S. business practices, may communicate more directly and challenge ideas more freely. In central and southern Mexico, teams often prioritize tact and face-saving, with disagreement handled privately or softened through relational language. Informal networks and friendships frequently determine how work gets done. A colleague's ability to collaborate effectively may depend as much on who they know and how they are trusted as on their formal role.

Building trust early strengthens collaboration across the team. Leaders working with Mexican colleagues should actively invite participation rather than assume it will surface on its own. Some team members may hold back not because they lack ideas, but because they are waiting to be asked or unsure whether speaking up would show disrespect. Clarifying communication norms up front helps prevent misunderstandings, particularly when team members come from regions or sectors with different expectations around directness. Informal check-ins and time spent on relationships outside formal meetings make teams more cohesive and willing to work through challenges together.
