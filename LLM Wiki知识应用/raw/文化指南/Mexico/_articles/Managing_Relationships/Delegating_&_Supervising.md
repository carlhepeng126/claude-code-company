# Delegating & Supervising

## Managing Relationships

Mexico's hierarchical workplace culture has roots in three centuries of Spanish colonial rule, which created a society where authority was concentrated at the top and survival depended on deference to those above. That legacy shaped a lasting social contract: the patron-client relationship, in which the powerful protect and provide for those below in exchange for loyalty. Today, managers are still expected to guide closely and care personally, and employees look to their leaders for clear direction rather than acting outside their defined scope. If you want initiative, you need to ask for it explicitly.

Central to this relationship is the concept of *dignidad* (dignity), the deep respect owed to every person regardless of rank. A manager who humiliates someone, even unintentionally, breaks the social contract of the workplace. Public criticism damages not only the relationship but also your authority. All sensitive feedback belongs in private, delivered thoughtfully and with support. In a culture where loyalty and effort are earned through genuine care, how you correct someone has lasting consequences.

Delegation in traditional firms often means assigning tasks while maintaining close oversight. Staying involved reflects both a lower tolerance for personal risk and a relationship-centered approach to management. In more modern or internationalized organizations, some managers are giving teams greater autonomy to decide how work is done within agreed objectives. Even so, decision-making authority remains concentrated at senior levels. In external meetings, the person in the room may not have the authority to commit, and pressing for immediate answers will yield little.

Investing in the relationship before the task is foundational. Knowing your team as people is how genuine effort is earned, and loyalty in Mexico runs both ways.
