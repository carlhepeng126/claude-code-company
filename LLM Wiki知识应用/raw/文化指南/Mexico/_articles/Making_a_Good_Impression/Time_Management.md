# Time Management

## Making a Good Impression

Mexico's approach to time is shaped by agricultural and Indigenous traditions that once followed seasonal and relational rhythms, not the clock. These influences remain visible today, even in corporate settings. While modern business centres like Mexico City increasingly value punctuality, relationship-building still influences how quickly decisions unfold and how meetings proceed. Time bends around people, and that flexibility reflects a social logic where trust and connection come before rigid scheduling.

Regional variation is significant. Northern Mexico tends to be more time-driven, often aligning closely with the pace of cross-border trade and manufacturing. The south typically follows a more relational and flexible approach. In multinational firms or export-oriented industries, deadlines and punctuality are taken seriously, especially with external partners. In family businesses or more traditional settings, time remains more fluid, shaped by the quality of the interaction rather than the minute it begins.

Meetings may start with relational warm-up, even when scheduled tightly. Arriving on time is appreciated and expected, particularly for subordinates or guests, but senior leaders may arrive later without offense. This reflects status and the priority given to relationships over schedules. Discussions can take longer than planned because building consensus and ensuring comfort are part of the process, not obstacles to it. A colleague from a culture where decisions are made quickly may read this as inefficiency, while someone from Mexico may see a rushed conclusion as dismissive or careless.

Set clear deadlines to anchor expectations, and build in buffer time for additional consultation or back-and-forth. This flexibility reflects responsiveness, and recognizing it helps collaboration move forward on terms that make sense locally.
