# Forms of Address

## Making a Good Impression

Choosing how to address someone in Mexican professional life means navigating a system where language marks respect, authority, and the boundary between formality and closeness. This is rooted in long social habits of protecting dignity in public interaction, especially across generations and unequal positions. The shift from *usted* (the formal "you") to *tú* (the informal "you") is a real social threshold, signaling whether you are outside or inside the circle of *confianza* (trust and familiarity). Moving to *tú* too quickly with someone senior or not yet familiar can read as disrespectful or presumptuous. Wait for the other person to suggest the shift, particularly with elders or those in positions of authority.

Professional titles function as prestige markers tied to Mexico's strong credential culture and the social weight of institutional affiliation. *Licenciado* or *Licenciada* (often shortened to Lic.), *Ingeniero* or *Ingeniera* (Ing.), and *Doctor* or *Doctora* (Dr.) are widely used, often well beyond their strict job-description meaning. They signal educational achievement and legitimacy, especially in bureaucratic and corporate settings. Omitting them when someone expects recognition can diminish status cues that people take seriously. Use the title with the person's first surname. For example, "Señor Miguel Martínez García" is addressed as Señor Martínez or, if a professional title applies, Ingeniero Martínez.

*Don* and *Doña*, placed before a first name, offer a warmer register of respect, particularly with elders or people with moral standing in traditional families and smaller-city contexts. They honor age or community-rooted importance without the institutional formality of a professional title. In more urban professional settings in Mexico City or Monterrey, first names and mixed registers may emerge faster, while smaller towns often sustain titles and *Don* or *Doña* longer. Matching the local register helps prevent being read as either overly stiff or inappropriately familiar.
