# Making Contacts & Scheduling Appointments

## Making a Good Impression

In Mexico, professional contact begins with personal warmth. Centuries of blended Indigenous, Spanish colonial and African influences have shaped a society where trust forms through connection, not credentials. A cold email or impersonal pitch may go unanswered, while an introduction from a mutual acquaintance — perhaps a lawyer, a business contact, or someone from your shared network — changes the tone completely. That introduction does more than open a door. It offers a form of social assurance, signaling that the relationship has been vouched for by someone already trusted. Business networking is valued and actively maintained, so making the effort to build those connections before you need them will shape how your outreach is received.

Appointments should be scheduled at least a week or two ahead and confirmed once you arrive in Mexico, ideally by phone and through your contact's assistant. Avoid booking visits around major holidays like Christmas, Easter, Day of the Dead, and July or August, when many professionals are away. Religious feast days and local patron saint festivals also influence availability, especially outside major cities. When scheduling, leave room in your day. Meetings often begin with relaxed small talk about family, recent events or shared acquaintances before turning to business. An invitation to lunch is a sign the relationship is progressing well, so plan time for it.

Arrive on time, but expect your counterpart may be delayed, particularly if they hold a senior role. Waiting 20 to 30 minutes is common, and for very senior figures or government officials, waits of up to an hour can occur, especially if no assistant provides an update. If your meeting is cancelled or no one appears after a reasonable period, reach out to the assistant or secretary rather than leaving a note. If someone is not interested, they may stop returning calls or say they will "study the situation" and never follow up. That's a polite refusal, and pushing further will not change the outcome.

Urban centers like Mexico City, Monterrey and Guadalajara move faster and may blend formal business style with warmer relational rhythms. In smaller towns or more rural areas, introductions still tend to come through trusted intermediaries, and business waits until the relationship feels ready. Flexibility matters throughout. Traffic, family obligations or local customs can shift timing, and showing understanding rather than frustration builds goodwill that lasts beyond a single meeting.
