# Business Etiquette

## Making a Good Impression

Business interactions in the Cook Islands reflect the country's deeply relational culture, where knowing someone's family connections, island of origin, or shared acquaintances establishes the basis for professional exchange. Meetings rarely begin with business. Instead, conversations open with personal questions, stories about mutual contacts, or gentle inquiries into how someone is settling in. This isn't delay or distraction. It establishes trust and shows you understand that work unfolds between people, not just titles. A colleague from a culture where efficiency is demonstrated through directness may wonder when the agenda will start, while a Cook Islander working with someone who moves straight to terms may read that haste as disinterest in the relationship itself.

Hierarchy is present but lightly expressed. Titles are acknowledged, and respect for elders and senior figures is clear, but formality is low. First names are the norm, even across levels, and there is little appetite for rigid protocol. Deference shows up more in listening than in language. Senior voices are heard first, and decisions often emerge from consensus rather than instruction.

Gift giving is understated. A small, thoughtful gesture when visiting someone's home or marking a milestone is welcomed, but grand or expensive gifts may create awkwardness. Modesty is preferred. Something local from your own country, or a contribution to a shared meal, fits the tone better than a branded item. Practicality and sincerity are valued over presentation.

Business cards are rarely exchanged. Contact details are more often shared informally, by text or in conversation. If you do offer a card, keep it simple and don't expect one in return.
