# Business Lunch & Dinner

## Meetings & Presentations

For Emiratis and many Muslim colleagues, halal certification is foundational, not a courtesy. Some will accept restaurants that simply avoid pork and alcohol in the dish itself, particularly in mixed professional settings. Others will only eat in fully certified venues or clearly Muslim-owned establishments where the entire supply chain is trusted. When hosting, default to proper halal options rather than forcing guests to navigate explanations at the table or decline quietly.

The pace and purpose of the meal varies sharply by setting. In DIFC or Dubai Media City, you'll see efficient working lunches, often under an hour, with laptops open and agendas tight. In more traditional circles, especially with Emirati senior leaders, a lunch in a majlis or private dining room unfolds more slowly. Conversation moves through family, regional affairs, and heritage before circling back to business. Deciding which style you're signaling and setting that expectation in the invitation helps both sides prepare.

Venue choice sends its own message. A fish restaurant on the Creek or in Abu Dhabi references maritime heritage. A steakhouse in a luxury hotel conveys international corporate fluency. A generous Arabic buffet says you want your guests to eat well and at ease. Emiratis read these choices, so think beyond convenience or trend.

Leaving immediately after the main course can feel abrupt. Lingering over Arabic coffee, tea, dessert, or shisha in the evening is where the conversation often opens into more candid territory. The best relationship-building may happen in that slower stretch after the plates are cleared.
