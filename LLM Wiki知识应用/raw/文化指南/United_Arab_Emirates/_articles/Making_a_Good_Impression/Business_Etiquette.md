# Business Etiquette

## Making a Good Impression

Preserving karama (dignity) shapes daily interaction in Emirati professional life. Public embarrassment or blunt contradiction can damage a relationship far more than the issue being debated, so feedback is softened, disagreement is wrapped in courtesy, and tone stays measured even under pressure. Visible irritation reflects badly on the person showing it, not the one who provoked it. A colleague from a culture where direct challenge is expected might read this restraint as evasiveness or lack of conviction. An Emirati counterpart, in turn, might interpret public pushback as disrespect or poor self-control. Greet the most senior or eldest person first, then others. Addressing a junior before a senior can misfire.

Disagreement is rarely stated outright. A direct "no" is often avoided in favor of phrases like "inshallah," "we will study it," or "it's difficult right now." These can signal soft refusal or serious hesitation. Learning to read these cues means you avoid pushing someone into an explicit refusal in front of others, which would force them to choose between honesty and preserving face.

Serving coffee, dates, and sometimes sweets at meetings, especially in Emirati offices, is part of relationship-building and tone-setting. Accept at least a small amount unless you have a clear reason not to, and use that time to build rapport rather than rushing to the agenda.
