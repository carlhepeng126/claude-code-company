# Making Contacts & Scheduling Appointments

## Making a Good Impression

The route into an Emirati circle is usually someone already inside it. Trust flows through family, tribal ties and long-standing networks, so a contact introduced through a respected intermediary, a majlis host or a senior Emirati will open doors that a cold email cannot. This is the practical reality of *wasta* (influence through personal connection): it establishes credibility before you say a word. If you're trying to reach decision-makers in ministries, local banks or family-owned groups, begin by identifying who can make that introduction for you. A formal letter sent through the right channel can work, but a personal referral works better.

The ecosystem you're entering also shapes the method. In Dubai's free zones like DIFC, JAFZA or Media and Internet City, professionals are used to LinkedIn outreach, global conferences and direct approaches. The pace is faster and the norms more international. In onshore government departments or Emirati-owned enterprises, relationships still come first and formality still counts.

Timing your approach around national priorities can also help. Many initiatives cascade from ruler and government visions in areas like AI, sustainability or Emiratisation. Contacts are often evaluated by whether the proposition aligns with these agendas, so framing your work in relation to them can sharpen interest.

Expect fast replies but slower decisions. Dubai and Abu Dhabi executives work across Europe and Asia time zones, and WhatsApp and email are checked constantly. Quick responsiveness shows you're serious. Internal approvals, though, may still take time, so prepare for a rhythm that moves quickly on the surface but more deliberately underneath.
