# Dress Code

## Making a Good Impression

National dress in the Emirates is professional attire. Many Emirati men wear kandura (a crisp ankle-length robe, often white) and ghutra or agal (head coverings), while Emirati women may wear abaya (a long robe) and shayla (headscarf), particularly in government and formal settings. These are everyday professional choices rooted in cultural identity and climate, reflecting both tradition and practicality in a region where heat and humidity demand breathable fabrics and modesty remains a social norm.

For expatriate professionals, the baseline expectation is conservative business dress in formal contexts. Long sleeves, covered shoulders, trousers or skirts below the knee, and no deep necklines are standard in ministries, courts, banks, family offices, and across more conservative emirates like Sharjah. The logic is relational: how you present yourself reflects the seriousness you bring to the partnership. Overly tight, transparent, or flashy clothing can read as disrespectful, particularly in government contexts where understated formality matters.

Sector and location shape what works. Tech hubs, creative agencies, and some free zones allow smart casual dress, though even here the emphasis stays on tidy and deliberate presentation rather than informal ease. A colleague accustomed to jeans and trainers in a European start-up might find the Emirates workplace more polished, even in relaxed sectors. Conversely, an Emirati counterpart visiting a casual office abroad might read informality as carelessness if context is not explained.

If you wear national dress from your own culture, keep it formal and understated. The distinction between professional attire and costume matters, and visible effort always reads better than visible status.
