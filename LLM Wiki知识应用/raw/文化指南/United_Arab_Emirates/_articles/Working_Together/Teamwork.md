# Teamwork

## Working Together

Teams in the UAE are rarely homogenous. Most bring together people from across the Arab world, South Asia, Southeast Asia, and the West, each with different contracts, security, and access to power. Emirati nationals often hold strategic or public-facing roles—government relations, leadership, communications—while much of the operational and technical work falls to expatriates. This division is structural, rooted in policies that prioritize employment for nationals, and it shapes who feels secure, who feels replaceable, and who can influence decisions. If you're managing a team, recognize that the person next to you may be on a fundamentally different trajectory. Two people doing similar work may have very different packages, housing allowances, and long-term rights depending on whether they're Emirati, Western, Arab, or South Asian. People know this, even when it's not discussed.

Language adds another layer. Meetings may happen in English, but side comments and emotional reactions among Emiratis often happen in Arabic. Expats switch into Hindi, Urdu, Tagalog, or Malayalam. These aren't just translations—they're moments of affinity and exclusion that create micro-bubbles within one team. If you're facilitating, establish English as the working language in mixed settings, but give people space to check nuances in their mother tongue without cutting others out entirely.

Young Emirati men who've completed National Service bring a shared reference point—discipline, networks, pride—that influences how they relate to authority and risk. That background deserves recognition, as does the need to build genuine pathways for Emirati colleagues to develop skills and responsibility, rather than symbolic titles supported entirely by expatriate labor. Teamwork here functions less through formalized structures and more through loyalty, trust, and quiet mentorship. Relationships anchor collaboration, and singling people out for individual praise can undercut group cohesion more than it motivates.
