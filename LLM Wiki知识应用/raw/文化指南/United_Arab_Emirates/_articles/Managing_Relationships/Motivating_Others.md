# Motivating Others

## Managing Relationships

Money clearly drives motivation across the UAE, particularly for the overwhelming majority of employees who work on temporary contracts. Tax-free salaries, car and housing allowances, bonuses, travel budgets, and end-of-service indemnities combine to create a practical pull that brings people from dozens of countries and keeps them through renewal cycles. For many expatriate employees, what happens here funds school fees, family homes, and university costs elsewhere. Disruptions to pay, allowances, or visa terms can undermine motivation instantly, because the stakes extend far beyond one person or one household.

For Emirati employees, motivation connects deeply to national ambition and family pride. Being linked to flagship projects like a Mars mission, a new cultural district, or a major global event generates a sense of contributing to something historic. Recognition here is often less about individual praise and more about proximity to senior leadership, inclusion in high-profile initiatives, and being invited to events where visibility and access matter. Symbolic rewards like appearing in photographs with ministers or leaders, being named in internal communications alongside a national project, or having parents honored at a ceremony can motivate as powerfully as financial incentives.

Creating a sense of belonging also boosts commitment. People respond to environments where they feel valued and part of a trusted group. Verbal communication, spontaneous appreciation, and recognition that feels personal rather than transactional all help sustain engagement. Fixed deadlines and rigid targets, however, often fall flat. Family time is protected, overtime is rare outside senior levels, and planning far into the future is seen as provisional. Managers who frame roles around contributing to visible national goals, who build in flexibility around timelines, and who understand what success means beyond the office will see stronger, more sustained performance.
