# Managing Suppliers & Customers

## Managing Relationships

The UAE's business environment is shaped by tightly interwoven family conglomerates and the enduring presence of government influence, even in ostensibly private deals. Large groups identified by "Al" family names often control diverse portfolios spanning logistics, construction, retail, and finance. A dispute with one division can ripple quietly across the ecosystem, closing doors elsewhere without announcement. If you're negotiating with a supplier or customer, understanding which entities sit under the same umbrella matters before adopting a hard-line position.

Government oversight functions as a constant, often invisible, reference point. The question "How will this look if a minister or ruler hears about it?" shapes decision-making in ways that colleagues from cultures where commercial and political spheres are more distinct may not anticipate. A complaint can escalate rapidly if someone influential feels slighted, so reputational care extends beyond the immediate counterpart to include how the relationship might be perceived at higher levels. This means contract terms alone never fully define the relationship. When resolving issues with key accounts, combine swift technical solutions with gestures that restore honor, such as a personal call from a senior leader or an upgrade, so the story told later reflects generosity rather than insult.

Service delivery in banks, airlines, and hotels operates on a dual track: highly personalized attention for VIPs, often Emiratis or high-net-worth clients, and more standardized treatment for others. Staff learn to read cues from dress, language, titles, and accompanying entourages. For international teams, this means assigning stable, relationship-focused account managers who understand personal preferences and family structures, not just contract milestones. Face-to-face contact remains the preferred channel for building trust. Relying on email or phone when a visit is possible signals disinterest, whereas repeated in-person meetings, even when initial conversations cover little transactional ground, establish the foundation for flexibility when problems arise.
