# Negotiating

## Working Together

Mongolian negotiating culture is shaped by a reciprocity ethic rooted in pastoral herding life, where relationships had to hold across seasons and shocks. Agreements are judged by their capacity to last, and reliability over time often matters more than the precision of initial terms. A partner who delivers consistently will be remembered; one who breaks reciprocity may find the memory of that lasts too. When negotiating, demonstrating you are thinking in seasons and not just quarters helps build the confidence needed to move forward. Leave room to show flexibility later rather than leading with your best offer, as this creates space for the back-and-forth that feels collaborative.

Tone and composure are treated as forms of respect. Mongolian counterparts may become animated during discussion, with multiple voices joining in and considerable energy in the room, but this reflects engagement rather than conflict. Direct contradiction of the most senior person present can be read as disrespectful and can close down the conversation. A colleague from a culture where rapid decision-making is valued might interpret the slower rhythm or the circling back to earlier points as indecision, while a Mongolian counterpart may view quick closure as a sign the relationship has not been properly tested or understood.

Contracts are often seen as living agreements that can be adjusted if conditions shift. This flexibility reflects a historically grounded comfort with volatility, particularly in rural contexts where environmental disruptions such as *dzud* (severe winter conditions that can devastate livestock and income) remain structurally familiar. In larger commercial negotiations, especially around mineral resources, fairness optics can be as influential as contractual language. Projects like Oyu Tolgoi are read through a public lens of equitable distribution and national benefit, which means negotiators may be responding to concerns beyond the table.
