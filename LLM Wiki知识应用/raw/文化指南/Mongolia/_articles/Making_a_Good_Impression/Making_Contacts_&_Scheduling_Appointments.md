# Making Contacts & Scheduling Appointments

## Making a Good Impression

Business in Mongolia starts with trust that runs through social connection, not institutional formality. Across centuries of pastoral mobility, contact depended on known lineages and continuity in smaller populations, and those patterns still shape how relationships form today. An introduction from someone your Mongolian contact already knows will accelerate trust far more effectively than a formal cold approach. Home province identity, school cohort ties, and family connections function as social routing systems that give weight to a new contact. Even in urban professional settings, especially beyond the most globalized companies, asking a mutual connection to make an introduction can open doors a direct email will not.

Relationships require time before business decisions move forward. The first meeting is typically used to establish rapport and gauge character, not to conclude agreements. Expect several follow-up sessions, and be prepared for discussions to extend beyond scheduled times or shift into informal meals where alcohol may be served at midday. Scheduling back-to-back appointments is unworkable. Two meetings per day is a realistic maximum. Book appointments at least a month ahead, longer if you are meeting government officials, and reconfirm by phone when you arrive. Decisions are made from the top down, and the process can be slower than in cultures where authority is more distributed.

Timing tightens around Naadam in mid-July and Tsagaan Sar, the lunar new year. Both are kinship-maintenance systems with strong expectations of participation and travel that reshape availability across the country. Plan around these periods rather than trying to schedule through them. In ger-district neighborhoods and among recent migrants, contacts may form through extended family pathways and home-area ties as much as through formal channels, especially when one or both parties still move between city and countryside.
