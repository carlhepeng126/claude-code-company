# Feedback and Coaching

## Managing Relationships

Mongolian dignity norms prioritise composure and avoiding public humiliation, which means feedback that threatens honor, especially in front of others or where seniority is relevant, can close down learning rather than open it. Correction offered in a group setting or formal meeting can feel exposing in ways that make it difficult to accept, even when the content is useful. If you need to coach someone on a misstep or suggest a different approach, do so in private and in a way that leaves their standing intact.

Respect in Mongolia is often enacted through physical cues and relational context, so the setting, tone, and who is present can shape how feedback is received as much as the words themselves. Posture, timing, and a calm delivery register more strongly than direct phrasing. A colleague from a culture where directness signals honesty may read this indirectness as evasion, while a Mongolian colleague may read blunt correction as disrespectful, especially if delivered in front of peers. The discomfort goes both ways.

Historical experiences of surveillance and institutional rupture during the socialist period have left patterns where admitting mistakes openly can feel risky, particularly in formal or unfamiliar settings. People may be careful about showing vulnerability until trust is established. This discretion is self-protection, grounded in memory, and it means coaching often works better once the relationship has been built through smaller, repeated interactions rather than a single scheduled conversation.
