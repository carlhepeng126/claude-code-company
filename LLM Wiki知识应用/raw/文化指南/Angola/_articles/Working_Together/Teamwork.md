# Teamwork

## Working Together

Angolan teams often carry two distinct histories. Many senior colleagues were shaped by guerrilla structures and command chains during the civil war, where survival depended on obedience and tight coordination under pressure. Younger staff have typically come through post-war universities or international workplaces, where initiative and independent problem-solving are valued. These groups approach risk, decision-making, and authority very differently. An older manager may expect instructions to be followed without question, while a younger team member may wait for permission to act even when encouraged to take the lead. Building a team that works well across these generations means creating space for both experience sets: let senior colleagues share how they navigated instability and scarcity, while encouraging newer voices to bring fresh methods and tools.

Mutual help underpins how Angolan teams function day to day. In environments where infrastructure is patchy and resources are thin, colleagues lend phones, share fuel, cover each other during family emergencies, and use personal networks to bypass bureaucratic blockages. This is how teams stay operational. A colleague who refuses to help in these ways will be seen as selfish or untrustworthy, and the team's cohesion will suffer. When working alongside Angolan colleagues, recognize these practices as professional resilience, and support them openly rather than dismissing them as informal or inefficient.

Visibility can bring risk. If a project becomes politically sensitive, team members may worry about being associated with the wrong faction or decision. This makes people cautious about owning outcomes or standing out, even when they are competent. Distribute responsibility clearly across the team so that no one person becomes a political scapegoat if something goes wrong. When encouraging more initiative or transparency, frame it in terms of shared protection and collective safety, rather than as a sign of modern management.
