# Decision-Making

## Working Together

Authority in Angola appears highly concentrated but functions through negotiated balances between factions, families, and regional blocs. Decades of intertwined party and state structures mean that many decisions follow internal loyalty calculations alongside formal policy logic. A senior manager may hold clear formal power yet still need to secure buy-in from multiple centers of influence before moving forward. Understanding who actually shapes the outcome often requires looking beyond the organogram.

Officials at all levels may hesitate to approve decisions if they fear later exposure, particularly as anti-corruption efforts and elite rivalries intensify. The risk is personal. A deal that looks straightforward today could be reframed tomorrow as a political liability or corrupt contract. When proposals arrive that lack strong compliance safeguards or transparent documentation, the person expected to sign off may stall or refer upward, less out of bureaucratic process than self-protection. Building transparency into your proposal from the start gives your counterpart something defensible if the decision is later questioned.

Many managers prefer collective cover to individual ownership, especially when outcomes are uncertain. A colleague from a culture where individuals are expected to decide quickly and take personal accountability may read this as indecisiveness. From the Angolan side, rushing to approve without consulting key stakeholders or allowing time for internal soundings can look reckless or politically naive. Structuring agreements in stages, with clear milestones and opportunities to review, lets partners move forward without staking their position on a single leap.

Keep relationships broad. When internal politics shift, approvals can reverse or stall with little warning. If you depend on one sponsor, their influence may evaporate overnight.
