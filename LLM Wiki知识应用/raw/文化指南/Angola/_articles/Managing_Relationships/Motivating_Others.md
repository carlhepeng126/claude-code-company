# Motivating Others

## Managing Relationships

Many Angolans grew up in conditions shaped by scarcity and civil conflict. A stable job that delivers regular pay, funds a house, covers children's schooling, and supports extended family is a meaningful achievement in itself. For people whose priorities are built on this lived experience, grand narratives about innovation or "changing the world" can sound hollow next to the real weight of making sure school fees are paid on time and relatives are supported. Tying your work together to concrete stability gains — predictable income, transferable skills, visible improvements in local services — connects more reliably than abstract mission statements.

Association with a respected institution also matters. Being part of a well-known hospital, ministry, company, or international NGO brings pride and status that extends beyond the workplace. Family and community see the affiliation, and that visibility translates into social respect. Giving Angolan colleagues real public recognition — credit in meetings, roles in conferences, opportunities to represent the project internationally — is not simply about ego. It serves as currency in a context where professional achievement reflects on the wider network.

Motivational language should reflect generational divides. Older colleagues often respond to narratives of nation-building and reconstruction, connecting their work to the country's recovery and future. Younger professionals are more likely to be drawn to individual opportunity, creative autonomy, and digital mobility. A pitch that blends both — framing a project as contributing to Angola's development while also opening career doors and expanding personal networks — reaches across age groups more effectively than a single story aimed at everyone.

Hard work is a given, but celebrating resilience as though it justifies more hardship misses the mark. People have already paid a high cost. Frame your collaboration as a way to reduce struggle, not to glorify endurance.
