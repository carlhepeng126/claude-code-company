# Business Etiquette

## Making a Good Impression

Angola's decades of liberation struggle and civil war have left a professional culture shaped by strong hierarchy and careful public conduct. Senior figures, whether in government, party structures or business, command visible deference. Contradicting a director or party cadre openly in a meeting risks embarrassment and can shut down the relationship. If you need to raise a difficult question, wait for a smaller, private setting where the person can respond without losing face in front of colleagues.

At the same time, interactions run warm once initial formality passes. Conversations often weave in personal history, family, war experiences and political connections. These anecdotes are more than social filler. They reveal how someone sees risk, loyalty and who they trust. A colleague who mentions their time in the movement or their rural origins is signaling where their networks lie and what shaped their view of the world. Listen for these biographical clues and let people tell their stories without rushing to the business agenda.

Meetings in Angola typically begin with handshakes all round, formal greetings in Portuguese and sometimes short speeches, even in modest settings. These rituals reflect the influence of Lusophone formality and a broader expectation that respect is shown through protocol. Greeting each person individually and making eye contact before sitting down is expected. A colleague from a culture where meetings start quickly and informal greetings are preferred may read this as slow or ceremonial, while an Angolan counterpart may perceive a rushed start as dismissive or impolite.

Public criticism of the ruling party or government structures should be approached with caution. Many professionals' livelihoods, histories and social standing are tied into these institutions, even when private views are more critical. Raising political concerns bluntly in mixed company can make people uneasy or defensive, not because the topic is off limits, but because the setting makes the stakes too high.
