# Time Management

## Making a Good Impression

Angolan time is shaped by infrastructure, hierarchy, and memory. For older generations who lived through decades of war, timing was never about clocks. It was dictated by safety, fuel, and orders from above. Plans changed because a road was blocked, a convoy was late, or a superior called. That expectation has not disappeared. Formal peacetime schedules sit on top of a deep assumption that contingencies will appear, and they often do.

In Luanda and many provincial towns, traffic jams, fuel queues, power cuts, and sudden service rationing can collapse a carefully planned day. When someone is late, the story is usually about infrastructure, not carelessness. At the same time, hierarchy reorders the day in ways that outsiders may not see. If a director, minister, or party figure calls, everything else slides. Staff may cancel or push meetings at short notice because *o chefe chamou* (the boss called). Responding to those summons is part of survival in a party-state system. When you get time with someone senior, land your key points quickly. They may be juggling demands you cannot see.

Luanda runs on a fast, hyper-urban rhythm where people try to pack in multiple meetings across distant neighborhoods. In rural areas or small towns, the pace looks different, but season, rain, and communal obligations such as funerals, church events, or harvests dictate time just as strongly. Outside Luanda, check agricultural calendars and local events before setting dates. Showing you respect those rhythms builds cooperation faster than insisting on your own timetable.

Double-confirm important meetings by phone or WhatsApp on the day, and keep backup plans. Interpret delays through the lens of infrastructure and hierarchy, not individual reliability. Ask what is blocking people rather than assuming disinterest.
