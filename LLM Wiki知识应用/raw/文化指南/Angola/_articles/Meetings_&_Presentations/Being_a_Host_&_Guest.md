# Being a Host & Guest

## Meetings & Presentations

Hospitality in Angola reflects moral standing. How you receive someone, even with limited means, says something about who you are. Offering food or at least a drink is close to compulsory, and a guest who declines everything or leaves quickly can be remembered as distant or ungenerous in spirit. Accept at least something, and show that you notice the effort. A comment on how the *funge* (maize or cassava porridge, a staple dish) or fish was prepared counts as part of building the relationship.

Being invited from a corporate office to a colleague's home in a *musseque* (informal settlement) or a family compound outside the city is a significant step. You are being allowed to see the whole person, beyond their professional role. Treat the space with the same respect you would bring to a minister's office. Status anxiety or visible discomfort will be noticed and will undercut the trust the invitation was meant to deepen.

In rural or peri-urban settings, protocol becomes more formal. Meeting a *soba* (traditional leader) follows a specific sequence: greet properly, often sit under a tree or in a yard, listen to an opening speech, then speak when your turn arrives. Skipping steps or rushing the process reads as disrespect toward the community's dignity and authority. Plan your greeting protocol with local partners in advance so you know who speaks, in what order, and how you acknowledge seniority.

When hosting Angolan colleagues yourself, choose settings that allow real conversation. Alternating between luxury venues and zero effort sends a confusing message. Find middle ground that feels welcoming rather than transactional.
