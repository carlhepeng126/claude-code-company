# Business Entertaining

## Meetings & Presentations

Hospitality in Tunisia draws on Amazigh, Arab and Islamic traditions that position shared meals as foundational to trust. When a Tunisian colleague invites you to lunch or dinner, the meal itself is a relational act, often more telling than the words exchanged. Tea, sweets and traditional dishes anchor these encounters, and accepting them warmly builds rapport in ways that formal meetings alone cannot.

Restaurants are the usual setting, especially in cities, and hosts may choose traditional Tunisian cuisine or something more international depending on the guest. Alcohol occupies varied ground. Some Tunisians drink socially, particularly in urban or secular circles, while others abstain for religious or personal reasons. Ordering wine or beer without first reading the table can create discomfort. Wait for the host to set the tone, or offer alternatives if you are hosting. A colleague from a culture where alcohol often accompanies business meals may misread abstinence as formality or distance, while a Tunisian counterpart may view a quick order of drinks as culturally insensitive or presumptive.

Conversation tends to be warm and wide-ranging, blending personal topics with light business references. The host decides if and when to turn to work matters. Raising contract details or timelines too early can feel transactional and undercut the relational foundation the meal is meant to establish. Reciprocating the invitation, either during your visit or later, acknowledges the gesture and reinforces the relationship.
