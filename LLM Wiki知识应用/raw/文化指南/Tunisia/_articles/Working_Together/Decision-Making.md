# Decision-Making

## Working Together

Hierarchy shapes how decisions unfold in Tunisia, but its influence works through consultation rather than command. Senior leaders or those with long-standing experience usually make the final call, yet they typically rely on private conversations with trusted advisors before reaching a conclusion. Consultation, known as *shura* (a practice rooted in Islamic tradition that emphasizes seeking advice), is not simply a procedural courtesy but a cultural expectation that helps maintain harmony and ensures people feel their voice has been heard. If someone important to a decision is left out of these conversations, extra time will be taken to bring them in, even if the outcome is already likely. This reflects a deeper commitment to dignity and collective cohesion. In practice, this means decisions are rarely finalized during formal meetings. Instead, real alignment happens afterward, in one-on-one exchanges or through intermediaries who can bridge positions without public disagreement.

Direct confrontation or open objection in meetings is avoided, so dissent or alternative views often surface informally once the room has cleared. A colleague from a culture where decisions are made quickly in meetings may read this as indecisiveness or delay. From a Tunisian perspective, rushing a decision without proper consultation risks damaging relationships and overlooking factors that only emerge through quieter, more relational channels. Modern corporate settings, particularly those with international exposure, increasingly incorporate data-driven and participatory approaches. Still, the underlying expectation remains: provide well-structured options, make the reasoning clear, and allow space for follow-up conversations before assuming a decision is final.
