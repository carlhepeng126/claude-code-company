# Teamwork

## Working Together

Tunisian teams draw on communal traditions that emphasize cooperation and shared responsibility. The way people work together reflects deep respect for hierarchy and age, which shapes who speaks, when, and with what degree of directness. In more established or formal settings, senior members often guide their colleagues through observation and side conversations rather than explicit instruction. This allows younger or less experienced team members to learn and contribute without risking public exposure or misstep. Diplomacy runs through most interactions, and open confrontation in group settings is rare.

In urban centers like Tunis and Sfax, teams increasingly blend these norms with influences from French corporate culture and Tunisia's growing startup ecosystem. Younger professionals may adopt more direct communication styles, but most still prioritize group harmony and relational trust over speed or individual assertiveness. A colleague accustomed to fast decision-making and open debate may read this restraint as indecisiveness or lack of initiative. Meanwhile, a Tunisian team member may view that same directness as abrasive or dismissive of the relationships that underpin effective collaboration.

Building trust takes time and happens through informal moments as much as formal meetings. Coffee breaks, shared meals, and time spent outside structured agendas strengthen the bonds that make cooperation possible. When facilitating participation, indirect encouragement works better than putting individuals on the spot. Asking someone to respond immediately in front of the group, especially if they are junior or if a senior colleague has not yet spoken, can create discomfort rather than engagement.

Praise is typically directed at the team rather than singling out individuals, which helps preserve both dignity and cohesion. Feedback, especially corrective, should be given privately and with care. What is said publicly affects not just the person but the group's sense of balance and respect.
