# Making Contacts & Scheduling Appointments

## Making a Good Impression

When making business contact in Poland, open with clarity rather than confidence. Say who you are, why you're reaching out, and what you're proposing, without overselling the opportunity or your credentials. Centuries of disrupted sovereignty and lived experience of systems where public and private realities could diverge have created a reflex to test credibility before moving into closeness. Big claims or polished self-promotion can trigger scepticism. Trust grows from consistency and verifiable competence, so early follow-through on small commitments does more to build the relationship than any initial pitch.

If you can, enter through a trusted connection. A colleague who introduces you to someone they already know, or a shared network from a conference or professional body, reduces uncertainty and speeds access. This approach works particularly well if your contact can vouch for your seriousness and track record in practical terms.

Schedule meetings at least four or five days ahead, and reconfirm a couple of days before. Polish professionals value punctuality and clear planning, so arriving on time and giving proper notice if plans change shows respect for their time. Avoid expecting meetings outside standard working hours unless the invitation comes from them. When you first arrive at an office, consider handing your business card to the receptionist along with your spoken introduction so they can pass on your details clearly, especially if your name is unfamiliar.

Poland's cities hold distinct professional identities. Warsaw is the reconstructed capital and business center, Kraków anchors heritage and education networks, and Gdańsk connects to maritime industry and the Solidarity legacy. Avoid assuming one default style across the country, and show awareness of local context where it's relevant to your work.
