# Delegating & Supervising

## Managing Relationships

Poland's workplace culture reflects two competing legacies. Forty-five years of communist rule embedded compliance, deference to hierarchy, and a learned habit of hiding mistakes rather than escalating them. The rapid shift to capitalism after 1989 demanded initiative, entrepreneurialism, and individual accountability. Both patterns still coexist, particularly in established industries and state-linked organizations. The result is that delegation in Poland requires unusually explicit direction. Work may stall if instructions are vague, not because people lack competence, but because the older instinct is to wait for clarity from above rather than fill gaps with assumptions. If you delegate a task without specifying expectations in detail, you may find nothing progresses until you check in. Be specific about scope, deadlines, and decision rights from the outset.

A generational divide complicates this further. Older managers often retain authoritative, top-down styles with limited room for input. Younger professionals, especially in multinationals and tech firms, expect collaboration, transparency, and real autonomy. Managing a mixed-age team means adjusting your approach accordingly. A participative style that energizes a younger team in Warsaw may feel uncomfortable or misplaced to senior colleagues in traditional sectors, where hierarchy still structures most interactions.

The communist-era habit of concealing problems has not disappeared. Creating an environment where people raise issues early requires more than declaring your door open. It must be demonstrated repeatedly and visibly that surfacing a mistake will not result in blame. Until that trust is built, silence may be the default.

Personal relationships help. Poles prefer working with people they know and trust. Investing time in informal connection makes delegation, feedback, and difficult conversations noticeably easier to navigate.
