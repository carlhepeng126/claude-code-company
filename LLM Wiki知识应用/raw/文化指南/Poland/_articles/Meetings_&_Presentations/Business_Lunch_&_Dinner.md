# Business Lunch & Dinner

## Meetings & Presentations

Business meals in Poland often feel different from cultures where rapport-building stays light and cheerful until trust is secure. Polish table life blends warmth with seriousness. Conversation may move quickly into substantive topics—politics, ethics, or professional concerns—without anyone treating it as a sign of tension. Understatement and sincerity tend to register more strongly than charm or humour used to smooth over discomfort. A colleague from a culture where meals are deliberately kept upbeat may worry the mood has turned too heavy, while a Polish host may feel that a superficial conversation wastes the opportunity the meal provides.

Lunch meetings are more common than breakfast, usually between noon and 1pm, while dinner takes place after office hours and is typically two courses. The person who invites generally pays. Business talk doesn't need to open the meal. Follow the host's rhythm: let the conversation establish comfort first, and introduce work topics gently when it feels natural. Pushing too early or too hard can feel transactional. Closing with gratitude and a light summary of next steps, rather than a hard ask, leaves the relationship intact.

Toasting with vodka or beer is still common in some settings, often using the phrase *na zdrowie* (to your health). The ritual serves as communal acknowledgment rather than encouragement to drink heavily. You can participate socially without matching others drink-for-drink; keeping a steady, friendly boundary is respected. If your host insists you try more food, it may reflect care rather than pressure. Hosting norms treat feeding as proof of regard. Polish cuisine includes *pierogi* (filled dough parcels), *bigos* (a stew of cabbage, meat, and spices), and *gołąbki* (stuffed cabbage rolls). New restaurants offer international options, especially in Warsaw, but many Polish colleagues have a preferred Polish spot they'll recommend.
