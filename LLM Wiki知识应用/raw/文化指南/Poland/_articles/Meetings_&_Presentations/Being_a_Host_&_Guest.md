# Being a Host & Guest

## Meetings & Presentations

Polish hospitality rests on an ethic of care that places the guest's comfort above almost everything else. The cultural logic is relational and moral, encoded in sayings like *Gość w dom, Bóg w dom* (Guest in the home, God in the home). A host's role is to ensure you are fed, warm, and properly welcomed, sometimes with gentle insistence that continues even after you've politely declined. Refusing food or a second helping can be read as rejecting the care itself, so if you need to decline, keep it brief and warm, and express clear thanks for the gesture. Accepting graciously, even if you take just a small portion, acknowledges the relationship the host is building.

When visiting a Polish home, bring a small gift such as flowers or sweets. Flowers should be in odd numbers, and avoid chrysanthemums, which are associated with funerals. These details signal that you understand the local code. If you arrive for an informal gathering, come about 15 minutes after the stated time to give the host space to prepare without feeling rushed, but not more than 30 minutes late. After the visit, a handwritten card thanking your host reflects the seriousness with which hospitality is given and received.

Traditions like *Wigilia*, the Christmas Eve meal, reveal the depth of this hospitality. Sharing *opłatek* (a thin wafer) while exchanging wishes or reconciliation, and leaving an empty place at the table for an unexpected guest, are expressions of a moral imagination rooted in care, forgiveness, and inclusion. These are not symbolic gestures only, they script how Poles understand hospitality as a lived responsibility.

If you are hosting Polish colleagues or guests, you don't need to replicate cultural ritual, but you can mirror the ethic behind it. Offer generously, create comfort, and make people feel properly received, not just accommodated.
