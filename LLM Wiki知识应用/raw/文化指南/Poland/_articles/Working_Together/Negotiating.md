# Negotiating

## Working Together

Polish negotiators tend to value practical realism over optimistic framing. Expect scrutiny of constraints, detailed conditions, and what will genuinely happen once the contract is signed. This is rooted partly in a historical alertness to unfair terms and asymmetry, a legacy of centuries when external powers controlled Polish sovereignty. That sensitivity has not disappeared. Your counterpart may be assessing the deal not only for commercial advantage but for whether it respects them and distributes risk fairly. If fairness is implicit rather than explicit, they may become more guarded.

Trust tends to build over time rather than arrive at the first meeting. Early discussions may feel more reserved, and Polish negotiators can be frank about problems or limits without adding emotional performance. It is often "say it clearly, don't perform it." Once credibility is established through consistency and honesty, the relationship can become straightforward and stable. Bring precision early. Clear scope, assumptions, and boundaries often feel more respectful than broad promises or salesy enthusiasm. Let logic and evidence lead the conversation.

Avoid pressure tactics, inflated enthusiasm, or ambiguous commitments. These can trigger skepticism rather than urgency. Show explicitly how risks and responsibilities balance across both sides, not just the benefits. If you revisit terms without good reason, you may lose ground. Polish negotiators are often flexible when the reasoning is sound, but they will not mistake vagueness for generosity.
