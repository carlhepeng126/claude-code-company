# Motivating Others

## Managing Relationships

Collective pride runs deep in Venezuela, and it can fuel real commitment when leaders tap into it. El Sistema, the music education program founded in 1975 by José Antonio Abreu, offers a telling example: excellence achieved through shared rhythm, mutual discipline, and the sense of building something together. That same logic can work in professional teams. People respond when they feel part of an effort that means something, where success is collective rather than individual. Recognition that feels personal and specific lands strongly. Noticing what someone contributed and why it mattered builds belonging in a way that generic praise does not.

Resilience is widespread but should not be mistaken for limitless capacity. Many Venezuelans have adapted to years of uncertainty, improvising solutions and making do. But motivation comes less from pressure and more from feeling respected, treated fairly, and not taken for granted. Clear priorities, consistent follow-up, and stability energize more than hype or crisis-driven urgency. Colleagues from cultures where tight deadlines and stretch targets are standard motivators may find that approach falls flat here, while Venezuelan team members working with those colleagues may interpret the relentless pace as a lack of care for people's real circumstances.

The diaspora has reshaped what many people carry. With more than 7.3 million Venezuelans having left the country since 2016, according to the World Bank, split households and remittance responsibilities are common realities. This background can quietly shape energy, availability, and what feels motivating. Flexibility around real-life constraints, without lowering standards, shows respect. Ask what support would make delivery realistic, then agree the plan together. Small collective rituals—regular check-ins, shared wins, moments to reflect and improve—echo that ensemble sensibility and reinforce the message: we coordinate, we listen, we succeed together.
