# Negotiating

## Working Together

Negotiation in Venezuela is a relational act as much as a commercial one. Dignity and fairness are experienced, not just calculated. A counterpart will listen not only to the terms you offer but also to how you offer them, whether your tone conveys respect or condescension. This sensitivity runs deep and is often linked to national identity shaped around sovereignty and resource politics. Humiliation or pressure tactics can end a discussion before it begins. What you say across the table may be weighed alongside how you said it, and both will be remembered long after the contract is signed.

Venezuelans bring a pragmatic realism to the table, shaped by years of navigating instability. Promises that assume perfect conditions or ignore contingencies will be met with skepticism. Creativity is valued, but only when it produces something workable. If you present a fixed solution without acknowledging potential disruptions or offering alternatives, expect questions about what happens when things change. Build fallback options and phased steps into your proposal from the start, and invite the other side to help shape them. That collaborative tone can shift the discussion from evaluation to co-design.

Relationship continuity often outweighs the immediate transaction. In networked professional settings, how you negotiated may be remembered as vividly as what you secured. A deal closed through ultimatums or gotcha framing can damage future opportunities, even if it succeeds in the moment. Written summaries after verbal agreements help prevent drift and clarify understanding without creating friction.

Expect variation within teams, especially where diaspora experience is present. Some counterparts will have adapted to more direct, structured negotiation styles abroad. Others will expect a slower, more relational pace. Ask early which approach feels right for the people you are working with.
