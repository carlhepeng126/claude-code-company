# Business Etiquette

## Making a Good Impression

Formality in Togolese business settings serves a specific function. It creates predictability in contexts shaped by limited institutional trust, where relationships substitute for formal enforcement. Greetings are extended and structured, moving through inquiries about family, health, and wellbeing before any business discussion begins. These exchanges aren't ceremonial filler. They establish mutual recognition and signal that you see the person before the transaction. Skipping this stage can register as dismissive, particularly with senior figures or in government settings where hierarchy and respect are tightly linked.

Dress reinforces the same principle. Professional dress is formal and conservative, especially in Lomé and during initial meetings. Men typically wear suits or tailored trousers with long-sleeved shirts. Women favour modest business attire that covers shoulders and knees. This isn't simply aesthetic preference. Appearance communicates seriousness and respect for the occasion. Arriving underdressed can undermine your position before you've spoken. Regional and generational variation exists: younger professionals in tech or creative sectors may dress more casually, but it's safer to err formal until the context is clear.

Titles matter. Address colleagues and counterparts by their professional or academic title followed by their surname. Using first names without invitation, even after several meetings, can feel presumptuous. This reflects a broader cultural expectation that status differences are acknowledged openly rather than minimised. If a colleague from a culture where informality builds rapport moves quickly to first names, a Togolese counterpart may interpret this as lack of respect rather than warmth. Conversely, Togolese formality may be read as distant or hierarchical by those accustomed to flatter organisational styles. The dynamic works both ways, and awareness of the gap helps prevent misreading.
