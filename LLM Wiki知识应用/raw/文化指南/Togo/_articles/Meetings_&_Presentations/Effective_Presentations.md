# Effective Presentations

## Meetings & Presentations

Presentations in Togo are shaped by respect for hierarchy and a preference for relationship before transaction. Your audience will be assessing not just your content, but your manner and your willingness to engage beyond the slides. Senior figures may be present but say little, while mid-level colleagues ask questions or take notes to feed back upward. Influence often moves through private channels after the formal session ends, so don't mistake a quiet room for disinterest or agreement.

Avoid hard-sell approaches or any tone that feels rushed or overly direct. Togolese professionals may read aggressive persuasion as disrespectful, particularly when it bypasses the relational groundwork that makes collaboration credible. Instead, emphasize your commitment to partnership, reference relevant experience, and frame your proposal as something developed with their context in mind. Storytelling helps. Anchor your points in real situations or case studies that feel familiar, rather than relying solely on abstract data.

Pace your delivery to allow for reflection and interpretation if needed. Silence doesn't mean confusion; it often reflects careful listening or deference to seniority. Don't rush to fill gaps. Supporting materials should be in French, the primary language of business, and kept visually clear. Dense text creates distance and may lead to misunderstanding.

Follow-up conversations often hold more substance than the presentation itself. Colleagues may prefer to ask clarifying or sensitive questions privately, away from the group, to avoid challenging a speaker publicly or revealing gaps in their own understanding. Treat these exchanges as essential, not peripheral. They're where trust deepens and decisions often take shape.
