# Business Etiquette

## Making a Good Impression

Business etiquette in American Samoa reflects the fa'asamoa, the Samoan way of life, where relationships are built through respect for people, not just process. Meetings often begin with what might feel like extended small talk, but these moments are purposeful. They allow people to read who you are, how you connect, and whether the interaction can deepen into something more than transactional. Asking about family or acknowledging someone's matai (chief) title shows you understand that identity and role are inseparable from work. If you treat introductions as boxes to tick before getting down to business, you risk signaling that the relationship holds little value.

Formality varies by setting. Government offices and older professionals may expect more structured exchanges, while younger colleagues working in Pago Pago or with international organizations may adopt a lighter, more direct style. Across generations, though, tone stays respectful. Titles like Mr., Mrs., or honorifics related to church or matai status are used until you are invited to do otherwise. Skipping them, even in casual conversation, can come across as presumptuous.

Gifts are rarely exchanged in formal business settings, but if you are invited to someone's home or a community gathering, bringing a small offering like food or a modest item for children shows appreciation. Avoid giving anything expensive or personal early on. What feels generous to you might create unease or suggest an imbalance of obligation. Offers should be made and received with both hands, a gesture that reinforces mutual regard.

Dress is modest and neat. Even in the heat, shorts and flip-flops remain too casual for professional contexts. Men wear collared shirts and slacks; women wear dresses or skirts that cover the knee. Adhering to this sends a quiet message that you take the setting, and the people in it, seriously.
