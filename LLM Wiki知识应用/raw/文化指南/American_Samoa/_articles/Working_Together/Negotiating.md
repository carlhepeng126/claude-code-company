# Negotiating

## Working Together

Negotiating in American Samoa begins with relationship, not terms. The process assumes that you are building something that will last beyond the immediate agreement, and that trust needs to be established before commitments can be made. This stems from fa'asamoa, the Samoan way of life, which centers on reciprocal relationships and collective wellbeing. People want to know who you are and whether you can be relied on over time, not just for this deal. If you open with pricing or timelines before that foundation exists, you risk signaling that the transaction is all you care about.

Expect the conversation to circle back. A point you thought was settled may be revisited later, or discussions may shift to something seemingly unrelated before returning to the original issue. This allows everyone to see how ideas connect and ensures that no angle has been missed. For someone from a culture where decisions are made sequentially and agreements are locked in as you go, this can feel like backtracking. From the Samoan perspective, reopening a topic shows care and thoroughness, not indecision.

Pace often reflects consultation rather than hesitation. Decisions may need to be discussed with family, matai (chiefs), or others whose opinions hold influence. This is especially true in community-facing sectors or when an agreement affects more than one person. Pushing for a quick close can undermine the very trust you are trying to build. Leaving room to adjust your position as the conversation unfolds is part of showing respect for the process.
