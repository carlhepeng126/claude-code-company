# Forms of Address

## Making a Good Impression

In Tonga, titles and proper forms of address reflect the deeply hierarchical nature of society, shaped by both traditional structures and Christian influence. Chiefs, senior family members, and elders hold authority that is acknowledged through language and gesture. Using the correct form of address is not simply polite; it demonstrates awareness of social order and respect for the relationships that sustain it.

Formal titles are essential in professional settings. "Mr." and "Mrs." are standard when paired with a surname, and should be used until the person clearly invites informality. If someone holds a professional or chiefly title, use it. In a business meeting, addressing a senior figure by first name before being invited to do so can undermine your credibility, as it suggests either ignorance or disregard for the social hierarchy. Colleagues from cultures where first names are used quickly may perceive this formality as stiff or distant, while Tongan counterparts may view rapid informality as presumptuous or disrespectful.

Written communication follows the same logic. Emails and letters should open with a formal salutation and close with respect. Even in digital exchanges, a degree of formality is maintained, particularly with senior colleagues or those you have not met in person. A brief, overly casual email can suggest a lack of seriousness about the relationship or the matter at hand.

Age and seniority shape how introductions unfold. In group settings, greet the most senior person first, and allow them to set the tone for how others should be addressed. This sequencing acknowledges the order of authority and ensures that social protocols are observed from the outset.
