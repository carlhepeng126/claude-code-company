# Business Lunch & Dinner

## Meetings & Presentations

Business meals in Tonga are still relatively uncommon in the formal sense. Much workplace interaction happens through more informal shared food moments, often tied to community or church events rather than scheduled restaurant appointments. When meals do take place in a professional context, they tend to reflect the broader social principle of *faka'apa'apa* (respect), which includes deference to seniority and an awareness of rank. The setting may be casual, but the relational undercurrents are carefully observed.

If a meal is hosted, expect generosity. Food will likely be abundant, and offering more than enough is a sign of hospitality and care. Declining food outright can be read as rejecting the relationship itself, so it's better to accept a small portion and express appreciation, even if you're not particularly hungry. Sharing food builds connection, and the act of eating together is often more meaningful than the content of what is discussed during the meal.

Timing is flexible and the pace unhurried. A colleague from a culture where meetings follow tight schedules may find the open-ended nature of a business meal unclear or inefficient. From a Tongan perspective, a colleague who watches the clock or tries to steer conversation quickly toward business may seem transactional or disrespectful, particularly if senior people are present. Allow space for informal talk and let others guide when work topics are raised.

Alcohol is generally not part of business meals, reflecting both religious influence and cultural norms around restraint in professional settings. If you're uncertain about what to order or bring, it's safer to follow your host's lead.
