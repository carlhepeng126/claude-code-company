# Time Management

## Making a Good Impression

Chileans distinguish between *en punto* (sharp) and *por ahí* (around), and the choice reveals how seriously they take a commitment. If a Chilean colleague says "a las 9 en punto," expect them at nine. If they say "a eso de las 9," expect them within a soft window. This distinction reflects a culture that values relationship and context over rigid timekeeping, but knows when precision matters.

Many Chileans aim to be on time, especially for formal or external meetings, but Santiago's unpredictability often intervenes. Traffic, protests, earthquakes, and long commutes across the city mean that even well-intentioned arrivals can be delayed by five to ten minutes. Sending a WhatsApp message ahead of time is considered decent behaviour, a small act that preserves the relationship while acknowledging the disruption. If you schedule back-to-back meetings without buffer time, you will find yourself out of sync with how the city actually works.

Chileans also treat deadlines differently depending on the source and consequence. Hard deadlines tied to regulators, mining shipments, or export windows are taken very seriously. Internal deadlines that feel aspirational or lack clear follow-through get more flexible treatment. If you want a deadline met, make it explicit why it cannot move and separate it clearly from less urgent requests. Otherwise, colleagues will prioritise based on their own reading of what truly matters.
