# Forms of Address

## Making a Good Impression

In Chilean business, how you address someone communicates where you position them and where you place yourself. Starting with the formal *usted* paired with *Señor*, *Señora*, or a professional title like *Ingeniero* or *Doctor* establishes respect, particularly with new contacts, senior colleagues, or anyone older than you. The shift to *tú* or the warmer hybrid of *usted* with a first name happens when the other person invites it, often subtly and over time. Moving to informality too quickly reads as careless or presumptuous, so wait for the signal rather than forcing familiarity.

The honorific *don* or *doña* followed by a first name adds a layer of warm respect without formality feeling distant or servile. Saying *Don Ricardo* or *Doña Marta* to an older colleague or someone held in esteem shows you recognize their standing while keeping the tone human. Academic and professional titles also matter in more conservative sectors, so if someone introduces themselves as *Ingeniero López*, use that title until they suggest otherwise.

Language itself functions as a social map. How you speak Spanish in Chile, whether you use local expressions like *po* and *cachai*, or soften them in formal settings, immediately locates you in terms of class, region, and register. Upper-class professionals may dial back localisms when addressing international or senior counterparts, while others lean into them to signal authenticity or solidarity. If you speak Spanish, be aware that your choice of register and vocabulary will be read as much as your words.

Listen to how Chileans address each other before settling on your own approach. If younger staff say *don* to a senior colleague, following that pattern shows you've noticed the relational contours of the room. Formality here is less about stiffness and more about acknowledging hierarchy and respect in ways that allow relationships to deepen from a solid foundation.
