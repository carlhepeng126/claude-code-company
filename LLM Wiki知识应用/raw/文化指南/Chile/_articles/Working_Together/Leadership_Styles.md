# Leadership Styles

## Working Together

Chilean leadership reflects a society reckoning with its authoritarian past while navigating present-day demands for fairness and transparency. Many older Chileans lived through the Pinochet dictatorship and then worked in highly hierarchical companies. This history has created divergent reactions: some still admire mano firme (strong hand) leaders who provide clear direction and control, while others distrust any leadership that feels authoritarian or heavy-handed. The result is a workplace culture that respects clear authority but expects it to be exercised with visible fairness and personal warmth.

The ideal is often described as a jefe buena onda, a leader who is approachable, humorous, and human, but who also protects their team and acts with integrity. Being friendly alone is not enough. A boss who appears nice but fails to stand up for the team or deflects blame downward quickly becomes known as a jefe falso, a false leader. In practice, this means that leaders need to demonstrate both backbone and loyalty. If you lead in Chile, be prepared to take clear decisions and defend them, while also making yourself accessible and treating staff as people rather than functions.

Ethics are judged by patterns, not statements. After repeated corporate and political scandals, employees watch closely to see whether leaders hire through pituto (connections), favour their inner circle, or cut corners. Transparency in hiring and promotions is expected, and staff will cross-check what you say against who actually advances. If your rhetoric does not match your decisions, trust erodes quickly.

Leadership style also varies by sector and region. In mining and heavy industry in the north, where unions are strong and physical risk is high, leadership tends to be more direct and hard-edged. In services, NGOs, and multinational offices in Santiago, a more consultative, discussion-oriented approach is common. Many Chileans move between these contexts over the course of their careers, so adapt your stance to the setting while maintaining consistency in how you treat people and make ethical choices.
