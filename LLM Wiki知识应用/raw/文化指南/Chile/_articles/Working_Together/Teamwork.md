# Teamwork

## Working Together

Chilean teams are shaped by competing loyalties and remembered divisions. In industries such as mining, salmon farming, forestry and ports, a sharp split runs between frontline workers at the faena or planta and office staff based in Santiago or regional capitals. Resentment builds when headquarters designs processes that ignore shift realities, safety culture or union agreements. Frontline teams will follow the paper but ignore the reality if they have not been given real voice and visible influence in how work is organized. If you are leading across this divide, bring faena representatives into process design early and make their input traceable in the final version.

Many teams have strong union traditions, and even non-unionized staff often have family members who have struck or been laid off. This history shapes how people receive productivity pushes, new systems and talk of efficiency. Language that feels neutral in one setting can feel loaded in Chile. A colleague proposing faster turnarounds may be read as disregarding the conditions that make the work safe or sustainable, while a Chilean colleague's caution around pace may reflect lived memory rather than resistance.

Within mixed teams, micro-groups often form around school background, university and comuna of origin. These clusters share information, templates and opportunities faster internally than across the wider team. If you notice who lunches together and who does not, you are seeing the informal map. Cross-cutting projects and mixed working groups can bridge these cliques without calling them out directly.

Chileans help each other often, sharing contacts, tips and shortcuts, but much of this collaboration happens off the formal stage to avoid appearing critical of official processes. If you recognize and legitimize these informal behaviors, some of what is currently under the radar can become acknowledged good practice.
