# Sharing Information

## Working Together

In many Chilean organizations, information moves vertically far more easily than it moves sideways. Sales may not share updates with support, head office may filter what regions hear, and managers often decide what travels down and what stays private. This vertical flow reflects both hierarchy and a learned habit of self-protection. When people have experienced restructuring, policy shifts, or stalled promises, keeping a private record or holding back certain details becomes a way to stay safe. The instinct is less about secrecy than about controlling risk in environments where information can be used unpredictably.

Much of what really matters gets settled in conversation, not in writing. Emails in Chile often begin with "según lo conversado" (as per our conversation), pointing back to a phone call or face-to-face exchange where the real negotiation took place. The written record exists to confirm, not to conduct. A colleague from a culture where email chains document every decision may find this frustrating and assume clarity is being avoided. From the Chilean side, a long email chain with no prior personal exchange can feel cold or legalistic, as though the relationship hasn't been established yet.

Social media and the risk of funas (public naming and shaming, often on social platforms) have made some professionals wary of putting blunt feedback or contentious opinions in writing. Screenshots travel, and once something is written, it can resurface in ways that damage reputation or relationships. For sensitive topics, it helps to agree explicitly on what will be documented and what stays verbal. Checking whether your message actually reached the people who need it, rather than assuming it flowed across the organization, avoids surprises later. When documenting what was agreed, make sure the email truly reflects the verbal understanding, or you may hear "sí, pero eso no fue lo que entendimos" (yes, but that's not what we understood) when the time comes to act.
