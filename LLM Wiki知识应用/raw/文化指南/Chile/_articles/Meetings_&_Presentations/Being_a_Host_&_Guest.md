# Being a Host & Guest

## Meetings & Presentations

Visiting a Chilean home means stepping into a space shaped by the country's sharp social divisions. A dinner invitation to a gated condominio in La Dehesa involves different codes from a gathering in Maipú or Puente Alto — security checkpoints, parking arrangements, and neighbourhood watch routines all make inequality visible in ways Chileans register instantly. Comment on the warmth of the home, the garden, or the books on the shelf rather than the size or cost of the space itself. In a class-conscious society, remarking on material scale can create discomfort rather than connection.

For social invitations, arriving exactly on time can feel stiff. Fifteen to twenty minutes after the stated hour is usually more comfortable unless the event is explicitly formal. Very late arrival without notice, however, reads as disrespectful. A thoughtful gift — decent Chilean wine, good chocolate, or something from your own region — is always welcome. Keep it considered but not showy. If you're invited to an asado as a social rather than business event, bringing meat for the grill is standard.

Many Chilean gatherings include children and dogs moving freely through the space. Showing ease with this shifts your status from formal guest to trusted presence. Conversation typically opens with family rather than work; people do not want to be defined by their job title. The host will usually propose a toast before anyone drinks, often a simple "Salud." Water is not automatically served, and you will need to ask for the bill at the end of a meal if you are paying.

Reciprocating hospitality matters. If you are hosted at someone's home, plan to return the gesture later, whether at your own home if you are resident in Chile or at a good restaurant.
