# Business Entertaining

## Meetings & Presentations

Business meals in Chile are structured around trust and observation, not transactions. Chileans use shared meals to understand who you are beyond your title and how you handle yourself in less formal settings. Lunch tends to be the main business meal, often running to two hours between 1pm and 3pm, though dinner invitations are common once rapport has been established. Either way, expect the conversation to stay social for some time. If you raise business matters too soon, especially before your host does, you risk appearing transactional or impatient.

The real work of a business meal often happens during the sobremesa, the extended conversation that follows eating. This is when people relax, talk more openly about internal tensions, politics, or controlling families, and allow more candid opinions to surface. For Chileans, the sobremesa reveals character and intent in ways a formal meeting cannot. If you excuse yourself immediately after the meal ends, you miss the portion of the gathering where trust is actually built. When you do need to leave, announce your intention and then stay for another fifteen minutes. Leaving abruptly reads as dismissive.

Venue choice also communicates intent. A meal at a country club in Las Condes sends a different message than lunch at a traditional picada or a seafood restaurant in Valparaíso. Chileans read these signals quickly and assess whether you understand the social mix of the group or default only to elite Santiago settings. If you are hosting, choose a location that reflects the backgrounds and regional ties of your guests, not just convenience or formality.

Alcohol is common, pisco sours and wine especially, but moderation is noted. Losing control is remembered, and framed as a lack of seriedad (seriousness). Equally visible is how you treat service staff. Chileans pay close attention to courtesy toward waiters and hosts as an indicator of how you will treat people lower in the hierarchy at work.
