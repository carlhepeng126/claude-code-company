# Forms of Address

## Making a Good Impression

Costa Rica uses formal address to express respect without asserting hierarchy. The default is *usted* (the formal "you"), even among colleagues at the same level. This reflects a national ideal of egalitarian respect. Shifting to *vos* or *tú*, the informal forms, happens when people feel a genuine warmth or closeness has developed. The choice is theirs to make, so begin with *usted* and follow the lead you are given. In more structured settings, pair it with *Señor* or *Señora* and the person's surname. Most Costa Ricans carry two surnames, the father's first, then the mother's. Only the first surname is used when speaking to someone.

Titles such as *Licenciado* (university graduate), *Ingeniero* (engineer), or *Doctor* are recognized, particularly in public institutions, healthcare, and law. They appear on business cards and may be used in direct address. But insisting on them too heavily can feel rigid in a culture that prefers measured warmth over visible rank. Where others use titles, do the same. Where they do not, kind and clear language holds more influence than credentials alone.

The form *Don* or *Doña* followed by a first name is reserved for older people, respected community figures, or long-standing colleagues. It blends affection with respect. If you hear it used around you, echo it. It shows cultural attunement and is especially valued in settings where local standing matters. As relationships deepen, first names become common, but the prefix *Don* or *Doña* often remains with elders as a lasting courtesy.
