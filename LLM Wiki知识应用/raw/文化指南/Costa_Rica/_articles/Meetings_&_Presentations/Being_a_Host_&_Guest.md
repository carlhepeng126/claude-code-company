# Being a Host & Guest

## Meetings & Presentations

Being invited to a Costa Rican home represents a meaningful shift in a relationship. The home is seen as intimate space, and opening it to a colleague or business contact reflects genuine trust. People take pride in their surroundings, whether it's a modest apartment or a house with a garden, and sharing that space is an expression of personal connection, not just courtesy. If an invitation is extended, expect it to be repeated several times before a firm date is set. This repetition is a way of confirming genuine intent rather than polite obligation.

Once you arrive, you will likely meet more than just your host. Extended family, children, elderly relatives, and neighbours often drop by, and social life is tightly woven with those who live nearby. A colleague from a culture where home visits are highly private and scheduled might find this level of informality unexpected. From a Costa Rican perspective, a home visit that feels too staged or formal can seem distant. Engaging warmly with whoever is present, asking about children or elders, and showing interest in pets or surroundings all help build confianza.

Good hosting means offering generous amounts of food and drink and creating unhurried time together. Gallo pinto, casados, fresh coffee, and pastries are common, and guests are expected to linger, not leave after twenty minutes. The emphasis is on warmth and ease, not on impressing with elaborate displays. As a guest, bring a small, thoughtful gift such as quality coffee, local chocolate, or something from your own country. Allow time in your schedule to stay and talk. Rushing off early can suggest the relationship is still transactional.

The person who extends the invitation is expected to pay if the visit involves a meal out, and while business dinners between men and women are acceptable, foreign women may find it difficult to pay for male colleagues, as it can conflict with ingrained notions of courtesy.
