# Dress Code

## Making a Good Impression

Norwegian workplaces tend toward simplicity and restraint. People dress well, but the goal is to blend rather than stand out. This reflects the egalitarian logic embedded in Janteloven, the cultural norm that discourages displaying superiority. Clothing that draws attention to status or wealth can create subtle distance in professional settings. Understated competence reads as social intelligence here, a way of showing respect for the collective rather than highlighting personal distinction.

Practicality often takes priority, shaped by Norway's climate and its deep cultural relationship with the outdoors through friluftsliv. Weather-appropriate clothing, such as quality outerwear and sturdy footwear, is read as preparation rather than simply functional choice. A colleague from a culture where formal business dress is expected year-round may interpret this emphasis on outdoor readiness as too casual. From a Norwegian perspective, arriving unprepared for the weather can suggest a lack of awareness or self-reliance. Having a second pair of shoes in winter to change into indoors is common practice, especially for women.

Formality exists, but in specific moments. The bunad, a richly embroidered traditional costume worn especially on Norway's Constitution Day (17 May) and other milestones, carries regional identity and historical memory. Its use shows how clothing can become a serious cultural statement in the right context, even in a society that otherwise favors restraint.

Urban centers like Oslo show more international fashion influence and awareness, while smaller towns may lean more toward functional norms. High-cost items do appear, but often in quiet, logo-free forms that align with social pressures against overt status display.
