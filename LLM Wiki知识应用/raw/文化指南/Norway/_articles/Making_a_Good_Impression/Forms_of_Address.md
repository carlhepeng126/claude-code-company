# Forms of Address

## Making a Good Impression

Norwegians usually introduce themselves by first and last name, and move to first names quickly. Titles and formal address can feel awkward or overdone, even in professional settings. This reflects a deeply rooted social expectation of equality, where visible hierarchy tends to be softened or avoided. Respect is shown through reliability and plain speech rather than formal honorifics. If you open with "Mr." or lead with a professional title, it may create unintended distance or suggest you are positioning yourself above the group. Wait to see what your Norwegian colleague offers. If they give their first name, use it.

That said, informality does not mean intimacy. Norwegians may be friendly in tone while keeping clear personal boundaries. Warmth shows up quietly, through consistency rather than overt familiarity. A colleague from a culture where relationship-building involves personal disclosure or frequent social gestures may find Norwegian colleagues reserved or hard to read, despite the use of first names. From the Norwegian side, excessive personal warmth early on can feel intrusive or performative, especially when the working relationship has not yet been tested.

In northern and minority contexts, particularly those involving Sámi communities, how you say and spell someone's name can hold deeper meaning. Language and identity have been contested and revitalized through institutions like the Sámi Parliament, and getting a name right signals recognition rather than procedural politeness. Ask once if you are unsure, and repeat it back to confirm.

Dialect also matters more than rank. How someone sounds can signal region, belonging, and cultural identity, not seniority. In written communication, keep the tone plain and respectful, but do not over-formalize. State your qualifications clearly on business cards or email signatures so colleagues can place your technical background without needing to ask.
