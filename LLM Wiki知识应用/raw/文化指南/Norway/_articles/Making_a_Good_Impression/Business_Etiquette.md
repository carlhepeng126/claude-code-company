# Business Etiquette

## Making a Good Impression

Business etiquette in Norway is shaped by a social preference for low-performance interaction and consistent treatment across people. Respect tends to be inferred from clarity, honesty, and follow-through rather than from flattery or ceremony. Excessive charm, theatrical gestures, or heavy small talk may be met with quiet skepticism, read less as warmth and more as social manipulation. A colleague from a culture where personal connection is established through extended conversation and visible enthusiasm might interpret Norwegian directness as coldness or disinterest. From the Norwegian side, that same enthusiasm can feel performative, raising doubts about what lies beneath the surface. Trust builds through what you do, repeatedly and reliably, rather than what you say about yourself.

The cultural admiration for dugnad, the tradition of unpaid communal work, extends into professional settings as an expectation that people contribute practically without needing recognition. Offering to share workload, stepping in where needed, and doing your part without fanfare can build credibility quickly. This isn't about self-sacrifice but about demonstrating that you understand community is maintained by people who do not make everything about themselves. Colleagues notice who contributes and who waits to be asked.

There is also an assumption of responsible autonomy, reflected in the allemannsretten principle of broad freedom accompanied by self-regulation. In collaborative work, this means clarity around boundaries and expectations can prevent friction, even when intentions are good. If you are granted discretion, the expectation is that you will use it thoughtfully and honor shared limits. Consistent standards applied across people and situations are often seen as a form of respect, sustaining trust in systems and relationships alike.

Norway's growing diversity and its public reckoning with historical assimilation policies toward Sámi, Kven, and Forest Finns have made cultural awareness part of professional sensitivity. Demonstrating that you recognize Norway's plural identities, particularly in mixed settings, signals seriousness about inclusion and avoids outdated assumptions about cultural uniformity.
