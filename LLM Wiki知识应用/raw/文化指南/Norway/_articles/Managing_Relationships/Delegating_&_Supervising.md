# Delegating & Supervising

## Managing Relationships

Norwegian managers lead as facilitators, not commanders. This style has deep roots in a society that developed without an entrenched aristocracy, where communities of farmers, fishermen, and merchants relied on collective decision-making and mutual accountability. The cultural concept of *Janteloven* (the Law of Jante, which holds that no one is above or better than anyone else) discourages hierarchy and individual self-promotion, shaping a workplace where authority is worn lightly. Decades of social democratic governance and strong labor laws have institutionalized worker voice, embedding consensus not just as preference but as structure. When delegating, define the goal clearly but leave the method to the employee. Telling someone exactly how to complete a task suggests you doubt their competence, which will be felt as both a personal insult and a managerial failure.

Trust underpins the entire system. Employees expect significant autonomy and will manage their work independently once a task is assigned. Micromanagement signals distrust and generates friction quickly. Managers remain accessible if problems arise, but stepping in too often undermines the relationship. A colleague from a culture where close oversight is standard may interpret this hands-off approach as disengagement or lack of support. Meanwhile, a Norwegian employee working under frequent check-ins may feel their professionalism is being questioned.

Success is framed collectively. Singling out individuals for praise runs against *Janteloven* and can make both the person praised and their colleagues uncomfortable. Acknowledge contributions as team achievements. Consensus takes longer to reach, but the buy-in it produces is genuine. Attempts to shortcut the process by imposing decisions from above will meet quiet resistance and slow implementation. Norwegian managers are expected to lead through expertise and facilitation, not rank.
