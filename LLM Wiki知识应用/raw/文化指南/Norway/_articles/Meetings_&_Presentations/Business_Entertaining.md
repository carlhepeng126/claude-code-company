# Business Entertaining

## Meetings & Presentations

Business entertaining in Norway tends to be low-key and friendly, shaped by a cultural preference for balanced reciprocity and protected boundaries. Norwegians may invite colleagues to lunch or dinner, and such invitations are meaningful, but the style is rarely lavish. A deeply rooted egalitarian ethic makes overt displays of wealth or hospitality feel uncomfortable if they create visible social debt or hierarchy. Choose a good restaurant, but avoid anywhere too flashy. The goal is to show respect through quality, not extravagance.

Invitations to a Norwegian home should be understood as particularly significant. The home is treated as a legitimately private sphere, and access to it reflects trust rather than routine courtesy. Arrive on time and dress neatly but not formally. You may be offered coffee, often with pastry, and accepting is a simple way to acknowledge the gesture. Reciprocating an invitation, whether during your visit or later, is appreciated and helps keep the relationship balanced.

Alcohol norms are context-specific. Drinking is socially acceptable in many settings, but Norway's history of temperance movements and state regulation through Vinmonopolet has left a cultural sense that alcohol is something to be deliberately managed, not casually celebrated. Follow your host's lead and do not assume one pattern fits all occasions.

Outdoor activities, such as a walk or shared time in nature, can function as a culturally comfortable alternative to formal hosting. They reduce the need for verbal intimacy or status talk, and landscape becomes a shared reference point. For colleagues from cultures where bonding happens primarily over meals and conversation, this may feel indirect, but in Norway it often reflects genuine inclusion.
