# Sharing Information

## Working Together

In Norway, transparency often feels like a fairness issue. The expectation is that information should be distributed predictably so everyone can make informed decisions and act on equal terms. This grows from a culture where rule-consistency and equal treatment are deeply valued. Knowledge is seen less as personal power and more as something that should be accessible to those who need it. In practice, this means colleagues expect to be kept in the loop on decisions that affect their work, and managers typically delegate readily, sharing context rather than controlling access to detail.

Written documentation is common and usually appreciated. Recording decisions and rationale can feel like protection from arbitrary treatment rather than a sign of mistrust. In a society that relies on systems to stay fair, a clear paper trail reduces the risk of informal gatekeeping or decisions being made on the basis of personal connections. For colleagues from cultures where authority figures expect information to flow upward before it is shared more widely, this preference for open documentation and broad access can read as a lack of hierarchy. From a Norwegian perspective, holding back relevant information without a clear reason can look like gatekeeping or undermining collective responsibility.

Privacy is respected as a form of autonomy. People generally share what is relevant to the work without volunteering personal detail until the purpose is clear. This boundary culture means colleagues may not ask probing questions about your life outside work, and they expect the same in return. The line between work-relevant and private is usually respected, so information-sharing tends to be direct and purposeful rather than socially embedded.

Language choices can also hold meaning. Norway has two written standards and a strong dialect culture, and in some professional or regional contexts, how something is said can be tied to identity, regional pride, or political history. For colleagues working across Norwegian regions or in sectors where language diversity is visible, this can add a layer of sensitivity to whose voice and knowledge are considered legitimate.
