# Motivating Others

## Managing Relationships

In Aruba, people give their best when the work environment feels personal and supportive. Recognition works well when it comes directly from a manager and acknowledges specific contributions, not just outcomes. A quick word of thanks after someone has helped meet a deadline, or inviting a team member to present their work to senior leadership, signals that their effort has been noticed and valued. Public acknowledgment in front of colleagues can be especially effective, as it reinforces standing within the group.

Financial incentives are understood and appreciated, but on their own they rarely sustain long-term engagement. What motivates more consistently is being treated with genuine respect and given the flexibility to manage personal and family commitments alongside work. Managers who build trust by showing interest in their team as individuals, and who adjust timelines when possible rather than pushing rigid deadlines, tend to see stronger loyalty and more creative problem-solving in return.

A colleague from a culture where aggressive deadlines and performance targets are standard motivators may find Aruba's more relational approach hard to read, mistaking flexibility for disengagement. From the other side, Arubans working with managers who rely heavily on formal metrics and little personal connection may feel undervalued, even if compensation is competitive. The relationship itself is part of what drives performance, and where that is absent, motivation weakens regardless of the incentive structure in place.
