# Dress Code

## Making a Good Impression

Aruba's professional dress culture reflects the island's blend of Caribbean ease and international business standards. In corporate settings, especially in finance, hospitality management, or government offices, business casual is the baseline. Men typically wear collared shirts with trousers, while women choose dresses, blouses with skirts, or tailored trousers. Full suits are less common than in many other contexts, but they do appear in senior-level meetings or when hosting international clients. The climate shapes these choices: light fabrics, breathable materials, and lighter colors are practical and accepted, even in formal settings.

In tourism and customer-facing industries, the approach shifts. Polo shirts branded with company logos, neat skirts, or pressed shorts often replace more formal office wear. The standard here is put-together rather than stiff, polished but not overdressed. What stays consistent across sectors is attention to grooming and neatness. Wrinkled shirts or scuffed shoes will be noticed, even if nobody comments directly.

For social occasions tied to work, like dinners or evening receptions, the formality rises slightly. A well-fitted dress or a jacket makes sense, but full evening wear is rarely expected unless the invitation specifies it. If you're attending an event at a beach club or outdoor venue, smart casual usually fits better than strict business attire. Asking a local colleague what they plan to wear is a practical way to gauge the setting without guessing.

Visitors sometimes overdress for Aruba's climate or underdress for its professional standards. The balance to aim for is neat, considered, and climate-appropriate, not rigid or overly relaxed.
