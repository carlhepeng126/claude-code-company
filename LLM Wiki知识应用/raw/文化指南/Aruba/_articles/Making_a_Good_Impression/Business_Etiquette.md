# Business Etiquette

## Making a Good Impression

Business interactions in Aruba are relaxed but rooted in respect. The island's small size means reputation travels quickly, and professional life reflects the broader cultural value placed on staying in good standing with others. Formality is low, but care in how you present yourself is high. A casual tone doesn't mean carelessness. People notice how you greet, how you listen, and whether you follow through. First impressions are formed quickly, and in a tight-knit environment, they last.

Greetings are warm and unhurried. A handshake is standard, often accompanied by a smile and direct eye contact. Taking a moment to ask how someone is doing before getting to business is expected, and skipping this step can come across as abrupt or transactional. Colleagues from cultures where meetings begin immediately with the agenda may feel this slows things down, while Arubans may read a quick pivot to business as impersonal or dismissive. This brief exchange isn't a delay; it's how trust begins.

Dress code depends on the setting. In tourism and hospitality, smart casual is the norm. In banking, legal, or government sectors, more formal attire is expected. When in doubt, err slightly more formal until you read the room. Showing up underdressed in a context that values polish can undermine credibility before you've said a word.

Business cards are exchanged casually and without ceremony. There's no ritual around how they're given or received, but having one ready signals professionalism. If your counterpart doesn't offer a card in return, it's not a slight. Digital contact details are increasingly common, especially among younger professionals. Gift giving is rare in business contexts and best avoided early on unless there's a clear social occasion, like an informal gathering or end-of-year celebration.
