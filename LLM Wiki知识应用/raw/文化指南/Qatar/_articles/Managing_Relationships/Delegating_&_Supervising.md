# Delegating & Supervising

## Managing Relationships

Qatar's workplace hierarchy is clear and respected, grounded in Islamic ethics and tribal tradition where authority flows from the top and loyalty runs through personal relationships. Decisions rest with senior figures, often Qatari nationals, and subordinates implement rather than interpret. In a country where roughly 88% of the population are foreign nationals, most teams are genuinely multicultural, and managers navigate daily between local expectations and the varied norms of an international workforce. Employees will not question decisions once made, nor will they act beyond the scope of what has been explicitly asked. Instructions need to be complete and unambiguous. If detail is missing, it will stay missing.

Public criticism or embarrassment is understood as a serious breach. Honour and face are central, and feedback must be given privately and with care. This extends beyond Qatari nationals to many colleagues from South Asia and other parts of the Arab world, where saving face shapes professional interaction. Managers accustomed to open correction in group settings may find their feedback creates distance rather than improvement. From the Qatari perspective, public challenge can look like a lack of respect, both for the individual and for the relationship.

Authority is rarely delegated in practice. Middle managers may hold responsibility but limited decision-making power, and policy questions are referred upward. Sending a junior colleague to represent you in a meeting or negotiation will be read as a lack of seriousness. The relationship between people, not just the efficiency of the transaction, determines how work moves forward. Time spent building trust through personal engagement is foundational, and that trust is earned slowly, through consistency and genuine attention. Religious observance structures the working week, so prayer times, Ramadan, and Islamic holidays should be built into planning from the start.
