# Business Lunch & Dinner

## Meetings & Presentations

Business meals in Qatar extend the hospitality you find in the majlis into shared food. Generosity, care for the guest, and attention to what's offered all reflect a receiving ethic rooted in tradition. The host sets the rhythm and ensures you're looked after. Accepting graciously and responding with courtesy is the expected part of the exchange. Hurrying through the meal or treating it purely as a transaction weakens the relational ground you're there to build.

Religious observance shapes the baseline. Qatar is an Islamic state, and halal norms are the default in almost all settings. Alcohol exists within tightly controlled channels, licensed venues and permits for home purchase through QDC, but it sits outside the cultural norm. If you're hosting, plan for halal options and don't assume alcohol is part of the social script. If alcohol does appear, it will be discreet and venue-appropriate. A colleague accustomed to wine with every business meal may read its absence as restrictive, while a Qatari colleague may read a casual assumption about drinking as culturally tone-deaf.

Ramadan materially changes the rhythm. Government working hours shift to accommodate fasting, typically running from 9:00 to 14:00 during the holy month. Daytime energy is lower, and social gatherings move to the evening. Invitations during this period should respect the altered tempo. Business entertaining still happens, often after sunset, and the evening meal carries added social warmth.

Context varies more than you might expect. A citizen-hosted setting, a diplomatic dinner, and an international hotel dining room can follow quite different unwritten rules. When you're unsure which world you're in, choose the more conservative baseline and let the host signal when to relax.
