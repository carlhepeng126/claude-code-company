# Forms of Address

## Making a Good Impression

Portuguese professional culture uses forms of address as a social calibration tool, balancing warmth with careful attention to distance. The language itself encodes respect through titles and pronouns, and people adjust these markers to reflect where a relationship stands. Formality is the starting point, but it softens gradually rather than all at once.

Begin with *senhor* or *senhora* followed by the surname. If you're working with someone senior or in a more traditional sector, this form will likely hold for some time. The shift to first names happens when the other person signals it, often by introducing themselves more casually or inviting you to drop the title. Moving too quickly to informality can feel presumptuous, particularly if the person on the other side is older or holds a higher position. A colleague from a culture where first names are used immediately may read this reserve as coldness, while a Portuguese counterpart may read instant informality as a lack of respect or awareness.

The title *Doutor* or *Doutora* appears far more widely than in English-speaking contexts. It's used as an honorific to acknowledge education or professional standing, not strictly a doctorate. If you hear it in use around you, treat it as a social marker of deference and mirror the level of formality in the room. Ignoring it or questioning its basis can come across as dismissive.

Warmth and courtesy coexist comfortably with respectful address. People may greet you with a smile, maintain steady eye contact, and speak in a friendly tone while still using formal language. The goal is to begin on the more courteous side and let the relationship open from there, rather than assuming closeness from the outset.
