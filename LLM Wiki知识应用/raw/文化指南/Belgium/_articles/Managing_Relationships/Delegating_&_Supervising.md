# Delegating & Supervising

## Managing Relationships

Belgium's linguistic divide shapes how delegation works in practice. In Flanders, organisations tend to be flatter and more consensus-oriented. Managers delegate responsibility downwards and expect employees to contribute to decisions. In Wallonia, structures are more hierarchical. Authority stays concentrated at the top, consultation happens less often, and a manager who seeks too much input before deciding may be read as uncertain or weak. The management approach that works well in Ghent will not necessarily land in Liège, and a team spanning both regions may need different handling depending on who sits where.

Regardless of region, Belgian managers provide explicit, detailed direction when delegating. The expectation is clear instruction with clear scope: what is needed, by when, and to what standard. Ambiguity generates anxiety and incomplete execution in a culture with high uncertainty avoidance. Vague briefs leave people unsure whether they have permission to proceed or need further approval, and that hesitation slows delivery. If you are used to delegating broad outcomes and letting people define the path, you will need to add more structure upfront.

Belgians are well-educated, work hard, and will feel frustrated if they are not empowered, but they are also modest and often unassuming. In Flanders especially, team members should be invited to put their views across, or listened to in private. They are pragmatic and will normally respond to deadlines, but overbearing follow-up will not be well received. A polite enquiry or reminder will produce better results than pressure.

Respect for work-life boundaries is not just cultural preference but legal reality. Contacting employees outside working hours is illegal for employers in some contexts, and doing so without strong justification will damage trust and create risk.
