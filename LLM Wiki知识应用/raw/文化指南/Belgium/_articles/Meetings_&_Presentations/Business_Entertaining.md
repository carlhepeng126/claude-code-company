# Business Entertaining

## Meetings & Presentations

Business entertaining happens, but it usually comes after at least some professional rapport exists. Belgians are enthusiastic about their food and beer traditions, and they will happily show them off, but the invitation itself tends to reflect a relationship already in motion rather than a strategy to start one. Meals are part of how people stay connected, not how they first open a door.

Regional style plays out clearly at the table. Wallonia and Brussels often lean into rich, multi-course meals with wine, echoing a French-influenced "Burgundian" approach where the meal itself becomes the event. Flemish contexts may do the same, but many prefer something more practical, like a dagschotel (a dish of the day) at a brasserie at lunch or a few beers at a brown café after work. The German-speaking east tends toward a more Germanic feel. The Brussels EU quarter has its own rhythm, with expat and international networks gathering in spots that can feel quite separate from a lunch with a Flemish SME in Kortrijk or a Walloon business contact in Namur.

Showing some curiosity about Belgian beer styles, such as Trappist or gueuze, reflects well because it shows you've noticed the culture. Still, knowing the names is less useful than reading the room. Many people are content with a single good beer or none at all, and drinking heavily to prove enthusiasm will come across poorly. Cafés and brasseries function as semi-public living rooms where people talk shop, politics, and sport, and much relationship-building happens in those settings rather than in white-tablecloth restaurants.

Let your Belgian counterpart choose the venue. They will instinctively match tone, budget, and region. If you reciprocate, pick somewhere with thought behind it, because the choice reflects how seriously you take the relationship.
