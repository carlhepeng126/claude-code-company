# Business Etiquette

## Making a Good Impression

Belgian professionalism is courteous but rarely stiff. Handshakes are standard when greeting someone or meeting a group; maintain eye contact and an upright posture without becoming overly familiar. A dry, quietly ironic humor often runs beneath the surface, especially when the conversation turns to politics, bureaucracy, or "la Belgique" itself. Testing you with a small joke is a way of gauging how rigid or relaxed you are. Keep a courteous tone, but be ready to meet irony with a light touch. Overly solemn behavior can make you seem naive or humorless.

Because Belgium is run by coalitions, the instinct to find middle ground shows up in daily business life. Belgians are wary of grand gestures and all-or-nothing positions, preferring small, workable compromises that everyone can live with. Avoid forcing stark choices in proposals or negotiations. Belgians are at their best when they can co-design balanced solutions rather than pick winners and losers.

In national or mixed-language contexts, language becomes etiquette. A Flemish colleague forced to function only in French, or a Walloon presented with materials exclusively in Dutch, may comply but feel quietly disrespected. In settings that bring both communities together, show you have thought about both Dutch- and French-speakers in how you greet, share materials, and facilitate. It is a core respect signal.

Belgians complain about their own bureaucracy and governmental complexity all the time, but when outsiders launch into "what a ridiculous system," it can feel off. The same "mess" also protects their social model and local autonomy. You can join in once they do it first, but also show you understand the value behind that complexity.
