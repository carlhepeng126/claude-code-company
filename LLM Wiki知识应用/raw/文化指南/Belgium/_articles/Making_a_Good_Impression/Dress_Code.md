# Dress Code

## Making a Good Impression

Belgian professional dress reflects a preference for quiet competence over visible status. The priority is looking neat, well-groomed, and appropriate rather than standing out. In most office settings, people choose good-quality fabrics in muted tones, sensible shoes, and understated tailoring. Flashy brands or overly fashionable cuts can come across as trying too hard. The goal is to signal that you've made an effort without drawing attention to the effort itself.

Context shifts the standard considerably. Brussels EU institutions and multinational headquarters tend toward formal business dress: suits, jackets, smart dresses. Creative sectors, tech companies, and some Flemish small and medium-sized enterprises are more relaxed but still tidy. In industrial settings in Wallonia or Flemish ports, office dress may mix with practical gear depending on whether you're visiting the floor or staying in the meeting room. If you're heading to a production site, ask ahead about protective equipment and bring appropriate footwear. Arriving unprepared reads as being out of touch with the reality of the work.

Weather and transport shape daily choices more than many visitors expect. In cities like Brussels, Ghent, or Antwerp, people walk, cycle, and take trams. Rain and cobblestones mean that professional appearance has to work alongside practicality. Colleagues may arrive with a jacket over weatherproof layers or swap shoes at the office. The balance between looking polished and being functional is part of the local logic.

For a first meeting, start with smart business dress and adjust once you've seen how others present themselves. Slightly overdressed is safer than too casual, especially in government or corporate environments. In smaller local firms, looking overly formal or precious can create distance. The aim is to fit in competently, not to impress with image alone.
