# Business Etiquette

## Making a Good Impression

Professional etiquette in Mauritius is shaped by a practiced commitment to keeping interactions workable across community lines. On a small island where social memory travels through family, school, and neighbourhood networks, discretion and smoothness help protect future relationships. This means communication often favors indirectness. Colleagues may soften disagreement, delay saying no, or tell you what they sense you want to hear, especially if directness might cause embarrassment or friction. The aim is to keep rapport intact in a dense social ecosystem where reputations move quickly and encounters are rarely one-off.

Understanding how messages are delivered requires attention to what is shown as much as what is said. A long story, a shift in tone, or a smile that doesn't quite match the situation can carry meaning that won't be made explicit. Code-switching between Creole warmth, French formality, and English officialness is a form of social intelligence, signaling awareness of hierarchy, intimacy, and context. Reading these shifts accurately helps you respond in ways that feel culturally fluent, not just polite.

Sensitivity around dignity and fairness runs deeper than everyday manners. Slavery resistance at Le Morne and indenture lineage at Aapravasi Ghat remain emotionally present in national memory, even when unspoken. This history influences how recognition and respect are felt in professional settings. Treating people with visible regard, avoiding any suggestion of exploitation or hierarchy based solely on origin, and being mindful of tone when giving feedback all matter more than formal gestures.

Sharing food can build trust quickly because Mauritian cuisine is a lived expression of plural belonging. Offering or accepting a meal, or participating in informal food moments during work, often opens connection in ways that structured conversation may not. Gift giving is less central. If invited to someone's home, bringing something modest like sweets or fruit is courteous, but lavish gifts may be read as inappropriate. Business cards are exchanged casually at the start or close of meetings. Offering one with your right hand and taking a moment to look at what you receive shows basic respect without requiring ceremony.
