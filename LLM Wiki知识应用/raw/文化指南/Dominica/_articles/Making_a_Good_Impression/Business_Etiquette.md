# Business Etiquette

## Making a Good Impression

Professional life in Dominica is shaped by the island's size and its strong tradition of interpersonal warmth. With a population under 75,000, business networks overlap heavily with family, church, and community ties. The person you meet in a formal office setting may also be someone's cousin, neighbor, or fellow parishioner. This means interactions begin with connection, often through greetings that go beyond a quick handshake. Asking about family or acknowledging a shared contact demonstrates you understand how relationships work here. Skipping this step and moving straight into business can come across as transactional or distant.

Formality in dress and address is expected, particularly in government and finance sectors. Men typically wear suits or long-sleeved shirts with ties; women favor conservative dresses or skirts. Titles matter. Use Mr., Ms., or professional titles such as Dr. or Minister until invited to do otherwise. First names may follow once rapport is established, but waiting for your counterpart to suggest it shows respect.

Directness is tempered by courtesy. Criticism, especially in group settings, is softened to avoid embarrassment. A colleague who says "we'll see" or "let me check on that" may be signaling hesitation without refusing outright. Listen for what isn't being said directly. For someone from a culture where feedback is delivered bluntly, this can feel unclear. From a Dominican perspective, a colleague who critiques openly in a meeting may appear insensitive or unaware of how relationships are maintained.

Punctuality standards vary by context. Government and formal business meetings tend to start closer to the scheduled time, but flexibility is common in less formal settings. Allow space in your schedule and don't interpret a delay as lack of commitment.
