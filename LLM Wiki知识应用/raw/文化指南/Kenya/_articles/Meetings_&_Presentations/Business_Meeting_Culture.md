# Business Meeting Culture

## Meetings & Presentations

Opening exchanges in Kenyan meetings often include small talk and may begin with a short prayer or expression of goodwill, reflecting both high religious observance and the relational foundation on which business is built. These early moments are not formalities to be rushed. They help establish shared connection and allow participants to gauge character and intent before moving into the agenda. Skipping this phase can leave the room feeling transactional, which weakens trust from the start. If you are hosting, offering tea is expected. It will likely arrive hot, milky, and sweet.

Respect for seniority shapes how the meeting unfolds. Authority traditionally flows from elders and senior figures, and this remains deeply embedded in professional settings today. The most senior person present will often guide the flow of conversation, and deference to their authority is assumed. Interrupting is considered poor form, and openly contradicting a senior colleague in a mixed group is typically avoided. This does not mean disagreement is absent. It means it is managed with care. Direct debate can happen, but it depends heavily on who is in the room. Teams with Nilotic members may lean toward more open discussion, while Bantu-influenced groups often favor harmony-seeking approaches. Cushitic communication styles may introduce negotiation-driven exchanges. Mixed teams regularly adapt to one another, and the tone will shift accordingly.

Decisions are rarely finalized in the meeting itself. Leaders may engage in consultative dialogue and build coalition support after the formal session ends. This follow-up is where alignment is confirmed, so do not assume silence in the room means agreement. Documenting what was discussed and circulating notes afterward helps clarify what was actually decided and what remains open. Timelines should be agreed explicitly, as they can shift if one person in a delegated chain is delayed or unavailable.
