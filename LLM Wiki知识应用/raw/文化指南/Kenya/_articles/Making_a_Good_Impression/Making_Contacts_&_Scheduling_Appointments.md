# Making Contacts & Scheduling Appointments

## Making a Good Impression

In Kenya, introductions through trusted contacts open doors far more reliably than cold outreach. This preference reflects a long history of navigating ethnic diversity and building cross-group alliances, shaped by British colonial grouping of highly distinct communities into one administrative unit. Kenyans became practiced at working through intermediaries and forming relationships across boundaries. Today, that instinct shows up in business life. A warm introduction from someone already known and respected signals that you've been vetted. Trust develops faster when you arrive through a shared network, so asking a colleague, industry contact, or business club member to make the connection will typically move things forward more smoothly than a direct email or LinkedIn message.

Initial meetings often include informal conversation before any discussion of business. This isn't small talk for its own sake. It's how people assess character and intent. Coastal Swahili culture, shaped by centuries of Indian Ocean trade, emphasizes relationship before transaction. Across Kenya's Bantu, Nilotic, and Cushitic communities, rapport comes first. Expect questions about your background, mutual acquaintances, or recent travels. These exchanges build the foundation for collaboration. Moving too quickly to proposals or timelines can read as transactional and may leave your counterpart uncertain about your commitment.

Schedule meetings two to three weeks ahead and reconfirm a few days before. It's wise to check in again on the day itself, as priorities shift and people are often pulled into unplanned commitments. Flexibility matters here. Meetings may start late, run longer than planned, or include unexpected participants. A colleague from a culture where punctuality anchors professionalism may read this as disorganization. From a Kenyan perspective, a colleague who rigidly adheres to a schedule without adapting to changing circumstances may seem inflexible or disconnected from the reality of how work gets done.

If your meeting involves someone from Kenya's Muslim or Hindu communities, be mindful of religious observances. Friday afternoons and major holidays may not be suitable for scheduling. Recognizing these commitments shows cultural awareness and strengthens the first impression. Follow up after any initial meeting with a written summary of what was discussed and agreed, thanking the person for their time. This confirms shared understanding and keeps momentum going.
