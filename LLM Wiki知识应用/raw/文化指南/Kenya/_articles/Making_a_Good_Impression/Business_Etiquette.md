# Business Etiquette

## Making a Good Impression

Business conduct in Kenya reflects a moral framework rooted in communal responsibility, respect for elders and ritual propriety. Age-set systems and indigenous traditions emphasised structured roles and respectful interaction, while colonial-era bureaucracy introduced formal expectations around courtesy and conduct. Christianity and Islam, layered onto older beliefs, reinforced humility, fairness and expressions of gratitude. The result is a professional culture where etiquette does real work, bridging ethnic and religious differences and maintaining harmony across a plural society.

Greetings are warm and unhurried. Handshakes tend to linger, especially among men who know each other well, and sometimes extend into a brief hand-hold as a sign of familiarity. Eye contact shows that you have genuinely acknowledged the other person. Between women, hugs are common. Public affection is generally avoided, though same-sex friends may hold hands without romantic implication. Kenyans use gestures to reinforce meaning, but pointing with a single finger or beckoning someone in this way is offensive. Motion with the whole hand instead, or use a nod.

Communication favors diplomacy over directness. Saving face and protecting dignity shape what is said and how. Open disagreement or blunt criticism, especially in a group, risks embarrassment and disrupts cohesion. Answers may be offered to smooth the moment rather than to state a true position. Listen for hesitation, allegory, or what is left unspoken. A calm, measured tone is expected, and raising your voice suggests poor upbringing or loss of control. At formal gatherings, prayers or blessings may open proceedings, a practice that extends religious values into professional life.

Gifts mark personal milestones or religious occasions rather than serving as routine business gestures. A thoughtful, well-wrapped present holds more meaning than something conspicuously expensive, which can read as bribery. When visiting someone's home, bring sweets, pastries or small gifts for children. Business cards are exchanged freely and may be handed out before or after a meeting, with no fixed protocol. Including your full title and qualifications adds credibility.
