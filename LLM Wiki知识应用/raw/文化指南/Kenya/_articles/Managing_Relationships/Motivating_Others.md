# Motivating Others

## Managing Relationships

People in Kenya respond when they feel they are building something together. Success is rarely just about individual gain. It is tied to family standing, community contribution, and the ability to support others. A project framed as a step toward shared progress, or as something that will reflect well on the team and its members' networks, taps into motivations that run deeper than personal advancement alone. Managers who connect work to collective achievement, or who acknowledge how someone's effort has lifted the group, often see stronger commitment than those who focus purely on individual incentives.

Recognition works best when it feels personal and rooted in real contribution. Public acknowledgment that names what someone achieved and why it mattered resonates strongly, especially when it links individual effort to team or organizational success. Younger professionals in urban centers may also respond to opportunities that signal growth, innovation, or alignment with Kenya's identity as a hub of entrepreneurship and technological ambition. The "Silicon Savannah" narrative has real weight in Nairobi and beyond, and framing work as part of that forward movement can be genuinely energizing.

Money is a clear motivator, particularly for younger employees who have grown up with economic constraints and see salary as a route to stability and family support. Job mobility is high, and skilled professionals will move if a better offer comes along. Stock options, performance bonuses, and transparent paths for advancement help retain talent. In the public sector or among older professionals, progression may be less meritocratic, and stability or seniority can mean more than rapid upward movement.

Workplace harmony also drives motivation. When relationships feel secure and the manager shows care for people as individuals, not just as workers, loyalty builds. A patriarchal leadership style that balances authority with nurturing and attentiveness to personal circumstances tends to be well received. Disruptions to group cohesion or changes that unsettle established relationships can dampen morale quickly.
