# Delegating & Supervising

## Managing Relationships

Managers in Kenya operate within a lasting tension. Authority is expected to be clear and concentrated, a legacy of both British colonial administration and diverse pre-colonial traditions of centralized leadership among Kenya's 45+ ethnic groups. Yet that authority depends on something that cannot be imposed: the perception that you genuinely care. The concept of *utu* (humanity, or the Ubuntu philosophy expressed in Swahili) shapes what earns respect. A manager who focuses only on output without showing real interest in team members' personal circumstances and wellbeing will struggle to build loyalty, even if their decisions are technically sound.

This means that delegating effectively requires more than clarity of task. It requires relationship. When you assign work, expect polite agreement in the moment, but do not assume that agreement reflects understanding or comfort with the request. Disagreement rarely surfaces in group settings. If someone has concerns, they will either raise them privately or stay silent. Build regular one-on-one check-ins and make it clear through repeated behaviour that honest feedback will not result in criticism or loss of standing.

Ethnicity remains a sensitive and powerful undercurrent in mixed teams, particularly for those who remember the 2007–08 post-election violence. Trust networks often follow ethnic lines, even when this is never stated openly. As a manager, visible fairness across groups, consistent treatment in delegation and recognition, and transparency in decision-making are not simply good practice; they are essential to team cohesion. Small signs of favoritism will be noticed and interpreted.

The harambee ethic of collective effort means that supervision works best when framed as shared responsibility rather than individual accountability alone. Teams expect mutual support across tasks, and a manager who isolates underperformance without addressing the broader group context may miss why the problem arose.
