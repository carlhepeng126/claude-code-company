# Negotiating

## Working Together

Kenyans are skilled negotiators, drawing on long traditions of trade, diplomacy and everyday bargaining. On the Swahili coast, centuries of Indian Ocean commerce shaped a culture that values persuasion and intercultural deal-making. Inland, diversity across Bantu, Nilotic and Cushitic groups means negotiation has always been central to bridging ethnic lines, building alliances and managing resources. Modern coalition politics reinforces this, where power-sharing and consensus-building define leadership. The result is a business culture where negotiation is expected, often enjoyed, and taken seriously as a relational skill.

Rapport comes before price. Kenyans typically want to understand who you are and whether the relationship will hold before they commit to specifics. Pushing for a quick decision or opening with your best offer can backfire. Start high if you're selling, leave room to move, and expect the same in return. A win-win outcome is the goal, and the process may take time as both sides test flexibility and build confidence. Politeness throughout is non-negotiable. Raising your voice or showing frustration is seen as a loss of control and damages trust.

Style varies by background. Some groups favor harmony and indirect compromise, others engage in vigorous debate, and still others bring structured assertiveness shaped by clan councils and pastoralist traditions. Knowing your counterpart's regional or ethnic context helps you adjust tone and pace. A colleague from a culture where directness signals efficiency may read Kenyan indirectness as evasion, while a Kenyan counterpart may see that same directness as impatient or dismissive of the relationship being built.

Always confirm you're speaking with the right person. Decisions are often discussed collectively but signed off by someone senior. Spending hours with a middle manager without access to final authority means starting over. And if a Kenyan counterpart agrees to something verbally, get it in writing. A polite "yes" may mean "maybe" or "we'll see," offered to preserve goodwill rather than close the door.
