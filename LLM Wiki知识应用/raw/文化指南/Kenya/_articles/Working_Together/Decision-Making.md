# Decision-Making

## Working Together

Decisions in Kenyan workplaces often unfold in two stages. The senior leader typically makes the final call, but that call usually follows a round of consultation with key stakeholders who may not have been in the room when the issue was first raised. This approach reflects both a historical deference to elder authority, rooted in council-based deliberation, and a contemporary expectation that decisions should serve collective benefit and be seen as fair. The British colonial administration reinforced centralised decision structures, but indigenous norms of moral legitimacy and consensus-building remained. Today, many leaders combine visible authority with behind-the-scenes negotiation, allowing them to bring people along without appearing indecisive.

Group input offers more than information. It spreads responsibility and reduces the risk of one person being associated with a decision that goes wrong. Decisions are examined from multiple angles, often in detail, and it is common for different voices to weigh in before agreement is reached. A colleague from a culture where decisions are made quickly by a single accountable person may read this process as slow or evasive. From a Kenyan perspective, rushing a decision without proper consultation can look careless and create the impression that relationships and collective welfare do not count.

Regional and ethnic traditions shape how people engage in this process. Colleagues from Bantu-majority backgrounds may lean toward consensus-building, while those from Nilotic groups may be more comfortable with open debate, and Cushitic norms often emphasise negotiation that leads to a clear outcome. These differences, layered over varied colonial experiences, influence who expects to be heard and how decisions gain legitimacy. Allow time for deliberation, clarify who holds final authority, and frame proposals in terms of shared benefit rather than individual gain.
