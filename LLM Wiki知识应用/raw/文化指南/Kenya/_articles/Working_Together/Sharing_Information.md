# Sharing Information

## Working Together

Information in Kenya has traditionally moved through trusted relationships rather than formal channels. Oral traditions placed knowledge with elders and community networks, where what was shared and with whom reflected care and responsibility. Colonial-era controls, including passbook systems used to surveil movement and identity, reinforced caution about written records and formal disclosure. These layers mean that in many workplaces today, information still follows the grain of relationship and hierarchy. A colleague may communicate concerns informally or wait to speak until trust has been established, rather than raising issues immediately in writing or in open forums.

Respect for hierarchy also shapes how information flows upward. Feedback to senior colleagues is often softened or held back, particularly when it involves disagreement or bad news. This reflects a broader cultural emphasis on preserving dignity and avoiding public challenge to authority. In teams where harmony is valued, people may choose to share selectively to avoid confrontation or exposure. In contrast, colleagues from Nilotic backgrounds may favour more direct discussion, while those from Cushitic traditions may expect structured argument and negotiation. These internal differences mean that communication styles can vary within a single team, and what feels natural to one person may read as evasive or blunt to another.

Urban professional settings, especially in Nairobi's corporate and tech sectors, increasingly expect transparency and rapid information exchange. This creates tension between modern organizational norms and older patterns of discretion. Establishing clear communication expectations early helps, as does creating informal opportunities for conversation where quieter voices can contribute without the pressure of a formal meeting. Asking directly for input, rather than assuming silence means agreement, brings more perspectives into view and builds the trust that makes open sharing possible.
