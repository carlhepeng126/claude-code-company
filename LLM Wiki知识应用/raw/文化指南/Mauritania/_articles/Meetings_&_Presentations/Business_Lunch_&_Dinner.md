# Business Lunch & Dinner

## Meetings & Presentations

In Mauritania, the relationship-building function of a business meal often happens through tea rather than food. Atay, the ritual of preparing and serving sweet mint tea, can matter more than the dishes themselves. Multiple rounds of tea create a shared rhythm that allows trust to develop gradually. Accepting this pacing, and staying through several servings, shows that you value the relationship. If you try to move quickly to business topics or leave soon after eating, you may undermine the recognition being offered through shared time.

Religious norms shape what feels comfortable at the table. In a strongly Sunni society, expectations around modesty, gender mixing, and social atmosphere influence dining settings. Be attentive to who sits where, and let your host guide seating arrangements. In some contexts, especially outside the capital, meals may be gender-segregated or held in environments where Islamic propriety is carefully observed. Understanding these norms helps the meal feel respectful rather than awkward.

Meals can also reflect social hierarchies. In a stratified society, who is honored, who serves, and how recognition is distributed at the table can reinforce or threaten dignity. Pay attention to gestures of respect, such as who is offered the best portions or seated in prominent positions. Misreading these cues or inadvertently disrupting them may cause unintended offense.

Regional variation is real. Coastal areas connected to fishing economies have different culinary rhythms than desert towns or the Senegal River Valley, where Sahelian food cultures prevail. These differences are ecological and historical, so don't assume one experience represents the whole country.
