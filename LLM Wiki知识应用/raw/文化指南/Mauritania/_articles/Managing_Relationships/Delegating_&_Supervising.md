# Delegating & Supervising

## Managing Relationships

Authority in Mauritania flows from the top, a pattern shaped by centuries of centralised religious governance under the Almoravid empire and reinforced through French colonial administration. This legacy means that managers are expected to direct and team members are expected to comply. Instructions will rarely be questioned, even when something is unclear, because challenging a superior risks undermining both the manager's standing and the social order that places weight on seniority and lineage. If you delegate a task without checking understanding, you may discover too late that it was not done as intended. Taking time to walk through the task step by step, and inviting questions in a way that feels genuinely safe, helps close that gap.

Personal relationships come before professional ones. Trust is built through connection, not competence alone, and people respond to the person giving the instruction as much as the instruction itself. A manager who moves straight to task without establishing rapport will likely receive compliance but not commitment. Time spent on greetings, small talk, and showing genuine interest in someone's family or wellbeing is time invested in the working relationship, and it will show up in how people respond when deadlines tighten or priorities shift.

Feedback must always be given privately. In a culture where personal honor and community standing are closely intertwined, public correction can damage not just the individual but also the manager's reputation for fairness. If something needs to be addressed, do it one-on-one, with care, and frame it in terms of the task rather than the person. Saving face is not about avoiding accountability, it is about preserving dignity so that the relationship can continue and the work can improve.

Religious observance shapes the rhythm of the working day. Prayer times, Ramadan, and Islamic festivals are not interruptions to plan around grudgingly but central to how people organize their time and energy. Scheduling meetings or deadlines without accounting for these realities will create unnecessary friction and signal a lack of respect for what structures daily life.
