# Business Etiquette

## Making a Good Impression

In Fiji, professional life is shaped by relationships first, transactions second. Business introductions begin with warmth and ease. Small talk about family, the journey to the meeting, or recent events is expected, and rushing through it can feel abrupt or dismissive. These opening exchanges help establish whether you see your counterpart as a person, not just a function. A colleague from a culture where meetings begin immediately with the agenda may interpret this as inefficiency, while a Fijian colleague may read a quick pivot to business as coldness or lack of genuine interest.

Physical presentation matters. Business dress leans conservative, especially in urban centers and formal sectors. Men typically wear long trousers and collared shirts; women wear modest dresses or skirts. Even in Fiji's warm climate, revealing clothing in professional settings can undermine credibility. In more informal or rural contexts, a bula shirt (a lightweight, often floral shirt worn untucked) is acceptable and widely understood as business-appropriate attire.

Tone and directness are calibrated to protect dignity. Open disagreement or blunt refusal in a group setting can cause embarrassment and strain the relationship. A soft "we'll see" or a shift in topic often means no. Colleagues may also defer to seniority in meetings, even when they hold a different view. If you need candid feedback, ask for it privately and frame your request in a way that invites honesty without putting someone on the spot.

Gift giving is common but informal. Small tokens after a meeting or when visiting someone's home are appreciated. Avoid anything too expensive, which can create discomfort or imply obligation.
