# Teamwork

## Working Together

Teamwork in Georgia runs on trust built through personal connection, and that trust develops slowly. Relationships come before formal structures, and team members expect to know each other beyond the boundaries of work. Colleagues often socialize outside office hours, sharing meals or marking life events together, because these moments build the foundation that working collaborations rely on. Without this relational base, teams can feel fragile. A manager who moves too quickly into task delegation without investing in personal rapport may find that coordination stalls or that colleagues hold back input in meetings.

Hierarchies matter, but they work best when leaders are seen as involved and accessible rather than distant. Team members look to senior figures for guidance and appreciate a hands-on approach where direction is clear but not impersonal. Feedback works well when delivered privately and framed as mentorship rather than critique. Public correction, even if mild, can undermine both the individual and the group's cohesion, as dignity and group harmony are tightly linked.

In practice, this means decisions often emerge through informal conversation rather than formal team meetings. A colleague may test an idea in a one-to-one exchange before bringing it to the wider group, or a manager may quietly gauge reactions before formalizing a plan. For someone from a culture where decisions are made collectively in real time, this can feel indirect. From the Georgian side, a team that jumps straight to debate without relationship may seem abrupt or transactional.

Younger professionals in Tbilisi, particularly those in tech or international firms, may blend these expectations with more structured team practices, but the instinct to build personal trust first remains strong across settings.
