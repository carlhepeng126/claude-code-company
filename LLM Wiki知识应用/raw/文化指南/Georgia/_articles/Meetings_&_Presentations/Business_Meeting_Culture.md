# Business Meeting Culture

## Meetings & Presentations

Meetings in Georgia are moments where relationship and rank meet, and both shape how the conversation moves. Invitations are often extended through personal or professional networks rather than cold outreach, reflecting the trust-based nature of Georgian business culture. Once a meeting is arranged, expect formality at the start. Titles should be used, and respect for hierarchy observed. The most senior person on the Georgian side will typically lead, and decisions are rarely made in the room without their involvement. If you arrive with a delegation, ensure your own senior representative is present. A mismatch in seniority can slow progress or be interpreted as a lack of commitment.

The first meeting serves a dual purpose. Information is exchanged, but so is trust. Small talk, often about family, health, or shared connections, is not filler. It allows your counterparts to assess your character and sincerity. Once the agenda begins, expect thoughtful questions and careful listening. Georgians may not voice concerns immediately, preferring to observe and reflect before responding. This does not mean disinterest. It reflects a preference for considering outcomes fully before committing. A colleague accustomed to fast-moving, agenda-driven meetings may read this as hesitation. A Georgian counterpart, in turn, may view rapid decision-making as impulsive or insufficiently grounded.

Disagreement is rarely direct. If something is not feasible, you are more likely to hear a polite deflection or a shift in conversation than an outright refusal. Watch for indirect cues and follow up privately if clarity is needed. Pressing for a firm answer in the moment can create discomfort and stall the process.

Follow-up after the meeting matters. A call or message that references the discussion and confirms next steps reinforces the relationship and keeps momentum alive. Agreements may take time to formalize, but consistency and personal engagement will move things forward.
