# Business Lunch & Dinner

## Meetings & Presentations

Business meals in Georgia are almost always social before they are strategic. Hospitality runs deep here, and the meal itself is treated as a chance to strengthen relationships rather than simply exchange information. Colleagues from cultures where business discussion begins as soon as the first course arrives may find the pace slow or the agenda unclear. Georgians, in turn, may read an early shift to business talk as impatient or even disrespectful. The meal is meant to build trust, and trust takes time.

Toasting is central to the experience, particularly at dinner. A *tamada* (toastmaster) is often appointed to lead a series of toasts that move through themes like friendship, family, and eventually business. These are not brief remarks. Each toast can last several minutes, and responding thoughtfully shows respect. If you do not drink alcohol, it is acceptable to raise a glass of juice or water. What matters is participation, not what is in the glass.

Georgian food is generous and communal. Dishes like *khachapuri* (cheese-filled bread) and *khinkali* (dumplings) are often shared, and hosts take pride in offering more than guests can finish. Refusing food outright can feel like a rejection of goodwill. If you are full, accept a small portion and express appreciation for the quality and effort. The gesture is what registers.

Expect meals to last two to three hours, sometimes longer. Business topics may emerge naturally toward the end, but they are rarely the focus. Treating the meal as purely functional misses the point. In Georgia, the meal is where the foundation for collaboration is laid.
