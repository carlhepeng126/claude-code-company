# Business Entertaining

## Meetings & Presentations

Sharing a meal in Georgia goes beyond courtesy. It reflects an older social ethic rooted in hospitality, where a guest's comfort and the bond formed over food are treated as central to any relationship. In business, this translates to invitations to lunch or dinner once initial contact has been made. These gatherings are rarely perfunctory. A Georgian colleague may host at a traditional restaurant, or in some cases at home, particularly in smaller cities or when the relationship has progressed. The meal itself can be generous and unhurried, often featuring toasts led by a tamada, or toastmaster, who guides the rhythm and tone of the occasion.

Wine is deeply embedded in Georgian culture and will almost certainly be offered, but heavy drinking is not expected and moderation is respected. Business talk may surface late in the meal or not at all. The purpose is often to observe how someone behaves in a less formal setting and whether they engage warmly with the group. A colleague from a culture where social meals are brief and agenda-focused may find this slow or indirect. From a Georgian perspective, moving too quickly to terms or timelines before trust has been built can feel transactional and undermine the relationship being formed.

Reciprocating an invitation is both expected and valued. It need not match the scale of what was offered, but the gesture should feel genuine and considered, not formulaic.
