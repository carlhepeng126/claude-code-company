# Motivating Others

## Managing Relationships

In Georgia, people respond most strongly when recognition is direct, personal, and shared with others. A manager who praises someone in front of the team, or who thanks them by name during a meeting, acknowledges both the individual and their contribution to the group. This public dimension strengthens commitment, because reputation within the team and the organization becomes a source of pride. Success is not entirely private. It reflects on the person's standing, and people are drawn to work that lets them be seen as capable and dependable by those around them.

Financial incentives play a role, but they rarely inspire on their own. A supportive manager who shows genuine appreciation, who checks in without formality, and who creates room for someone to shine on a visible project often does more to sustain effort than a bonus structure alone. Motivation grows when people feel they are contributing to something that will be noticed and valued, not just measured.

Rigid targets and inflexible deadlines can drain energy rather than build it. Georgian workplaces tend to move with a more relational sense of time, where personal and family priorities often come first. Pushing for clock-driven results without flexibility may lead to disengagement, especially if it conflicts with family commitments or important personal obligations. Managers who adapt timelines while keeping the purpose clear, and who show understanding when circumstances shift, will see stronger and more consistent effort.
