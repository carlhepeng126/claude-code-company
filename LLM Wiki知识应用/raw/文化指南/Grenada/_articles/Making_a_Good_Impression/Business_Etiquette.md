# Business Etiquette

## Making a Good Impression

Grenadian business culture favours a personal touch. Greetings are warm and often unhurried, and it's normal to exchange a few words about family, the weather, or recent events before turning to work. This opening ritual builds connection and shows that you see the person, not just the transaction. Jumping straight to business can feel abrupt or impersonal. A colleague from a task-focused setting might read this as inefficiency, while a Grenadian counterpart might interpret a quick pivot to business as coldness or a lack of genuine interest. Allowing time for this relational preamble lays the groundwork for smoother collaboration.

Formality is respected, especially in initial meetings and in dealings with senior figures or government officials. Titles like Mr., Mrs., or professional designations are used until you're invited to switch to first names. This deference reflects a broader cultural preference for showing respect through form and courtesy. As relationships deepen, interactions often become more relaxed, but the shift is gradual and usually led by the Grenadian counterpart.

Dress leans toward the formal. Men typically wear long trousers and a collared shirt, often with a tie, and women favour modest, polished attire. Business casual is acceptable in some settings, but erring on the side of formality is safer, particularly in government or finance. The climate is hot, but lightweight formal wear is the norm. Dressing well is understood as a sign of professionalism and respect for the occasion.

Punctuality expectations can vary. Meetings may start a little late, and schedules sometimes shift without advance notice. Flexibility is appreciated, and frustration is best kept private.
