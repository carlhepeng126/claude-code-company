# Dress Code

## Making a Good Impression

Professional appearance in Grenada reflects personal pride and respect for the occasion, shaped by the island's British colonial legacy and the enduring formality of its public institutions. In government offices, banks, and established businesses, men typically wear long trousers with a collared shirt, often a tie, and sometimes a lightweight suit jacket. Women choose tailored dresses, skirts, or trousers with a blouse. The climate allows for lighter fabrics, but the expectation is still formal. Showing up in business casual to a first meeting may read as underprepared or dismissive, particularly with senior colleagues or public sector counterparts who hold presentation in high regard.

Outside Port St George's or in creative and tourism sectors, the formality softens. Tech firms and hospitality businesses lean toward smart casual, and younger professionals may skip the tie or jacket. Even here, though, clothes are deliberately chosen. Turning up in beach sandals or overly casual wear can undermine credibility, especially early in a relationship when impressions still form.

For social occasions tied to business, dress more carefully than the venue might suggest. A dinner invitation at a restaurant or a cultural event calls for something polished. Men might add a jacket; women often opt for a dress or smart separates. Grenadians tend to dress well when they go out, and matching that effort signals engagement and reciprocal respect.

If attending a church service or formal public ceremony, cover shoulders and knees. Modesty in these settings reflects an awareness of local values rooted in religious observance and community tradition.
