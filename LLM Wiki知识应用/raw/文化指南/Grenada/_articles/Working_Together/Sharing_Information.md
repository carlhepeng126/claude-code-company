# Sharing Information

## Working Together

In Grenada, information moves along personal lines before it moves through formal ones. Relationships shape what gets shared, with whom, and when. People are more comfortable passing on details once they feel confident in the connection. This means introductions through mutual contacts open doors that cold emails often do not. If you are working with a new team or partner, expect initial conversations to focus on building rapport rather than diving straight into technical detail. Trust is established through repeated, reliable interaction, and information flows more freely once that foundation is in place.

Within organizations, knowledge often stays within close-knit groups rather than spreading automatically across departments or levels. Colleagues may hold back updates if they are uncertain how the information will be received or used, particularly if it touches on setbacks or internal tensions. This reflects a broader cultural preference for maintaining harmony and protecting dignity. If you need input from multiple areas, go through relationships rather than formal requests alone. A well-placed introduction or a conversation over lunch can surface insights that structured reporting channels might miss.

The pace and tone of information sharing also vary by setting. Younger professionals and those in international or tourism-facing sectors tend to communicate more openly and quickly, especially in written formats. In more traditional workplaces or with senior figures, expect a slower rhythm and more reliance on face-to-face exchange. If you push for immediate answers or full transparency before the relationship has matured, you may find responses vague or delayed. Allow time for the connection to develop, follow through consistently on what you say you will do, and reciprocate by sharing your own updates and context. This builds the trust that makes information sharing feel safe rather than risky.
