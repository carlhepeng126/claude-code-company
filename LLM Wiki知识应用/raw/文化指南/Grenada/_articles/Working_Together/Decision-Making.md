# Decision-Making

## Working Together

In Grenada, decisions are rarely made in isolation or at speed. Relationships come first, and major choices often require consultation with family members, trusted colleagues, or community figures before being finalized. Even in professional settings, a manager may seek informal input from people they trust before announcing a direction. This reflects a deeply relational culture where preserving harmony and building consensus outweigh the pressure to decide quickly. Skipping this step can weaken buy-in or create the sense that someone has overstepped their role.

Authority remains meaningful. Senior figures, whether by position, age, or experience, hold real influence, and their approval often signals that a decision can move forward. But that authority is rarely exercised in a top-down, unilateral way. Leaders are expected to listen, consult, and ensure that a choice fits within the social fabric of the team or organization. A manager from a culture where decisions are made quickly and individually may find this process unclear or slow, while a Grenadian colleague working in that same environment may feel decisions are being rushed without proper input or care.

Flexibility is built into the process. Plans may shift based on family obligations, community ties, or changing circumstances, and what looks like a firm agreement may still be open to adjustment. If you need a decision by a fixed date, say so early and explain why the timeline exists. If consultation is happening, give it room rather than pressing for speed.
