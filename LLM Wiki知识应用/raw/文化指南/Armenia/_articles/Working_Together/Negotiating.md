# Negotiating

## Working Together

Negotiation in Armenia is built on personal trust and long-term relationship, not just the terms on the table. Armenian businesspeople want to know who they are working with before they commit to what they are working on. This means early meetings may focus more on understanding each other's background, reliability, and intentions than on pricing or timelines. A colleague from a culture where deals move quickly through structured stages may see this as slow or indirect. An Armenian counterpart may view a rush to contract terms as transactional or even distrustful, a sign that the relationship has not been given its proper foundation. Take time to build rapport before pressing for agreement.

Expect the process to circle back. A point you thought was settled may be reopened, or the conversation may shift to a seemingly unrelated topic before returning to the original issue. This is not confusion or delay. It reflects a need to test flexibility, consult with others, or make sure all concerns have been heard. Decision-making often involves family members, senior advisors, or trusted colleagues outside the room. If progress seems to stall, it may mean your counterpart is waiting for input or a better offer. Leave room to negotiate and avoid putting your best terms forward too early.

Concessions are expected and understood as part of the process. Bargaining is a way to reach balance and demonstrate goodwill, not a sign of mistrust. Offering small gestures or adjustments shows you are invested in the partnership, not just the transaction.
