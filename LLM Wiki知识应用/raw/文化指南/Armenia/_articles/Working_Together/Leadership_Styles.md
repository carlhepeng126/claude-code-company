# Leadership Styles

## Working Together

Leadership in Armenia reflects the legacy of Soviet institutional culture, where authority was centralized and decisions flowed from the top. That vertical habit persists in many organizations, particularly in state institutions and older enterprises. Bosses are expected to be decisive, protective, and paternalistic. A manager who delegates without clear oversight or asks for input on matters they are expected to decide may be seen as uncertain or uncommitted to their role. Loyalty to the leader as a person remains more influential than loyalty to a system or set of procedures.

At the same time, younger and internationally exposed managers, especially in tech and service sectors, often adopt more consultative approaches. They may invite discussion, share reasoning, and make space for challenge. But this style is not yet the norm, and it can clash with ingrained expectations. A colleague from a culture where decisions are made collaboratively may read a top-down directive as inflexible, while an Armenian team member may interpret repeated consultation as a sign that the leader lacks confidence or clear direction.

Even where consultation happens, the final decision typically stays with the leader. Input is valued when it strengthens the outcome, but it rarely shifts the course once a direction has been chosen. If you are managing in Armenia, make your position clear early, explain the reasoning behind decisions, and involve the team in shaping how a plan is carried out rather than whether it will proceed.
