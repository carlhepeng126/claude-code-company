# Feedback and Coaching

## Managing Relationships

Feedback in Haiti is filtered through the lens of respect and interpersonal warmth. Direct critique, especially in front of others, risks undermining dignity and damaging the relationship. Managers and senior colleagues tend to soften negative observations with care, offering guidance in private settings where the individual's standing is protected. Public recognition is appreciated, but public correction can cause lasting harm to trust and morale.

Indirect approaches work better than blunt assessment. A manager might share a personal story or frame a concern as general advice rather than naming the issue outright. This allows the person to reflect without feeling exposed. For a colleague accustomed to direct, task-focused feedback, this style might read as unclear or evasive. From a Haitian perspective, the same directness can feel abrupt and disrespectful, lacking the relational foundation that makes feedback credible.

Coaching often extends beyond the workplace. Family, close friends, or respected community members may be consulted before someone acts on feedback, particularly if the guidance involves a shift in role, responsibility, or visibility. A decision that seems individual to an outsider may in fact be collective, shaped by conversations at home or within trusted networks.

Formal performance systems can feel impersonal if not paired with genuine conversation. Rating forms or written evaluations work best when followed by face-to-face discussion that reaffirms the relationship and explains the reasoning behind the assessment.
