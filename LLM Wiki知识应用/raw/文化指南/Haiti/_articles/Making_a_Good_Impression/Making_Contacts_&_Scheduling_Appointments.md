# Making Contacts & Scheduling Appointments

## Making a Good Impression

Personal connection opens doors in Haiti far more reliably than cold outreach. Business relationships begin with trust, and trust grows through shared contacts and face-to-face conversation. If you're trying to arrange a meeting without an introduction, expect slower responses or polite delays. A recommendation from someone already known and respected can shift the tone immediately. In practice, this might mean asking a colleague or local partner to make the first call, or arranging an informal introduction over coffee before any formal agenda is discussed.

Scheduling works best when you build in time and flexibility. Appointments are often made a few days to a week in advance, but confirming the day before is standard practice. Meetings may start later than planned, and other people may join partway through. Priorities can shift quickly, and what begins as a one-on-one conversation might expand into a broader discussion. Colleagues from cultures where schedules are tightly structured may find this unsettling, while Haitians working abroad sometimes feel that rigid timekeeping leaves little room for the human exchanges that make collaboration meaningful.

Showing up in person, even without a formal appointment, can sometimes move things forward faster than a series of emails. If rapport already exists, dropping by when you're nearby signals commitment and availability in ways that virtual communication cannot. Relationship comes first; the business follows once the connection feels solid.
