# Effective Presentations

## Meetings & Presentations

In Haiti, a presentation succeeds not through polish or technical delivery, but through the relationships you build while speaking. Haitian professionals are listening for who you are and whether you understand their reality, not just what you're proposing. A carefully formatted slide deck will mean little if the tone feels impersonal or detached from the day-to-day challenges your audience faces. Lead with respect, acknowledge constraints openly, and frame your message around shared goals rather than imposed solutions.

Avoid overly formal or corporate language. Haitian culture values warmth and directness in communication, and a stiff presentation style can create distance rather than credibility. Use stories or concrete examples that connect your content to lived experience. This resonates more deeply than abstract data alone and makes your message easier to follow, especially in a context where many professionals are balancing multiple responsibilities and limited resources.

Expect interruptions, late arrivals, or people stepping out to take calls. These aren't signs of disengagement. They reflect the realities of working environments where infrastructure is unpredictable and people are managing competing demands in real time. Stay flexible and welcome questions whenever they arise, even if they shift the flow of your presentation. Rigidly sticking to your planned structure may come across as inflexible or out of touch.

Keep materials simple and accessible. Dense slides or overly technical language can alienate rather than persuade. If you're presenting in French, confirm in advance that everyone is comfortable with that choice, as some may prefer Haitian Creole depending on the setting. Offering printed handouts shows consideration, particularly where internet access or follow-up documentation may be unreliable.
