# Business Meeting Culture

## Meetings & Presentations

Many meetings in Ethiopia begin with prayer. An Orthodox blessing or a Muslim prayer may come first, followed by formal thanks to hosts and a recap of the meeting's purpose. The opening minutes establish respect and shared intent before any business point is raised. If you move too quickly to agendas or slides, the group may feel you have skipped over what makes the gathering legitimate. Allowing time for these opening rituals shows you understand that a meeting is a social and spiritual moment, not only a transactional one.

Expect to hear a wide range of voices in Addis Ababa, especially in federal or NGO settings, where participants may speak up confidently in English or Amharic. In regional meetings, the tone often shifts. Mixed-ethnic or mixed-status groups can be quieter, with participants waiting to see where authority is leaning before they offer their views. If you hold a meeting outside the capital, build in time to speak individually with regional staff or elders away from central figures. This gives space for views that may not surface in the formal room.

When a senior figure has spoken, the question "Do we all agree?" often serves as a ritual close rather than an open debate. Public agreement is common, even when individuals plan to slow or reinterpret the decision later. To understand what has genuinely been decided, schedule smaller follow-up conversations with different groups after the formal session. Check positions with the regional bureau, technical staff, and local representatives separately.

The meeting does not end when people leave the table. Serious side conversations take place in the compound afterward, under a tree, in the car park, or over a second round of coffee. Plan to stay after the formal close. What happens in these informal spaces often clarifies what was left unsaid inside.
