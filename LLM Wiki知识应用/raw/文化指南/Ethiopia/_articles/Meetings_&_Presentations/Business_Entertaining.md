# Business Entertaining

## Meetings & Presentations

In Addis Ababa, an evening at a cultural restaurant like Yod Abyssinia or Habesha 2000 is more than a meal. Guests watch dancers perform styles from across the country — Amhara, Oromo, Tigray, Gurage, Somali — in a presentation of Ethiopia's regional diversity. This format became popular after 1991 and reflects a national narrative: we are many peoples, one Ethiopia. Hosts use these evenings to show international colleagues how they themselves see the country's breadth. Accepting the invitation and treating it as a learning opportunity, rather than tourist entertainment, is read as genuine interest.

Alcohol appears in some settings and is entirely absent in others. Tej (honey wine), tella, beer, or areke may flow among some colleagues, while devout Muslims and many Protestants abstain. Ordering or serving alcohol without reading the room can put people in an uncomfortable religious position. If you are hosting, check the faith mix discreetly beforehand and make sure strong non-alcoholic drinks are visible and equivalent in quality, not an afterthought.

Guests may be invited to join dancing, especially eskista, the shoulder-based traditional style. A brief, good-natured attempt is usually appreciated as openness. But becoming overly theatrical or visibly drunk will be remembered as a loss of kab (dignity), especially if you hold a leadership role. Self-control during social events is watched closely and treated as a sign of character.
