# Decision-Making

## Working Together

Decisions in Ethiopian organizations often rest with senior leaders, though what appears as simple hierarchy reflects deeper patterns rooted in political history and ethnic federalism. In government institutions and many public enterprises, choices are still shaped by party-state logic. Even when criteria appear technical, people understand that outcomes must align with what ruling structures expect. A proposal that seems efficient on paper can stall or unravel if it appears to favor one region, language group, or ethnic constituency over another. People will ask, "Who is this really for?" This means that decisions need political legibility as much as operational soundness.

After cycles of purges and shifting alliances, middle managers frequently avoid taking clear positions. Saying yes exposes them to personal risk if the environment shifts, so "we are waiting for guidance from above" becomes protective cover. A colleague from a setting where managers are expected to decide quickly may see this as delay or deflection, while Ethiopian counterparts may read rapid unilateral decisions as politically naive or even reckless.

To move things forward, map the political terrain of your sector early. Identify which ministries, regional bureaus, and party-linked actors need to feel comfortable before a decision can survive implementation. In proposals, show how different groups benefit, or at least how none are visibly harmed. This is often more persuasive than emphasizing speed or efficiency.

Frame choices as reversible where possible. Pilot projects, phased rollouts, and joint sign-off from a steering group reduce individual exposure and make agreement more likely.
