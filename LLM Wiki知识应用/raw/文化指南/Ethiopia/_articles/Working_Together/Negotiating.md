# Negotiating

## Working Together

In any deal touching land, water or grazing, you are negotiating with more than the person across the table. Communities shaped by resettlements, villagisation and large-scale agricultural projects remember promises broken in the past. Before you discuss numbers, understand the history of the area: who was moved, who lost access, and what was committed before. That history will surface, directly or indirectly, and skipping it risks placing your agreement on unstable ground.

Alongside the formal contract, there may be a *shimglina*, a mediated understanding led by elders that covers compensation, jobs, or road access. This parallel layer is not informal in the sense of being unimportant. Violating it can provoke stronger backlash than a technical breach of the written document. Ask explicitly what people expect beyond the contract and treat those commitments with the same seriousness as the terms you sign.

In NGO and donor contexts, counterparts may speak fluent logframe language while negotiating internally with party structures, elders and youth groups who hold very different priorities. Polished presentations and command of donor terminology do not always signal full alignment. Make sure the stakeholders who actually shape local decisions are on board, not just the people who produce the paperwork.

Bargaining is expected and woven into everyday Ethiopian life. Start with room to concede. Ethiopians understand that good agreements come from give and take on both sides, and if you open with your final offer, you remove the space for that shared process. Allow time across several meetings, and recognize that patience on the Ethiopian side may be strategic as well as cultural.
