# Making Contacts & Scheduling Appointments

## Making a Good Impression

Access in Ethiopia still flows through people more than platforms. Strong contacts come via *beteseb* (extended family), clergy, professors, or people connected to the Prosperity Party and regional administrations. A recommendation from a respected elder or official opens doors more reliably than a polished cold pitch because it vouches for your character and intent before any business discussion begins. This preference reflects a history where personal trust has mattered more than formal credentials, especially in times of political uncertainty. Building contact chains through families, churches, mosques, and political networks delivers results that LinkedIn rarely can.

Many senior people, particularly outside Addis Ababa, trust direct phone calls over email. The typical sequence involves someone giving you a number, you calling and speaking respectfully in Amharic, Afan Oromo, or Tigrinya, then following up with a written request. If you only send email, you may never register in their world. Treat phone numbers with care and use them to establish a voice connection first, then confirm details in writing.

Getting a meeting at headquarters in Addis can be straightforward, but aligning provincial and *wereda* (district) officials often requires physical visits to offices, compounds, and sometimes homes, plus time drinking coffee in the corridor. Relationships at *wereda* and *kebele* (neighborhood) level rarely form through Zoom. Plan for repeated on-the-ground visits if your work extends beyond the capital.

Where possible, partner with a trusted diaspora Ethiopian who understands both local etiquette and external expectations. Returnees from Washington, Minnesota, Dubai, or Israel often bridge worlds and can legitimize you with both elders and modern institutions.
