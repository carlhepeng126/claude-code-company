# Business Etiquette

## Making a Good Impression

In Ethiopian meeting rooms, seniority often speaks before expertise. The person who opens the discussion may not be the one with the technical knowledge, but the elder or most senior figure present. Challenging or correcting them in front of the group is a serious breach. Ethiopian culture places high value on respect for elders and preserving face, so any disagreement or redirection should be offered quietly, one-on-one, after the meeting. If you need to raise a concern, address it to the senior person privately rather than in the room where others can witness the correction.

Criticism and disagreement are rarely delivered in plain language. Ethiopians communicate through sem-en werq, a style of layered meaning in which the literal "wax" conceals the real message, the "gold." A colleague might use a proverb, a biblical reference, or a story to express pushback or doubt. The objection is there, but it arrives indirectly. If you miss the subtext, you may assume agreement when the opposite is true. Listen for metaphor and tone, not just the words themselves.

Decisions are shaped by yilugnta, the question of what others will say. Even when a proposal is technically sound, it may be filtered through concerns about reputation, how it will be perceived by the community, the church, the mosque, or elders. An idea might be rejected because of how it looks, regardless of logic. When pitching something sensitive or new, address the optics alongside the rationale. Show how the decision can be explained and defended within the networks your counterparts answer to.

Hospitality in a business setting is more than courtesy. When a meeting includes coffee brewed on coals, incense, bread, or even a fasting meal, you are being treated as someone worth investing in. Accepting this hospitality is part of the relationship. The time spent around the buna table often builds more trust than the formal agenda that follows.
