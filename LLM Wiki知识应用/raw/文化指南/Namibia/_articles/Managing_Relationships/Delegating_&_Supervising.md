# Delegating & Supervising

## Managing Relationships

Namibia's approach to authority is shaped by a difficult history. German colonial rule, which included the genocide of the Herero and Nama peoples, was followed by decades of South African apartheid administration that imposed racial hierarchy and severely restricted economic participation and education for Black Namibians. Independence came only in 1990, and the transformation since then has been ongoing but uneven. This history means that dynamics of race, authority, and trust continue to influence how people relate to managers, particularly when managers come from different ethnic or cultural backgrounds. Many employees remain cautious about raising concerns or challenging decisions openly, a wariness embedded by generations of hierarchical control. If you delegate a task and hear no questions, it may mean complete clarity or unvoiced uncertainty. Create regular, private opportunities for people to speak openly about progress or concerns, rather than relying on group settings where people may stay quiet.

The philosophy of ubuntu, which emphasizes collective humanity and mutual care, continues to shape expectations of leadership across many Namibian communities. Managers who take time to know their people, who show genuine interest in their wellbeing, and who position themselves as part of the group rather than above it, operate within a framework that resonates culturally. This does not mean informal authority. Clarity, accountability, and confident decision-making remain expected. But a manager who listens carefully and demonstrates that decisions take people's needs into account will earn deeper loyalty and more honest communication than one who relies on formal authority alone.

Namibia's workforce is ethnically diverse, with Ovambo, Herero, Damara, San, mixed-heritage, and white Namibians bringing distinct backgrounds and experiences of authority. Avoid assuming that norms around initiative, feedback, or hierarchy apply uniformly across your team. Individual backgrounds matter, and what feels respectful to one person may feel distant or overly controlling to another.
