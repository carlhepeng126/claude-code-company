# Being a Host & Guest

## Meetings & Presentations

In many Namibian settings, hosting is understood as a moral act, especially in communities where people depend on kin and church networks. Making a guest feel seen and protected reflects on the host's standing. If you are invited to someone's home, your acceptance of the invitation and how you behave once there are read as recognition of that trust. Refusing food or showing impatience can register as social rejection rather than personal preference, because sharing staple foods, meat, or tea functions as an act of inclusion more than refreshment.

Respect for elders often anchors household order. A guest who greets the oldest person present first, or waits to be seated, shows understanding of the dignity structure that many families rely on. In some homes, certain rooms or topics remain off-limits, while other households are highly open. These boundaries often reflect religion, generation, and whether the setting is urban or more rural, so follow your host's lead rather than assuming one pattern.

Hosting obligations can also carry formality that reflects historical influence. Dinners may not begin until a senior guest arrives, and leaving before that guest departs can be read as disrespect. If you are the host, be aware that the person who invites typically pays, and the gesture should be returned on another occasion. A small gift when visiting, wine, fruit, or something from your home country, recognizes the effort involved in hosting.

Warmth can come quickly, but deeper trust may develop more slowly. Colonial and apartheid-era histories have shaped how some people pace intimacy. Hospitality remains generous, but the layers beneath it take time to reveal themselves. Recognize the welcome for what it offers, and let the relationship settle at the pace your host sets.
