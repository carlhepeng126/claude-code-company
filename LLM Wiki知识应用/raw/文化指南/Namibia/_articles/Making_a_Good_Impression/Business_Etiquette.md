# Business Etiquette

## Making a Good Impression

Politeness runs through professional life in Namibia, but the way it shows up can vary across communities. Address people formally until invited to do otherwise. When meeting someone older or in a position of customary or institutional authority, wait for them to speak first. These gestures recognize both age and social standing, especially in settings where colonial and apartheid legacies have shaped sensitivity to hierarchy and how people claim space. Restraint protects dignity and demonstrates respect, particularly when elders or community leaders are present.

Communication style differs between groups. German and Afrikaner Namibians tend toward directness, while many Namibians from African heritage communities favor indirectness and measured speech. Across all settings, composure matters. Public displays of strong emotion, whether anger or affection, can be read as a loss of control. A colleague from a culture where open debate or visible intensity are seen as signs of engagement may find Namibian meetings unusually reserved, while a Namibian counterpart may read the same intensity as aggressive or disrespectful. When entering a room, greet each person individually with a handshake. Maintain eye contact during conversation and avoid wearing sunglasses, which can suggest you have something to hide.

Reputation travels through overlapping networks of family, church, school, and region, especially outside urban centers. What you do in one context may be known in another, so consistency over time builds trust more reliably than a strong first impression. Follow through on commitments and recognize that social memory can be long.

Gift giving at home is expected. Bring food, wine, or something from your own country when visiting. In professional settings, keep gifts modest. A small token is enough. Business cards should be in English and clearly show your qualifications and title, as education and formal credentials help others place you in context.
