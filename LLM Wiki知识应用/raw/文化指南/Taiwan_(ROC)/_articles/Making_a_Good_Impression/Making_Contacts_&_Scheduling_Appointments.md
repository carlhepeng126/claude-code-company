# Making Contacts & Scheduling Appointments

## Making a Good Impression

In Taiwan, a warm introduction opens doors more reliably than cold outreach. This reflects social structures built on family networks, alumni ties, temple communities, and extended circles of personal connection. Whether you're approaching a family-run manufacturer or a listed tech firm, people want to know who vouches for you before they invest time or trust. A shared contact, a former classmate, or even a mutual connection through a local temple committee can make the difference between a polite deflection and a prompt meeting. The introduction doesn't just provide information. It places you inside a network where trust already exists.

Urban centers like Taipei and Hsinchu move quickly, and appointments can often be arranged within a few days through professional channels. Even so, a brief personal opener at the start of the call or message is expected. Skipping straight to business can read as transactional. In smaller cities and towns, community and kinship ties play a more visible role, and introductions through local figures or family acquaintances still shape how business begins.

Timing your approach requires awareness of the calendar. Lunar New Year, Ghost Month, and major temple festivals are periods when people are less available or focused elsewhere. Scheduling around these rhythms shows respect and improves responsiveness. Punctuality is valued, especially in corporate and technology settings, but if plans shift, a clear explanation is expected rather than a brief apology.

Once the meeting is set, allow time at the outset for rapport. A few minutes of conversation before turning to the agenda is standard practice, and colleagues from cultures where efficiency means skipping preamble may find this slow. From the Taiwanese side, diving straight into business without acknowledging the person first can feel abrupt or dismissive.
