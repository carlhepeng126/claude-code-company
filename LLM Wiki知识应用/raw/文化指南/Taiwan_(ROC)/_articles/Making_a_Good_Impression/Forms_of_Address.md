# Forms of Address

## Making a Good Impression

In Taiwan, forms of address follow a Confucian-inflected social logic where age, seniority, and role determine how people speak to one another. Respect is communicated through choosing the right relational form, anchoring every interaction to where people stand in relation to each other. Using surname plus title, such as 陳老師 (Teacher Chen) or 林經理 (Manager Lin), is a common and safe approach in professional settings. It communicates seriousness and proper distance without sounding cold. Launching into first names too quickly can misread the situation and flatten the social architecture that colleagues are working within.

Kinship terms like 阿姨 (auntie), 叔叔 (uncle), 哥 (older brother), and 姐 (older sister) are used widely beyond literal family. They create instant social mapping, placing people in a moral structure of care and deference. This system is less about warmth alone and more about relational placement. A colleague from a culture where informality builds rapport might read these forms as overly careful or old-fashioned, while Taiwanese professionals might find rapid first-name use presumptuous or face-threatening, especially when hierarchy has not yet been acknowledged.

Language choice adds another layer. Mandarin is the dominant public language, but Taiwanese Hokkien conveys warmth, local belonging, and intimacy in many families and communities. Switching between languages shifts the social world being invoked. Even in modern, globally oriented workplaces, younger urban professionals code-switch depending on context rather than abandoning the system. Watching for these cues and matching formality level prevents mis-ranking and supports trust.

When meeting a group, ask for team details in advance to understand hierarchy. Address the most senior person first, and introduce your own team in hierarchical order to signal who holds authority. This prepares the ground for every exchange that follows.
