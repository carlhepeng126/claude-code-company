# Business Meeting Culture

## Meetings & Presentations

Meetings in Taiwan blend respect for seniority with a commitment to group harmony. The most senior person present may speak less than others, but their cues shape the room. Watch for subtle signals such as a nod, a shift in tone, or a brief interjection. These small gestures often indicate direction or approval, and decisions rarely move forward without them. Pushing for a visible, vocal consensus may miss the real decision-making already happening through quieter channels.

Harmony also means disagreement is expressed carefully. Rather than direct contradiction, you may hear a well-framed question, a shift in topic, or silence. A colleague from a culture where debate is expected to be open and immediate may read this restraint as avoidance. Meanwhile, a Taiwanese participant may read that same bluntness as disrespectful or tone-deaf. Pre-meetings or side conversations help align positions before the formal discussion, reducing the risk of public disagreement and making the main meeting more efficient.

In tech firms and multinational environments, meetings tend to be structured and time-conscious, but relational awareness remains. Agendas are followed, but politeness and attentiveness to how things are said, not just what is said, still guide the flow. Sending materials in advance allows participants to prepare thoughtful input and avoids putting anyone on the spot.

Linguistic and generational variation also plays a role. While Mandarin dominates most business settings, some older professionals or those from specific regions may be more comfortable in Hokkien or Hakka. Across these differences, considered speech and avoiding confrontation remain shared expectations.
