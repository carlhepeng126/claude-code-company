# Feedback and Coaching

## Managing Relationships

Feedback in Iraq is shaped by a difficult history and by deep respect for what people have survived. Many professionals have lived through war, sanctions, and personal loss. Being corrected by a much younger or less experienced outsider can feel humiliating if handled without care. At the same time, there is strong pride in learning and rebuilding. The challenge is to offer feedback in a way that honors both the trauma and the resilience. Acknowledge what someone has already managed under pressure before turning to what could be stronger. Frame the conversation as joint problem solving rather than pointing out gaps.

Criticism rarely arrives directly. In hierarchical settings and among older colleagues, feedback often comes wrapped in indirection: "maybe next time we could..." or "in our context, it works better if..." Sometimes it travels through a third person rather than face to face. This approach protects dignity and keeps relationships intact. When you need to raise concerns, follow the same pattern. Use coaching language, model what good looks like in the local context, and avoid singling people out in front of others.

Generational differences are real and growing. Younger Iraqis influenced by the Tishreen protests and global online culture, especially in civil society and tech spaces, are more open to direct feedback and participatory styles. In an Erbil startup or a Baghdad NGO, you may find younger colleagues comfortable with transparent exchanges that would feel too blunt in a ministry or with war-era leaders. Adapt your directness to the setting and the generation.

Reciprocity also reshapes the tone. Invite Iraqi colleagues to coach you on context, risk, or language. This makes developmental conversations feel less one-sided and colonial, and it builds the mutual respect that makes all feedback easier to give and receive.
