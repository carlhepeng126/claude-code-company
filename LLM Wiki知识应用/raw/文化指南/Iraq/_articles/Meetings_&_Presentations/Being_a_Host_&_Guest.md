# Being a Host & Guest

## Meetings & Presentations

When someone invites you into their home in Iraq, you are entering under their protection. This is more than good manners. In tribal and many urban contexts, a guest's safety and comfort become the host's moral responsibility. Any harm that befalls you while under their roof reflects badly on the host's honor and standing. If you wander off alone or disregard security guidance, you place your host in a difficult position. Follow their cues on where to go and when, and let them manage your well-being visibly.

Hospitality is deeply gendered in many Iraqi homes. Male guests may be hosted by men in one room while women cook and host female guests elsewhere. In more cosmopolitan or younger urban circles, families may host together, but separate spaces remain common. If you are guided to a men's or women's room, accept the arrangement. Pressing for mixed company can make your hosts uncomfortable rather than progressive.

Many homes you visit will have been rebuilt after conflict, displacement, or looting. Hosts may share this indirectly through photos, certain objects, or even which room they choose to receive you in. A brief, sincere comment acknowledging what it took to rebuild goes further than elaborate sympathy. When you host Iraqi colleagues in return, whether in Iraq or abroad, match the logic of protection. Show visible concern for their safety, dignity, and comfort, and never leave them to manage logistics alone. The quality of your care, not just the food, is what will be remembered.
