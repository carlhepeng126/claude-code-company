# Business Entertaining

## Meetings & Presentations

Business entertaining in Iraq is shaped as much by security and sectarian geography as by tradition. Where you meet, what you drink, and who else is in the room all reveal calculations about safety, religious observance, and social standing. An invitation to a simple kebab shop may have more relational depth than a hotel meal, and a gathering in someone's home or tribal guesthouse represents a level of trust that shouldn't be mistaken for casual hospitality.

Venue choice is rarely neutral. In Baghdad and Basra, security constraints mean that entertaining often takes place in hotel restaurants inside safer zones or compounds. Evening gatherings may be clustered in areas with lower militia presence or easier movement. In Erbil and the Kurdistan Region, there's more freedom, and you might find yourself on a rooftop bar or in a mid-range restaurant in Karrada. Your Iraqi counterpart is reading risks you can't see, from sectarian fault lines to clan visibility, so follow their lead on location rather than proposing your own.

Alcohol is a visible dividing line. In much of federal Iraq, it's restricted or socially sensitive. In Kurdistan and some Baghdad enclaves, it's more accessible. If alcohol appears on the table, treat it as optional and watch who abstains. Pressing someone to drink, or ordering without reading the room first, can create real discomfort. The presence or absence of alcohol often signals the religious and social orientation of the group, and that context shapes the evening as much as the food.

An invitation to a family home or mudhif, a tribal guesthouse, is a relationship contract. It places you inside a circle of trust and accountability that extends beyond the business itself. Respond with discretion and seriousness, not as though you've been given a cultural tour.
