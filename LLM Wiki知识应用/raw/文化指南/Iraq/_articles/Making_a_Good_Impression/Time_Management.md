# Time Management

## Making a Good Impression

In Iraq, time bends around forces larger than the calendar. Checkpoints, protests, militia movements, sudden road closures, and power cuts can shatter carefully planned schedules. In some areas, people have lived for years with curfews or travel warnings. Ministries and state companies may have official hours, but actual decision-making often happens in short windows when the right director is in, the power grid is up, and security permits movement. This makes time agreements real, but rarely fixed. A delay is often someone navigating constraints you cannot see.

Regional differences matter. In the Kurdistan Region, especially Erbil, institutions often run on a more predictable clock, closer to European norms. Federal Baghdad or provincial cities are more vulnerable to disruption and last-minute reshuffles. If you are working across both contexts, adjust expectations accordingly. In the Kurdistan Region, align with stricter punctuality; in federal areas, show reliability while recognizing that your counterpart may be managing obstacles beyond their control.

Build redundancy into your plans. Do not design a project that collapses if one meeting slips. Reconfirm closer to the day and ask explicitly about security or travel concerns. When a delay happens, ask what is blocking progress before assuming disinterest. Often the answer lies not in motivation, but in the unpredictable environment that shapes every working day. Treat time commitments seriously, but plan for the possibility that circumstances will intervene.
