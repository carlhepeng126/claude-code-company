# Feedback and Coaching

## Managing Relationships

In Nigerian professional settings, feedback is read through the same codes that govern respect, seniority, and social standing. How you correct someone reveals character as much as competence. A harsh or blunt tone can signal arrogance or poor upbringing, even if the content is accurate. Social intelligence means knowing how to guide someone without humiliating them, and that skill often outweighs directness.

Seniority and age structure who can speak with authority. A junior colleague offering blunt feedback to someone older or more senior can be seen as disorder, a breach of the moral order that shapes relationships. Senior people, by contrast, are expected to correct with both firmness and restraint, carrying responsibility for the person's development. This means managers need to choose not just the content of feedback but the voice delivering it, especially in settings where hierarchy runs deep.

Public face extends beyond the individual. Shame touches family, ethnic group, and community standing. This is why feedback that happens privately allows learning without social damage. Critique delivered in front of others can close down the relationship rather than open up change. Humor, proverbs, and story are culturally familiar ways to guide behavior while keeping warmth intact. A well-told example allows someone to adjust without being pinned to a failure.

Moral framing appears often in coaching, drawing on religious or ethical language around integrity and responsibility. When used to guide, it resonates. When used carelessly, it can feel like a judgment of character rather than performance, and that distinction is closely monitored.
