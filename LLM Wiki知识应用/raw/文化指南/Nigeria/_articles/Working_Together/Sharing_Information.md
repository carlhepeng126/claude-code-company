# Sharing Information

## Working Together

In many Nigerian workplaces, information follows hierarchy. What gets shared publicly, and how, is shaped by respect for seniority and the need to avoid causing embarrassment. Sensitive details are often contained, delivered privately, or softened to protect dignity and keep relationships intact. A concern raised in a large meeting might be handled with discretion later through a more appropriate channel. The structure of information flow reflects the broader social expectation that authority is acknowledged and face is protected, not that people lack transparency. If you share a problem or correction publicly when hierarchy is visible, the messenger can become the issue as much as the message itself.

Who delivers information can affect how it is received. A point raised by someone without the right standing or relationship to the recipient can feel disrespectful or out of place, even if the content is accurate. This is why some updates or feedback are routed through specific people. Message legitimacy is connected to the messenger's position and their social proximity to the recipient. If you need to raise something with a senior colleague, consider whether the message should come from you directly or be carried by someone whose status makes the exchange appropriate.

Indirectness is often a form of precision. Humor, proverbs, or softened phrasing can communicate warning, disagreement, or correction without triggering public conflict. This is social accuracy in practice: conveying truth while preserving dignity. If a colleague uses a proverb during a discussion or shifts to humor when tension rises, they may be signaling more than the surface suggests. Listen for meaning in how something is said, not only what is said.

Language choice also plays a role. English signals official business and national reach. Local languages signal intimacy, identity recognition, and trust. Code-switching can create warmth and inclusion, but it can also mark an inner circle. Keep track of when core information is delivered in a language not everyone in the room understands, and clarify key points afterward if needed.
