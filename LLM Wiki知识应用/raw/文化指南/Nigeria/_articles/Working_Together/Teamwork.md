# Teamwork

## Working Together

Nigerian teamwork is anchored in the expectation that collaboration includes visible mutual support. Extended family and kin networks function as primary safety nets across much of the country, and this social reality shapes what people expect from colleagues. Reliability means standing with people when circumstances become difficult, not only delivering on tasks. Trust deepens when team members show they will back each other in practical, relational terms. A colleague who performs well but does not offer this kind of support may be seen as technically competent but not fully dependable. In mixed teams, this can create friction: a team member from a culture where professional and personal spheres are more separate may interpret this expectation as over-involvement, while a Nigerian colleague may read their distance as coldness or a lack of commitment.

Influence within teams often sits with connectors as much as with formal roles. Elders, mentors, religious leaders, and well-connected figures shape how groups cohere and how decisions gain legitimacy. These relationships are part of the social architecture of collaboration. Knowing who holds informal authority can make the difference between a proposal that moves forward and one that stalls, even when the idea itself is sound. Ignoring these channels can reduce buy-in, regardless of the quality of the work.

Respect rituals help collaboration feel properly ordered. Many Nigerian settings maintain hierarchy through greeting order, politeness tone, and visible deference, especially where age and seniority carry moral weight. People may wait to signal respect before expressing disagreement openly. Regional variation shapes how this plays out. Yoruba contexts often feature expressive sociality and visible respect rituals. Northern contexts shaped by emirate and Islamic scholarly traditions may value more restrained decorum and clearer respect lines. Southeastern contexts often combine communal pride with directness and entrepreneurial confidence. These are distinct social climates. Mixed regional teams may need different registers of warmth and formality to feel cohesive, since what reads as normal engagement in one context may feel too formal or too casual in another.
