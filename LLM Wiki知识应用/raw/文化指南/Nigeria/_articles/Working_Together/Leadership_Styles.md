# Leadership Styles

## Working Together

Leadership in Nigeria is rarely assessed on outcomes alone. Personal legitimacy often comes from social standing, maturity, visible dignity, and the ability to protect others. Age, titles, lineage, and connections all feed into how authority is read. A leader who delivers results but lacks moral credibility or relational presence may struggle to build lasting trust.

The "big man" archetype runs deep in Nigerian public and organizational life. Leaders who signal generosity, who provide resources, who visibly care for their people, and who can navigate systems on their behalf often command loyalty. This style draws partly on traditions where elders are expected to shield the group and ensure its welfare. But it is also morally ambivalent. When largeness tips into arrogance, extraction, or entitlement, critique follows. Many Nigerians hold both readings at once: appreciating protective leadership while watching closely for abuse of it.

Regional differences shape what strong leadership looks like. In northern contexts, restraint, hierarchy, and decorum tend to define authority. In the southwest, charisma and public presence may blend with deference rituals. In the southeast, visible commercial success, assertiveness, and competence often earn respect. A leadership register that works in Lagos may not land the same way in Kano or Enugu. Adapting tone and formality by audience is not code-switching, it's cultural fluency.
