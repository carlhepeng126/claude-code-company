# Business Etiquette

## Making a Good Impression

Greeting well in Nigeria is a sign of upbringing and moral seriousness. It acknowledges age, relationship, and status, and a colleague who skips this step or rushes through it can come across as dismissive of the person in front of them. The exchange sets the tone for everything that follows. In a meeting, this might mean taking time to greet each person individually before sitting down, and being prepared for handshakes that last while the conversation continues. Someone used to brief nods before getting down to business may feel the pace is slow. From a Nigerian perspective, the colleague who skips this opening may seem distant or poorly raised.

Hierarchy operates through dignity. Correcting someone openly, especially a senior figure or elder, risks public embarrassment and can damage the relationship. Many people soften disagreement with tact, humor, or indirect phrasing to preserve face. Feedback tends to be delivered privately or wrapped in warmer language. If a point seems vague or softened in a group setting, follow up one-on-one to hear what was actually meant.

Humor and proverbs are tools of social intelligence. Nigerians often use banter to test warmth, ease tension, or say hard truths in a way that protects the relationship. An indirect phrase or well-placed proverb can signal cultural competence and wisdom. Listen for the meaning beneath the delivery. Hospitality also functions as credibility. Offering food and drink signals goodwill and honor, and receiving it well helps build trust.
