# Time Management

## Making a Good Impression

Time in Nigeria is shaped by relationships, religious rhythms, and infrastructure realities rather than by the clock alone. Weddings, funerals, naming ceremonies, and faith gatherings form core obligations that can reshape schedules without warning. These are not interruptions but calendar anchors, especially in extended family and community networks. In Muslim communities, prayer times and Friday congregational patterns structure the week. In Christian communities, Sunday commitments hold similar weight. Religious holidays and festivals shape energy and availability across the year. When scheduling meetings or setting deadlines, treat these obligations as structural rather than discretionary.

Lagos presents a particular challenge. Traffic and infrastructure constraints make punctuality unpredictable, often through no fault of the individual. Delays are produced by the city itself, which has created a cultural habit of building flexibility into plans. A colleague from a context where meetings start on time may read a late arrival as lack of commitment. A Nigerian professional may see rigid adherence to start times, when logistics are out of anyone's control, as disconnected from the reality on the ground. If timing is critical, state it clearly and confirm logistics in advance rather than assuming shared expectations.

Nigeria does not have one time rhythm. Lagos can feel intense and opportunistic. Abuja often runs more administratively, with greater schedule discipline. Port Harcourt and other oil-linked cities carry a different tempo again. Northern commercial centres may be shaped by trading rhythms and prayer schedules. Calibrate your expectations by city and sector. Multinational firms and export-oriented industries tend to hold deadlines firmly. Family-owned businesses and government offices may operate with greater flexibility, especially when decisions involve senior figures or when relational considerations outweigh task urgency.
