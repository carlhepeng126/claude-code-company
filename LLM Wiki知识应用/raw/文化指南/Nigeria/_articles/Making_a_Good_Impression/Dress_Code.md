# Dress Code

## Making a Good Impression

Appearance speaks before you do in Nigeria. The way you present yourself conveys your understanding of respect, occasion, and social awareness. Clothing functions as public proof that you take the setting seriously and recognize the dignity of those you meet. In professional contexts, a polished appearance signals credibility and self-respect, and anything that reads as careless or under-prepared can undermine your position before the conversation even starts. Men typically wear well-fitted suits or smart shirts with trousers, and while ties are common, they're optional in many settings. Women choose tailored dresses, skirts, or trousers with blouses, often with attention to color and fabric quality.

In Lagos especially, style culture makes effort highly visible. Appearance is tied to identity, confidence, and social positioning in a city where people read competence through presentation. This doesn't mean flashiness for its own sake, but it does mean that showing up looking sharp is part of how you demonstrate that you're ready for business. A colleague accustomed to casual office norms might interpret this emphasis on appearance as superficial, while a Nigerian counterpart might read casual dress as a lack of seriousness or respect for the occasion.

Regional and religious contexts shape expectations around modesty and formality. In northern Muslim-majority areas, modesty is more explicitly enforced: women should cover their heads, arms, and legs, and both men and women should dress conservatively. In southern contexts, norms can be more flexible, but respectability still registers through neatness and attention to the occasion. Traditional attire such as the baban riga for men or brightly colored wrappers for women can signal ethnic pride and belonging. Wearing traditional dress to social or ceremonial events can be well received, but only if done with genuine respect and context awareness, never as costume.
