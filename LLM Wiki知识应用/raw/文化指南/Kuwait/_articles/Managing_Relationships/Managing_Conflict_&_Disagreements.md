# Managing Conflict & Disagreements

## Managing Relationships

In Kuwait, disagreement rarely surfaces as open confrontation in the office. Instead, conflict tends to move through reputational channels outside the formal workplace. A dispute left unaddressed in private can re-emerge as critical talk in a diwaniya, pressure exerted through a member of parliament, or pointed commentary on social media. The meeting room may remain quiet, but the issue is being aired and escalated elsewhere. This reflects the strength of informal networks and the social cost of public shaming, known as *ʿayb* (a word denoting actions seen as shameful or socially inappropriate). If you sense tension during a project discussion but hear nothing directly, ask a trusted Kuwaiti colleague whether there is quiet dissatisfaction building. Addressing the concern early and privately can prevent it from becoming public.

Tribal and family affiliation shapes how conflict unfolds. A disagreement with one individual can widen quickly if that person belongs to a prominent tribe or well-connected family. Others may feel loyalty requires them to take sides, even if the original issue was minor or purely professional. This means that resolving the matter with the individual alone may no longer be enough. In such cases, bringing in a respected intermediary, often someone with standing in a relevant diwaniya or family circle, can help mediate in a way that allows everyone to step back without losing face.

Written complaints or public call-outs, especially via email or social platforms, are read as attacks on honor and can close doors permanently. The preference is to fix problems quietly and in person, preserving dignity on all sides. A colleague from a culture where directness and documentation are valued might interpret this as avoidance, while a Kuwaiti counterpart may view a formal written grievance as unnecessarily aggressive and damaging to the relationship. Both assumptions miss the intent. Address serious issues face to face, show willingness to listen and adjust, and avoid turning internal friction into a matter of public record.
