# Delegating & Supervising

## Managing Relationships

Authority in Kuwait is highly concentrated and personal, shaped by a social system in which family standing, personal connections, and who-you-know — known as *wasta* (the informal network of relationships and influence that governs professional access and decisions) — determine not just who makes decisions, but who has the relational access to make them happen. In public-sector and government-adjacent organizations especially, delegation is rare. Major decisions flow upward and remain with senior figures, often owners or senior family members, even when day-to-day operations are managed by experienced expatriate professionals. Understanding this structure means recognizing that formal titles may not reveal where decision authority actually sits. If you need approval or direction, identify who holds the relevant relationships, not just who holds the role.

The workforce itself is deeply bifurcated. Kuwaiti nationals are concentrated in public-sector roles, where the state functions as a major employer and career expectations reflect social standing and family ties. Expatriates, who make up over 70 percent of the population, dominate the private sector and often occupy middle-management positions with operational responsibility but limited strategic authority. This creates a management environment in which norms differ by nationality and sector. A Kuwaiti employee may operate within a different set of expectations around autonomy, feedback, and accountability than an Indian or Lebanese colleague in the same office.

Supervision tends to be close, especially in family-run businesses, but feedback is given infrequently and with care. Public correction risks damaging face, both for the individual and for the manager. If you need to address performance, do so privately and frame concerns in ways that preserve dignity and acknowledge effort. In teams with members from South Asia, the Middle East, and the West, managing these differences requires recognizing that what feels like micromanagement to one person may feel like appropriate attention to another.
