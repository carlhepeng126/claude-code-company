# Negotiating

## Working Together

Negotiations in Kuwait draw on old merchant traditions and more recent political realities. The country's trading history gave it a culture of bargaining, testing, and pushing hard on price. That instinct is still there. At the same time, oil wealth and the centrality of the state have introduced a different set of considerations. Deals are judged not only on commercial terms but on whether they visibly benefit Kuwaiti citizens through jobs, contracts, training, or prestige. A proposal that looks efficient but tilts rewards toward foreign firms or expats may face sharp criticism in Parliament or in diwaniyas, where these agreements are scrutinized and debated. Shape your narrative around citizen benefit early, and help your counterpart prepare defensible answers to the question of how the deal serves Kuwait.

Expect the process to be fluid and the opening positions to be broad. Kuwaitis may ask about price before you have finished your pitch, or jump between topics mid-discussion. This is part of the testing dance, not a signal of disinterest. Silence can indicate a breakdown, while interruptions often show engagement. Price will be central, and your counterpart will expect to see movement. Build room into your opening offer so you can make concessions without undermining your position. The need to secure a discount is as much about demonstrating hard bargaining as it is about the final number.

Historical memory shapes caution. Past controversies around foreign concessions and inflexible contracts make many Kuwaitis wary of being locked in or exploited. Avoid exclusivity clauses, long commitments, or opaque pricing unless you can justify them clearly. The risk your counterpart is managing is reputational and political, not survival. A deal that later looks like it sold Kuwait short, even if technically sound, can become a liability. Be ready for delays driven by parliamentary schedules, public optics, or the need to consult advisors. These are not stalling tactics but part of the deliberative rhythm of decision-making in a country where deals are public currency.
