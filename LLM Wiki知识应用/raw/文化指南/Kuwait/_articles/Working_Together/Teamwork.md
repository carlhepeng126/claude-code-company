# Teamwork

## Working Together

A team in Kuwait may look uniform on paper, but it often brings together people working under very different legal and social realities. Kuwaiti citizens typically have secure public-sector roles or strong contracts, expatriates hold renewable visas tied to their employers, and in some sectors Bidoon staff work with far fewer protections. These differences shape how comfortable people feel speaking up, challenging a decision, or taking initiative. A Kuwaiti colleague might push back informally on a plan, knowing their job is secure, while an expat on the same team may stay quiet to avoid visa complications. Public-sector teams often move at a steadier rhythm, shaped by job security and bureaucracy, where harmony and predictability count for more than speed. Private-sector teams, especially in multinationals or family businesses, run faster and face clearer performance pressure, though wasta and state expectations still influence who gets which responsibilities.

Influence inside a team rarely follows the organization chart. Unspoken distinctions between urban hadar and Bedouin-origin colleagues can shape who is seen as polished, who has ties to ministries or MPs, and who brings tribal support. These connections often determine informal leadership and who gets protected when things go wrong. A junior employee from a well-connected family may quietly open doors or shift decisions, while a manager without local networks may struggle to gain traction despite their title. Understanding these affiliations helps explain who really influences whom, beyond what titles suggest.

Trust develops slowly through repeated, low-pressure interaction rather than structured exercises. Real cohesion often builds outside meeting rooms, in cafés, office lounges, or side conversations where reliability and presence become visible over time. What registers is showing up regularly, in person, and in ways that demonstrate commitment. Occasional company dinners, national celebrations, or Ramadan iftars serve as informal moments to affirm loyalty and belonging, not through facilitated activities but through shared participation and appropriate conduct.

Gender also shapes how people collaborate. Many Kuwaitis attended gender-segregated schools and universities, and while mixed-gender teams are common, some colleagues feel more comfortable raising doubts or disagreeing in same-gender subgroups or quieter channels. Building multiple routes for input, such as smaller side meetings alongside plenary sessions, can draw out voices that stay silent in larger mixed forums.
