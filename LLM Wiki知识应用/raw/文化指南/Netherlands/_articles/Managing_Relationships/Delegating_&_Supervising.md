# Delegating & Supervising

## Managing Relationships

Dutch managers lead through expertise and facilitation rather than authority. This approach has deep roots: the Netherlands developed without a strong feudal aristocracy, relying instead on networks of guilds, merchants, and civic institutions that required constant negotiation and practical problem-solving. The *polder model*, named after the collaborative effort needed to reclaim land from the sea, reflects this history. Survival depended on everyone working together, and that philosophy became a lasting cultural value. Protestant Calvinist tradition reinforced it further, emphasizing individual conscience and a fundamentally equal relationship with God that translated into comfort with questioning authority. The result is one of the lowest power distance scores in the world: Dutch employees expect to challenge their manager's view, and a manager who discourages this loses credibility.

Delegation in Dutch organizations means real autonomy. Tasks are assigned with the expectation that the person will determine the method themselves. Supervising closely while claiming to delegate will be read as distrust. Flat structures mean representatives from multiple departments often attend meetings, and decisions about who does what can be made on the spot. This works because employees at all levels expect to be consulted on decisions that affect their work, and information flows openly across the hierarchy.

Consensus takes time, and trying to shortcut it by pushing decisions through unilaterally generates friction. A manager from a culture where decisions are made quickly by those in charge may find the process slow, while Dutch colleagues may see unilateral action as arrogant or reckless. Once agreement is reached, however, commitment is genuine and implementation follows smoothly.

Feedback is expected to be direct and mutual. Supervisors give constructive criticism, but they should expect the same in return. Honesty is valued over social grace, and a manager who consistently softens difficult truths will eventually lose the team's respect. Failing to point out a mistake is seen as poor judgment, because learning depends on knowing where things went wrong. Criticism should be delivered privately rather than singling someone out in public, but the expectation is clarity, without hedging.
