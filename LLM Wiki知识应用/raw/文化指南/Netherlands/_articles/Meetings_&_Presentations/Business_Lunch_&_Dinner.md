# Business Lunch & Dinner

## Meetings & Presentations

Business meals in the Netherlands have a practical, balanced feel. Ordering modestly aligns with a wider cultural preference for restraint and avoiding excess. An elaborate multicourse meal with expensive wine might be seen as wasteful or out of step with the emphasis on efficiency and equality. A colleague from a culture where hospitality means abundance may feel uncertain about whether a simpler menu signals warmth or welcome, while a Dutch host may read lavish ordering as unnecessary or even uncomfortable. Choosing straightforward dishes keeps the focus on conversation and connection without creating distance.

Dietary preferences are accommodated without fuss. Vegetarian and vegan options are widely available, and health-conscious choices are normal rather than noteworthy. If you have specific needs, stating them plainly works better than apologizing or framing them as an imposition. The Dutch appreciate directness here as much as in other professional exchanges.

Alcohol is optional and often minimal. Many colleagues drink sparingly or not at all, and no one will expect you to keep pace or match rounds. Offering choice rather than assuming everyone will drink wine or beer shows respect for personal preference. Even in social settings with colleagues, staying composed and aware of your limits reflects professionalism. Losing control, even late in the evening, can affect how you're perceived in the working relationship.

Conversation during meals may touch on business topics, but the tone stays pragmatic rather than pitched or performative. Light personal topics are fine, but overly personal questions can feel intrusive. Keep exchanges friendly, grounded, and genuine without pushing for intimacy.
