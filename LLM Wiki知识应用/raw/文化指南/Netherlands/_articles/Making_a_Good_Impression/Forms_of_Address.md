# Forms of Address

## Making a Good Impression

The Netherlands tends toward quick informality in how people address each other, and this plainness has deep roots. Calvinist-inflected modesty, especially strong in the north and in Protestant histories, links virtue to restraint. Foregrounding rank through elaborate titles can feel like self-inflation rather than respect. Meanwhile, the legacy of pillarisation trained generations to navigate difference without ceremony. For much of the 20th century, Dutch life was organized through pillars (Protestant, Catholic, socialist, liberal), each with its own schools, unions, and social clubs. People learned to keep interactions workable across boundaries using predictable, low-drama social rules rather than elaborate honorifics.

First names arrive quickly in most professional settings, often within the first meeting. Handshakes are standard on arrival and departure, firm but not prolonged, and people typically say their family name during the initial greeting. Academic or professional titles are rarely used in spoken conversation and can feel boastful if mentioned too often. Written correspondence is more formal. Use "Dhr." (Mijnheer, Mr.) or "Mevr./Mw." (Mevrouw, Mrs. or Ms.) followed by the last name in emails and letters until the person invites you to drop it. The polder model, rooted in collective water management, favors "we" language over deference. Legitimacy comes from demonstrating you can participate, not from being deferred to, so address systems tilt toward flatness even when power differences exist.

This informality does not mean closeness. Dutch culture pairs casual address with strong privacy boundaries. You may be on first-name terms quickly while personal topics, emotions, or home life remain carefully bounded. A colleague from a culture where titles reinforce respect throughout a relationship may read this quick shift to first names as presumptuous or overly familiar. From the Dutch side, insisting on titles after the first meeting can feel like creating unnecessary distance or making the hierarchy heavier than the work requires. Directness culture reduces the social need for cushioning titles because clarity is treated as respect. Elaborate formality can make intentions harder to read.

Regional variation shifts the warmth-to-plainness ratio. The Randstad (Amsterdam, Rotterdam, The Hague, Utrecht) tends toward brisk, efficient, sometimes irony-forward interactions. Smaller towns may feel warmer and slower to trust. In Friesland, the choice to acknowledge Frisian language and identity can function as a belonging marker. Maintain physical distance during conversation, about an arm's length, and avoid back slaps, arm grabs, or prolonged eye contact, all of which can feel intrusive.
