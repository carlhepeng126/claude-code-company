# Business Etiquette

## Making a Good Impression

Dutch business etiquette grows from Protestant values of honesty, modesty and egalitarianism. This means that elaborate courtesy, emotional display or excessive formality can weaken your credibility rather than strengthen it. Respect is shown through listening, keeping your word and treating people as equals, regardless of rank. Juniors speak openly in meetings and expect to be heard. If you come from a culture where deference to seniority is central, this can feel uncomfortably flat. From a Dutch perspective, formal hierarchical language can seem either performative or evasive.

Communication is direct and plain. Compliments are offered sparingly, and saying something is "not bad" is genuine praise. Criticism is given openly and taken lightly; the Dutch will argue without taking personal offense. If your style relies on indirectness to preserve harmony, prepare for bluntness that feels abrupt. But this directness is pragmatic, and rarely personal. Eye contact is steady and sentences are short. Avoid elaborate gestures, emotional appeals or superlatives. These distract rather than persuade.

Physical contact is minimal. Handshakes are firm and brief. Keep a comfortable distance and avoid touching someone's arm or shoulder mid-conversation. Back slapping or embracing will make people uncomfortable. The humor is dry, and body language is subdued.

Business cards may not be exchanged until late in a meeting, if at all. Include any degree above a bachelor's on your card, and note the difference between "Drs." (university graduate) and "Dr." (PhD). If invited to someone's home, bring flowers or chocolates. Small, tasteful corporate gifts are acceptable, but anything lavish risks being read as a bribe.
