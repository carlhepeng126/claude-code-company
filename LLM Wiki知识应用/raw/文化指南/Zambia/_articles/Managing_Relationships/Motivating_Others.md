# Motivating Others

## Managing Relationships

Motivation in Zambian workplaces is often woven into a wider web of responsibility. Doing well usually means being able to provide for an extended family, meet community obligations, and support people who depend on you. This relational frame means that pride and pressure are socially distributed, and a person's sense of purpose often extends well beyond individual achievement. If you frame goals only in terms of personal ambition or standout performance, you may miss what actually drives people. Linking work to shared purpose, to what it enables for others, or to how it strengthens the team tends to resonate more deeply than emphasizing solo wins.

Security and steadiness hold real emotional weight, especially where livelihoods are shaped by fluctuation. Predictability is often interpreted as care, so consistency in expectations, fairness in process, and reliable follow-through can be more motivating than high-energy targets or rapid change. Where a manager from a culture that values agility and fast pivots might see predictability as bureaucratic, a Zambian colleague may read that same stability as evidence of respect. In return, a manager who frequently changes direction or shifts priorities without explanation may be seen as unreliable, even if they believe they are being responsive.

Recognition works differently depending on context and person. Public praise can be welcomed, but for some people, especially where modesty norms or social safety are at play, quiet respect is more comfortable and more meaningful. Managers who vary their approach, who notice dependability and character as well as high-profile results, and who acknowledge contribution without making it a performance, tend to build stronger engagement. National unity ideals and Christian or humanist values elevate fairness, steadiness, and contribution as markers of dignity, so being seen as decent and dependable often matters more than being seen as exceptional.
