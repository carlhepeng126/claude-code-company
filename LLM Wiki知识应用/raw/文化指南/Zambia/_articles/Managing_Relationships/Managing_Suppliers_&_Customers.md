# Managing Suppliers & Customers

## Managing Relationships

Trust in Zambian commercial relationships builds through demonstration rather than promises. Long experience of supply volatility, infrastructure constraints, and changing conditions has given rise to a grounded realism about what can actually be delivered. Credibility forms through small visible steps and repeat follow-through, not sweeping assurances. This means that the first transaction, and the next few after it, function as proof. If you need a supplier to accommodate a tight timeline or a customer to commit to a larger order, expect to earn that confidence incrementally. Start with what you can clearly deliver, and let the relationship deepen from there.

Face-to-face contact and regular check-ins strengthen the relationship in ways that email alone cannot. Reputation circulates through overlapping social circles, including church communities, extended family networks, and neighbourhood ties, which means that fairness and consistency are remembered long after a transaction closes. A counterpart from a culture where decisions are made quickly and then left to run on their own may interpret this emphasis on continuity as inefficiency. A Zambian colleague, in turn, may read a hands-off approach as disengagement or lack of seriousness. Building in regular contact, even when no immediate issue exists, signals commitment and reliability.

When problems arise, handle them privately and focus on repair rather than blame. Respect and dignity remain central, even under pressure. Payments and processes should be made as clear and practical as possible. Mobile money is now woven into daily exchange, and accommodating locally common transaction pathways without treating them as less legitimate builds trust. In border-linked trade contexts, particularly around crossings like Kasumbalesa or Nakonde, allow for realistic lead times and buffer for conditions that formal schedules do not always capture.
