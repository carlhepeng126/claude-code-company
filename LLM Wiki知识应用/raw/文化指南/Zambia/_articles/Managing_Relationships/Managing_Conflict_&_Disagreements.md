# Managing Conflict & Disagreements

## Managing Relationships

Disagreement in Zambian workplaces is often managed in ways that protect cohesion and personal dignity, especially when social and professional networks overlap. Public confrontation raises the stakes considerably because it risks humiliating someone in front of witnesses, which can make repair far more difficult. If you need to address a sensitive issue, move the conversation into a private setting. A colleague who feels cornered in a meeting may withdraw or agree outwardly while the underlying problem remains unresolved.

Silence in meetings should not be taken as agreement. People may withhold dissent to preserve group harmony or out of respect for hierarchy. If you need honest input, ask open questions in smaller groups or one-on-one, giving colleagues room to share concerns without exposing themselves publicly. A manager from a culture where disagreement is voiced openly in group settings may misread this reticence as disengagement, while a Zambian colleague may view direct public challenge as unnecessarily aggressive or disrespectful.

When conflict does surface, the goal is often to restore the working relationship, not just assign blame or solve the immediate issue. Insaka-style counsel, where trusted figures help guide resolution, remains a culturally coherent route. In some cases, inviting a senior colleague or even a respected intermediary from outside the organization can help unlock a stalled conversation, especially when hierarchy or emotion blocks direct dialogue. This reflects a practical recognition that not all conflicts can be solved face-to-face without support.

Regional and generational differences also shape how directly people prefer to engage. What feels appropriately frank in one setting may feel blunt or disrespectful in another, so take time to understand the local codes of respect within your team.
