# Delegating & Supervising

## Managing Relationships

Zambian workplace culture reflects the Ubuntu philosophy that runs through southern and central African societies, the understanding that individual identity and wellbeing are inseparable from community. The Bemba concept of "umuntu ngumuntu ngabantu" (a person is a person through other persons) shapes how employees expect to be treated and how they respond to direction. A manager who shows genuine interest in an employee's family, community, and life outside work earns a qualitatively different level of loyalty than one who limits engagement to professional matters. This means asking after relatives, attending social events when invited, and recognizing that the human relationship is the foundation on which performance is built. Teams do not respond well to being treated as task-delivery units.

The workplace is formal and hierarchical, and Ubuntu's communal warmth coexists with a clear respect for rank, age, and seniority. Leaders are expected to command authority with dignity, and the "chitakumusha" practice of indirect communication means that employees will express disagreement or concern through circumlocution and hint rather than direct statement. Managers who only hear what is explicitly said may be missing the real picture. This can create tension for colleagues from cultures where direct feedback is the norm: a manager accustomed to hearing concerns stated plainly may assume silence means agreement, while Zambian employees may feel they have already communicated the issue through careful phrasing. In the other direction, a Zambian employee may find blunt feedback from an international colleague unnecessarily harsh when a more indirect approach would preserve dignity and still be understood.

Decision-making sits at the top, but Zambian culture expects a degree of consultation before decisions land. Subordinates do not expect to determine outcomes, but being consulted signals respect for their standing as members of the community. Correct privately and with care. Public criticism violates the communal values that underpin professional life, and individuals are unlikely to excel individually when the good of the group is more valued than standing out.
