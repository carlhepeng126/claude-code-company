# Sharing Information

## Working Together

Information flows freely among peers in Zambia, grounded in a long oral tradition where the spoken word has served as the main medium of knowledge exchange. Zambians are outgoing communicators who make the effort to share and explain. At the same time, openness depends on relational safety. In mixed-status settings, people calibrate what they say to avoid publicly embarrassing someone. Sensitive issues are best raised in private, where candor becomes possible without the risk of causing shame or undermining dignity. A colleague may hold back in a group meeting but speak frankly once the relationship feels secure and the setting is one-on-one.

Trust itself is often filtered through networks. Family, church, and community connections function as credibility markers. A message may land not only because of what it says, but because of who delivers it. A report arriving through a known contact may be accepted more readily than the same content from an unfamiliar source, regardless of formal authority. When building new relationships, expect some reserve at first. Demonstrating reliability and reciprocating openness helps move the exchange from polite to genuine.

Formal English is standard in professional settings, but tone still matters. A message can be structurally clear yet feel harsh if the delivery lacks warmth or respect. Soften language where needed, especially when raising concerns or correcting course. Politeness should also not be mistaken for agreement. Follow up gently to confirm understanding, and create informal channels where people can raise questions without exposure.

Zambia's regional position shapes how some professionals frame information. Cross-border trade and regional corridors are lived realities, and stakeholders may think in terms of connectivity beyond national boundaries. Acknowledging this broader geography can make conversations more relevant and grounded in the context people already navigate.
