# Negotiating

## Working Together

Credibility in negotiation rests on what can feasibly be delivered. Zambia's copper economy has lived through repeated cycles of boom and bust, and that history gives rise to a grounded attention to what will actually work under local conditions. Opening with realistic specifics and clear next steps builds confidence. Overpromising, even with enthusiasm, undermines trust quickly. Early conversations may probe constraints openly, and co-designing options around what is genuinely feasible is valued more than ambitious claims that later fall apart.

Relationships function as a stabilizing system when circumstances shift. Because conditions can change, people look for partners who stay steady when they do. Negotiation becomes an opportunity to build reputation over the long term, and continuity after the deal is signed is watched closely. Checking in, following through, and repairing honestly when something goes wrong all reinforce that you can be relied on when the environment gets uncertain. A counterpart from a culture where deals are finalized quickly and then left to run independently may read this relational attention as unnecessary maintenance, while a Zambian colleague may read the lack of follow-up as a sign that the partnership was transactional from the start.

Public confrontation reduces room for repair. Face is managed carefully, and firmness is most effective when it is wrapped in respect and delivered with a controlled tone. Pressure tactics in front of others raise the stakes and can make it harder to reach agreement. Raising your voice or showing frustration is read as poor form and can close down the conversation. Long silences during discussion usually mean the other person is genuinely considering the point, and rushing to fill the gap interrupts that process.

When trust needs to be built gradually, small pilots or staged milestones allow both sides to test the partnership without overcommitting at the start.
