# Business Entertaining

## Meetings & Presentations

Business entertaining in Zambia works best when hospitality is understood as care rather than spectacle. Inviting someone to share a meal is a relational gesture, and the attention and warmth you show as host often count for more than the venue itself. Offering food or tea is a form of respect, and the gesture communicates inclusion. For a visiting colleague arranging an event, this means prioritizing comfort and atmosphere over prestige. A relaxed setting where people feel at ease will usually be more effective than a formal or expensive one.

Food can also carry identity. Nshima, a maize-based porridge often paired with relish such as ifisashi (greens cooked with groundnuts), is widely seen as more than a staple. Serving it signals "you are with us," and receiving it appreciatively is part of the exchange. When food is offered, avoid evaluative comments or comparisons. Accept it as a gesture of belonging, not a dish to critique.

Variation in social norms is shaped strongly by faith and community. Colleagues may have different comfort levels around alcohol, late finishes, or venue type, and both approaches can be equally normal within their circles. Choose inclusive formats by default: make alcohol optional, allow for early departures, and avoid assuming one social map fits everyone. In settings where regional identity is salient, such as Lozi contexts with strong ceremonial traditions, aesthetics and dignity may be read more closely.

Let local counterparts guide venue choice and tone, especially when entertaining in their community. Early socializing should be low-pressure and easy to opt into or out of. The goal is connection, and that depends on making people comfortable, not on performing generosity.
