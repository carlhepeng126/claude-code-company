# Forms of Address

## Making a Good Impression

Address in Zambia works on a foundation of respect tied to age, seniority, and social standing. Titles such as "Mr.," "Mrs.," or professional designations like "Doctor" or "Pastor" are used with surnames on first meeting and should continue until the individual offers their first name. In many settings, showing respect also means adjusting your tone and pace, rather than relying solely on formal titles. A measured tone and a calm pace of speech read as courteous, especially with senior colleagues or older counterparts. Starting too casually can come across as socially rough, even when no offense is intended.

Greetings carry real weight. The handshake is standard, typically with the right hand, and placing your left hand on your right elbow as you shake is a widely recognized gesture of respect. In some regions, local handshake styles vary, and learning the appropriate form signals genuine regard. Taking the time to ask "How are you?" and wait for a reply is expected. Rushing this exchange or skipping the social preamble can feel abrupt, particularly during a first meeting.

Zambia's ethnolinguistic diversity means names and pronunciation carry identity cues. Bemba, Nyanja, Tonga, Lozi, and other language groups shape how people see themselves and each other. Making the effort to pronounce a colleague's name correctly and use their preferred form of address consistently helps people feel recognized in a multi-identity environment. In mixed groups, people may adjust which language they greet in depending on who is present, and this code-switching reflects belonging rather than formality alone.

Build warmth without collapsing respect. Joking familiarity too early, especially across generational or hierarchical lines, can unsettle the social order that underpins trust. If you are uncertain, mirror the most respectful norm in the room and let others set the pace toward informality.
