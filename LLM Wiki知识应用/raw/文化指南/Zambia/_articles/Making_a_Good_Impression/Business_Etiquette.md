# Business Etiquette

## Making a Good Impression

Professional courtesy in Zambia begins with greeting properly. What might feel like a formality elsewhere functions as social acknowledgment here, a way of confirming that you see the person in front of you and that they matter. Even in otherwise English-speaking offices, you'll often hear greetings in local languages like Nyanja (such as "Muli bwanji?") or expressions of appreciation like "Zikomo." Skipping this step can feel like skipping the person. The exchange also sets the tone for everything that follows, so the opening moments of an interaction deserve attention and warmth, not hurry.

Tone and approach in conversation are shaped by two overlapping commitments: protecting individual dignity and maintaining group cohesion. There is a strong preference not to embarrass anyone in front of others. Correction, disagreement, or criticism is often softened, deferred, or moved aside entirely to keep someone's standing intact. This reflects a broader social skill: treating harmony in the room as a shared responsibility. What reads to a colleague from a culture where directness is prized as evasiveness or vagueness is, from a Zambian perspective, a deliberate choice to preserve the relationship and the wider group. And the reverse can feel equally jarring: what reads as frankness to one person may land as harshness to another.

Christian moral language shapes much of public interaction, from blessing phrases to restraint in phrasing. The national constitution declares the country Christian while protecting freedom of religion, and this public culture influences how politeness is heard. Courtesy often registers as a moral posture, not just style, and harshness stands out more sharply as a result. Zambians are also used to switching language, tone, or references to suit the group in front of them, reflecting the "One Zambia, One Nation" ethos. In practice, this means etiquette often serves to protect unity in a multi-ethnic, multilingual environment, and jokes or comments that hinge on group identity can destabilize that balance.
