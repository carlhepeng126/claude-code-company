# Feedback and Coaching

## Managing Relationships

Direct criticism, especially in front of others, risks humiliation and can damage workplace relationships long term. Feedback in the DRC is shaped by respect for hierarchy, sensitivity to honor, and the need to protect social cohesion. A manager who corrects someone publicly may be seen as abusing their position rather than developing talent. Feedback is better delivered in private, framed relationally, and ideally through trusted intermediaries when the issue is sensitive.

Praise works differently depending on context. Public recognition for the team or group reinforces unity and shared achievement. Singling out an individual, however, can create awkwardness or resentment, particularly if it highlights performance gaps among peers. Positive feedback is most effective when it acknowledges contribution without isolating the person from the group.

Coaching rarely happens through formal systems alone. Mentorship is often embedded in personal networks and shaped by age, seniority, and kinship ties. A young colleague may quietly consult a relative, elder, or trusted senior before acting on feedback, especially if it affects family expectations or long-term reputation. Decisions that appear individual are often social, and input from beyond the workplace can influence whether advice is accepted or acted on.

Indirect language helps preserve dignity while encouraging change. A manager might reference a shared story, use a conditional phrase, or speak generally rather than naming the problem outright. This allows reflection without confrontation and gives the recipient room to adjust without losing face.
