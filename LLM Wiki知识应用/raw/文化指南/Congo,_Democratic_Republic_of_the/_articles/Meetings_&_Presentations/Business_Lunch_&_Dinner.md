# Business Lunch & Dinner

## Meetings & Presentations

Business meals in the Democratic Republic of Congo are built around relationship rather than agenda. These occasions offer rare, protected time to deepen trust and demonstrate personal regard in a context where formal structures are often fragile and personal connection determines whether collaboration moves forward. Launching into proposals or timeline discussions early in the meal can come across as transactional, suggesting the relationship exists only to serve the deal. Congolese colleagues are more likely to use the first part of the meal to understand who you are, how you relate to others, and whether you see them as more than a means to an end.

The meal itself unfolds at a measured pace. Courses arrive when the kitchen is ready, and conversation flows around family, shared acquaintances, and broader life circumstances before circling back to work. Rushing through dishes or signalling impatience with timing can suggest discomfort with the relationship itself. A colleague accustomed to faster service or tightly scheduled meals may read the slower rhythm as inefficiency, while a Congolese host may view impatience as a sign that the guest values speed over people.

Sharing food is a gesture of inclusion. Offering a taste from your plate or accepting one in return reinforces reciprocity and signals openness. Declining without explanation, especially if the offer is repeated, can feel like a small withdrawal. If dietary restrictions apply, it helps to explain them early and with warmth rather than as a firm boundary without context.

Alcohol may or may not be present depending on the setting and the individuals involved. If offered, accepting a drink can be part of the social fabric of the meal. If you prefer not to drink, a brief, friendly explanation is usually enough and rarely becomes an issue.
