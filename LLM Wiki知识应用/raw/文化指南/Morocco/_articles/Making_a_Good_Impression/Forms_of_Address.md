# Forms of Address

## Making a Good Impression

Moroccan address practices are shaped by a layered sensitivity to dignity, social position, and relational context. Titles and honorifics matter, especially in formal or status-conscious settings. Using someone's professional title alongside their surname is standard in business and institutional environments, and switching to first names happens only when invited. In more traditional or rural contexts, terms like *Lalla* (a respectful honorific for women with roots in regional, religious, and royal usage) can appear in everyday speech, signaling respect that goes beyond politeness. Greeting everyone present individually when you enter or leave a meeting reflects a social ethic of acknowledgement, not formality for its own sake.

Language choice itself functions as a form of address. Morocco's multilingual reality means that opening in French, Modern Standard Arabic, or Darija (Moroccan Arabic) can signal different things about your institutional position, educational background, or regional belonging before any content is exchanged. A colleague who assumes French is neutral may be read as distanced or elite-coded in settings where Darija or Amazigh languages are the everyday register. Paying attention to which language your counterparts use first, and matching that register where possible, shows you are reading the social context rather than imposing your own.

Beyond formal titles, kinship terms such as *khouya* (brother) or *khti* (sister) are common in neighborhoods, smaller towns, and service interactions, creating warmth and social closeness without actual family ties. This is especially typical in less corporate settings and varies by region and age. When meeting a colleague for the first time, wait for cues on physical greeting. Men typically shake hands with men, and women with women. If you are a man meeting a woman, let her extend her hand first. Handshakes tend to be lighter in grip than in some other cultures, reflecting courtesy rather than hesitation.
