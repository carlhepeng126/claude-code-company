# Sharing Information

## Working Together

Credibility in Morocco often flows through relationships before it reaches documents. Information accepted through a trusted contact or mutual connection can move faster and land with more weight than what appears in an email or formal report. People tend to assess reliability based on who vouches for someone, what is known about their background, and whether the pathway itself feels secure. If you are presenting data or recommendations to a new colleague or stakeholder, introducing it through a shared contact or in a setting where trust has already been built can make the difference between polite acknowledgment and genuine engagement.

Morocco's oral tradition continues to hold cultural prestige, reflected most vividly in Jemaa el-Fna, recognized as intangible heritage for its living storytelling and public speech. This reinforces a broader comfort with information conveyed through voice, presence, and narrative coherence rather than solely through written form. Spoken explanation often persuades more effectively than a slide deck alone. A colleague from a context where written documentation anchors credibility may find this reliance on oral exchange frustratingly informal, while a Moroccan counterpart may read an over-reliance on written briefs as impersonal or disengaged from the human dimension of the exchange.

Hchouma (shame norms) can make disclosure feel reputationally risky, especially when sharing might expose a mistake, internal disagreement, or uncertainty. People may manage what they reveal carefully to protect public image in a society where social judgment circulates quickly. This means information may be shared selectively, especially early in a relationship or in hierarchical settings. Creating opportunities for private, low-stakes conversation can encourage more open exchange than formal meetings where visibility is high.

Language choice also shapes how information is received. Darija conveys intimacy and directness, French often signals institutional authority or elite positioning, and Modern Standard Arabic can lend formality or seriousness. The same message delivered in different registers may be heard as more personal, more official, or more defensible depending on audience and context.
