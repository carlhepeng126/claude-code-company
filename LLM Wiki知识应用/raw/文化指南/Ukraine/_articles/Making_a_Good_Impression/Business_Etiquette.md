# Business Etiquette

## Making a Good Impression

Ukrainian business etiquette grows from a cultural foundation that prizes sincerity over polish. Shaped by Slavic respect norms and a history where dignity (hidnist) had to be defended rather than assumed, professional interactions tend to be direct but warm, measured but genuine. Formality exists, but it's the kind that expresses respect rather than distance. What's valued is clear intention, reliable follow-through, and the sense that you see the person across the table as an equal worthy of honest engagement.

This means politeness is expected, but the superficial kind backfires. A constant smile without substance can read as insincere. Steady eye contact and a calm demeanor signal credibility. When greeting, a firm handshake works well, but avoid shaking hands across a doorway, a superstition still observed and noticed when overlooked. Once rapport develops, conversations may feel closer in both tone and physical proximity than colleagues from more reserved professional cultures might expect. A light touch on the arm during a point, particularly among men, is not unusual. Women may experience more formal distance in cross-gender settings unless warmth has clearly been established.

Public criticism or dismissiveness can do lasting damage. Given Ukraine's history of occupation and the fight for recognition, arrogance or belittling behavior is read not just as poor manners but as a deeper lack of respect. Feedback should be given privately and framed constructively. Attention to cultural symbols also builds trust quickly. Even a basic greeting in Ukrainian, such as Dobryi den (Good day) or Dyakuyu (Thank you), signals respect for the language and, by extension, for national identity. Some professionals, particularly older colleagues or those in certain regions, may still use Russian, but Ukrainian is increasingly the preferred and safer choice in professional life.

Gift-giving is modest but meaningful. Small gestures, chocolate or something from your own country, work well once a relationship has been established. If bringing flowers, choose an odd number; even numbers are reserved for funerals. Lavish gifts create discomfort rather than goodwill. Business cards should include Ukrainian on one side, presented face-up as a quiet gesture of cultural consideration. Many professionals will also want to connect digitally, so be ready to exchange LinkedIn details or messaging apps alongside the card.
