# Negotiating

## Working Together

Trust comes first. Ukrainian negotiators look for sincerity and fairness before committing, a reflection of historical experiences with exploitation that made dignity and respectful treatment central to how deals are done. A structured proposal backed by clear data helps, particularly in sectors like IT, engineering, and logistics where efficiency blends with relational awareness. But numbers alone won't close the agreement if the personal connection feels absent or rushed. Beginning with genuine rapport and transparent intentions sets the ground for substantive discussions to follow.

Direct disagreement is common and expected when framed respectfully. Rooted in Cossack and Slavic traditions that valued pragmatic compromise and personal honour, Ukrainian negotiators often speak plainly, test positions, and push back when terms feel unbalanced. A colleague from a culture where indirect language softens objections might read this directness as confrontational, while a Ukrainian counterpart might interpret overly polite hedging as evasive or insincere. Naming concerns openly and responding to them directly keeps the process moving.

Regional variation shows up in how negotiations unfold. Western colleagues may bring expressive energy to the table, central regions tend toward directness tempered with courtesy, southern regions often use humour to ease tension, and eastern regions lean practical and technical. Younger or internationally experienced professionals may favor collaborative, win-win structures, while others apply pressure tactics or compare offers to gauge your commitment. Holding your position calmly and avoiding early concessions reinforces credibility.

The process takes time. Consultation with internal teams, partners, or family may be needed before agreement, and revisiting earlier points doesn't signal backtracking but thoroughness. Document what's agreed as you go to avoid ambiguity later.
