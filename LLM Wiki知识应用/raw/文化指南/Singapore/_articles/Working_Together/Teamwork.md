# Teamwork

## Working Together

Teams in Singapore are usually diverse by design. It is common for a single project group to bring together Chinese, Malay, Indian, and "Others," alongside colleagues from several foreign nationalities. This mix is familiar and expected, but it comes with a practical sensitivity around language. Using Singlish or Mandarin in short bursts is normal and often builds rapport, but extended side conversations in a language that excludes others will alienate teammates quickly. The expectation is that shared work happens in a language everyone can follow.

Many Singaporeans bring a strong achievement drive shaped by the country's high-stakes education system, which streams students early and rewards top performance. That same system also leaves some people carrying anxiety about being publicly ranked or labeled as second-tier. Introducing explicit A/B/C performance rankings or calling out "slow" team members in front of others can reopen old wounds. Recognition tends to work better when it highlights effort and growth across the team rather than singling out top scorers in ways that echo classroom hierarchies.

For male colleagues who have completed National Service or continue reservist training with the Singapore Armed Forces, Police, or Civil Defence, those bonds often become strong informal networks inside and across organizations. In-camp training is a non-negotiable commitment, and supporting colleagues through it builds real loyalty. Treating ICT as an inconvenience rather than an obligation will damage trust.

Harmony within the team is maintained through small, regular rituals rather than forced intimacy. Friday hawker lunches, festive treats, or shared birthday cakes help knit a highly diverse group together without overstepping the boundaries that many prefer to keep between work and personal life.
