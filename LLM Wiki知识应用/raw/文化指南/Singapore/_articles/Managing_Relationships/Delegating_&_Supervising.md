# Delegating & Supervising

## Managing Relationships

Delegating effectively in Singapore means understanding how hierarchy and meritocracy coexist. Authority comes with formal position, but it must be earned through demonstrated competence. A manager who cannot show technical knowledge or clear thinking will struggle to command genuine deference, even if their title suggests otherwise. This dual expectation shapes how teams respond to delegation: they will follow direction, but they are also quietly assessing whether the person giving it knows what they are doing.

Instructions need to be comprehensive and unambiguous. Asking questions can suggest that the manager has not explained clearly, so team members often stay silent even when uncertain. This reticence reflects Confucian values of respect and group harmony, not passivity. To work around this, build in structured check-ins at early stages of a task. These create a face-saving way to surface misunderstandings without implying criticism of the original briefing.

Face governs all feedback. Public correction, even mild, causes real harm. All sensitive input belongs in private conversation, with full attention to preserving dignity. Similarly, apparent agreement in meetings may mean only that someone has heard you. Silence often indicates discomfort rather than consent. Create informal channels where team members can raise concerns without risking embarrassment.

Singapore's workforce is genuinely multicultural. Chinese Singaporeans may lean toward greater hierarchy and indirectness; Malay Singaporeans bring Islamic values of communal respect; Indian Singaporeans contribute their own frameworks. Managing well means recognizing that these differences exist within the same office, not just across borders.
