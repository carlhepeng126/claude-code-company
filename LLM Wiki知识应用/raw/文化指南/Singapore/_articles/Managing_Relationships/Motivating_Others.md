# Motivating Others

## Managing Relationships

In Singapore, what motivates people at work reflects a tight balance between stability and competitive edge. Many employees draw energy from being seen as competent professionals who also lead respectable, stable lives: owning a home, supporting aging parents, giving children opportunities. This is how status is built and recognized. A manager who understands this will frame opportunities around both professional growth and long-term security, not just one or the other. Promotions, certifications, and projects that lift someone's market standing all speak to this dual drive.

The idea of *kiasu*, often glossed as "fear of losing out," can be channeled into drive rather than treated as anxiety to manage. Many Singaporeans take quiet pride in staying competitive as individuals and as a nation. Offering access to training budgets, regional exposure, or rotations that keep someone's CV sharp taps into this energy. People want development paths that are credible and visible, not vague promises of future possibility. When the route forward is clear and tied to real skill-building, motivation follows naturally.

Especially among younger professionals, tangible impact matters as much as salary. Showing how a project improves a user's experience, supports citizens, or contributes to something new can spark genuine engagement. This connects to a broader civic pride that sits quietly beneath the surface. A colleague used to fast results may read this focus on meaning and contribution as idealistic, while a Singaporean counterpart might view a purely transactional approach as short-sighted or disconnected from the work's real value.

Respecting work-life boundaries also sustains motivation over time. People are more committed when they feel their family and health are not being sacrificed for performance theater. Flexibility that allows someone to meet personal responsibilities without guilt builds loyalty and long-term dedication.
