# Managing Suppliers & Customers

## Managing Relationships

Singapore's commercial environment is shaped by regulatory presence, a small and connected market, and customer expectations set by high state service standards. In finance, telco, healthcare and transport, agencies like MAS, IMDA, MOH and LTA function as ever-present reference points. Suppliers who fail to demonstrate awareness of these regulatory requirements and local standards risk being dismissed early. Generic global presentations lack credibility here. When pitching or scoping work, show that you understand what compliance means in practice and where the lines are drawn.

Singapore is often described as a village in a skyscraper. Word travels quickly about supplier failures, bad customer experiences and contract disputes. A few high-profile mistakes can close doors across the ecosystem, including with organizations you have not yet approached. Treat every contract as if it is visible to regulators, social media and potential future partners. This is not a market that separates behavior from reputation. Relationships with suppliers and customers are personal and professional at the same time. Trust is earned through consistent delivery, transparency and speed of response when issues arise.

Singaporean customers, whether corporate or individual, are used to efficient government digital services, reliable transport and clean public infrastructure. Private providers are compared to that standard, often implicitly. Customers here are price-sensitive and will leave quickly if service reliability drops. After-sales support and communication during problems are expected to be prompt and clear. For foreign suppliers, this means investing in local responsiveness and follow-through, not just competitive pricing at the point of sale. Pay local SMEs and freelancers on time. Stories of late payment or scope creep spread fast through small business networks and can affect your standing well beyond a single transaction.
