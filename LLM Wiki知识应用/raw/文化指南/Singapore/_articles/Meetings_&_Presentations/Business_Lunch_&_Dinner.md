# Business Lunch & Dinner

## Meetings & Presentations

Strong working relationships in Singapore are often built over hawker centre lunches as much as hotel restaurants. A $6 meal at a shared table in an industrial canteen can build trust just as effectively as a corporate buffet. Knowing how to navigate trays, reserve a seat with a tissue packet (known locally as *tissue-chope*), and eat efficiently in a crowded space shows local literacy and signals genuine engagement with the culture.

Singapore's religious and ethnic diversity shapes every meal choice. In a typical team, you may have Muslim colleagues who eat only halal food, Hindus who avoid beef, Buddhists who avoid certain meats, and vegetarians. People will usually adapt quietly rather than voice discomfort, but they notice who thinks ahead when choosing a venue. When you organize a meal, check halal certification and menu range so that everyone can eat, not just attend. Leaving a colleague to drink Milo while others order freely doesn't build goodwill.

Working lunches with bento or packet meals are common in fast-paced sectors. They keep things moving and are understood as functional, not a replacement for all social meals. If you lead one, keep the agenda tight and let people eat. Talking non-stop while everyone is chewing wastes the meal and the time. Lunch hours are often compressed, and a stated 12 to 2 window may really mean 12:30 to 1:30 once travel is factored in.

Singaporeans appreciate genuine curiosity about local food but dislike having it exoticized. Trying unfamiliar dishes without theatrics or commentary builds rapport. Treating the meal as a performance does the opposite.
