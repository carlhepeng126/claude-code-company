# Business Etiquette

## Making a Good Impression

In Filipino professional life, smoothness in relationships comes before efficiency in transactions. The cultural practice of *pakikisama* (maintaining harmony and group cohesion in everyday interactions) shapes how people greet, respond, and negotiate. Small courtesies like warm greetings, expressions of gratitude, and attention to tone build trust early and anchor partnerships later. A counterpart who moves straight to agenda items without establishing rapport may seem efficient elsewhere, but here the relationship itself is doing foundational work. The same principle applies in reverse: a Filipino colleague working in a culture where business opens with minimal preamble may quietly read that directness as coldness, even when none is intended.

Filipinos communicate indirectly to protect relationships and avoid public embarrassment. The word "no" is rarely used outright. Instead, responses like *mamaya* (later today) or *saka na* (sometime later) soften refusal without stating it directly. A colleague may cancel a meeting by being unavailable rather than declining face to face. If something needs to be challenged or discussed, move it to a smaller setting or a private message so that dignity stays intact. What appears as vagueness is often careful relational management, rooted partly in historical layers of authority where speaking openly in mixed-status settings carried risk.

Physical closeness and touch are normal among colleagues of the same gender. Handshakes may be softer than in other contexts, and a pat on the arm signals warmth. Facial expressions carry meaning: raised eyebrows without a smile can mean no, while raised eyebrows with a smile greet a friend. Lips may be pursed to point instead of using a finger. A smile does not always mean agreement; it can mask embarrassment or discomfort, so confirm gently rather than assuming alignment. Raising your voice, even in frustration, breaks the norm of calm, controlled tone and can damage your standing quickly.

Gift exchanges mark the progression of a relationship. At Christmas, gifts are expected across a wide circle, from colleagues to suppliers. After signing a contract, a modest gift completes the relationship rather than initiating it. Timing and context separate courtesy from pressure. Gifts are not opened in front of the giver, and an invitation to a home calls for flowers, sweets, or chocolates, not alcohol or food unless it's a regional specialty.
