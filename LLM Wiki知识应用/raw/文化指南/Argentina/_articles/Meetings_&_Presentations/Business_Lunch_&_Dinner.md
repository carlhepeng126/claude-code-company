# Business Lunch & Dinner

## Meetings & Presentations

A business lunch in Argentina is rarely just about the agenda. Conversations can start with a long discussion of economic instability, political scandals, or the latest football drama before circling back to the work at hand. This is how people assess your character and test whether you can be trusted. A colleague who pushes for business talk too early may come across as transactional. The meal itself is a working session, but also a form of shared venting in a context where economic uncertainty is constant. Let the lunch breathe.

Traditional business meals are often heavy on meat, pasta, and bread — comfort food in a country where stability is rare. A parrilla (grill) lunch or a plate of milanesa is more than sustenance; it reflects shared identity and a way of coping. Health trends are visible in urban areas and among younger professionals, but many still see a business meal as a chance to eat well, not to restrict yourself. If you have dietary restrictions, state them early and simply, without framing them as moral choices. Openness to local classics will be noticed.

Who pays can be surprisingly loaded. Inflation makes picking up the whole bill a genuine financial gesture, not just a symbolic one. Splitting can signal equality or pragmatism; insisting on paying can come across as generous or slightly performative, depending on context. If you invited, be prepared to pay or suggest splitting directly with a simple "Shall we split?" to avoid awkwardness.

Business dinners regularly start after 9pm and can end near midnight. Yet many of the same people will begin work early the next morning, often after a long commute. Avoid scheduling high-stakes meetings the morning after a late dinner. Build in margin.
