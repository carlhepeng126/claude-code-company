# Time Management

## Making a Good Impression

Time in Argentina is elastic by design, shaped by decades of economic and political turbulence that have taught people to plan around disruption rather than rely on predictability. Currency shocks, strikes, sudden regulation changes and transport blockages blow up carefully laid schedules with enough regularity that the calendar becomes aspirational. This is structural, not a lack of discipline. People internalize that holding too tightly to a timeline can mean wasting energy on something a crisis will unravel anyway.

Language reflects this flexibility. Words like *ahora* (now), *ya* (already) and *en un rato* (in a bit) stretch to fit the situation. *Ahora* can mean immediately or sometime soon, once the current chaos settles. Context and tone tell you which. If you hear "now" in a planning conversation, clarify gently without making people feel policed: "Do you mean today, this week, or when the strike ends?" This shows you understand the environment, not that you doubt commitment.

Argentina runs on two clocks. Socially, events start late and run long. In top-tier multinationals and export-facing firms, there is more emphasis on on-time delivery and global calls, but local disruptions still seep in. Build contingency time into projects for elections, protests and currency volatility. For global coordination, lock critical milestones to external anchors like regulatory deadlines or shipments, not just internal wishes, and over-communicate time expectations.

Long, tangential meetings sometimes substitute for missing structure. People use them to process uncertainty, reassure each other and negotiate priorities in real time. Summarizing periodically helps: "So far we've agreed A, B, C." This lets people see progress despite the sprawl.
