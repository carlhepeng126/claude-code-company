# Business Etiquette

## Making a Good Impression

Being *simpático* (warm, good company, easy to like) is almost a civic virtue in Argentina. People expect a bit of charm, humour and emotional intelligence even in formal business settings. A very cold, purely transactional style feels *antipático* and untrustworthy. Argentines want to know who they're dealing with, not just which company, so expect to be tested on your story, your values, perhaps your views on football. The person behind the proposal matters as much as the proposal itself. This means bringing some of your human self into the room: stories, moderate self-critique, a willingness to join in conversation that ranges beyond the contract. For colleagues from cultures where business personas are deliberately neutral, this can feel exposing. For Argentines, a colleague who keeps their personality sealed off may seem guarded or fake.

Warmth coexists with directness. It's normal to combine politeness with open complaints about politics, inflation, taxes or bosses. Criticising "the system" is part of the social glue, and people watch how you respond. Joining in moderate system-bashing signals empathy with everyday frustrations, not political alignment. At the same time, Argentines are uncomfortable with overt deference. Overly submissive behaviour toward a boss or foreign visitor reads as *careta* (fake) or even colonial. The ideal is informal equality with strong personalities. Treat everyone with collegial respect, including receptionists and junior staff. Argentines notice and dislike classist attitudes.

You'll also encounter *viveza criolla*, a cultural script around being street-smart: knowing how to navigate bureaucracy, bend rules that seem absurd, and exploit gaps without seeing oneself as corrupt. Admiration and resentment toward this behaviour coexist. Know your non-negotiable ethical lines, but don't treat every workaround locals mention as moral collapse. Ask why they do it, and listen to the logic beneath the manoeuvre.
