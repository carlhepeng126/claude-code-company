# Forms of Address

## Making a Good Impression

Argentine business culture moves quickly to first names, often within the first conversation. The shift reflects a relational warmth that runs through professional life. Formality that continues too long can feel stiff or distrusting. Once rapport begins, you may hear affectionate diminutives like Pablito for Pablo or Caro for Carolina, used comfortably even in meetings. If colleagues are introducing themselves by first name and you continue using titles or surnames, you risk creating unnecessary distance in a setting where closeness builds credibility.

Professional titles still have real weight, especially in law, medicine, academia, and the public sector. A colleague who introduces herself as Doctora or Ingeniero has earned that recognition and expects it to be acknowledged, at least in early interactions. These titles reflect years of study and a social standing that has often been fought for. In sectors where titles are less emphasized, they may fade after the first exchange, but when someone uses theirs consistently, mirror it until they explicitly invite you to drop it.

There is growing awareness of gender-inclusive language in Buenos Aires and other urban centers, particularly among younger professionals and in progressive organizations. Traditional masculine defaults and macho assumptions are increasingly questioned in these settings. In more conservative or rural contexts, older forms persist. Pay attention to how people introduce themselves and the language they use around gender and identity, especially in mixed-generation teams where expectations may vary.

Start with Señor, Señora, or a relevant professional title paired with the surname until the other person signals a shift. Once they use your first name or offer theirs, follow. The transition is usually quick and mutual.
