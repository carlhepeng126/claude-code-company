# Making Contacts & Scheduling Appointments

## Making a Good Impression

In Argentina, business relationships start with trust built over coffee, not contracts sent over email. Shaped by decades of economic and political uncertainty, Argentines look for people who will stand by them when things go sideways, not just when the deal looks good. That means first meetings often feel more like exploratory conversations than formal pitches. Expect talk about football, politics, family, or current events before anyone turns to the project itself. This is not a detour from business. It's how Argentines assess whether you share their values and whether they can count on you when circumstances shift. A warm introduction through a mutual contact, a lawyer, an accountant, or someone in a local network, will open doors faster than the most polished cold email.

The pace and style of making contacts varies across the country. In Buenos Aires, relationships can form quickly but remain fluid. People are alert, direct, and used to navigating change. In the provinces, the process tends to move more slowly, with greater emphasis on family name, local reputation, and political connections. Understanding who influences decisions, including union networks or regional stakeholders, becomes especially important outside the capital. If you're working in a smaller city or rural area, take time to map those relationships early and let introductions unfold at their own pace.

Schedule appointments well in advance and reconfirm a day or two before. Be on time yourself, but understand that your Argentine counterpart may arrive late, particularly if they hold a senior position. Working days often extend into the evening, so meetings scheduled for 7pm or later are not unusual. Avoid planning initial trips in January or February, when many people are on holiday. Once you're in the country, showing up in person without an appointment can sometimes work, especially if there's already some rapport. The phone system was unreliable for years, and that habit of flexibility around unannounced visits has persisted in many contexts.
