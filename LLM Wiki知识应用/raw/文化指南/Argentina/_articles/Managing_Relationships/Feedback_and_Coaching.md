# Feedback and Coaching

## Managing Relationships

In Argentina, feedback lives in a space between affectionate bluntness and deep emotional awareness. In cities like Buenos Aires, where therapy is common and widely normalized, people are comfortable analyzing motives, childhood influences, and the ways social context shapes behavior. A feedback conversation can easily expand beyond the immediate issue into broader reflections about personal history or how economic crises have affected someone's work. This psychological fluency means Argentines often want to understand the full human picture, not just the performance gap.

Among colleagues who trust each other, feedback can be remarkably direct and colorful. Phrases like "esto es un desastre" (this is a disaster) or "estás colgadísimo" (you're really out of it) are used freely, but they are wrapped in warmth, humor, and clear signals of personal regard. The sting is allowed because the relationship is strong. For a manager from a culture where directness is softened with formal language or where critique is carefully staged, this style can feel abrupt or even unprofessional. From the Argentine side, overly cautious or indirect feedback can feel cold or evasive, as though the manager does not trust the relationship enough to be honest.

Underneath this openness runs a deeper sensitivity shaped by historical memory. Abusive authority, whether from the dictatorship era or exploitative bosses, has left a cultural wariness of humiliation and top-down control. Public criticism, threats tied to job security, or feedback that feels dismissive of someone's circumstances can trigger strong resistance, not just to the message but to the power structure behind it. Argentines hold a rich vocabulary around injustice, exploitation, and dignity, and if feedback feels unfair or disconnected from lived reality, it will be framed politically, as one more instance of being stepped on.

Effective coaching in Argentina anchors critique in respect and belief in the person's potential. Saying "Esto no está a tu nivel, y justamente porque sé lo que podés hacer, quiero que lo veamos juntos" (This isn't at your level, and precisely because I know what you can do, I want us to look at it together) combines high standards with emotional presence. Keep strong criticism private and calm. Give room, within clear limits, for people to name the context they are managing, such as inflation or family pressures, while helping them separate what they can control. Coaching that connects improvement to collective pride or historical narratives can be powerful: "This team can be the example everyone cites when they say 'in Argentina, we still know how to do things well.'"
