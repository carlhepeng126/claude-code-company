# Sharing Information

## Working Together

In Argentina, every official communication is quietly tested against a parallel question: what is *la posta*, the real story? This instinct to cross-check comes from decades of broken promises around pensions, savings, and job guarantees. People have learned to listen for what is vague, what is missing, and who stands to gain. The official version is rarely assumed to hold the whole truth. Instead, employees look to corridor whispers, group texts, union representatives, or a brother-in-law at AFIP (the tax agency) to piece together what is actually happening. If you want information to land, plan for the fact that it will be interrogated and that an alternative narrative will almost certainly form alongside it.

That tendency to doubt becomes sharper when corporate language echoes the polarized tone of Argentine media and political discourse. TV news, radio shows, and social media are saturated with competing narratives, and employees instinctively filter company messages through that lens. Does this sound like government X, party Y, or the kind of spin we have learned to distrust? Avoid slogans or upbeat generalizations that feel like propaganda. Plain, non-propagandistic language works better, even when the news itself is difficult. Transparent bad news often builds more credibility than cheerful spin, because it suggests you are not trying to manage perception.

To counter parallel narratives, create spaces where people can question, challenge, and share what they have heard elsewhere. A PDF announcement on its own will not be enough. Hold meetings where doubt can surface and be addressed directly. Say explicitly what you do and do not know yet, and what could still change. Work with union representatives and informal influencers to align facts, even if interpretations remain different. This approach acknowledges that information in Argentina is not simply transmitted; it is negotiated, tested, and only trusted once it has survived scrutiny from multiple sources.
