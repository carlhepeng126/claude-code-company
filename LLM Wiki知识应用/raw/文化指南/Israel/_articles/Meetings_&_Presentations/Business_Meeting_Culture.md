# Business Meeting Culture

## Meetings & Presentations

Meetings in Israel are participatory and direct. Everyone in the room is expected to contribute, regardless of rank. Sitting quietly and deferring to the most senior person suggests you have nothing to offer. The sabra ideal — a cultural emphasis on directness and a flat social register — carries into the meeting room. Hierarchy exists and is respected, but it doesn't determine who speaks. If you have a view, offer it. Waiting to be invited or deferring to seniority reads as disengagement. Presentations that invite no discussion or challenge can be seen as weak. Come prepared to defend your position and engage in real-time debate.

Small talk happens but stays brief. Israelis move quickly to substance. The relationship-building happens through the work itself, through open debate and testing ideas, rather than through extended preliminaries. A colleague from a culture where small talk establishes trust before business begins may find this abruptness unsettling. From the Israeli side, extended pleasantries before getting to the point can feel inauthentic or evasive.

Interruptions are common and rarely personal. Israelis may take phone calls during meetings, answer questions addressed to someone else, or have uninvited guests walk in. The culture assumes people can manage multiple tasks at once, and informality governs most professional settings. If a deal is reached, follow up in writing. Business may feel done on a handshake, but a formal contract still matters.
