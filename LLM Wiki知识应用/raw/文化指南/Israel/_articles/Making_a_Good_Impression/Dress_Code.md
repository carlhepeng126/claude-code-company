# Dress Code

## Making a Good Impression

Israeli professional dress leans casual by global standards, and this preference runs deeper than simple comfort. The sabra ideal, which values authenticity and directness, extends to appearance. Overdressing can suggest you have misread the setting or are prioritizing form over substance. In most business environments, smart casual is the norm. Suits appear mainly in banking, law, and senior government roles. Elsewhere, they are uncommon enough to stand out.

The tech sector takes this further. In Tel Aviv startups, jeans, t-shirts, and trainers in meetings with senior executives are genuinely normal, reflecting a Silicon Valley-style informality. Arriving in a suit to such a meeting may create distance rather than credibility, signaling unfamiliarity with the environment. If you are meeting a company for the first time and are unsure of the setting, smart casual with the option to adjust downward is a safer starting point than formal tailoring.

Context matters considerably. Jerusalem tends toward greater formality than Tel Aviv, and government, finance, and legal settings expect more traditional business dress. Even within casual sectors, religious or cultural sites require modesty. Women visiting ultra-Orthodox areas or places like the Western Wall should cover arms and legs, and men should wear long trousers and closed shoes.

The practical read is straightforward: ask in advance, observe the sector, and match the setting. Demonstrating that you understand the local tone will serve you better than defaulting to formal conventions from elsewhere.
