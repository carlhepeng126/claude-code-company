# Delegating & Supervising

## Managing Relationships

Authority in Germany is earned through demonstrated expertise, a legacy of the *Bildung* tradition that shaped 19th-century professional ideals. That framework held that genuine education develops technical depth, ethical character, and intellectual rigor together. Today, the respected manager is one who has truly mastered their domain. A senior leader who cannot speak credibly to the substantive details of their field will find their authority questioned, not openly challenged but persistently tested. This means delegation happens within clearly defined areas of competence. You assign tasks based on what people are trained to do, and you respect the boundary of their professional judgment once the work is theirs.

Germany's post-war constitutional settlement embedded worker participation directly into the economic structure. The *Mitbestimmung* (co-determination) system gives workers formal rights on supervisory boards in larger companies, and works councils (*Betriebsräte*) hold legally enforceable consultation rights over many management decisions. This is not a courtesy or a morale exercise. It is a legal requirement that shapes how managers must operate. A foreign manager who informs the works council after a decision has been made, rather than consulting them genuinely beforehand, creates both legal exposure and lasting trust damage. Managing around the council rather than with it will be read as a fundamental misunderstanding of how German organizations function.

Feedback and disagreement are expressed directly. Ambiguity is seen as poor thinking, and clarity as professional respect. A colleague from a culture where feedback is softened or implicit may hear German directness as blunt or even harsh. From the German side, overly cushioned criticism can seem evasive or patronizing, leaving the actual issue unresolved. If you supervise in Germany, be specific about what needs to change and why. If you receive feedback, respond in kind with substance, not defensiveness.
