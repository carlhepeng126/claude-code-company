# Feedback and Coaching

## Managing Relationships

Feedback in Germany comes direct and task-focused. The roots lie partly in the apprenticeship tradition, where a *Meister* (master craftsperson) corrected errors on the spot to ensure quality and safety. That straightforward style has carried into modern workplaces. Managers give clear, often blunt observations about what went wrong or needs changing, and they see this as responsible and respectful. The assumption is that naming a problem helps fix it, and avoiding honesty wastes everyone's time. When a German colleague tells you the report was late and incomplete, they are addressing the work, not questioning your competence. Emotional cushioning is minimal, but the intention is usually constructive. To work well with this, respond to feedback factually rather than defensively, ask for specifics if the point is unclear, and recognize that directness here is a form of care, not hostility.

German grading culture sets a strict benchmark. In school and in performance reviews, an "OK" may already mean "good," and praise is measured out carefully to keep standards sharp. Over-praising is avoided because it can dilute meaning and lower expectations. A manager saying "very good" is giving high praise; a neutral "that's fine" often signals approval. For colleagues from cultures where positive reinforcement is frequent and explicit, this can feel cold or discouraging. From the German side, lavish praise without clear justification can seem hollow or manipulative. When giving feedback in Germany, be specific and evidence-based. When receiving it, read the grade in context and look for what is being affirmed, not just what is being corrected.

Serious criticism is usually given privately. While Germans are direct, they are also conscious of not humiliating people in front of others. A manager will raise a significant issue in a one-on-one meeting or the appropriate forum, not across an open-plan office. Coaching conversations focus on concrete improvement steps and clear targets. People respond well to knowing exactly what success looks like and what they need to adjust. Frame development in terms of measurable outcomes rather than personal growth narratives.
