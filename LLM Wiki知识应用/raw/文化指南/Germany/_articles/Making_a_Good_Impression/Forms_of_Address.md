# Forms of Address

## Making a Good Impression

The formal register in German business settings reflects a culture that separates professional and personal life, and where hierarchy is respected through language itself. Most colleagues use the formal pronoun *Sie* with each other, paired with *Herr* (Mr.) or *Frau* (Mrs.) and the surname, even after years of working together. This is especially true when addressing those senior to you. The informal *Du* is used only when explicitly invited, and the invitation typically comes from the more senior person. Moving to first names and *Du* before that point risks appearing presumptuous or disrespectful.

Professional titles hold weight. Addressing someone as *Doktor* or *Professor* acknowledges their qualifications and the effort behind them. Omitting a title when one exists can be read as a slight, particularly in sectors such as engineering, law, or academia. If you are unsure whether someone holds a doctoral degree, their email signature or business card will usually clarify.

Handshakes remain the standard greeting in business contexts: firm, brief, and accompanied by eye contact. Being introduced by a third party is preferred, but introducing yourself is acceptable if no one else is present. The formality extends into written communication, where openings and closings follow established conventions and tone remains measured even in routine email exchanges.

The expectation shifts in international teams. When a German colleague joins a team that operates in English, the cultural default often adjusts. Many German professionals will adopt first names and a less formal register when working in cross-cultural settings, recognizing that the linguistic markers of hierarchy do not translate directly. Even so, if you are working with a German team domestically or in German, defaulting to *Sie* and surnames shows awareness of the professional culture and keeps the relationship on appropriate footing.
