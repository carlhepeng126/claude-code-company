# Negotiating

## Working Together

German commercial culture is built on the principle of *Kaufmannsehre* (merchant's honor), the expectation that agreements are fair and commitments kept. A reputation for reliability often outweighs short-term financial gains, which is why long-term supplier and customer relationships spanning decades are common, especially among Mittelstand firms. This makes trustworthiness a negotiating asset in itself. If you are known to deliver what you promise, that history will strengthen your position more than aggressive tactics or last-minute concessions.

Negotiations zoom into detail. Expect discussions to move clause by clause through contracts, service levels, and technical specifications. Security comes from clarity, so cost breakdowns, legal constraints, and objective criteria such as benchmarks or lifecycle costs will be scrutinized closely. Vague assurances or appeals to personal relationships carry little weight. If your proposal relies on phrases like "because headquarters said so" without underlying rationale, it will be considered weak. Build your case around standards, measurable constraints, and verifiable data.

The style is principled rather than theatrical. German negotiators argue from logic and costs, and aggressive brinkmanship or artificial deadlines risk being read as manipulation. A counterpart from a culture where high-energy persuasion or last-minute pressure is common may find the tone flat or overly technical. From the German side, the same techniques can seem disrespectful, as though the other party is unwilling to engage seriously with the substance of the deal.

Allow time for this process. Line-by-line contract review is standard, and decisions require internal consultation. Proposals that emphasize long-term partnership and a proven track record will resonate more than those framed as one-off wins.
