# Sharing Information

## Working Together

Germans keep detailed records. Meeting minutes, email summaries, and notes to file (Aktennotizen) are standard practice, serving as both memory aids and risk management tools. If a decision is later questioned, the documentation provides evidence of what was agreed and who committed to what. This habit reflects a risk-avoiding culture where accountability is taken seriously. For colleagues from environments where verbal agreements are trusted to hold, the insistence on written confirmation can feel overly cautious or even mistrustful. For Germans working with partners who rely on conversation alone, the absence of a paper trail can feel uncomfortably vague. Following up meetings with a brief written summary listing decisions, responsibilities, and deadlines builds confidence on both sides.

There is a clear line between fact and opinion. Germans distinguish sharply between confirmed data (Zahlen, Gesetze) and personal views (Meinung). Mixing the two without signaling which is which undermines credibility quickly. When presenting, label assumptions and hypotheses clearly rather than blending them with verified information. This precision is valued as professional rigor, and it helps colleagues assess proposals on solid ground.

Bad news is expected early. Withholding problems until they become critical is seen as a breach of trust. Bringing up difficulties promptly, along with proposed options, is treated as part of professional responsibility. Germans would rather hear about a delay or obstacle while there is still time to adjust than be surprised later.

Privacy and data protection are taken seriously, shaped by historical sensitivity around surveillance. Sharing personal or confidential information requires care. Explaining how you handle data and respecting legal and ethical boundaries reassures German partners that you understand the gravity of the responsibility.
