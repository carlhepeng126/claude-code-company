# Business Lunch & Dinner

## Meetings & Presentations

A business lunch in the Dominican Republic, especially in Santo Domingo, can easily stretch beyond two hours. This length reflects how trust is formed. The meal is where deals are tested and character assessed through storytelling, humour, and informal probing. Rushing the meal or cramming it between back-to-back calendar slots suggests you are more interested in ticking a box than building a relationship. If you arrive with a clear agenda and steer conversation back to business too quickly, your counterpart may read you as transactional. Schedule time generously and let the conversation unfold at its own rhythm.

Hierarchy shows up quietly but clearly at the table. Who selects the restaurant, who orders first, who pours drinks and who gets served all reflect relative seniority. Junior colleagues often wait for cues before contributing. If you are the guest, follow your host's lead rather than taking initiative that might feel presumptuous. Dominicans value warmth and generosity, and the senior person typically guides the tone and pace of the meal.

Food itself becomes part of the relational exchange. Conversation often turns to regional dishes like sancocho, mofongo, or mangú, or fish from particular coastlines. Showing genuine curiosity about what is served, accepting recommendations, and commenting positively on a beloved dish builds rapport. Colleagues from cultures where meals are brief and business-focused may find this prolonged attention to food unnecessary, while Dominicans may interpret a colleague's reluctance to engage with the meal as distant or closed off.

Dominican coffee is widely respected, and acknowledging its quality when it is served signals appreciation, not just of the drink but of the culture. The meal ends when the conversation winds down naturally, not when the plates are cleared.
