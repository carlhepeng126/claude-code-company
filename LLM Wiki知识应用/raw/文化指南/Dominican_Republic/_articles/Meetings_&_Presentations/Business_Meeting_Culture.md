# Business Meeting Culture

## Meetings & Presentations

Meetings in the Dominican Republic often unfold in two stages. The first part can be highly performative: formal greetings, status statements, sometimes a political figure or company owner making an appearance. Real negotiation frequently shifts afterwards to a smaller group in someone's office, a hotel lobby, or over lunch. This reflects the importance of relationship and hierarchy. The front-stage meeting establishes respect and positions; the back-stage conversation is where concerns are explored and decisions take shape. If you treat the formal meeting as the only moment that counts, you may miss where the real work happens.

During the meeting itself, expect mobiles to ring, people to step in and out, and side conversations to occur. This flows from the way many Dominican leaders juggle multiple roles across business, politics, and family. What can look like distraction to a colleague from a culture where meetings are tightly controlled often reflects the realities of holding several responsibilities at once. Watch instead for when they refocus on you and how they summarise what has been said. That tells you where their attention actually lies.

Disagreement is rarely stated directly in the room. Phrases like "vamos a ver" (let's see) or "hay que analizarlo bien" (we need to analyze this carefully) can signal strong resistance that will emerge only after the meeting, in one-on-one conversations or through an intermediary. For someone from a culture where decisions are made openly and quickly in the meeting itself, this can feel evasive. From a Dominican perspective, preserving harmony in the group and addressing concerns privately protects relationships and allows space for face-saving adjustments. If you hear these phrases, treat them as an invitation to follow up, not as agreement.

Seating, proximity to the head of the table, and who speaks when reveal real power. The person making the decision may say very little until late in the conversation. Reading these non-verbal cues and addressing the underlying concerns of the quiet decision-maker will move things forward far more effectively than pushing for commitments during the meeting itself.
