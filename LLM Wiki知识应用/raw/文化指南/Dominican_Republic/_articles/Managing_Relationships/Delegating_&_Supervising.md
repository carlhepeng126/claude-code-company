# Delegating & Supervising

## Managing Relationships

In Dominican workplaces, the manager-employee relationship is explicitly paternalistic. Managers are expected to function as protector and provider figures, offering genuine care and guidance in return for respect and loyalty. This is not just interpersonal warmth, it is the structure through which work gets done. A manager who delegates tasks without investing in personal relationships will find those tasks executed minimally or not at all. This reflects the cultural principle of personalismo, the primacy of the personal relationship, which governs how delegation actually works. Tasks given to someone within your trusted network will be carried out with commitment; tasks given across a thin or transactional relationship will not.

Hierarchy is clear and expected. Employees want strong, visible leadership and need to understand their place in the structure. However, problems are often resolved among team members before they reach the manager. Employees will go to considerable lengths to protect their manager from bad news, seeing it as their role to handle issues quietly and keep the manager's reputation intact. This means the picture a manager receives is filtered. If you want early visibility of issues, you will need to build sustained trust and genuine safety over time, recognising that managing upwards carefully is the cultural default.

When things go wrong, employees may raise concerns indirectly or in hypothetical terms rather than state a problem outright. They may also discuss it among themselves or, in serious cases, escalate over the manager's head to a more senior figure. Asking a manager for clarification can feel like admitting incompetence, so employees often turn to colleagues instead. Managers who invest time in personal connection, make themselves approachable, and learn to listen for what is being signalled rather than only what is stated will be far more effective. Both praise and correction should be given privately. Public recognition or criticism, even if well-intended, can affect personal dignity in ways that undermine the relationship.
