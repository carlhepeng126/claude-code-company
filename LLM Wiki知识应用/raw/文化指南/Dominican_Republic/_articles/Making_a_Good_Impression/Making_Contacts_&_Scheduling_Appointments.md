# Making Contacts & Scheduling Appointments

## Making a Good Impression

Getting through the door in the Dominican Republic usually depends less on what you want to discuss than on who can vouch for you. The phrase "confianza primero, agenda después" (trust first, agenda later) reflects a deep-rooted preference for knowing someone's background and connections before committing time or resources. A warm introduction from a respected figure — a business owner, lawyer, pastor, union leader, or even a cousin in the diaspora — can open doors far more quickly than a formal email sent cold. The person introducing you lends you their credibility, and that social endorsement matters more than the polish of your pitch. If you're entering a new market or sector without a local intermediary, expect the first meetings to focus on building rapport rather than advancing your agenda.

WhatsApp has become the practical backbone of professional coordination, often more reliable than email for confirming meetings or following up on conversations. Voice notes and quick chats flow outside strict office hours, and a message that sits ignored in an inbox may get an immediate reply once it lands on WhatsApp. Once you've met someone in person, asking if it's okay to follow up via WhatsApp is usually welcomed, provided you're respectful about timing and frequency.

Geography and social class shape where and how you make contact. In Santo Domingo and Santiago, business elites and middle-class professionals often circulate in distinct physical spaces — private office parks, high-rise torre lobbies, or exclusive clubs rather than open public areas. Reaching the right decision-maker sometimes means physically entering their world. Understanding where your counterpart lives and works, and offering to meet them on their turf, shows both practical awareness and respect for local realities. Many influential Dominicans also have close family ties to New York, New Jersey, Florida, or Madrid. A passing reference to work with Dominican communities in those cities can create an instant sense of shared understanding and belonging.
