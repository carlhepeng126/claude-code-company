# Forms of Address

## Making a Good Impression

Respect in Dominican workplaces is expressed through how you address someone, and the choice of title, name, and pronoun reveals how you understand both their status and your relationship. Professional titles like Licenciado (for anyone with a university degree), Ingeniero (engineer), or Doctor are used widely, sometimes even aspirationally. Addressing someone as Licenciada when you're uncertain of their exact qualification can show that you recognize their position. Don or Doña paired with a first name offers warmth alongside respect, particularly when speaking with older colleagues or people in positions of authority outside the most corporate settings. These forms of address acknowledge social standing in a culture where family background, education, and rank continue to shape how people see one another.

When first meeting someone, begin with usted and pair it with the appropriate title and surname, such as Señor López or Doña Carmen. The shift from usted to tú marks the development of confianza, the trust and familiarity that allows formality to relax. That shift is usually initiated by the person of higher status or seniority, and some relationships will remain at usted due to age or hierarchy. Colleagues from cultures where first names are used immediately may read this formality as coldness or distance, while Dominicans may find a quick shift to tú and first names presumptuous or tone-deaf to the social calibration underway.

Listen closely to how others in the room address senior figures and mirror that choice. In more informal settings, people may be known by apodos, nicknames often tied to physical features or personality. Outsiders using them too soon can feel intrusive. But getting someone's full name right, pronouncing it carefully, and using the form they are known by in that context shows attentiveness and respect in a relational culture where how you speak to someone is never neutral.
