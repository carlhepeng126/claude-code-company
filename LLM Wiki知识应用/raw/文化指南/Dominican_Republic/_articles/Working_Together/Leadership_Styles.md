# Leadership Styles

## Working Together

Leadership in Dominican Republic is shaped by historical memory and everyday relational expectation. The country lived under Trujillo's dictatorship for three decades, and then under Balaguer's personalist rule. People know what abusive strongman authority looks like, and they recognize the echoes. At the same time, they still expect leaders to be visible, decisive, and capable of solving problems by activating relationships and calling the right people. The modern manager operates in both realities: the need to lead clearly without slipping into authoritarian tones, and the expectation to show up personally, not just administratively.

Being a good leader includes being accessible. That means picking up the phone, replying to WhatsApp messages at unexpected hours, and attending a funeral or hospital visit when someone on the team is affected. Emotional presence counts as much as technical competence. A manager who is efficient but distant may be seen as cold or uncommitted. This ties to the broader importance of personalismo: leadership is relational before it is procedural. A colleague from a culture where professional and personal spheres are kept separate may find this level of contact intrusive or unclear. From a Dominican perspective, the same colleague's reserve may read as indifference or aloofness, a sign the leader does not truly care about their people.

Authority is concentrated and hierarchy is clear, but how that authority is exercised varies by setting and generation. In traditional family businesses and older organizations, leaders may frame decisions in moral and religious language, using phrases like "eso no es correcto" (that is not right) or invoking faith in everyday speech. This blends ethics and business in a way that younger managers in multinational firms may use less frequently. What remains constant is the expectation that leadership be firm, fair, and approachable, and that it avoid repeating the macho or authoritarian patterns still remembered from the dictatorship era.
