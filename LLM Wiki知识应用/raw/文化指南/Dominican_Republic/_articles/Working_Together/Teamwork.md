# Teamwork

## Working Together

Dominican teams often function like *familia extendida* (extended family), where the warmth, noise, and loyalty of family life shape how people collaborate. Joking, scolding, and visible affection sit alongside expectations of protection and reciprocity. Conflicts can be emotional and public, but they're absorbed into the ongoing relationship. This reflects a deep group orientation where the team is understood as something you belong to, not just something you perform within. The practical effect is that building strong interpersonal relationships becomes the foundation for getting work done. If you introduce new processes or timelines without first investing in those bonds, the team may comply formally but will be slow to invest real energy or discretion.

Unofficial roles often shape how work actually happens. Many teams include a *tíguere*, someone who knows how to solve problems with a phone call or a favour, a *seria* who quietly holds structure and remembers deadlines, and sometimes a colleague known for religious conviction who brings a moral perspective to decisions. These archetypes hold real influence, often more than the org chart would suggest. If you're coordinating across a Dominican team, identify these informal anchors early. Working through them—rather than around them—will help you understand how decisions actually move and where loyalty sits.

Many team members have lived or worked abroad, in New York, Puerto Rico, or Spain, and bring different expectations around time, formality, or communication. This creates internal variation. Some colleagues will push for tighter timelines or clearer documentation, while others will resist what feels like imported rigidity. Rather than assuming you're introducing international standards alone, ask openly who has worked outside the country and invite their experience into planning. This recognizes their knowledge and reduces the risk of positioning yourself as the sole source of change.
