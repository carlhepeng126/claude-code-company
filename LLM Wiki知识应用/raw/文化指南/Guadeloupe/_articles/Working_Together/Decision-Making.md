# Decision-Making

## Working Together

In Guadeloupe, decision-making reflects both French administrative culture and Caribbean social values. Formal hierarchy matters. Approval often rests with senior leaders or directors, and moving forward without consulting the appropriate level can stall progress or create confusion. At the same time, relationships shape how decisions are refined and accepted. Managers are expected to consult informally with trusted colleagues or stakeholders before finalizing a choice, especially when the decision affects the team or involves change. This consultation happens alongside the formal process, not instead of it.

The result is a dual structure. A manager may already know the direction but will still take time to gather input, test reactions, and build alignment within their network. This is not delay for its sake. It reflects a belief that decisions should fit the social as well as the operational reality. Skipping this step, even when hierarchy gives you the authority to decide alone, can undermine buy-in and make implementation harder. If you are used to faster, more individualized decision cycles, build in time for this informal soundcheck and recognize that the manager's final word still depends on having consulted the right people first.

Flexibility also plays a role. Once a decision is made, how it is applied may shift depending on the people involved or the circumstances at hand. This is not inconsistency. It is an acknowledgment that relationships, timing, and context can all influence what works in practice.
