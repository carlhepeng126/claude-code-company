# Negotiating

## Working Together

Negotiations in China often start before formal discussions begin. Multiple conversations build rapport and establish the working foundation on which detailed terms can eventually rest. Relationship-building is the necessary groundwork, not just courtesy, and rushing straight to commercial pressure before that rapport exists usually backfires. Proposals seen as one-sided trigger resistance that goes beyond commercial calculation. A deal perceived to favour one party too heavily undermines the reciprocal relationship that both sides depend on to make the agreement work. Balance in framing mutual benefit, even in small ways, helps avoid the perception of dominance.

Language at the table tends to stay polite and indirect, even when positions are firm. Outright rejection is reframed or softened. For a counterpart from a culture where directness signals clarity, this indirectness can feel ambiguous or evasive. From the Chinese side, blunt refusal or confrontation risks causing loss of face and closes down future flexibility. Watching behavioural cues alongside formal words gives a clearer read. Meeting attendance, response speed, and the pace at which follow-up is scheduled all indicate genuine interest more reliably than what is said in the room.

Contracts are important, but they do not stand alone. Long-term reliability and consistent behaviour over time hold equal weight. Demonstrating follow-through after initial agreement reinforces trust and strengthens the partnership beyond what legal terms can secure on their own.
