# Motivating Others

## Managing Relationships

Motivation in China runs through multiple channels at once. People are driven by material success and visible status, certainly, but also by family obligation, collective achievement, and increasingly by a sense of national pride rooted in rapid economic transformation. Many employees remain closely tied to a narrative of upward mobility — their parents or grandparents may have lived through poverty, and the responsibility to repay those sacrifices through career success and intergenerational security runs deep. Work becomes a way to honor the family and secure its future, which means that linking individual performance to family wellbeing or framing goals as contributions to a larger shared success often inspires more sustained effort than individual recognition alone.

At the same time, the rise of social media has intensified the visibility of success markers. Digital platforms amplify lifestyle comparisons, and many employees are acutely aware of how their achievements measure up. Recognition that is public and shareable — praise on WeChat, visible wins within the team, symbolic gestures like digital red envelopes sent by senior leaders during festivals — resonates strongly. These gestures signal inclusion and status in a way that private feedback does not. A manager from a culture where one-on-one praise is preferred might read this appetite for public acknowledgment as performative, while a Chinese colleague might experience private feedback as underwhelming or even unclear about where they truly stand.

Yet among younger professionals, especially in first-tier cities, a counter-current is emerging. The pace of competition, long hours, and relentless pressure to outperform have led to movements like *tang ping* (lying flat) and discussions around *neijuan* (involution), signaling fatigue with endless striving. For this cohort, motivation increasingly depends on work that feels meaningful and sustainable, not just lucrative. Managers who acknowledge burnout risks, offer pathways beyond pure competition, or connect roles to innovation and cultural pride will engage more effectively than those who rely solely on financial incentives or appeals to hierarchy.
