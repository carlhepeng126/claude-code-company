# Delegating & Supervising

## Managing Relationships

Delegation in China works within a moral framework shaped by Confucian principles that have structured society for over two millennia. Authority flows downward and deference flows upward, not as corporate convention but as deeply rooted social obligation. A manager is expected to know more, decide more, and carry visible responsibility. Employees typically follow instructions precisely, doing what was asked and no more. This reflects a workplace logic where acting outside your defined scope without permission is presumptuous rather than proactive. When a manager from a culture where initiative is expected without prompting delegates broadly and waits for questions, Chinese team members may interpret the silence as clarity and deliver exactly what was said, nothing beyond it. From the other direction, a Chinese manager's detailed instruction may read as micromanagement to someone accustomed to greater interpretive freedom, when it is intended as thorough guidance that honours the relationship and minimizes risk.

Mianzi (面子), or face, governs how correction and failure are addressed. Pointing out an error in front of others, even diplomatically, damages the individual's standing and fractures the relationship. All feedback on sensitive matters belongs in private, framed carefully. A manager who visibly defers to a subordinate or admits uncertainty in public risks losing face with the wider team, which in turn weakens their authority.

Guanxi (关系), the web of personal relationships and reciprocal obligation, underpins how delegation functions day to day. Trust is relational, not institutional. A strong personal connection makes delegation smoother; a weak one makes it fragile. This is why time spent in meals, informal conversation, and attention to personal matters is operational work, not rapport-building on the side. If you want genuine initiative, you must invite it explicitly and repeatedly, and make it safe to offer. Proactive behaviour will not emerge on its own in a context where unsolicited contributions risk embarrassing both the speaker and their manager.
