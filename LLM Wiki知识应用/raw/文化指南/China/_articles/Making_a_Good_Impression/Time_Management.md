# Time Management

## Making a Good Impression

Punctuality in Chinese business life shows respect and serious intent. Arriving on time for a scheduled meeting is expected, particularly in formal settings or when meeting for the first time. This reflects a collective orientation where being late disrupts the group and suggests a lack of commitment. In fast-paced urban centers like Shanghai and Shenzhen, where productivity goals drive quarterly cycles and results-oriented management dominates, timeliness has become increasingly non-negotiable. If you arrive late without explanation, colleagues may read it as disregard for the team's time, not just your own.

At the same time, time in China bends when relationships require it. WeChat operates around the clock, and responsiveness outside formal hours is common. This constant connectivity serves both efficiency and relationship maintenance. A manager might message late in the evening or over the weekend, and a prompt reply demonstrates availability and dedication to the collective effort. For colleagues from cultures where work and personal time are more clearly separated, this can feel intrusive. From a Chinese perspective, delayed responses in urgent moments may suggest disengagement or a lack of team commitment.

Deadlines are increasingly explicit, particularly in corporate environments driven by KPIs and quarterly reviews. Clear timelines prevent ambiguity and the loss of face that comes with misaligned expectations. Yet face considerations still soften enforcement. A missed deadline might not trigger immediate confrontation if the broader relationship and visible effort remain intact. Younger professionals, especially in China's major cities, are beginning to push back against extreme overtime cultures like the once-common 996 schedule, balancing ambition with growing awareness of burnout. This shift is generational, and expectations around working hours vary noticeably between older managers and younger team members.
