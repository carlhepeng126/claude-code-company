# Forms of Address

## Making a Good Impression

Recognizing hierarchy in the opening moments of a meeting shapes how the rest of the interaction unfolds. Direct your first greeting to the most senior person in the room, and if you are meeting as a team, line up in order of seniority so your Chinese counterparts can quickly understand who holds what role. This helps them address the right person on the right issue, and shows you understand how decisions flow.

Physical greetings have shifted in recent years. A handshake combined with a slight nod was once standard, but head bowing is now less common, and the pandemic changed how often people shake hands at all. Make eye contact and wait for your counterpart to set the tone rather than extending your hand first. This gives you room to respond appropriately without making an incorrect assumption.

First names were once reserved for family and very close friends, and using them assumed a level of closeness that had to be earned over time. That formality has relaxed considerably as global business practices take hold, especially in private companies and multinational settings. Even so, take your cue from your Chinese colleagues. In more traditional state-owned enterprises or government-linked organizations, titles such as Mr., Ms., or professional designations like Doctor or Engineer paired with the family name may still be expected. In many commercial environments, however, colleagues will introduce themselves by first name or invite you to use it early on.

Chinese names place the family name first, followed by the given name. Shunchen Duan, for example, would be addressed as Mr. Duan in formal settings, with Duan being the family name. Women keep their family name after marriage, so do not assume a shared surname between spouses.
