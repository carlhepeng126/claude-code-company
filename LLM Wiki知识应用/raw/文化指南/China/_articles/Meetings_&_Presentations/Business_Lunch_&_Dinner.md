# Business Lunch & Dinner

## Meetings & Presentations

A formal business meal in China follows careful choreography. Where people sit and who offers the first toast conveys who holds status and how much the relationship is valued. The host will typically guide guests to specific seats, with the most senior guest positioned facing the door or at the centre of the table. This arrangement sustains hierarchy and demonstrates respect, both of which form the foundation for trust. Waiting to be shown where to sit, rather than choosing your own place, acknowledges this structure.

Toasting, often with *baijiu* (a strong grain spirit), accelerates the building of *guanxi* (networks of reciprocal relationships). Rounds of toasts create moments of shared obligation, binding people together in a group setting. You may be encouraged to drink more than you're comfortable with. Declining entirely can close off rapport, but sipping rather than draining your glass is usually accepted. If you do abstain, it's clearer to do so from the start than to stop after one drink, which can read as half-hearted participation.

The meal itself often lasts several hours and includes many courses. This extended pace allows relationship building to unfold naturally, blending conversation about business with personal exchange. Rushing through courses or steering talk back to contract details too quickly can feel transactional. The choice of regional dishes also communicates effort and investment in the shared experience, creating a cultural memory that strengthens the connection over time.
