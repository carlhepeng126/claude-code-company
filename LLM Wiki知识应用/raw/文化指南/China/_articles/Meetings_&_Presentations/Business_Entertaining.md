# Business Entertaining

## Meetings & Presentations

Formal meals function as relationship investment in China, offering a structured setting where trust, hierarchy, and reciprocity can be demonstrated and observed. Banquets are taken seriously because they allow counterparts to assess compatibility and commitment beyond what's said in the meeting room. The gathering itself builds *guanxi* (mutual obligation networks rooted in personal connection), and accepting an invitation indicates willingness to enter or deepen a relationship. Declining without good reason can be read as disinterest in the partnership.

Seating, service order, and toasting follow hierarchy closely. The host positions senior guests prominently and initiates toasts, often with *baijiu* (a strong grain spirit), addressing the most senior person first. Participating in these rounds, even if you drink modestly, honors the structure and reinforces reciprocity. A colleague from a culture where alcohol is optional at work events might see this as pressure, while a Chinese counterpart might read non-participation as a reluctance to engage fully or show respect to senior figures present.

Tea service and regional cuisine also play a role. Offering high-quality tea or showcasing local specialties demonstrates care and refinement, creating shared experience that supports relationship building. Business may be discussed lightly or not at all during the meal. The evening is often about observation and connection, and pushing for outcomes too early can seem transactional.

Always allow your Chinese host to issue the first invitation, then reciprocate promptly. If you have dietary restrictions, mention them discreetly in advance or frame them as medical or religious to help your host plan without losing face.
