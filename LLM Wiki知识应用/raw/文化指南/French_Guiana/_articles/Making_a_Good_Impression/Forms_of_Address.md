# Forms of Address

## Making a Good Impression

In French Guiana, forms of address follow French metropolitan conventions, reflecting the territory's status as an integral part of France rather than an independent nation. French naming customs apply fully, and the same formality norms that shape professional life in France are equally relevant here. Titles matter, and they are used to mark both professional standing and appropriate social distance until a relationship has developed.

Address colleagues and clients as "Monsieur" (Mr.) or "Madame" (Ms./Mrs.) followed by the surname until explicitly invited to do otherwise. Professional titles such as "Docteur" for those with PhDs or medical degrees, or "Maître" for lawyers, are used routinely and expected in written and spoken exchange. Moving to first names happens gradually and is typically initiated by the senior or longer-standing person in the relationship. Jumping to first names too quickly can feel presumptuous, particularly when hierarchy or unfamiliarity is still a factor.

Written communication maintains formality longer than spoken interaction. Emails should open with "Monsieur" or "Madame" and close with phrases like "Cordialement" or "Bien cordialement." Even in routine digital exchanges, dropping the greeting or using overly casual sign-offs can come across as careless rather than efficient. A colleague accustomed to informal email styles may read this as stiff, while someone from French Guiana may interpret brevity or informality as a lack of professionalism or respect for the relationship.

Greetings in person typically begin with a handshake. Among colleagues who know each other well, the *bise* (a light cheek kiss, usually two) is common, though this varies by workplace culture and personal preference. Wait for local colleagues to initiate.
