# Time Management

## Making a Good Impression

Sri Lanka's sense of time is shaped by a calendar marked by religious observance and astrological rhythm. Full moon *Poya* days are public holidays, and the Sinhala and Tamil New Year brings a distinctive pause when normal work is suspended during the *nona gathe*, the neutral period between years. Timing for significant events is often guided by astrologically determined auspicious moments, known as *nekath*, which shape not only personal milestones but also when certain professional activities feel right or appropriate. This is not simply religious observance. Many Sri Lankans across different faith backgrounds treat astrological timing as a socially meaningful coordination preference, one that can influence scheduling in ways international colleagues may not anticipate.

What this means in practice is that calendars require advance checking. Scheduling major deadlines, key meetings, or launches on *Poya* days or around the New Year window without confirming availability is likely to clash with observed rhythms. If a colleague references auspicious timing when proposing or deferring a date, the reference is not symbolic. Treat it as you would any other legitimate scheduling constraint. Offer alternative dates rather than pressing ahead, and build in flexibility during culturally dense periods when response times may naturally slow.

Regional variation also affects how time is experienced. Colombo operates at a faster tempo, shaped by traffic congestion and late-day scheduling norms. In provincial towns or estate regions, travel time, community obligations, and local holiday observance reshape the working day. When coordinating across regions, confirm practicalities rather than assuming metropolitan patterns apply everywhere. A colleague from a culture where schedules are tightly controlled may read this flexibility as disorganization, while a Sri Lankan counterpart may view rigid adherence to the clock as disregard for context and relationship.
