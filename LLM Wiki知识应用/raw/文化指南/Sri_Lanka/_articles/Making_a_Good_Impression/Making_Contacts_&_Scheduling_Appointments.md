# Making Contacts & Scheduling Appointments

## Making a Good Impression

Trust in Sri Lanka often moves through layered networks of family, school, hometown, religion, and language. These aren't formal gatekeepers, but they shape how people place you before opening doors widely. A cold approach can work, but it may take longer to gain traction than a message that names a mutual contact, shared institution, or alumni link early. The decades of civil war and identity-based tensions that followed independence, including language laws that shifted access and belonging, haven't disappeared from memory. Many people still assess new relationships with a quiet awareness of who feels familiar and who doesn't. This means introductions that travel through reputation chains—someone who can vouch for you—often carry more weight than credentials alone.

When reaching out, warmth and clarity help reduce stranger distance. Open with a brief human greeting, locate yourself (who you are, how you found them), and mention any shared reference points. Keep initial messages identity-safe: avoid assumptions about someone's background and let them disclose what they choose to. Sri Lanka's history makes mislabelling feel sharper, and a neutral, respectful tone allows the relationship to develop on safer ground.

Scheduling works best when you offer choice rather than fixed demands. Propose two or three meeting windows and stay flexible. People's calendars are shaped by family duty, religious observance, and sometimes diaspora ties that cross time zones. Appointments are usually honored, but the rhythm around them can feel more fluid than strictly sequential. If a meeting shifts or expands, that's often a sign of engagement, not disinterest. 

Where possible, involve an intermediary. A mutual contact who can make the introduction—whether through a phone call, an informal coffee, or a brief message—can accelerate trust more effectively than repeated follow-ups sent into silence.
