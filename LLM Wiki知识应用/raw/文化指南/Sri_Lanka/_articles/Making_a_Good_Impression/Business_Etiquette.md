# Business Etiquette

## Making a Good Impression

Politeness in Sri Lanka centers on protecting dignity and avoiding embarrassment. Many professionals soften disagreement through indirect phrasing, gentle questions, or quiet hesitation rather than open contradiction. This reflects a broader social intelligence where harmony is often preserved through restraint. A colleague may tell you what they believe you want to hear, not to mislead but to avoid causing discomfort. A translator may edit what's said, filtering out anything they think might sting. If you disagree, frame it as a proposal or ask clarifying questions rather than correcting someone directly, especially in front of others.

Sri Lanka's religious and community diversity means that etiquette shifts across settings. Temple, mosque, and church norms shape everyday manners, as do food rules and gender expectations. What works in one context may not translate cleanly to another, even within the same city or company. When in doubt, watch for cues and follow your counterpart's lead. Use your right hand for greetings, gifts, and gestures; the left is often seen as unclean. A side-to-side head wobble usually signals uncertainty or gentle acknowledgment rather than agreement.

The country's history of language policy and ethnic tension has made identity a sensitive subject. People can be cautious about being categorized too quickly, even when curiosity is well meant. If background or community comes up in conversation, keep your questions human rather than diagnostic, and follow their lead on how much to share.
