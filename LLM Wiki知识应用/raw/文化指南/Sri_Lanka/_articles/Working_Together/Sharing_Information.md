# Sharing Information

## Working Together

Information travels through relationships before it reaches the page. In Sri Lanka, knowing who to ask often outweighs what you ask for, because trust chains rooted in family networks, alumni ties, religious communities, and regional connections shape how people interpret what something actually means, beyond what it formally says. This reliance on personal networks comes from a broader cultural logic where indirect communication protects relationships and preserves dignity. Softened phrasing and circumspect language serve as relationship-protection tools, especially when information might embarrass someone or stir communal sensitivities linked to language or identity. A colleague from a culture where updates flow openly across teams may read this indirectness as evasiveness, while a Sri Lankan counterpart may read that same directness as blunt or even unkind.

Within organizations, information typically flows through hierarchy rather than across it. Teams bond closely and share freely among themselves, but withholding from rival departments is common. Middle managers may not know the reasoning behind decisions made above them, and junior staff are unlikely to question the gap. This reflects a teaching culture that emphasizes respect for authority rather than challenge or critique. That said, Sri Lankans' natural gregariousness means conversations happen often, and informal exchanges can reveal more than formal channels do. Building trust takes time, but once established, people share more readily. If you need clarity, ask gently and directly rather than waiting for it to be offered.

Language choice carries more than functional meaning. Switching between Sinhala, Tamil, and English can signal respect, neutrality, education, or community alignment, shaped by language's historical link to political memory and dignity. When drafting communications, use simple structure and avoid labels that might unintentionally mark someone's background. Timing also shapes disclosure. Some people prefer certain moments for difficult conversations, guided by festival rhythms or beliefs about auspiciousness. Asking when a conversation would be easiest, and respecting those preferences without debate, clears the path for genuine exchange.
