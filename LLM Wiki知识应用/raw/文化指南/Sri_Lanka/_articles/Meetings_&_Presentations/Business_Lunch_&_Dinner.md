# Business Lunch & Dinner

## Meetings & Presentations

In Sri Lanka, how you conduct yourself at a meal reveals much about your character. People observe how you receive food, whether you treat restaurant staff with courtesy, and how you include those who speak less. A guest who turns unfamiliar dishes into a performance, or who eats quickly and shifts back to business talk, can undermine months of careful relationship-building. The table is where humility, gratitude, and social awareness are quietly assessed.

Rice and curry anchors most meals, but there is no single version. What arrives varies by region, religion, and family tradition. Hill-country plantation communities, coastal households, and urban Colombo each bring distinct rhythms to the table. Someone accustomed to working meals in cultures where food is secondary to discussion may find the pace slow and the attention to each dish unusually deliberate. For a Sri Lankan colleague hosting a visitor who rushes through courses or checks their phone between dishes, the behavior can suggest that the relationship holds little value. Conversely, if that colleague dines in a culture where meals are brief and functional, they may initially read the brevity as efficiency rather than indifference. Both sides benefit from recognizing what the meal is meant to do.

Major festivals and Poya days create what many describe as "family gravity." Scheduling a business dinner during Sinhala or Tamil New Year, or on a Poya day, can place a colleague in an awkward position, even if they agree. Check preferences before extending invitations during these periods.

Conversations at meals tend toward gentler ground, especially early in a relationship. Because histories can be painful and narratives contested, many people prefer to keep harmony at the table unless trust is already in place. If war or politics arises, listen carefully, avoid sweeping statements, and resist the urge to offer solutions to someone's lived experience.
