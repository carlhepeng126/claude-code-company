# Business Entertaining

## Meetings & Presentations

Business entertaining in Sri Lanka is often generous in spirit but shaped by identity, restraint, and a strong sense of care. Relationships grow through shared meals and hospitality, though the format and tone may vary depending on religious observance, community background, and the setting. Tea—offered freely and often—is one of the most consistent rituals of welcome. It slows the pace, creates ease, and signals attentiveness without demanding formality or disclosure. In offices, hotels, and homes, offering tea is a quiet form of relationship maintenance.

Food and drink choices reflect communal and religious belonging more visibly than in many other professional contexts. A colleague may abstain from alcohol for religious reasons, avoid certain foods during Poya observance, or follow dietary practices tied to Hindu, Muslim, or Buddhist tradition. Pushing someone to explain their preferences at the table, or making assumptions about what "most people" will be comfortable with, can create unnecessary discomfort. Asking discreetly in advance, or letting the host set the menu, avoids putting anyone in the position of having to justify their choices publicly.

Entertaining is usually relationship-focused rather than transactional. Business may come up lightly during a meal, but the gathering itself is often the goal: a chance to observe how someone relates, listens, and engages beyond the meeting room. A visiting colleague from a culture where meals are used to advance specific decisions may wonder when the work conversation will begin. From a Sri Lankan perspective, the meal is already doing the work—it's building the trust and familiarity that make future decisions smoother. Reciprocating invitations, whether during the visit or later, is appreciated and expected.

Timing around religious and civic days should be respected. Poya days, for instance, shift the social tone in many areas, and proposing late-night or alcohol-centered events during those periods can feel tone-deaf. Let your counterpart propose the occasion if the timing overlaps with a significant observance.
