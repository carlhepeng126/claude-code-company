# Feedback and Coaching

## Managing Relationships

Feedback in Sri Lanka travels more effectively when it protects dignity and respects seniority. Public critique can generate shame that extends beyond the workplace and into a person's wider social network, especially where communities remain tightly connected. Indirectness and privacy are not evasions; they function as care. Addressing an issue privately, with calm tone and specific reference to the behavior rather than the person, allows someone to adjust without losing face. This is particularly true when feedback touches on qualities like reliability or decency, which can be read as judgments on character and family standing, not just performance.

Generational and hierarchical differences shape how feedback is received. Junior colleagues may defer to seniors even when corrections feel disproportionate, while a younger manager offering direct input to an older team member risks being read as disrespectful. Starting with acknowledgment before moving to the issue can soften this tension, especially when seniority is visibly present. Framing feedback as support rather than judgment helps it land as intended.

Language also shifts the emotional temperature of a message. The same sentence delivered in Sinhala, Tamil, or English carries different levels of formality and warmth, depending on which politeness conventions the listener applies. Asking someone to reflect back what they heard, without framing it as a test, helps catch misinterpretation early and allows you to adjust tone before a gap widens.

Avoid linking feedback to someone's region, community, or background, even indirectly. These associations can turn a work conversation into something heavier and harder to untangle.
