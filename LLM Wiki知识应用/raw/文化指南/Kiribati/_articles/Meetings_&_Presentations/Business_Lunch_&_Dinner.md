# Business Lunch & Dinner

## Meetings & Presentations

Formal business meals are rare in Kiribati. Most professional interaction happens informally, often around shared food in community or family settings rather than in restaurants. When business discussions do take place over food, they typically follow extended relationship-building and would feel more like visiting a colleague's home than attending a structured meeting. The meal itself becomes part of ongoing connection rather than a separate commercial occasion.

Food is almost always shared. Dishes are placed centrally and people help themselves, reinforcing the cultural emphasis on collective participation over individual portions. Traditional meals revolve around fish, rice, and root vegetables like breadfruit and taro. Coconut appears in many forms, from fresh milk to fermented toddy, though the latter is not typically offered in professional contexts. Meals are often simple and reflect what is locally available rather than restaurant formality.

Pace is unhurried. Conversations move between topics, and there is no expectation that business will be addressed directly or early. A colleague from a culture where meals have clear agendas and outcomes may find this frustrating, interpreting the lack of structure as avoidance. For someone from Kiribati, the same directness might seem rushed and impersonal, bypassing the relational foundation that makes any agreement meaningful. Trust is built through shared time and presence, not through segmented discussion.

If you are invited to share a meal, participate fully. Accept what is offered and eat at the group's pace. Declining food or leaving early can feel like withdrawal from the relationship itself.
