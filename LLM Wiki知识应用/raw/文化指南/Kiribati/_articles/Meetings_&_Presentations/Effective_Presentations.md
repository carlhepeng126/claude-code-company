# Effective Presentations

## Meetings & Presentations

Presentations in Kiribati work best when they feel personal and conversational rather than formal or polished. The i-Kiribati workplace is rooted in communal values and relationship building, so a speaker who comes across as approachable will earn more trust than one who relies on technical brilliance or slick visuals. Keep your tone warm and inclusive. If you open with a brief acknowledgment of those present or reference a shared experience, you set a tone of respect that your audience will recognize and appreciate.

Pacing should be slow and unhurried. Information is expected to be absorbed collectively, and people may need time to reflect before they respond. Rushing through slides or pushing for quick agreement can feel dismissive. Allow pauses, repeat key points in different ways, and leave room for questions that may emerge gradually rather than immediately. A colleague from a culture where presentations are tightly timed may read this rhythm as unfocused, while an i-Kiribati participant may find a fast-paced delivery overwhelming or disrespectful of the group's need to process together.

Avoid hard-sell tactics or aggressive persuasion. I-Kiribati professionals value consensus and harmony, and a presentation that feels confrontational or overly assertive can create discomfort. Frame your proposal as something the group can shape together rather than a decision to be made on the spot. If follow-up conversations happen informally after the session, treat them seriously. That's often where real clarity emerges.
