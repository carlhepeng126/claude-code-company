# Business Meeting Culture

## Meetings & Presentations

The three islands of the Caribbean Netherlands share some baseline Dutch influence, but local meeting culture reflects a Caribbean rhythm shaped by small populations, face-to-face relationships, and geographic distance from the European Netherlands. Meetings are rarely rushed. People expect time to build rapport before moving into business detail, and pushing for a quick outcome risks coming across as transactional. Trust develops slowly and through repeated interaction, so an agenda that works on paper may not unfold at the pace you expect if the personal connection has not yet been established.

Formality in meetings is moderate. Titles are used less rigidly than in much of Europe, and while there is respect for seniority, the tone tends to be relaxed once introductions are made. Still, directness is appreciated more than vague language or overstatement. Be clear about what you need and what you can deliver, and follow through. On smaller islands especially, word travels quickly, and credibility is built through consistency rather than polish.

Scheduling meetings can require more lead time than the small size of the islands might suggest. Government offices and larger employers often juggle limited staff across multiple roles, and key decision-makers may travel frequently between islands or to the Netherlands. Confirming availability well in advance, and reconfirming closer to the date, helps avoid last-minute changes. If your meeting involves multiple stakeholders, expect coordination to take longer than it might in a larger, more centralized setting.

Follow-up after a meeting matters. Written summaries or next steps are valued, especially when working across time zones or organizational structures. What is agreed in conversation should be reflected in writing soon after, and checking in regularly between meetings reinforces commitment and keeps momentum going.
