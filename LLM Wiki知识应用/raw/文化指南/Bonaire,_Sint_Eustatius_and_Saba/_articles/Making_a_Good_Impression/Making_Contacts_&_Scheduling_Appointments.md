# Making Contacts & Scheduling Appointments

## Making a Good Impression

The Caribbean Netherlands functions with a small-island rhythm where relationships develop through repeated, informal contact rather than formal introductions. With communities of just a few thousand people on Sint Eustatius and Saba, and under 25,000 on Bonaire, most professional networks overlap with personal ones. Trust builds gradually through showing up, being visible in everyday settings, and demonstrating reliability over time. Cold emails or LinkedIn requests rarely yield results. What works better is attending community events, stopping by offices in person, or being introduced through someone already embedded locally. The islands reward presence and consistency more than credentials alone.

Scheduling feels more fluid than fixed. Appointments can be made by phone or email, but expect some looseness around timing. Meetings may start later than agreed, run longer than planned, or include unexpected participants. This flexibility reflects the practical realities of island life, where urgent matters, ferry schedules, or limited resources can shift priorities quickly. Someone working across multiple roles may have competing demands that reshape their day without warning. Building buffer time into your own schedule and staying adaptable signals that you understand how things actually work here.

There are differences across the three islands. Bonaire, with more economic activity and tourism infrastructure, tends toward slightly more structured scheduling, especially in government offices or larger businesses. On Saba and Sint Eustatius, where populations are smaller and roles more blurred, informal drop-ins remain common and relationships matter even more acutely. Everywhere, though, persistence without pushiness opens doors. Following up in person after an initial contact, checking in again a week later, or simply being around long enough to become a familiar face will move things forward more reliably than pressing for immediate commitments.
