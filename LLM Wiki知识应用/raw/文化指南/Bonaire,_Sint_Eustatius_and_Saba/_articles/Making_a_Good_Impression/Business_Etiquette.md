# Business Etiquette

## Making a Good Impression

Business interactions across Bonaire, Sint Eustatius and Saba combine Dutch institutional influence with Caribbean ease and a strong sense of local identity. Each island has its own rhythm. Bonaire, shaped by tourism and expatriate presence, tends toward informality in many settings, though Dutch-influenced businesses still expect punctuality and professional presentation. Sint Eustatius and Saba, smaller and more closely-knit, place greater emphasis on personal connection. Knowing someone, or being introduced by someone trusted, often smooths the way in a first meeting.

Greetings are straightforward but warm. A handshake is standard in formal business contexts, though people who know each other may greet more casually. Use titles and surnames until invited to do otherwise, especially with senior colleagues or public officials. On Saba and Statia, where nearly everyone knows everyone, respect for elders and established figures runs deep. Being overly familiar too quickly can come across as presumptuous.

Communication is generally direct, but there's a preference for maintaining harmony. Open criticism in group settings, particularly in front of others, is rarely well received. If something is off track, expect a quiet word afterward rather than a public correction. Colleagues from cultures where feedback is given immediately and explicitly may read this as avoidance. From a local perspective, addressing issues privately protects relationships and keeps the work environment steady.

Business cards are not always exchanged, and their absence means little. A phone number written down or a LinkedIn connection may serve the same purpose, especially among younger professionals or in less formal sectors. Offer a card when it feels natural, not as a formality to check off.
