# Leadership Styles

## Working Together

Leadership in Peru is shaped by a history of stratification that runs from colonial hierarchies through Lima-centric power structures to the marginalisation of rural communities. Authority is not simply a matter of rank. It is read through the lens of whether someone treats others with dignity across lines of class and region. Leaders who ignore this relational dimension, or who rely only on formal power, risk losing credibility even when they hold all the positional authority.

Legitimacy comes more from social accountability than procedural correctness. In settings where reciprocity governs relationships, leaders are expected to demonstrate responsibility, fairness, and a willingness to take care of the group, not just issue directives. This is especially true in contexts where the state has historically felt distant or uneven, and community systems have felt protective. Personal accountability matters more than institutional language. Leaders who follow through, own problems, and remain consistent build trust more reliably than those who rely on charisma or titles alone.

The legacy of internal conflict from 1980 to 2000 has left a lasting sensitivity to coercion, public humiliation, and opaque uses of power, particularly in communities that were most affected. Leadership styles that rely on pressure or public shaming can provoke withdrawal rather than compliance. Calm authority works better than dominance. Decisions that visibly consider people, not just outcomes, are more likely to be accepted.

In teams that span regions, avoid treating Lima as the default reference point. Demonstrating respect for regional realities and constraints is a practical way to signal fairness and build credibility across the country.
