# Decision-Making

## Working Together

Decision-making in Peru operates on two tracks: the formal and the relational. Official approval may rest with senior leaders, but legitimacy is built through what the decision reflects about respect, fairness, and the person making it. People assess not only whether a decision is technically sound, but whether the process acknowledged their standing and whether the rationale feels grounded in lived reality. A choice imposed without explanation or visible regard for those affected can meet passive resistance, even when hierarchy would seem to support compliance.

Hierarchy is real, but its exercise is shaped by dignity. Authority is more readily accepted when it is used visibly and carefully, especially across differences in class, region, or role. Middle managers may not hold final say, but they often have direct access to those who do, and their input can quietly reshape outcomes. Dismissing someone as procedurally junior can mean missing the person whose view will ultimately inform the decision. In reciprocity-oriented settings, decision-makers strengthen their position by demonstrating they will follow through and absorb consequences, rather than simply announcing outcomes from a distance.

Caution runs deeper than risk aversion. The decades of conflict between 1980 and 2000 left a lasting imprint, and sudden shifts or opaque reasoning can trigger unease. People may withhold full alignment until they see how a decision unfolds in practice. This means that in cross-cultural teams, a colleague accustomed to fast closure may read hesitation as indecision, while a Peruvian counterpart may view speed without consultation as reckless or dismissive.

Make decision pathways visible: clarify who is being consulted, what inputs are considered, and when closure will happen. Communicate reasoning clearly and acknowledge how different realities were weighed. For significant changes, use phased rollouts or checkpoints to build predictability. Once a decision is made, consistent follow-through often does more to secure trust than the initial announcement.
