# Negotiating

## Working Together

Agreements in Peru grow from relationship and reputation before they rest on terms. Whether you seem trustworthy, respectful, and likely to honour your word shapes the outcome as much as price or timeline. This means the early stages often focus on connection rather than clauses, and a counterpart may return to probe the same ground several times to test whether your approach is consistent and your intentions sound. If the conversation circles back or jumps between topics, this is part of how both sides make sure every angle has been weighed.

Fairness is often understood through reciprocity rather than a single balanced transaction. The Andean concept of *ayni* (an ongoing cycle of mutual obligation and balanced exchange) influences how people evaluate whether a deal feels right, particularly outside Lima. Frame your proposals in terms of mutual benefit over time and what protects both parties, rather than only immediate advantage. Pushing for quick closure without that sense of balance can make the relationship feel extractive.

Regional pride and historical memory add layers to how offers are received. In the highlands and the Amazon, proposals may be filtered through questions of whose constraints are being acknowledged and whether the agreement respects local realities or centres Lima's. In communities that experienced conflict most intensely, attention to security, predictability, and the moral character of the counterpart may run higher. Ask about local priorities and constraints before assuming your preferred structure will work, and avoid framing urgency as a justification for skipping consultation.

Close with clarity. Summarize what has been agreed in plain terms and confirm next steps together, so that trust can attach to what happens after the room clears.
