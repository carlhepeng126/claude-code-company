# Business Meeting Culture

## Meetings & Presentations

Meetings in Peru typically open with a relational warm-up before any substantive discussion begins. This reflects a society where trust has traditionally been built through people and reputation—family ties, shared region-of-origin, or community connectors—rather than through institutional certainty alone. The few minutes spent on greetings, asking about wellbeing, or finding common ground are not filler. They set the emotional temperature and establish mutual recognition. Jumping straight to the agenda can feel transactional or socially careless. If you chair a meeting, allow time for this phase and participate genuinely rather than treating it as a formality to rush through.

Hierarchy shapes how respect is expressed, and this goes beyond titles. Peru's history of colonial-era stratification and enduring class and rural-urban divides means people are often attuned to tone, who is given space to speak, and whether someone is treated as credible regardless of background. Disagreement or challenge delivered bluntly in front of others can feel like a dignity issue, not just a difference of opinion. Frame questions with curiosity, keep your tone calm, and if you need to raise something sensitive, follow up privately rather than putting someone on the spot in the room.

Peru's regional diversity—coast, highlands, and Amazon—can show up in a single meeting as different pacing, conversational comfort, and signals of trust. What reads as efficient directness in one context may feel abrupt or dismissive in another. If your group is regionally mixed, make inclusion explicit: recap key points, check understanding without singling anyone out, and avoid assuming Lima norms as the default.

For some participants, especially those from communities most affected by the 1980–2000 internal conflict, there may be caution around authority, risk, or speaking candidly on politically or identity-linked topics. People often feel out the safety of a space before opening up. Don't expect immediate candour on sensitive issues. Let trust and context guide the depth of conversation over time.
