# Business Lunch & Dinner

## Meetings & Presentations

In Peru, the table functions as both equalizer and measuring instrument. Shared meals allow people to read character through small gestures — how someone shows gratitude, whether they notice what others prefer, how they speak about their own background. Where social origin can still shape how people are treated, these cues take on real meaning. A visitor who dismisses the food, interrupts stories, or treats service staff dismissively may damage trust before business discussion ever begins.

Peruvian cuisine carries regional and family identity. Ceviche, recognized by UNESCO for its communal preparation and consumption, offers more than a meal — it narrates coastal life, shared technique, and collective pride. When a colleague describes a dish or suggests a particular restaurant, they are often sharing something personal. Responding with curiosity rather than comparison allows the relationship to deepen. Avoid ranking what you eat against food from elsewhere; that flattens what is being offered.

Conversation typically moves from broad to personal. Early talk may center on place, family, or travel, settling into rhythm before heavier topics surface. Colleagues from cultures where business discussion begins immediately may feel the meal lacks focus, while Peruvian counterparts may read that directness as transactional or dismissive of relationship. Let the opening course pass before raising difficult subjects, and when you do, keep your tone calm and your phrasing indirect enough to protect dignity.

Timing around festivals and pilgrimages can shift meal culture entirely. Availability narrows, energy redirects, and collective life takes precedence. Keep invitations flexible and be ready to adjust plans without frustration.
