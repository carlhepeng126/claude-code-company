# Managing Suppliers & Customers

## Managing Relationships

Trust in Peru is built through relational proof, not just contracts or credentials. Suppliers and customers rely on what trusted connectors report and what they have seen firsthand. A strong proposal can lose ground if there is no shared contact who can vouch for your reliability. This means investing in face-to-face presence, showing up consistently, and following through visibly on commitments. If something goes wrong, acknowledge it quickly and propose a concrete remedy that preserves the relationship while fixing the issue.

Peru's economic reality includes substantial informality, reflected in national statistics on informal production and employment. This shapes how people judge risk and continuity. A supplier may assess whether you will actually deliver based on reputation and repeat behavior, not solely on formal agreements. Build credibility through predictable accountability: clear commitments, calm ownership of problems, and steady follow-through over time.

Regional identity influences how products and services are received. What works in Lima may feel out of place in the sierra or selva. Customers can judge whether communication respects local context or feels extractive. Avoid assuming Lima as the default market, ask about regional constraints, and tailor messaging accordingly. In areas where Indigenous languages are prevalent, Spanish-only communication can feel like distance or disrespect. Offering translation-friendly formats and clear, respectful language signals service quality and inclusion.

Face-to-face contact remains essential. Relationships should not be taken for granted, especially if you are not based in Peru. Regular visits, even brief ones, reinforce commitment and keep the relationship active in a context where presence is part of how trust is sustained.
