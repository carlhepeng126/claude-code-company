# Feedback and Coaching

## Managing Relationships

In Peru, dignity shapes how feedback is received. In a society marked by visible inequality and class awareness, correction that feels humiliating can register as social demotion rather than professional guidance. People are alert to status cues, and public criticism risks lasting damage to a person's standing within the team. Feedback given privately and calmly allows someone to process the issue and respond without losing face, especially where differences in education, language ability, or background already create uneven ground.

Trust determines how much frankness is possible. Historical conflict and institutional inconsistency have made people cautious in settings where relational safety has not yet been established. Early in a working relationship, a manager's critique may be met with quiet compliance but little real engagement. As trust builds, people become more willing to ask questions, acknowledge gaps, and accept direction. This means feedback is most effective when the relationship has been given time to settle, and when it is framed with curiosity rather than judgment. A question like "can we look at this together?" opens dialogue without triggering defensiveness.

Learning in Peru often happens through guided participation rather than abstract critique. Competence is built by doing alongside someone who models the task, a style rooted in apprenticeship traditions still visible across trades, hospitality, and family businesses. Coaching that offers a clear example, a simple plan, or a shared run-through tends to resonate more than listing what went wrong. Pointing out gaps without offering a path forward can leave people uncertain rather than motivated.

Small relational repairs after critique help preserve the connection. Reaffirming someone's value or acknowledging their effort once the issue has been addressed signals that the feedback was about the work, not the person.
