# Business Lunch & Dinner

## Meetings & Presentations

Business meals in the Central African Republic tend to be modest and practical rather than elaborate affairs. Even in the capital, Bangui, the dining infrastructure is limited, and most restaurants cater primarily to expatriates or aid workers. Local colleagues may prefer invitations to their homes if hospitality is to be extended, as this offers more control over the meal and reflects the warmth of personal welcome. In professional settings, meals are seen as opportunities to strengthen relationships, but the emphasis is on conversation and presence rather than culinary display.

Cuisine draws on cassava, plantain, grilled fish, and stews often flavored with palm oil or peanut sauce. Meals may be served communally, with diners sharing from a central plate, though individual servings are also common depending on the setting. If invited to someone's home, expect the host to guide when to begin eating and how food is served. It's courteous to accept what is offered, even if portions are small, and to eat at a steady pace without rushing.

Alcohol is generally available in hotels and some restaurants in Bangui, but it is not always present at business meals, particularly if colleagues observe Islamic practices or if the setting is more conservative. Following your host's lead on drinks is the safest approach. Similarly, dietary restrictions should be communicated early, as vegetarian or specialized options may not be readily available.

The pace of a business meal is unhurried, and the conversation may move between personal topics and work without a clear boundary. Colleagues from cultures where meetings follow tight schedules may feel the meal is drifting, while Central African colleagues may view a rushed meal as a missed opportunity to build the trust that underpins future collaboration.
