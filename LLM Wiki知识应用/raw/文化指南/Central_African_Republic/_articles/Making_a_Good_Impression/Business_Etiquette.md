# Business Etiquette

## Making a Good Impression

Formal business etiquette in Central African Republic reflects the country's French colonial legacy, but it sits alongside social norms rooted in respect for hierarchy, kinship ties, and community standing. French is the language of official business, and professional settings often mirror French formality in dress, address, and administrative process. Men typically wear suits or formal shirts and trousers, while women wear modest dresses or tailored outfits. Appearance conveys seriousness and regard for the occasion, and turning up underdressed can undermine credibility before a conversation begins.

Greetings take time. Handshakes are standard, but they often come with inquiries about health, family, and wellbeing that should not be rushed. These opening exchanges establish tone and signal whether the relationship will be transactional or grounded in mutual recognition. Skipping this stage, or moving straight into business, can read as disrespectful or dismissive. In settings where seniority is evident, greet the most senior person first and wait for them to set the pace of the meeting.

Titles and formality are expected until a relationship deepens. Using "Monsieur" or "Madame" followed by a surname is the norm in initial meetings. Switching to first names too quickly, especially with senior figures or elders, can feel inappropriate. Conversely, once rapport is established, formality may ease, but let your counterpart lead that shift rather than assuming it.

Gift giving is uncommon in strictly professional contexts and may raise concerns about impropriety. In social or semi-formal settings, however, modest gifts such as quality pens, notebooks, or items from your home country are appropriate gestures once trust has been built.
