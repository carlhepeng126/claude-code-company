# Business Lunch & Dinner

## Meetings & Presentations

Business meals on Christmas Island reflect the island's multicultural makeup, shaped by Chinese, Malay, and Anglo-Australian communities working and living side by side. Dining together strengthens relationships and shows a willingness to engage beyond formal settings. Restaurants tend to be casual rather than formal, often family-run spots known for their laksa, satay, or seafood caught locally. The tone is relaxed but still professional, and conversation flows more naturally here than it might in a boardroom.

Shared dishes are common, especially in Chinese and Malay-style settings where platters are placed in the middle of the table and everyone serves themselves. This communal style invites participation and signals inclusion. Taking small portions and waiting for others to begin shows awareness. Declining to try a dish repeatedly or eating only from your own plate may come across as distant or overly formal.

Alcohol is available in some venues, but the island's Muslim population means it's often absent from menus or simply not offered. Checking in advance avoids awkwardness. If you're hosting, letting your guests choose the venue or asking about preferences shows respect and makes everyone more comfortable.

Meals move at a slower pace than in high-pressure business environments elsewhere. Lingering over tea or coffee at the end is expected, not inefficient. Wrapping up too quickly can suggest you view the meal as purely transactional rather than relational. Letting the conversation wind down naturally builds trust and signals that the relationship extends beyond the immediate business need.
