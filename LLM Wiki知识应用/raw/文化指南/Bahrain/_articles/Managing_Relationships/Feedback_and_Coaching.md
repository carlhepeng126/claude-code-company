# Feedback and Coaching

## Managing Relationships

In Bahrain, giving feedback requires attention to dignity and social standing. Being corrected in front of others can wound a person and their family, and public humiliation lingers in memory for years. Being praised publicly, by contrast, raises a person's standing. The distinction between public and private is not procedural. It reflects the collective dimension of reputation and the care required to protect it.

Feedback often comes wrapped in religiously tinted language. A manager might say "inshallah next time we improve this" or "God knows you work hard, but…" The spiritual framing softens the critique and signals goodwill, turning correction into shared reflection rather than personal attack. This approach helps preserve the relationship even when the message is difficult. Frame criticism in future-oriented terms and affirm the person's value explicitly before addressing the problem.

Younger Bahrainis, especially those with global exposure, often want more direct and actionable feedback, though still within a respectful, face-saving structure. They may ask for clarity in private follow-up conversations rather than in the moment. A junior colleague may nod through feedback they don't fully accept, because challenging a senior openly feels disrespectful, particularly when others are present. Polite agreement does not always mean understanding or buy-in. Invite private questions after the conversation and check for genuine clarity, not just surface compliance.
