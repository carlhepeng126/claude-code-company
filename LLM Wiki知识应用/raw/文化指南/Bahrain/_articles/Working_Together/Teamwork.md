# Teamwork

## Working Together

Collaboration in Bahrain often reflects the structure of a *fareej*, the traditional neighbourhood where families know each other, look out for their own, and share information quickly within trusted circles. That logic can resurface inside departments, where colleagues from the same village, school or extended family cluster naturally, forming informal blocs that exchange news faster and protect each other when things go wrong. For someone outside these circles, the team can feel opaque. Information that travels instantly among some members may reach others slowly or not at all. Earning a place inside that loop takes time and trust, not just competence.

In mixed teams, Bahraini and expatriate colleagues often carry different kinds of work, and those differences can go unacknowledged. Bahraini staff frequently manage the relationship load: navigating ministries, liaising with prominent families, reading regulatory signals, and maintaining the social bridges that keep projects moving. Expatriates, especially those in technical or process-heavy roles, may shoulder more of the execution. Both sides can feel invisible to the other. Understanding this split and naming the value of both contributions helps avoid one group feeling taken for granted while the other feels sidelined.

Even when English is the official language, Arabic often becomes the emotional channel. Tension, humour, and real opinion surface in side exchanges that don't make it into the formal meeting. A discussion can sound calm in English while the Arabic aside signals that the idea won't work or that someone is unhappy. If you don't speak Arabic, watch for shifts in tone or energy, and check in privately with trusted colleagues afterward to understand what wasn't said aloud.

Age and family background shape who speaks up. A younger colleague, especially one who is unmarried or from a less prominent family, may hesitate to contradict an older team member from a well-known lineage, even when their expertise is stronger. Creating smaller subgroups or one-to-one settings gives quieter voices more room and allows contributions to surface without the weight of public hierarchy.
