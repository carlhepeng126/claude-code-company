# Motivating Others

## Managing Relationships

Motivation in Japan is rooted in belonging to a company that behaves properly toward customers, society, and staff. When an organization acts with integrity, employees feel pride and energy. Scandals, cover-ups, or exploitative practices erode that sense of alignment, even when pay remains unchanged. Managers working with Japanese colleagues can tap into this by connecting shared work to company honor and social contribution, such as customer safety, disaster resilience, or community trust, rather than framing projects primarily around revenue targets.

The concepts of *gaman* (enduring hardship without complaint) and *ganbaru* (doing one's utmost) give moral meaning to endurance. People can be highly motivated by shared sacrifice for a valued goal, whether that is earthquake recovery, a crucial product launch, or saving a client relationship. However, a colleague from a culture where individual work-life boundaries are clearly protected may see repeated all-nighters as a sign that something is broken in the workflow. From the Japanese side, that same colleague's readiness to leave on time might initially read as lack of commitment to the team's shared challenge. The tension eases when managers focus on redesigning workloads rather than praising heroic overwork.

Being given an important customer, a technically challenging task, or confidential information often motivates more than public praise. It signals trust and inclusion in the inner circle. Offering Japanese colleagues more responsibility and autonomy in a partnership can be a bigger reward than a flashy award.

Women and younger men increasingly juggle eldercare and childcare alongside long-work-hour norms. For them, a leader who protects personal life boundaries can be more motivating than one who offers only career opportunity. Aligning collaboration with more flexible, humane work patterns makes you a long-term ally, not another source of pressure.
