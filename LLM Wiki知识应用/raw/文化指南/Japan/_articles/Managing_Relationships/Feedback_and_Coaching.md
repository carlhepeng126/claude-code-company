# Feedback and Coaching

## Managing Relationships

In Japan, a manager's role includes protecting the face of the group, not just evaluating individual performance. Publicly singling someone out as a problem threatens the collective sense that "our section is respectable." So critique is softened, wrapped in general terms, and often directed at "we" or "this process" rather than "you." This approach preserves group cohesion and allows the person to correct course without public exposure. For colleagues from cultures where direct, individual feedback is the norm, this indirectness can feel unclear or evasive. From the Japanese perspective, blunt personal critique in front of others can seem unnecessarily harsh and damaging to team morale.

A common coaching pattern involves the senior colleague, or *senpai* (a more experienced peer or mentor), silently rewriting a document, redrawing a slide, or rephrasing an email, then returning it without explanation. The correction itself is the feedback. You are expected to study the difference between versions and understand what needs to change. This method teaches through example rather than discussion, placing the responsibility for learning on the person receiving guidance. If you expect a verbal debrief and don't get one, compare the original and revised versions closely—that comparison is where the lesson lives.

Self-critique after projects or in evaluations is valued as a sign of modesty, moral seriousness, and group orientation. Naming your own shortcomings shows you are reflecting thoughtfully, not that you lack confidence. Asking, "What would you like to do differently next time?" creates room for this kind of reflection and should be taken seriously, not dismissed or met with additional criticism.

Upward feedback is high-risk. Criticizing a superior threatens harmony within the team and challenges the hierarchy that stabilizes the group. Even if a manager personally invites input, others may view open critique as disloyal. As a result, feedback directed upward is heavily filtered, extremely indirect, or delivered through trusted intermediaries. If you want honest input on how a collaboration is working, offer anonymous channels or a third-party facilitator rather than assuming silence means satisfaction.
