# Delegating & Supervising

## Managing Relationships

Authority in Japanese organizations is clear, but it does not work the same way it might in cultures where a senior person can delegate a decision and expect the individual to act independently. Delegation here means assigning responsibility for execution, not handing over the right to decide alone. Decisions still need to move through *nemawashi* (informal groundwork done before any formal proposal) and often through *ringi* (the circulated-approval document system). By the time a meeting happens, the outcome has usually been agreed through prior one-to-one conversations. The meeting confirms rather than debates. A manager from a culture where decisions are made quickly in the room may read this as slow or bureaucratic. From a Japanese perspective, skipping the informal preparation risks rejection not because the idea is weak, but because people have not had time to form their position privately.

Reading what is unsaid is central to supervision. *Honne* (a person's true feelings) and *tatemae* (the public face presented in formal settings) operate as a constant duality. In professional contexts, *tatemae* governs. Disagreement, frustration, or doubt are almost never voiced directly, especially upward. Silence and polite affirmation can mask significant reservations. Building a private channel for honest input, and showing consistently that *honne* will be received without consequence, is how trust is built and how real feedback surfaces.

The *senpai-kōhai* (senior-junior) relationship structures daily interaction, from how knowledge is transferred to how respect is shown. This is rooted in *nenkō joretsu*, the seniority-based progression system that once tied age directly to rank and salary. Though weakening in some private-sector firms, it still shapes expectations around who has the right to speak and how deference is expressed. Understanding where you sit in this web, and navigating it with visible respect for seniority, determines whether you are trusted.
