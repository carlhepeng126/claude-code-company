# Time Management

## Making a Good Impression

Punctuality in Japan reflects respect for others' time and the collective schedule. Trains apologize publicly for delays of thirty seconds, and being even a few minutes late to a meeting without prior warning can undermine trust. The expectation extends beyond start times: presentations, deliverables, and even social commitments are held to the same standard. Arriving five to ten minutes early is common practice, giving time to settle before the formal start. If a delay becomes unavoidable, messaging as soon as you know is expected. The timing of that notification matters as much as the message itself.

Long-term planning is valued over speed, and decisions involve rounds of internal checking, alignment, and *nemawashi* (informal groundwork and consensus-building before formal meetings). What appears from the outside as a quick approval often requires weeks of coordination behind the scenes. A colleague from a culture where decisions are made quickly may interpret this process as slow or bureaucratic, while a Japanese counterpart may view rapid sign-offs as reckless or poorly thought through. When setting joint timelines, account for the time needed to bring everyone along, not just the final decision point.

Last-minute changes create *meiwaku* (trouble or burden for others) and disrupt carefully coordinated plans. Detailed scheduling and early notice of shifts are part of being a reliable partner. When urgent requests are genuinely unavoidable, acknowledge the disruption clearly and explain the reasoning. Even then, the frequency of such requests affects how you are perceived over time.

Overtime culture and the social pressure to stay late remain strong in many firms, despite gradual shifts in some sectors. If you notice chronic late-night emails or weekend responses, consider whether your collaboration is adding unsustainable pressure, particularly if you are working across time zones.
