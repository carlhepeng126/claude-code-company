# Making Contacts & Scheduling Appointments

## Making a Good Impression

Traditional Japanese business culture relies on trusted intermediaries to open doors. A warm introduction from an existing partner, industry association, or senior colleague lowers the perceived risk of bringing someone new into an established network of relationships. This preference stems from concern about *meiwaku*, the trouble or inconvenience a new relationship might create for the group, and from the value placed on knowing someone's reliability before commitment. Cold outreach can work in some sectors, particularly among younger professionals or in companies frustrated by the obligations that come with formal introductions, but it remains a slower and less certain route.

Early meetings are shaped by the *uchi-soto* divide, the distinction between insider and outsider. When you are new, you are firmly *soto*, and formality, careful language, and structured exchanges protect both parties. Becoming *uchi* takes time and evidence that you will invest in the relationship. Demonstrating flexibility in your schedule and willingness to meet more than once, even when early discussions feel procedural, shows that commitment.

Japanese calendars are influenced by seasonal rhythms and fiscal cycles. Golden Week, Obon, New Year, and fiscal year-end in March can make diaries very tight or suddenly clear. Trade shows and quarterly deadlines cluster appointments. Many people work long hours, so daytime schedules often look packed even when evenings remain available for informal drinks.

Behind any formal meeting lies *nemawashi*, the quiet groundwork where proposals are socialized one-to-one before they reach the table. That invisible time is often more decisive than the official appointment. If you want durable agreement, allow space for this process and ask who else should be consulted.
