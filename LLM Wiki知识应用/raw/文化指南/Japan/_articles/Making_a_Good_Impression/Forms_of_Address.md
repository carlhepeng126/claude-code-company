# Forms of Address

## Making a Good Impression

The default in Japanese workplaces is family name plus *san* (Suzuki-san), and that formality holds across most professional settings. First names are rare, even among colleagues who have worked together for years, and switching to given names is usually limited to younger employees in global-facing companies. The suffix *san* expresses standard respect and works for nearly everyone. *Sama* is more honorific and reserved for customers or senior figures. *Sensei* applies specifically to teachers, doctors, lawyers, and politicians. Getting these suffixes right shows you understand the basic grammar of respect.

Japanese also has built-in honorific and humble verb forms that adjust to the relative rank of speaker and listener. Even when the conversation happens in English, your Japanese counterpart is managing those distinctions mentally. That means titles and roles are doing real work. A department head or director expects to be recognized by title, especially in formal settings like meetings or introductions. If you learn someone's title, use it at the right moments. It confirms you have read the structure correctly.

Business cards function as mini-identity documents. How you receive them, whether you study them, and where you place them during the meeting all communicate whether you are paying attention to role, company, and hierarchy. Accept cards with both hands, read them carefully, and keep them visible on the table in front of you. This helps you track who holds what position and signals that you take the relationship seriously.

In email, mirror the formality you receive. If someone signs "Taro Suzuki," still open with "Dear Mr. Suzuki" unless they explicitly invite you to do otherwise. The shift to informality is not assumed. It is offered, and rarely.
