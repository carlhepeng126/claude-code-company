# Business Meeting Culture

## Meetings & Presentations

In Japan, meetings function as formal stages where decisions that have already been shaped in private conversations are affirmed, rather than workshops where ideas are openly debated. The real work happens through *nemawashi*, literally "going around the roots": the practice of quietly briefing stakeholders, sensing reactions, and building alignment before anything reaches the full group. If a meeting feels smooth and orderly, someone has usually invested significant effort beforehand. Judging success by how lively or interactive the discussion appears will miss the purpose entirely.

This approach reflects a culture where preserving group harmony and respecting hierarchy take precedence over individual persuasion. Participants continually *read the air* (*kuuki o yomu*), watching for subtle signals from senior figures, facial expressions, and pauses. Speaking in a way that embarrasses a superior, contradicts the group too bluntly, or shifts the mood unexpectedly brings reputational risk. What might seem like hesitation to a colleague from a culture where decisions are made through open debate is, from the Japanese perspective, the careful work of maintaining collective alignment. In return, that colleague's directness may be read as disregarding the group's shared understanding or rushing past the relational groundwork that makes agreements durable.

Formalities such as seating order, business card exchange, and the careful serving of tea provide a shared script that reduces ambiguity, particularly when people of different ranks or unfamiliar organizations meet. These rituals are part of how seriousness and cultural sensitivity are assessed. If you want to influence an outcome, invest time in pre-meetings with key stakeholders, ask how they prefer to frame the issue, and who else should be involved. Avoid pushing people into open disagreement in front of seniors. Instead, create opportunities for them to offer feedback privately or in smaller groups where the risk to harmony is lower.
