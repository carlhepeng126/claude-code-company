# Business Entertaining

## Meetings & Presentations

Business entertaining in Japan is a deliberate form of relationship-building. The classic *nomikai* (drinking gathering) offers a controlled release from the formality of the office, creating space for jokes, complaints, and even confessions that would never surface in a meeting. Hierarchy relaxes for the evening but returns the next morning, intact and understood. For colleagues from cultures where off-duty time is kept separate from work, this can feel intrusive or performative. From the Japanese side, a refusal to join in at least occasionally may suggest a lack of commitment to the relationship itself, not just the project.

The host's role goes beyond booking a table. Venue choice, price point, food style, and seating arrangements are chosen with care so that no one loses face. The boss should feel respected, junior staff should be able to afford the setting if reciprocity is expected, and outsiders should feel looked after. A poorly judged venue can register as a social misstep, not just an inconvenience. This attention to face reflects the principle of *omotenashi* (hospitality that anticipates needs), though the term itself is rarely used in this context.

In more traditional circles, the first party remains relatively polite. The second round (*nijikai*) and sometimes a third (*sanjikai*) are where more candid talk and genuine bonding often happen. Younger employees, particularly those with caregiving responsibilities or who simply prioritize private time, may leave earlier without being seen as disloyal. The pattern persists, but the pressure to participate until the end has softened, especially in larger companies and among younger men and women.

Accepting some invitations, especially early in a working relationship, shows a willingness to engage beyond the transactional. Declining every offer may be read as disinterest in the people, not just the evening. If hosting, choose venues that are easy to reach, reasonably priced, and able to accommodate dietary or alcohol preferences. That practicality is how care becomes visible.
