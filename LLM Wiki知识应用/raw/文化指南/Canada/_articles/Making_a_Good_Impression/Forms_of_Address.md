# Forms of Address

## Making a Good Impression

Canadians often move to first names quickly, but this ease can mask expectations about courtesy that still run deep. Informality is not the same as carelessness. Respect is expressed through softening tone, avoiding imposition, and keeping interactions socially safe, especially with people you don't know well. Even when someone introduces themselves by first name in the first five minutes, watch how they hold distance through pacing, politeness markers, and how much personal detail they offer. The shift to informal address can be immediate, but the shift to real familiarity takes longer.

In Francophone Canada, particularly Quebec, address choices follow different logic. The distinction between *tu* (informal "you") and *vous* (formal "you") can function as a meaningful respect marker, and the move to *tu* often signals a shift in relational closeness rather than a workplace default. Professional titles like *Monsieur* or *Madame* may be used more deliberately than in Anglophone settings, where titles like Dr. or Professor tend to appear selectively in institutional or credentialed contexts such as universities, healthcare, or legal environments. A manager in a Toronto tech firm may never use a title; a professor in Montreal may expect it in writing and introductions.

Indigenous communities may anchor respect differently, through acknowledging elders, kinship roles, and local protocols rather than relying on Anglophone or Francophone honorifics. Who speaks first, how introductions are made, and whose role is named can reflect community governance and relational responsibility. Defaulting to generic courtesy formulas without recognizing these structures can read as culturally disconnected, even when well-intentioned.

Regional variation also shifts tone. Urban centers like Toronto and Vancouver may normalize quick informality but remain privacy-protective. The Prairies can lean toward friendly directness. Atlantic Canada may bring more overt warmth and conversational ritual. Workplace culture, sector, and individual background often shape the actual interaction more than geography, but knowing the local baseline helps avoid misreads of distance or warmth.
