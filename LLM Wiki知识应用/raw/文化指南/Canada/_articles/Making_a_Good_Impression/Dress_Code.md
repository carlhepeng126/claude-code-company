# Dress Code

## Making a Good Impression

Canadian workplaces value modest, neat, and context-appropriate dress. The expectation reflects a blend of North American practicality and European-influenced professionalism, particularly in major cities and government settings. How you present yourself communicates respect for the context and the people you work with, but extremes stand out. Overly flamboyant or revealing clothing may be read as unprofessional, while high-fashion statements are less important than being well put together. 

Formality varies by sector and region. In government, finance, and law, business formal remains standard: suits and ties for men, tailored suits or dresses for women. In tech and creative industries, smart casual is more common, though people still dress deliberately. Smaller cities and towns may lean more casual overall, but the broader norm is conservative without being rigid.

Climate shapes what counts as appropriate. Layered, functional winter wear is expected and unremarkable. Arriving at a meeting in boots or carrying a heavy coat doesn't signal casualness; it reflects good sense. A colleague from a culture where professional dress means polished shoes year-round might initially read weather-appropriate footwear as too informal, while a Canadian might view rigid adherence to formal dress in snow or rain as impractical. Switching into office shoes once inside is common and removes any ambiguity.

Regional differences matter less than sector and setting, though urban centers like Toronto or Montreal lean slightly more formal than smaller markets. The underlying principle stays consistent: dress thoughtfully for the context, avoid drawing attention through extremes, and recognize that practicality and professionalism coexist comfortably in Canada.
