# Teamwork

## Working Together

Canadian teams favor cooperation and shared decision-making, a style rooted in both Indigenous traditions of collective deliberation and a civic expectation that everyone deserves a voice. Leadership tends to be soft rather than directive. Managers guide more than they instruct, and team members expect to contribute ideas, take responsibility, and participate in shaping outcomes. Singling someone out for excessive praise or criticism in front of the group can create discomfort, both for the individual and for others who may feel the balance of recognition has been disrupted. Teams often socialize together outside work, and the bonds formed matter in how collaboration unfolds day to day.

This doesn't mean decisions are left unstructured. Canadian teams value clarity and equitable participation, but the process is typically democratic rather than top-down. A colleague from a culture where authority is more concentrated may interpret this openness as a lack of leadership, while Canadians working in more hierarchical settings may feel unsure when to speak up or how much autonomy they truly have. Both dynamics benefit from early clarity about how decisions will be reached and who holds final accountability.

Teams are frequently multicultural, which means facilitation needs to account for different styles of contributing. Some members may speak up readily; others may wait to be invited. Creating space for quieter voices without singling anyone out is part of what good teamwork looks like here. Tone also plays a role. Calmness, empathy, and humor help maintain cohesion, particularly when tensions arise.

Regional style varies. Francophone teams may be more animated in discussion, while Anglophone teams may lean toward restraint. In both contexts, respect and emotional intelligence anchor collaboration.
