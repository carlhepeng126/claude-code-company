# Leadership Styles

## Working Together

Leaders in Canada are expected to be fair, transparent, and genuinely consultative. Authority is widely distributed across the organizational structure, and legitimacy comes less from title than from the ability to build agreement and demonstrate respect for others' input. This approach reflects a broader cultural expectation that power should be exercised with humility, not displayed through dominance or personal charisma. Public shows of ego or hierarchical assertion typically undermine a leader's standing rather than reinforce it.

This preference has deep roots. Traditional Indigenous leadership emphasized listening, stewardship, and responsibility to the collective, values that echo in the modern Canadian ideal of servant leadership. The country's multicultural reality also shapes expectations: leaders must navigate diverse teams with cultural sensitivity and inclusiveness, and are judged on their ability to balance differing perspectives without imposing one view. Colleagues want to be heard and involved in decisions, and a leader who consults genuinely and explains their reasoning is seen as stronger, not weaker.

For colleagues from cultures where decisions are made quickly and direction is given clearly from the top, Canadian leadership can feel slow or indecisive. The time spent gathering input may seem procedural rather than strategic. From the Canadian side, a more directive style may come across as dismissive or inflexible, particularly if it skips the relational work of building buy-in. Understanding this helps clarify why a Canadian leader might return to the team for another round of discussion before committing to a course of action.

Effectiveness in Canada means combining clarity with approachability. Leaders should be prepared to explain decisions without defensiveness and to treat consultation as a genuine step in shaping outcomes, not a formality to be completed before proceeding with a predetermined plan.
