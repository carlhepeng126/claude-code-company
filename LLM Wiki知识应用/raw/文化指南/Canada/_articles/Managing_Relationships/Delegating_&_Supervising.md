# Delegating & Supervising

## Managing Relationships

Canadian managers tend to delegate by setting goals and stepping back. The expectation is that people will determine their own approach once they understand what needs doing and why. Micromanagement reads as distrust, and autonomy is understood as a core part of professional respect. When assigning work, explain the context and the outcome you're aiming for, then let people figure out the method. Checking in is welcome, but hovering or prescribing every step will likely cause friction.

Consultation before decisions are finalized is standard practice across most of Canada. Managers are expected to seek input from their teams, share the reasoning behind choices, and make room for people to push back or propose alternatives. This doesn't mean consensus is required, but skipping the conversation tends to create disengagement. A manager from a culture where decisions are made quickly and announced from above may find this process indirect or time-consuming, while a Canadian team member may read the absence of consultation as dismissive or controlling.

Quebec is a clear exception. Workplaces there are more hierarchical, with greater deference to authority and less expectation of bottom-up input before decisions are made. The same approach that works well in Toronto or Calgary may feel out of step in Montreal. If you're managing across both regions, be prepared to adjust how much consultation you build in and how much autonomy you offer upfront.

Disagreement with a manager is normal and not seen as disrespectful, as long as it's raised constructively. Employees across levels are comfortable voicing concerns or offering alternative views, including to senior leaders. Silence in a meeting doesn't always mean agreement.
