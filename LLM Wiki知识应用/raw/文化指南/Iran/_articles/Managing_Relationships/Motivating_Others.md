# Motivating Others

## Managing Relationships

Behind every resignation conversation in Iran is a silent one about leaving the country. Many professionals are quietly weighing whether to stay or emigrate to Canada, Germany, Australia, or the Gulf. Work that feels stagnant or humiliating tilts them toward the airport. Work that feels dignified, developmental, and socially useful pulls them back. For managers, this means showing high-skill staff how a role builds future options, not just loyalty to one firm. Portable skills and visible growth can partially counter the pull of *mohajerat* (emigration).

Motivating people also means understanding what stability looks like in a high-inflation economy. Housing prices in Tehran, children's education, private healthcare, and caring for aging parents weigh heavily on middle-class families. Benefits that stabilize these concerns, like reliable pay in rial terms, health insurance, schooling support, and predictable bonuses, are more motivating than flashy perks or abstract incentives. Explain these clearly and concretely, not as percentages or future promises.

Reputation also motivates. *Khosh-nameh* (good name) is the sense of being known as someone who did something decent under hard conditions. Projects that tangibly improve a local hospital, university, or neighborhood tap into this. When people can say, "If I'm still here in ten years, I'll be proud I did this," and see that impact unfold visibly, the work becomes meaningful in a way that survives cynicism.

Decades of grand ideological language have bred exhaustion with empty slogans. People have heard "sacrifice," "development," and "service" too many times without matching behavior. They now orient toward concrete, small wins they can see and touch. Communicate progress in those terms, and follow through on what you promise.
