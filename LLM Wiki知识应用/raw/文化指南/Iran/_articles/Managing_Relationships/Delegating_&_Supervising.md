# Delegating & Supervising

## Managing Relationships

Authority in Iranian organizations is concentrated at the top, and delegation tends to follow a clear vertical path. Major decisions are made by senior management, and middle managers often have limited room to act independently. Junior staff are given operational tasks to execute, but they do not expect to influence direction or strategy. This concentration reflects both the formal hierarchies that structure organizational life and the relational logic beneath them. In a setting where personal networks often determine career paths and family connections fill key roles, trust flows through established relationships rather than formal processes. Managers supervise closely, and the work gets done through loyalty and proximity rather than autonomous initiative.

Communication is rarely explicit. Employees do not challenge authority openly, and disagreement surfaces indirectly if at all. Managers are expected to notice when something is wrong through tone, gesture, or what is left unsaid. The gap between what is formally stated and what is actually meant requires consistent attention. A colleague from a culture where team members speak up directly in meetings may find the silence frustrating or interpret it as disengagement. From the Iranian side, that directness may read as socially clumsy or disrespectful, particularly if it bypasses the interpersonal care that makes honest exchange possible. Building the kind of trust that allows real concerns to surface happens in private conversations, not formal settings.

Ta'arof, the system of ritual politeness that governs surface interaction, adds another layer. Agreement expressed in a meeting may be social performance rather than commitment. Follow up individually to confirm what was actually understood and whether concerns remain. Creating space for candid conversation means investing in the relationship first, not just managing the task. In public sector contexts, where systemic constraints and limited incentives can affect motivation, this relational investment becomes even more central to getting work done.
