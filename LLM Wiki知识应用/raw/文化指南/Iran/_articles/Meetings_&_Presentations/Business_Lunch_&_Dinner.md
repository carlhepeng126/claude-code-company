# Business Lunch & Dinner

## Meetings & Presentations

In many Iranian workplaces, lunch serves a different function than dinner. Midday meals are often quick and practical — a plate of kebab or khoresh with rice at the office canteen or a nearby restaurant, eaten efficiently before returning to work. Evening meals, by contrast, open up time and privacy for the kind of conversation that builds trust. The longer format allows for subjects to unfold naturally, and the setting itself becomes a space for real exchange rather than refueling. If you're planning a substantive discussion or hoping to deepen a relationship, propose dinner. Lunch may be too rushed to allow for it.

At the end of a meal, expect a brief, sometimes theatrical exchange over the bill. The person who invited typically pays, but the ritual of resisting — "let me pay," followed by "absolutely not, I insist" — is common, rooted partly in ta'arof and partly in genuine generosity. If you extended the invitation, be ready to pay after one or two polite refusals. When hosting, choose a restaurant where you can secure a quieter corner or private area. Conversations that matter require discretion, and open dining rooms can feel exposed.

During Ramadan, iftar meals begin precisely at the call to prayer, starting with dates, water, and soup before moving to heavier dishes. In Muharram, some colleagues may avoid festive settings entirely or prefer simpler, more subdued venues. Rather than assuming the usual rhythm applies, ask directly: "How would you prefer we meet around meals this month?" and follow their lead.
