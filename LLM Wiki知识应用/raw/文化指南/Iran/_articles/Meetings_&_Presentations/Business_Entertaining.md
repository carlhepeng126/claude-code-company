# Business Entertaining

## Meetings & Presentations

Much of the entertaining that builds trust and deepens business relationships happens in private homes, not in public venues. North Tehran apartments, garden houses and family salons become the setting for evenings that may stretch past midnight, with thick carpets, shared plates and wide-ranging conversation that moves between history, politics and personal stories. Being invited to such a gathering means you are being brought inside a circle of trust. These evenings blur the line between personal and professional, and the invitation itself often says more about the state of the relationship than anything discussed at the table.

When entertaining does take place in restaurants, especially in larger cities, the setting will usually be chosen for quality and discretion. Alcohol is prohibited under Iranian law and is not served in public or formal venues. Possession and consumption are illegal and can result in fines or imprisonment, so foreign visitors should expect gatherings to be alcohol-free and should not inquire about or bring alcohol.

Gender and generational expectations vary. In some families, men and women mix freely at the table. In others, particularly when older relatives are present, people may separate into different rooms or circles. Younger hosts sometimes navigate between the expectations of their parents and the comfort of international guests. Follow the lead of your host rather than trying to reshape the gathering to match norms from elsewhere.

Conversations can include music, old Shah-era songs, political jokes and stories about the diaspora, but people are highly aware of who is listening and what might be repeated. You may hear things in a private home that would never be said in a café. If political talk emerges, listen more than you speak. Your hosts carry the consequences of what is said in their home long after you leave.
