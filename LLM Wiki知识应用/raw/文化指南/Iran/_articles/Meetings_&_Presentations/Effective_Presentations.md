# Effective Presentations

## Meetings & Presentations

Iranian professionals expect presentations to stand up to intellectual scrutiny. Engineers, technical specialists and senior managers alike have a high tolerance for dense logic, numerical detail and careful reasoning. They will interrogate your assumptions, test your trade-offs and expect you to show how you arrived at your conclusions. Flowery language or generalized claims without supporting evidence will undermine your credibility quickly. Build your deck around clear problem definition, transparent reasoning and scenario thinking, rather than aspirational slogans. State your credentials early, especially academic or technical qualifications, as expertise commands respect and sets the tone for how seriously your content will be received.

Expect responses wrapped in metaphor. An Iranian counterpart may reply to your proposal with a line from Hafez, a historical parallel or a proverb that conveys approval or reservation indirectly. This is a culturally literate way of signaling agreement or concern without issuing a blunt rejection in front of others. Missing the subtext can mean you walk away believing you have consensus when you have just heard a polite "no." If someone responds with verse or story, follow up privately afterward and ask how that should be understood in practical terms. It signals respect for the cultural register and keeps you aligned.

Any proposal that ignores the realities of sanctions, banking restrictions, import limits or reputational risk will feel naive. Iranian audiences expect you to have thought through which parts of your plan are actually feasible in their context. Include a slide that addresses these constraints explicitly, even if you cannot resolve every detail. It shows you understand the operating environment and are not proposing ideas disconnected from the ground. Prepare materials in both English and Farsi, especially technical notes, to ensure clarity and demonstrate thoroughness.
