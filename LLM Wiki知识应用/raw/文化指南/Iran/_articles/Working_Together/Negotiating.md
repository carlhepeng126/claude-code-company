# Negotiating

## Working Together

Many Iranian negotiators bring what might be called a bazaar mentality to the table, even in modern sectors. The assumption is that your first number hides room and that the real price will emerge through emotional talk, pauses, and a gradual dance toward agreement. Decades of multiple exchange rates and chronic inflation have reinforced this instinct. Your counterpart will expect to test your floor and make space for concessions. Arriving with a single fixed offer can sound either naive or dishonest.

Sanctions have made payment routes a core part of any serious discussion. Iranians have lived through frozen accounts in Dubai, blocked SWIFT transfers, and foreign partners who withdrew under regulatory pressure. If you cannot explain exactly how money will move, which bank will handle it, which currency you will use, and what the backup plan is if that channel closes, you will lose credibility before the commercial terms are even discussed. Put compliance on the table early.

For some partners, particularly state firms, bonyads, and entities with links to the Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps, negotiation is more than commercial. They are also weighing whether the deal aligns with Islamic values, revolutionary legitimacy, and national dignity. Even if these concerns go unspoken, they shape what can be accepted. A small symbolic concession, such as a clause on technology transfer or public framing that emphasizes Iranian capability, can matter as much as the spreadsheet.

The process itself often unfolds slowly. Meetings may pause for prayer, stretch across multiple sessions, or be postponed until after Ashura. The side that remains calm longest often secures better terms.
