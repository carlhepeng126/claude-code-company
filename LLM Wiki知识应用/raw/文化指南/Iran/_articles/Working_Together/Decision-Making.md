# Decision-Making

## Working Together

The person with the title is not always the person who decides. A director-general or CEO may hold formal authority, but a brother in the Revolutionary Guards, a senior figure in a bonyad (foundation), or a trusted clerical adviser may quietly shape the outcome. These invisible players rarely appear on an org chart, yet they can tip a decision yes or no. Insiders know whose blessing is needed; outsiders often do not. Before finalizing a project, ask a trusted colleague directly, "Apart from you, whose approval does this really need?" and listen for non-official names.

Decisions that are politically awkward may never be rejected. Instead, they stop moving. The file goes to sleep in someone's drawer because no one wants to sign a refusal that might be quoted later. This is a form of decision-making in itself, shaped by the need to avoid visible responsibility in a context where factions shift and loyalties are scrutinized. If a proposal stalls, ask honestly whether something political has changed and whether it makes sense to pause or reframe rather than send weekly follow-up emails.

Before a big move, many decision-makers consult beyond the organization. They may check with clerical advisers in Qom, a senior family member, a diaspora cousin, or a contact in another ministry. This informal consultation shapes outcomes but leaves no paper trail. Understanding this means recognizing that the decision timeline is longer and more networked than a formal process suggests.

Publicly, decisions are justified in terms of law, national interest, or Islamic principle. Privately, the motive may be risk avoidance, personal safety, or positioning within a factional struggle. To support a decision-maker, provide two narratives: a clean, defensible public frame and a candid, in-person explanation of how you will manage the real-world risks they face.
