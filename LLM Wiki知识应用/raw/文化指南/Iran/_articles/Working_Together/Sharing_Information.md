# Sharing Information

## Working Together

Sharing information in Iranian organizations follows a strong need-to-know logic, shaped by hierarchy and long experience of political sensitivity. Senior leaders often hold strategic details close, and employees are expected to stay within their defined roles rather than question decisions or ask for broader context. This instinct runs especially deep in government-affiliated or large state-owned enterprises, where formal communication can be limited and opaque. The caution is not bureaucratic habit alone. In a setting where words can be screenshotted, forwarded, and reinterpreted through ideological or political filters, discretion becomes survival. Writing sensitive content in email or group chats is avoided. Face-to-face conversations and one-on-one calls are where delicate topics actually get discussed.

That formality has an informal counterpart. Colleagues exchange updates, gossip, and operational insight over tea or lunch, often with more candor than any official briefing. These peer networks are not a workaround to the system, they are the system. Being connected to the right people often yields more reliable intelligence than any written report. Humor, poetry, and indirect allusion are common ways to surface issues that cannot be named directly. If you hear a joke or metaphor that feels layered, follow up privately later. That is often where the real information sits.

For external partners, this means relationship-building is not a preamble to knowledge transfer, it is the transfer itself. Trust unlocks access. Approach competitive tenders carefully, especially with government bodies. Sometimes the purpose is benchmarking rather than awarding a contract. Assess whether a genuine relationship is forming before sharing detailed proposals. Balance approachability with discretion. Be present and human, but do not create risky visibility for local colleagues, such as posting their images online without consent. In a context where information flows through trusted circles, your credibility and care determine what you learn.
