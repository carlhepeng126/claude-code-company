# Dress Code

## Making a Good Impression

The French Southern Territories host scientific research stations, not traditional business environments, and dress here reflects the demands of extreme Antarctic and subantarctic conditions rather than professional formality. Base staff and visiting scientists wear functional cold-weather gear issued by national polar programs: insulated layers, windproof shells, and thermal boots designed for survival in temperatures that can drop well below freezing. The priority is safety and operational efficiency, and appearance takes a distant second place to staying warm and mobile in a hostile climate.

That practical focus extends indoors. Once inside the heated stations, people typically dress in comfortable casual clothing: fleeces, jeans, thermal layers. There are no client meetings or public-facing roles here, and the small, tight-knit community of researchers and support staff operates with minimal ceremony. Shared meals in the station canteen or evening gatherings in common areas carry no expectation of smart dress. The setting is closer to a remote field camp than a corporate office, and the informality reflects that reality.

For visitors arriving on supply ships or official delegations, the same principles apply. Bring weatherproof outer layers and warm base layers suited to subpolar conditions, and expect to adapt quickly to the station's rhythms. The culture values competence and contribution over presentation, and anyone who turns up prepared for the environment will fit in without difficulty.
