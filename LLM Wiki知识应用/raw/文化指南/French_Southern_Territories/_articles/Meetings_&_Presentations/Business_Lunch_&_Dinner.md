# Business Lunch & Dinner

## Meetings & Presentations

The French Southern Territories have no permanent civilian population and no commercial dining culture. The islands host only rotating scientific research teams and seasonal fisheries personnel, largely based at Port-aux-Français on Kerguelen and smaller stations on Crozet and Amsterdam Island. Meals are taken communally in station refectories, not restaurants, and follow the operational rhythms of isolated field research rather than external business protocol.

When researchers or officials from different national programmes meet, the setting is functional: meals are served at fixed times in shared dining halls, often with limited fresh ingredients due to supply ship schedules. The tone tends to be collegial and informal, shaped more by the egalitarian norms of scientific fieldwork than by diplomatic hierarchy. French administrative presence means French is the dominant language in most settings, and meal conventions reflect metropolitan French habits where they can: bread with meals, wine when available, and a longer midday break when field conditions allow.

For anyone coordinating with teams in the territory, the practical constraint is logistical, not cultural. Visits require months of advance arrangement through the French Polar Institute. Business discussions, if they happen at all, occur around research priorities, environmental monitoring, or fisheries management, and are more likely to take place in meeting rooms than over a formal meal.
