# Feedback and Coaching

## Managing Relationships

In Uganda, feedback works when it feels like care rather than judgment. Correction delivered without relational grounding can feel morally wrong, not simply ineffective. Religious communities reinforce languages of humility, gratitude, and public dignity, and humiliating someone in front of others conflicts with deeply held values about respect and personhood. This means that even necessary criticism needs to be framed in a way that protects the relationship and the person's standing within the team.

A greeting or brief check-in before delivering feedback is part of what makes a hard message bearable. Among the Basoga, for example, the practice of *okulamusa* (formal greeting and acknowledgment) functions as more than politeness. It establishes respect before critique. Without this relational settling, feedback can feel abrupt or dismissive, regardless of how constructive the content. Managers who move straight into performance issues without this opening may find that the message is heard as attack rather than guidance.

Indirect methods carry real cultural legitimacy. Proverbs, stories, and moral framing are respected vehicles for instruction, especially when the relationship between manager and team member involves differences in age, education, or seniority. A manager might use an analogy or shared example to surface a problem without naming the person directly. This allows the recipient to reflect and adjust without losing face. The practical step should still be clear, but the story creates room for self-correction rather than defensiveness.

Feedback should be given privately, with specific examples and a calm tone. Follow-up support, whether a short plan or a regular check-in, signals that coaching is a shared responsibility rather than a one-time judgment. This turns correction into collaboration and makes it easier for people to act on what they've heard.
