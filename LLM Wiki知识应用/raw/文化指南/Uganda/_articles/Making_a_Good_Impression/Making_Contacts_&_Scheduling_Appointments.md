# Making Contacts & Scheduling Appointments

## Making a Good Impression

In Uganda, making contact is more than exchanging information. It's about establishing where you come from in the social sense: who you know, who introduced you, what networks you share. A person is understood through their connections, and introductions work as a transfer of trust. When someone reputable brings you into a conversation, they're lending their name, and that gesture opens doors in ways that a cold email or LinkedIn message rarely can. This logic comes from a deeply relational culture where family, home area, language group, and shared institutions like schools or religious communities anchor identity. Rather than reaching out directly with a project brief, look for mutual contacts who can make a warm introduction, or open your message with a human locating line that explains how you found the person and what shared context you're coming from.

Language shapes ease as much as content does. English is an official language and widely used in professional settings, but everyday belonging often runs through Luganda, especially in and around Kampala, or through other regional languages. Many Ugandans are multilingual and will switch naturally depending on who's in the room. If you're working across language contexts, ask gently what works best and mirror their preference. Don't treat local-language asides as exclusion. They're often a moment of relationship building that supports, rather than undermines, the broader conversation.

Kampala's urban environment produces faster social agility and more mixed-region networks, while rural areas tend to hold denser reciprocal expectations and stronger local authority. These differences affect how relationships are initiated and how much time early-stage trust building requires. In both settings, build in extra time for what might look like casual conversation. Getting situated, socially and personally, is part of how credibility is established, and trying to skip that step to "get to business" can read as impersonal or transactional. In practical terms, this means scheduling meetings with buffer time, confirming appointments by phone rather than relying solely on email, and treating the first conversation as relationship groundwork, not a pitch.
