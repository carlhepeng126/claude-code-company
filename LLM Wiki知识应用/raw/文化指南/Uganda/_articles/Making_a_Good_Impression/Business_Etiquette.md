# Business Etiquette

## Making a Good Impression

Greetings in Uganda reflect identity and care. Offering a warm hello, asking after someone's family or health, and showing patience during these exchanges builds the foundation for trust. This rhythm comes from communal life and oral traditions where relationship and moral grounding precede business. Rushing through small talk or launching straight into agenda items can register as coldness or even unreliability. When someone asks how you are, answer briefly and return the question. If a colleague offers tea or something to eat, accept when you can. Hospitality is how people communicate safety and credibility, especially to visitors.

Communication is often shaped by the need to preserve dignity and relationships. Direct criticism in a group setting, blunt refusals, or open disagreement can feel shaming. Instead, feedback may come wrapped in analogy, through a proverb, or softened with a pause or gentle deflection. A colleague from a setting where candor is valued might read this as vagueness or avoidance, while a Ugandan counterpart might find abrupt critique disrespectful or socially unsafe. Listen for what lies beneath politeness. A delayed answer, a shift in tone, or a story offered instead of a yes or no often holds the real response.

Body language and physical contact vary by region and setting. In many contexts, friends of the same gender may gesture freely or hold hands while speaking, though this is less common in formal business situations. Across genders, contact is more reserved. Eye contact norms also differ. In more traditional or rural areas, especially in Buganda, averting the gaze when speaking to someone senior is a sign of respect, and sustained direct eye contact can read as challenging. Pointing directly at someone is rude. To beckon, use a flat hand with the palm down and fingers curling inward.

Gift-giving requires discretion. Modest gifts that reflect your organization or country are appropriate once a relationship has been established, but avoid anything lavish that might highlight wealth disparity or suggest impropriety. If your counterpart is not Muslim, alcohol may be acceptable. Do not bring flowers or gifts for a colleague's wife without asking first. Business cards have no strict protocol but should be in English, with your title and credentials clearly shown, as status matters in professional introductions.
