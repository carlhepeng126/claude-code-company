# Being a Host & Guest

## Meetings & Presentations

Accepting an invitation to dine with an Ivoirian colleague or client builds the relationship in ways that office meetings cannot. Declining without a strong reason can weaken trust, so if you are invited, go. Reciprocate when you can. The person who extends the invitation pays, and because dining out in Abidjan can be costly, budget accordingly if you are hosting. If you are choosing a menu in advance, remember that many colleagues may be Muslim and decide thoughtfully whether to serve alcohol.

Expect your guests to arrive later than the stated time. Traffic congestion in major cities often delays people, and flexibility around timing is common. At the table, the most senior person eats first. Wait for them to begin before you do. Avoid reaching across others for food or drink, and do your best not to cough or sneeze while eating. These details reflect broader patterns of respect for hierarchy and shared space.

If you are invited to someone's home, the setting can vary. In urban areas, you are more likely to experience a meal served at a table with cutlery. In rural or traditional contexts, you may be invited to eat communally from a shared bowl on the ground, using your right hand to scoop rice and sauce. Men and women may eat separately in these settings. Follow the host's lead: wash your hands when they do, watch how food is served, and begin only after the most senior person has started. If your host is Muslim, alcohol will likely not be offered.

Ivoirians tend to be forgiving of minor slips in etiquette from international guests, but observing these courtesies shows you take the relationship seriously. A toast to the host or the occasion before drinking is a small gesture that registers warmth and respect.
