# Business Lunch & Dinner

## Meetings & Presentations

Traditional Ivoirian food is built around tubers, grains, and bold flavors. Fufu, a sticky dough of plantain and cassava paired with meat and spicy sauce, anchors everyday meals. Along the coast, fish is common, often served with kedjenou, a sauce made from eggplant, tomato, peanut, and okra. The heat in local peppers can surprise newcomers, so it's worth mentioning any preferences around spice early. In upmarket city restaurants, French wine and cocktails are typically available alongside local drinks like ginger beer and palm wine.

For a less formal setting, maquis are outdoor food spots where grilled chicken or fish is served and people gather to eat and talk. Garba, a combination of cassava semolina and fried tuna, is a popular maquis dish. These settings offer a way to share food and build rapport outside formal business environments. If you've only just arrived, ask a trusted local colleague to recommend a maquis and guide you there. Food quality can vary, and acclimatization matters.

Business meals are social as well as professional. Eating together builds connection before work topics surface. Rushing through courses or steering conversation too quickly toward agenda items can feel transactional and may undercut the trust you're hoping to establish. The meal itself is where the relationship forms, and the timing reflects that priority. 

After dinner, colleagues may continue to a bar. Not all are suitable for professional settings, so seek advice from a local contact or hotel concierge before choosing where to go.
