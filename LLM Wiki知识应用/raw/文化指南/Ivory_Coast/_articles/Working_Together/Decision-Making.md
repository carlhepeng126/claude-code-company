# Decision-Making

## Working Together

In Ivory Coast, the most senior person holds the final decision, much like a village chief in a traditional setting. But reaching that point involves consultation and, often, time. Trusted advisors, family members, and sometimes religious or spiritual considerations all feed into the process. The goal is to weigh not just the business merit but the social implications of a choice, especially for the extended network around the decision-maker. Pressing for speed undercuts your standing. It suggests you haven't grasped the relational and reputational stakes involved.

Risk avoidance runs deep, particularly when a decision involves significant change. New deals or major proposals will move slowly through layers of red tape and consultation, not because of inefficiency, but because the cost of a misstep extends beyond the organization. A colleague from a culture where decisions are made quickly and delegated may read this as hesitation or lack of confidence. From an Ivorian perspective, that same colleague's pace may look reckless, as though relationships and consequences don't factor into the equation.

Once agreement is reached, get everything in writing. Specify prices, timescales, and deliverables clearly. Verbal understanding alone won't hold the same force in practice. Junior staff are unlikely to make decisions independently. They look to their manager for direction, so if you need a call made, direct the question to the appropriate level from the start rather than expecting initiative from below.
