# Managing Suppliers & Customers

## Managing Relationships

Business relationships in Ivory Coast rest on personal trust and a proven track record of reliability. Connections are built person to person, not company to company, which means that if a key contact leaves, the relationship will need to be rebuilt from the beginning with their successor. This person-to-person logic also shapes expectations of reciprocity. Once a connection has been established, both parties will expect to draw on it when needed. The question is not whether the relationship will be used, but how and when.

Investing time in face-to-face contact with suppliers strengthens reliability and helps avoid misunderstanding. Clearly articulating what is expected, and why a deadline matters, lays the groundwork for delivery. Suppliers may be working with more constrained resources than you assume, so building in buffer time and checking that the required tools or materials are in place can prevent delays. Offering training is welcomed, as long as it is framed in a way that respects capability and does not imply incompetence.

A colleague from a culture where contracts carry more weight than relationships may view this reliance on personal trust as inefficient or risky. From the Ivorian side, a focus on formal process without investing in the relationship first may come across as transactional and may weaken commitment. Delivering what you promise, consistently and visibly, builds the trust that underpins everything else in the working relationship.
