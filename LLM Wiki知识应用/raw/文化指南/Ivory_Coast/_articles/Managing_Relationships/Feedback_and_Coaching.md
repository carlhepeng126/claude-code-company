# Feedback and Coaching

## Managing Relationships

In Ivory Coast, feedback is shaped by respect for hierarchy and the importance of preserving dignity. Criticism, even constructive, is rarely given directly. To speak bluntly about someone's shortcomings risks public embarrassment and can damage the relationship in ways that are difficult to repair. Managers tend to offer guidance through suggestion rather than correction, framing feedback as advice or observation rather than judgment. This indirect approach reflects a broader cultural preference for maintaining harmony and avoiding confrontation, particularly in settings where personal relationships underpin professional collaboration.

Positive feedback, by contrast, can be shared more openly, especially when it reinforces team effort or collective achievement. Public recognition that highlights the group rather than singling out one individual is generally well received. Praise that draws too much attention to a single person may create discomfort, particularly if it sets them apart from their colleagues or disrupts the sense of shared contribution.

When addressing performance concerns, privacy is essential. A quiet conversation away from others allows the person to receive the message without losing face. Framing the discussion around future development rather than past mistakes makes it easier to accept. Asking questions or offering reflections that invite the person to draw their own conclusions can be more effective than delivering a clear assessment. This method respects the relational foundation of the workplace and gives the individual room to adjust without feeling exposed or judged.

For colleagues accustomed to more direct communication, this indirect style may feel slow or unclear. From an Ivorian perspective, however, directness can seem harsh and dismissive of the care required to maintain trust.
