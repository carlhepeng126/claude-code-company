# Time Management

## Making a Good Impression

Professional Ivoirians can be punctual and pragmatic about time, particularly in business settings, but the broader approach is shaped by relationships and circumstances rather than the clock. Time is flexible because people come first. The day is governed by who needs attention and what matters relationally, not just by what is scheduled. This is a deliberate logic, reflecting the value placed on maintaining connection and showing respect through presence and responsiveness to others.

Status shapes punctuality in visible ways. The more senior a person is, the longer they may keep others waiting. This reflects hierarchy and the understanding that a senior person's time is pulled in many directions. In social contexts, arrival times can stretch several hours beyond what was agreed, and this is expected rather than questioned. Heat, traffic, and unexpected visitors all contribute to delays, but these are not treated as excuses. They are part of the rhythm of daily life.

Deadlines often lack the urgency they hold in cultures where time is treated as a finite resource to be maximized. When someone says they will do something "tomorrow," it may mean later this week, or simply at some point in the future. In traditional companies especially, the drive toward profit maximization or tight customer service timelines may not have been instilled. Close follow-up is needed if a deadline genuinely cannot move. A colleague from a culture where commitments are time-bound may read this as disorganization or lack of seriousness. From the Ivoirian side, rigid insistence on deadlines without regard for context or relationship can seem impersonal or disrespectful.

Proposals sometimes go unanswered, either because they hold little interest or because someone is exercising influence quietly. Decisions move slowly, and this requires sustained engagement rather than impatience.
