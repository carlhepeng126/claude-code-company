# Feedback and Coaching

## Managing Relationships

In Colombia, feedback can be direct and even blunt as long as it comes wrapped in evident care. The phrase "rudo pero con cariño" captures this: people can absorb tough, specific criticism when it arrives with warmth and a clear path forward. What cuts deeply is coldness, particularly in front of others. Feedback that feels clinical or cutting reads as aggression, not guidance. When delivering critique, Colombian colleagues expect both honesty and humanity. Frame it concretely ("en esta presentación pasó X, tuvo Y efecto") and tie it to the person's growth ("quiero que quedes mejor parado la próxima vez"). This combination makes the feedback feel constructive rather than punishing.

A smile and "todo bien, tranquilo" does not always mean everything is fine. It can be a way to preserve dignity in the moment, a shield against public embarrassment or shame. The real reaction may emerge later through absenteeism, disengagement, or quiet complaints shared with trusted colleagues. Managers accustomed to cultures where people voice hurt or disagreement directly may misread this composure as acceptance. Meanwhile, Colombian team members may see immediate confrontation as culturally tone-deaf or socially reckless. After difficult conversations, check in again privately to see how the person is really feeling and what support they need. This follow-up turns a single exchange into a sustained act of care.

Upward feedback is far riskier. Telling a boss their idea is flawed or that a process is broken can feel professionally dangerous. Instead of speaking up directly, employees may funnel concerns through a trusted intermediary, a union representative, or a manager known to be approachable. If you want honest critique from below, build explicit, safe channels such as anonymous surveys, external facilitators, or regular office hours, and visibly reward those who raise issues constructively rather than penalizing candor.
