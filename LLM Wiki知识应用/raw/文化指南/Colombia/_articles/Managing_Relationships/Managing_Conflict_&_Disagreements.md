# Managing Conflict & Disagreements

## Managing Relationships

Many Colombians will work hard to avoid being seen as someone who escalates or stirs up trouble. The phrase *echarse la gente encima* (having people on top of you) captures the social cost of confrontation. Being labelled conflictive can damage your standing with colleagues, and formal complaint channels may feel risky rather than protective. In some regions, the memory of real physical danger attached to confronting the wrong person still shapes behaviour in offices and boardrooms today. Even when that threat is distant, the instinct to downplay problems until they become unavoidable runs deep.

This means disagreements often surface indirectly: through sarcasm, subtle boycotts, sudden absences, or side conversations that bypass the person at the centre of the issue. When these signals are ignored or dismissed, colleagues may describe the situation as *se dañó el ambiente* (the atmosphere is ruined). Once that phrase starts circulating, productivity drops and people begin quietly looking for other jobs or shifting their alliances. Few will name the core problem aloud, but the damage is visible.

For colleagues from cultures where disagreements are raised quickly and directly, this approach can feel evasive or inefficient. From the Colombian side, a colleague who escalates a small issue to a manager may come across as aggressive, naive about how workplaces function, or willing to sacrifice relationships for short-term wins. Both may be acting logically within their own frame, but without adjustment on both sides, the gap widens.

When mediating, focus on repairing relationships and ensuring everyone can move forward without loss of face. Offering confidential routes to raise concerns only helps if people see that using them leads to resolution, not retaliation. Watch for early signs of trouble and intervene gently before the atmosphere is declared ruined.
