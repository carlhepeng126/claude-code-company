# Making Contacts & Scheduling Appointments

## Making a Good Impression

Access in Colombia runs through relationships and shared circles. The concepts of *rosca* (a tight-knit network or "inner circle") and *palanca* (leverage or pull, typically through a well-connected contact) describe how opportunities and introductions actually move. A cold approach from someone unknown often goes nowhere, while a warm referral from a shared lawyer, former employer, supplier, or university contact opens doors quickly. This reflects a business culture built on trust and personal vouching. Before people commit time or share information, they want to know who stands behind you.

Many professionals, including senior executives, coordinate logistics through WhatsApp messages and voice notes rather than long email threads. A brief voice note in polite Spanish, such as "Doctor, le mandé la propuesta por correo, ¿me confirma?" ("I sent you the proposal by email, can you confirm?"), often gets a faster response than a formal follow-up. This preference for quick, conversational contact reflects how relationships are maintained in real time. Following up a formal email with a short WhatsApp message shows you understand how things actually get done.

Security and logistics shape when and where people agree to meet. Colombians weigh neighbourhood safety, time of day, potential protests, roadblocks, weather, and crime risk when accepting invitations. Night meetings or unfamiliar zones can feel risky, particularly for women or those without a car. Suggesting a central, well-known location and scheduling earlier in the day increases the likelihood someone will say yes. Explicit, respectful communication about meeting times and return routes demonstrates awareness of the practical realities people navigate daily.

Regional differences also matter. A lawyer in Bogotá may be more punctual and formal in scheduling, while a client on the coast in Barranquilla may be more spontaneous and flexible with timing, and a *paisa* entrepreneur in Medellín highly responsive but managing multiple deals at once. Adjusting expectations based on region and sector, rather than treating Colombia as a single rhythm, helps avoid misreading responsiveness or follow-through.
