# Sharing Information

## Working Together

Information in Colombian organizations often moves along two parallel tracks. One is formal and documented: email records, PDF reports, slide decks distributed from Bogotá headquarters. The other is intimate and spoken: WhatsApp voice notes, hallway conversations, and face-to-face explanations where people can share nuance, express tone, and deliver warnings they would never commit to writing. The voice note has become a preferred channel for "real talk" because it allows people to convey coded detail and emotion safely, without creating a permanent record that could be screenshotted or forwarded. When someone says "le cuento en persona" (I'll tell you in person), they mean the kind of information that doesn't belong in an audit trail.

This wariness around written records reflects lived experience. Corruption investigations, online smear campaigns, and the presence of armed groups in some regions have taught many Colombians to protect themselves by controlling what they put in writing. Sharing sensitive concerns or accusations via email feels risky. People hold onto information not only for strategic advantage but also for self-preservation. Knowledge circulates more freely within trusted circles, particularly among senior family members in family-owned businesses, but rarely flows openly across departments or down hierarchies.

Regional teams face another layer of difficulty. Staff outside major cities often navigate patchy connectivity, shared computers, and competing local urgencies. Long documents sent from Bogotá may be skimmed or not opened at all. If you design information flows with only urban infrastructure in mind, you risk losing frontline teams entirely. Lighter files, summaries in Spanish, and space to ask clarifying questions help bridge that gap. Complementing formal emails with short, respectful voice notes or calls creates room for people to surface what's really happening without fear.

More progressive multinationals are beginning to share information more openly through intranets and internal newsletters, and younger professionals increasingly see transparency as essential to innovation. Trust still determines what gets shared. Offer channels where people can speak without leaving a digital trail, listen carefully, and respect confidentiality. That's how you move from polite silence to genuine exchange.
