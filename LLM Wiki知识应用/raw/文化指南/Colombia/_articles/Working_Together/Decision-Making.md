# Decision-Making

## Working Together

In Colombia, a yes in the meeting room is often the start of a longer process, not the end of one. Decisions typically require multiple layers of approval that may include legal, compliance, regional bosses, and sometimes tacit sign-off from political patrons or family members. The person you are negotiating with may have the title and the authority, but they rarely hold every lever needed to move forward. Understanding this layered reality helps explain what can look like delay or hesitation to colleagues from cultures where senior figures decide quickly and independently. From the Colombian side, a counterpart who pushes for immediate closure may come across as unaware of how organizations actually work, or worse, as someone trying to bypass necessary safeguards.

Risk calculations in Colombia include factors that rarely appear on a slide. In certain regions, investment decisions take into account how different armed groups might respond, or whether a venture might attract unwanted attention. This is not background context. It is live assessment that shapes what gets approved. The concept of "no dar papaya" (don't make yourself an easy target) runs through decision-making at every level. Proposals that expose the company to scandal, involve dubious partners, or lack clear documentation make approval much harder, because they increase vulnerability. If you can show explicitly how your plan avoids giving papaya through robust controls, transparent partnerships, and solid governance, you make yes easier to give.

When operating in sensitive areas, acknowledge security and informal power structures in side conversations, even if they do not feature in formal presentations. Ask early about internal approval paths and help your counterpart build the case for other forums. Decisions in Colombia are shaped by relationships, emotion, and context as much as by data, and treating the visible decision-maker as the only one who needs convincing will slow everything down.
