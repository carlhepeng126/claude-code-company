# Business Etiquette

## Making a Good Impression

Professional life in Belize runs on informality, warmth, and a real sense of personal connection. Business interactions feel closer to conversation than ceremony. People use first names quickly, greetings are friendly, and a relaxed tone is the norm, even in settings that might carry more formality elsewhere. This reflects a culture where relationship comes before procedure, and where trust is built through personal warmth rather than polished performance.

Handshakes are common and generally casual. Between men and women, a handshake is fine unless someone signals otherwise. Eye contact is welcomed as a sign of sincerity, though it tends to be softer than in more direct communication cultures. Someone from a setting where firm handshakes and sustained eye contact are read as confidence might register Belizean ease as reserved. From the other direction, a Belizean colleague might find a highly structured introduction overly formal or slightly stiff.

Small talk is more than politeness. Asking about family, weekend plans, or recent events helps establish who you are as a person, not just a role. Jumping straight into business before this groundwork is laid can feel abrupt. This is especially true outside Belize City, where a slower pace and stronger community ties shape how people relate. In urban settings or with younger professionals, interactions may be quicker and more transactional, but the expectation of friendliness still holds.

Dress codes vary by sector and setting. Government or formal meetings call for business wear, but many workplaces lean toward business casual, especially given the heat. Following your host's lead is the safest approach.
