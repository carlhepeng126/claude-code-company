# Business Lunch & Dinner

## Meetings & Presentations

Lunch in the Czech Republic is still often a hot, cooked meal, even for people working in offices. Many workplaces have canteens or nearby restaurants that offer a daily polední menu, making a midday business lunch feel natural rather than formal. Suggesting a place known for its lunch menu shows you understand how people actually eat, and it keeps the setting grounded rather than overly ceremonial. This means lunch can be a useful way to discuss work while still feeling routine.

Czechs often introduce difficult topics during pauses between courses or after dessert rather than announcing them upfront. A colleague might raise pricing concerns, timelines, or risks as the meal winds down, framed casually but meant seriously. A counterpart from a culture where agendas are strictly followed may wonder why the real issue arrives so late. From the Czech side, raising something delicate during a formal agenda point can feel abrupt or confrontational. The meal creates room to test the ground first. Be ready for these moments and treat them as part of the conversation, even if they weren't signaled in advance.

Regional food often becomes a way to share identity without revealing much personal detail. Moravian wine and duck, South Bohemian carp, or newer Prague fusion menus are chosen deliberately to show you a version of the country. Showing curiosity about what's on the table gives your host an opening to take quiet pride in something local. It's a low-risk way to build rapport without forcing intimacy.
