# Effective Presentations

## Meetings & Presentations

Czech audiences often think like engineers, regardless of their formal background. They want to understand how something works, where it might fail, and what it will cost. A polished story without underlying mechanics will feel insubstantial. Presentations that succeed anchor benefits to architecture diagrams, process steps, or real numbers. This approach reflects a broader preference for factual, linear argument over persuasive narrative. People listen carefully and won't interrupt, but they're mentally testing each claim for robustness as you speak.

A recurring reflex to anticipate is the "not in our conditions" filter. Czech professionals will instinctively scan a global case study for reasons it wouldn't apply to their context: smaller market size, different labor regulations, or local customer expectations. If you present without acknowledging these distinctions, your credibility drops quickly. Show how you've adapted the solution to Czech realities rather than importing it wholesale. This doesn't mean apologizing for ambition, but it does mean demonstrating that you've done the work to understand the constraints your audience navigates daily.

Self-aware humor can strengthen rapport, particularly when it acknowledges bureaucratic or structural absurdities. A visual that nods to tangled processes or local quirks signals that you see their reality clearly. This builds trust without trivializing genuine frustrations. Keep language stripped down, whether in Czech or English. Ornate phrasing or corporate jargon makes your message harder to decode and suggests you're more interested in polish than clarity. Straightforwardness earns respect; boastfulness or overenthusiasm makes people uncomfortable.

Expect limited verbal feedback during the session. Thoughtful consideration often happens in silence, and questions may only surface later in smaller settings. Don't mistake quiet for disengagement.
