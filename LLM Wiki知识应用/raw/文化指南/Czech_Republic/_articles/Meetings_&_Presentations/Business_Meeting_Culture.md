# Business Meeting Culture

## Meetings & Presentations

The formal meeting in Czech workplaces follows protocol, but the real verdict often comes later. Decisions are made slowly and hierarchically, but candid views surface in the *hospoda* (pub) afterward, where colleagues admit what made sense and what did not. Leaving immediately after a meeting means missing the actual assessment. If a project matters, stay for that second conversation and ask directly what seems unrealistic. This is where genuine concerns get aired without the politeness that can mute the meeting itself.

Criticism does not always arrive head-on. If people think a plan is flawed, expect hyper-literal questions, innocent misunderstandings, and jokes that expose weak points. This indirect resistance reflects a Švejk-style reflex: absurdity gets revealed through exaggerated compliance or humor, not confrontation. If the same issue keeps appearing in a humorous guise, treat it as a serious concern. Colleagues from cultures where disagreement is voiced directly in the room may read this as evasion, while Czechs may find blunt challenge aggressive or culturally tone-deaf. Recognizing humor and questions as data, rather than ignoring them, keeps negotiations grounded.

Regional tone varies. Prague meetings can feel drier and more ironic, with quick cutting remarks. Moravia may bring warmth and folklore references; Ostrava humor tends toward the blunt and dark. The content stays serious, but the conversational style shifts.

Silence is not necessarily agreement. A quiet room may mean polite skepticism, especially if no one wants to derail proceedings in front of a foreign partner. Follow up privately after a very quiet session and ask individuals what they would change. You will hear more honestly one-on-one.
