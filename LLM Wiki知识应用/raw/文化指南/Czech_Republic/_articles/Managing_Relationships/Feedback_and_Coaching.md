# Feedback and Coaching

## Managing Relationships

Critical feedback in Czech workplaces is expected to be concrete, delivered privately, and free of unnecessary emotional framing. Many Czech professionals are uncomfortable with confessional or feelings-based language of the kind common in some American workplace cultures. Feedback that centres on "I feel" or "this made me feel" can register as theatre or manipulation. What works better is a description of what happened, what broke as a result, and what needs to change. The focus stays on behaviour and outcome, kept slightly dry and to the point. This preference reflects a broader cultural wariness of performative emotion in professional settings, where being direct without excess is a mark of seriousness.

Public criticism violates slušnost, a core Czech norm roughly translated as basic decency or propriety. Calling someone out in front of others, even if framed lightly or humorously, can feel like public shaming. People may not react visibly in the moment, but trust erodes quietly. The person giving the feedback may find themselves mocked later or simply avoided. For a manager from a culture where robust group discussion or public challenge is common, this can feel puzzling. From a Czech perspective, a colleague who delivers critique in front of the team may seem to lack social awareness or respect, regardless of the content itself.

Humour often functions as a feedback channel. Running jokes about "our legendary reporting system" or "our agile transformation" are rarely just banter. They usually contain precise criticism about deadlines, tools, or leadership decisions, framed as entertainment to avoid direct confrontation. If the same theme keeps surfacing in team jokes, treat it as information. Ask what specifically is causing frustration and be prepared to address it. Dismissing the humour as harmless venting misses the point.

Generational differences shape expectations around frequency and flow. Older employees may be used to feedback mainly when something has gone wrong. Younger professionals, especially in tech or creative sectors, expect regular two-way conversations and will leave quietly if they feel ignored or stagnant. Offering clear development steps, inviting upward feedback, and creating space for dialogue can make the difference between retention and quiet resignation.
