# Business Etiquette

## Making a Good Impression

In Czech professional life, people respect colleagues who know their work and deliver quietly. The admired figure is someone who solves problems without posturing, who speaks plainly and doesn't inflate their role. Big egos and grand gestures tend to land badly. This preference reflects a long exposure to ideological language under communism, followed by waves of corporate rhetoric after 1989. The result is a deep scepticism of slogans. If you speak in brochure language about vision, transformation, or passion, your colleagues will mentally file it as marketing and wait to see what you actually do. Show competence through preparation and follow-through, and translate corporate-speak into grounded, honest language that says what you mean.

In a culture where religious affiliation is rare, moral standing is measured by fairness, reliability, and how power is used. Someone can be openly atheist and still be judged highly moral. Someone else can talk about values all day and be seen as hollow. The term *slušný člověk* (a decent person) describes this standard: steady, modest, and fair in dealings. National or religious rhetoric does not win favour here. Behave like a *slušný člověk*, and that will be noticed and remembered.

Humour has a place in Czech professional etiquette, particularly the self-mocking kind. A little self-deprecation softens edges and shows you don't take yourself too seriously. But humour should not replace responsibility. Colleagues are watching to see if you can be both light and serious when it counts.
