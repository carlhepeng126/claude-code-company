# Teamwork

## Working Together

Czech teams value the person who quietly carries their share. This expectation has roots in weekend rituals: generations raised on tramping trips and time spent at the family chata (cottage) or chalupa (country house) learned early that everyone chops wood, washes dishes, and carries their own backpack. The ideal teammate does unglamorous work without performing or seeking credit. In practice, this means the colleague who stays late to fix a broken process or who picks up someone else's loose end without announcing it earns respect. The person who only manages, presents, or directs without helping with practical fixes can be seen as dead weight.

This extends to how teams value skill. The colleague with zlaté české ručičky (golden Czech hands), who can tinker, improvise, or repair when official systems fail, holds real standing. Recognition flows to the quiet fixers who make things work, not just to those who talk about them. A manager from a culture where visibility and self-promotion drive advancement may assume a Czech team member lacks initiative when they do not speak up about their contributions. In turn, that Czech colleague may view the manager's self-presentation as inflated or unearned if it is not matched by hands-on problem solving.

Levelling humor binds Czech teams together. Everyone, including the manager, can be teased. Taking yourself too seriously or bristling at harmless jokes signals you are outside the group. Joining in, and accepting being teased yourself, shows you are ready to be treated as one of the team rather than a fragile outsider. This humor is a form of equality, not hostility.

Regional styles shape how people contribute. A dry, ironic analyst from Prague, a warm South Moravian colleague who brings wine and cake, and an Ostrava engineer with blunt humor all consider themselves Czech, but their social tempo and comfort with small talk differ. Mixed teams work better when Prague voices do not drown out Moravian or industrial-region colleagues in discussions.
