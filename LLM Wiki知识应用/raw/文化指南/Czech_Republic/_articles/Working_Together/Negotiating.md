# Negotiating

## Working Together

Czech negotiators expect to see the working. Conversations often end with someone suggesting they "throw it into Excel" — *pojďme si to hodit do Excelu* — and build a shared spreadsheet where both sides can model scenarios, check margins, and test assumptions together. This reflects a wider preference for transparent, checkable numbers over black-box pricing. If you arrive with a fixed figure but cannot explain how you got there, your counterpart is likely to push for access to the logic underneath. Come prepared to open your calculations and model options in real time, rather than defending a final number from behind closed doors.

The tone around the table is usually calm, slightly ironic, and rarely theatrical. Voices stay measured, humor is dry, and the atmosphere can feel surprisingly relaxed. But this surface composure can mislead. A lack of drama does not mean a lack of conviction. Czech negotiators often hold firm positions under that quiet exterior, and a collegial vibe should not be mistaken for easy concessions. If you need a point to move, explain why in the same rational, even style, and expect the same in return.

When a proposal feels unworkable, many Czech counterparts will respond with apparently innocent, literal questions rather than open confrontation. This draws on a long tradition of using straight-faced inquiry to expose absurdity — a conversational strategy familiar from the character Švejk. Treat these questions as early warnings, signaling that part of your offer will not survive local reality: supply chains, labor law, or on-the-ground norms. They are invitations to rethink, delivered without fanfare.

A fair deal is one that both sides can actually sustain over time — *ať to vydrží*, it has to survive reality. Pressure that leaves the smaller partner with no buffer, even if they sign, will be seen as short-sighted or extractive. Frame your offer in terms of mutual durability, not just your own win, and show how the margin allows both businesses to weather a difficult year.
