# Teamwork

## Working Together

Traditional Korean teams run on loyalty, respect for seniority, and a shared understanding that the group's success reflects on everyone inside it. Individual preferences take second place to collective goals. This isn't a soft preference; it's how credibility is built and how work gets done. Age and rank shape who speaks first, who mentors, and who executes. Older colleagues often take on informal mentoring roles, guiding younger team members through lived example and side conversations rather than formal instruction. Younger colleagues, in turn, shoulder much of the execution load and demonstrate commitment through long hours and visible effort.

Shared rituals reinforce this cohesion. Team dinners, company outings, and year-end events aren't optional add-ons; they build the sense of belonging and mutual obligation that holds teams together. These gatherings also serve a practical function: they create the relational trust that makes coordination smoother and disagreement safer to navigate later. If you're leading a mixed team, recognizing group achievements rather than singling out individuals aligns with this collective identity. Publicly praising one person can create discomfort for them and resentment among others.

Generational contrasts are now common. Younger professionals, especially those in global companies or startups, expect more horizontal collaboration and structured opportunities to contribute. Older norms of deference and top-down decision-making still hold in many settings, but newer expectations around feedback and input are reshaping how teams function. Setting explicit norms around how decisions are made and who speaks when reduces the friction that arises when these two models meet. Structured turns to speak, for example, help quieter or junior members contribute without having to challenge hierarchy directly.

Leaders who show genuine care beyond task delivery strengthen both loyalty and performance. Checking in on wellbeing, acknowledging personal challenges, and supporting team members through difficulty are read as signs of leadership quality, not overreach.
