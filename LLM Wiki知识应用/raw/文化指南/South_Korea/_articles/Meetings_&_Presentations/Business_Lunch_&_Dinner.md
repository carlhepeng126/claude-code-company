# Business Lunch & Dinner

## Meetings & Presentations

Business meals in South Korea blend hierarchy, care and relational warmth. The phrase "have you eaten?" is a common greeting that reflects how food is tied to well-being and concern for others. Inviting a colleague to lunch or dinner is not just hospitality but a way of showing that the relationship extends beyond the transactional. Mealtimes offer space for personal conversation, humor and rapport-building. If the entire meal focuses only on work, the interaction can feel one-dimensional and may miss the opportunity to deepen trust.

Korean meals are communal by design. Shared side dishes called *banchan* (small vegetable or pickled dishes) and central platters encourage everyone to eat from the same table. Traditional foods like barbecue, stews and noodles sit comfortably alongside global cuisines. The senior or host typically leads the ordering and begins the meal, so let them set the tone before starting. Following this order reflects respect for hierarchy and allows the event to unfold smoothly.

Hosts often encourage guests to eat generously, though finishing everything is not expected. Korean food is often heavily seasoned with garlic and chili, so checking on spice tolerance or dietary needs in advance helps avoid discomfort. If you are open to trying key dishes, it signals interest and respect. Bringing up dietary restrictions early allows the host to adjust without awkwardness.

For a colleague from a culture where meals and business are kept separate, the personal turn that South Korean business dinners take may feel unfamiliar. From the Korean side, a guest who remains strictly formal throughout the meal may seem distant or hard to connect with. The meal is where the relationship takes shape.
