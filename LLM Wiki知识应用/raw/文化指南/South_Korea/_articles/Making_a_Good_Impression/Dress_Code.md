# Dress Code

## Making a Good Impression

In South Korea, presentation reflects respect for the people you are meeting and the setting you are in. Historically rooted in Confucian norms that value modesty and restraint, this attention to appearance has evolved in a culture now shaped by K-pop, fashion industries, and global media. What has remained constant is the belief that how you look reflects how seriously you take the occasion. Even in casual settings, people tend to dress deliberately rather than carelessly.

In formal sectors like finance, law, government, and large conglomerates, conservative business attire is expected. Men wear dark suits with neutral ties, and women choose suits or tailored dresses in subdued tones. Grooming matters as much as the clothes themselves. Neat hair, polished shoes, and a put-together appearance show that you have taken the time to prepare. Avoid anything too revealing, overly flashy, or eccentric, especially when meeting senior colleagues. A colleague from a culture where personal expression through clothing is encouraged may find this level of restraint limiting, while a Korean colleague might read bold or highly individualized dress as a lack of awareness about professional norms.

Start-ups, tech companies, and creative industries allow more room for style and a relaxed baseline, particularly among younger staff. Even here, the standard is polished rather than casual. The difference is in degree, not in principle. People still notice the effort you put in, and neatness remains a baseline expectation across all sectors.

If you are uncertain, err toward formality and watch how colleagues at your level dress. Adjusting your presentation to match the context is not conformity. It is a practical way to signal that you understand the setting and the people in it.
