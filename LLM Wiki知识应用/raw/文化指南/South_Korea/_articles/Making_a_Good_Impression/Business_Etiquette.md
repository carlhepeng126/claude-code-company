# Business Etiquette

## Making a Good Impression

Professional behaviour in South Korea is shaped by Confucian ethics that still define how people relate across age, rank and seniority. Language levels, bows and two-handed gestures when giving or receiving items are not simply polite habits. They mark your awareness of hierarchy and your willingness to participate in patterns that preserve respect and minimize friction. Skipping these small acts, like handing over a business card with one hand or failing to acknowledge the most senior person first, can suggest unfamiliarity or disregard, even when that's not your intent.

Business cards are exchanged with both hands, received thoughtfully and placed carefully rather than pocketed immediately. Greetings, seating arrangements and even eye contact follow seniority. Someone joining from a culture where informal address and flat interaction signal openness may read this formality as stiff or overly cautious. A Korean colleague, in turn, may interpret informal greetings or casual posture as a lack of seriousness or poor preparation, especially early in the relationship.

Public confrontation or blunt criticism risk causing loss of face, which in Korean is tied to *kibun*, a sense of dignity and emotional equilibrium that underpins trust. This means feedback is typically given in private, disagreement is softened and apologies or expressions of gratitude are used generously to demonstrate character and intent. If you work in a setting where direct challenge is valued as clarity, recalibrate. What shows competence in one context can close doors in another.

This formality is strongest in traditional sectors and among senior professionals. Younger Koreans and those in global industries may adopt more relaxed styles, but the underlying expectation of respect through gesture and tone remains present. Showing humility, attentive listening and care with ritual details builds credibility faster than asserting expertise alone.
