# Delegating & Supervising

## Managing Relationships

French management culture reflects a legacy of centralized power rooted in the Napoleonic era, when the *grandes écoles* system was created to produce a technically and intellectually superior leadership class. More than 80% of leaders in France's top 40 companies today graduated from just three schools: HEC, ENA, and Polytechnique. This produces a distinctive kind of authority: French managers lead because they are understood to have a broader, more rigorous grasp of complexity than those beneath them. Authority without intellectual depth is quietly but consistently undermined. If you arrive with a title but without substantive understanding of your domain, expect your ability to lead effectively to erode quickly.

This centralized structure shapes delegation in practice. Power concentrates at the top, information flows along formal hierarchical lines, and junior team members often pass problems upward rather than taking initiative on the spot. A customer complaint, for example, may need to travel all the way to the supervisor before it can be resolved, because those lower in the chain do not see it as their responsibility or authority to act. Managers from cultures where delegation means granting broad autonomy may read this as passive or risk-averse. From a French perspective, a manager who distributes decision-making too widely may seem unaware of their own role or unwilling to provide the clarity their team expects.

Debate is valued, but within a clear framework. The French tradition of *disputatio* welcomes vigorous intellectual challenge before a decision is made, and doing it well earns respect. Team members who push back are showing engagement, not disrespect. Shutting down this debate too early loses both their best thinking and their trust. Once a direction is set, however, it should be closed decisively. Visible uncertainty or consensus-seeking after a decision has been made erodes credibility. French professionals are comfortable with authority that is clear and well-reasoned, and they expect their manager to provide it.
