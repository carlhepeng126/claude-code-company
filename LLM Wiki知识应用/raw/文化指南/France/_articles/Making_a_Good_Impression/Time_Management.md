# Time Management

## Making a Good Impression

French professionals respect scheduled meetings and agendas, but start times can flex slightly, particularly in internal settings or in cities where traffic is unpredictable. This elasticity does not mean meetings are unimportant. It reflects a view that a few minutes of delay should not derail the broader purpose of the conversation. External meetings, especially with clients or senior stakeholders, call for sharper punctuality, where showing up on time signals both professionalism and respect for hierarchy.

Meetings in France tend to run longer than in cultures where the focus is purely operational. Time is spent framing the problem, discussing context, and debating principles before moving to action. This reflects a deeper intellectual culture where understanding why something should be done holds as much weight as deciding how to do it. For colleagues from cultures that move quickly from discussion to decision, this can feel repetitive. For French participants, a colleague who pushes for conclusions without sufficient debate may seem superficial or overly tactical. If you need genuine buy-in, allocate enough time for this exchange. Compressing it into a short slot often backfires.

Lunch still holds cultural significance, even in big cities where the window has shortened. Midday is not just another calendar slot. Many people dislike formal meetings scheduled deep into the lunch hour, and proposing one without asking preferences first can create quiet frustration. Pre- or post-lunch slots tend to work better for collaborative sessions.

Holidays, long weekends, and strikes can disrupt project timelines. Many French people view vacation as a right, not a luxury, and working through August or school breaks is rare. Build these periods into joint schedules from the outset to avoid last-minute tension.
