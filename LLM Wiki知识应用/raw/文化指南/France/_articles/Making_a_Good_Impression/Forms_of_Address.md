# Forms of Address

## Making a Good Impression

Initial professional contact in France opens with "Madame" or "Monsieur" paired with the surname, and a clear "Bonjour." Skipping the greeting or moving straight to a first name reads as abrupt or dismissive, because formality here protects dignity and establishes mutual respect before anything else. This applies whether you are meeting face to face, writing an email, or entering a shop. The form creates a respectful boundary that both parties control.

The shift from *vous* to *tu* is a negotiation, never automatic. *Vous* maintains polite distance and recognizes professional standing. *Tu* signals either intimacy or hierarchy, such as a manager addressing a junior colleague or a parent speaking to a child. Moving to *tu* happens when the senior person or the French counterpart invites it, often after months of working together. In email, open with "Bonjour Madame Dupont" rather than "Hi Claire" until that invitation comes. A colleague from a culture where first names are used immediately may read this formality as coldness, while a French colleague may read the reverse as presumptuous or careless with social codes.

Professional titles—Doctor, Professor, Maître for lawyers, Ingénieur in some technical settings—matter in formal contexts such as invitations, client events, or public meetings. In day-to-day email once a relationship is established, these titles become less prominent, but their presence in introductions and ceremonies shows that you understand local codes and take the person's role seriously.

Making an effort in French, even if imperfect, is read as respect for the person and their culture. Many international firms switch to English, but opening a meeting or email with a short French greeting builds goodwill and softens the transition into negotiation or collaboration.
