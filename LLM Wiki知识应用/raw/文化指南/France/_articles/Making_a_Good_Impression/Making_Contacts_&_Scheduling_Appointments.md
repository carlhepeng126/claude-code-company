# Making Contacts & Scheduling Appointments

## Making a Good Impression

In France, introductions often travel through institutional channels that have deep roots. The school you attended, particularly one of the *grandes écoles* (elite higher education institutions), the professional body you belong to, or the ministry network you trained in can all quietly open doors. These networks matter because they signal shared formation and a common frame of reference. When seeking access to a senior contact or a guarded organization, knowing who studied with whom or who belongs to which *corps* (alumni network from schools like ENA or Polytechnique) can shift a cold outreach into a warm one. If you share an institutional link or can be introduced through someone who does, use it. That connection resonates more strongly than credentials alone.

Formal process still shapes the approach. Emails should be well-written and clear, assistants are real gatekeepers, and written requests may be expected. But things often move when someone inside the system personally champions your case. Combine a structured outreach with an informal nudge through a contact who can sponsor you internally. A polite email to a general address may sit unanswered, but a follow-up from a mutual colleague can bring a reply within hours.

Paris remains the gravitational center. Many headquarters and key decision-makers sit in or near the capital, and even when you're working with a regional office, the Paris calendar often sets the pace. Factor this into your planning when scheduling meetings or aligning timelines across sites. Recognizing where authority sits helps you direct your requests appropriately and avoid waiting for decisions that need to travel back to the center.

August, school holidays, and periods of strikes or demonstrations can heavily disrupt availability. People guard vacation time carefully, and trying to schedule around these rhythms rather than through them will make collaboration smoother and signal that you understand how the year is structured.
