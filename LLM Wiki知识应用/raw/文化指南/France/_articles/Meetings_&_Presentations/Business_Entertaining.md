# Business Entertaining

## Meetings & Presentations

Business relationships in France are often built over coffee at a café, apéros (pre-dinner drinks), or a leisurely bistro lunch. The line between professional and social runs fluid here. Time spent discussing politics, culture, travel, or ideas isn't a warm-up to the real conversation—it's where people assess intelligence, compatibility, and trustworthiness. A colleague from a culture where meals focus tightly on business may wonder when the work talk will begin, while French counterparts may read a quick pivot to transactions as a sign that the relationship hasn't deepened. For them, the exchange of ideas is relational groundwork, not detour.

Restaurants are the usual setting, and lunch is more common than dinner. If you issue the invitation, you're expected to pay. Ask your French contact for suggestions and book ahead. Wine or apéritifs are standard, but professional contexts value appreciation over excess. Knowing when to stop reflects savoir-vivre, the sense of what's fitting. Being visibly impaired undermines credibility.

Home invitations are rare and deliberate. The French don't casually open private space to outsiders, so being asked to dinner at someone's home signals real trust. Spouses are typically included. Bring flowers, fine wine, chocolates, or a book with intellectual appeal—avoid logo gifts or anything too personal unless you know the person's taste well. As trust grows, reciprocate thoughtfully. The invitation itself, not just the meal, cements the partnership.
