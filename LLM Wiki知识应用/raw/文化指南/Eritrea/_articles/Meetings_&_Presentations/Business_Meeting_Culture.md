# Business Meeting Culture

## Meetings & Presentations

Meetings in Eritrea tend to be formal, especially when government institutions or senior figures are involved. The country's social structure gives considerable weight to hierarchy and seniority, so invitations should reach decision-makers directly, and it's often those at the top who determine outcomes. Inviting the wrong level can slow progress or result in follow-up meetings that could have been avoided. Arranging a meeting may take longer than expected. Communication infrastructure can be limited, and approvals sometimes move through several layers before confirmation arrives. Building in extra lead time and confirming details more than once will help avoid misunderstandings.

Once the meeting begins, expect a degree of reserve. Eritreans often use the opening phase to assess trustworthiness and intent, particularly with new or foreign partners. Small talk may be brief or minimal, especially in government or parastatal settings where formality prevails. Directness should be measured. Overly assertive questioning or pushing for quick commitments can be read as disrespectful or impatient. Decisions are rarely made on the spot, and pressing for closure in the room is unlikely to speed things up.

Regional and generational differences exist. Younger professionals in Asmara, particularly those with international exposure, may engage more openly and move through agendas more fluidly. In rural areas or among older generations, deference to authority and a slower conversational rhythm remain stronger. Gender also plays a role: women are increasingly present in professional settings, but leadership remains predominantly male, and mixed-gender meetings may still reflect traditional social norms.

Follow-up is where progress is secured. Written summaries are useful, but persistence without pressure is often what keeps momentum alive. Repeated contact, framed respectfully, shows commitment rather than urgency.
