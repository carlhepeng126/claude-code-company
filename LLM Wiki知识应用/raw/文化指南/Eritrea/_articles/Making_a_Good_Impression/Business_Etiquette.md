# Business Etiquette

## Making a Good Impression

Eritrean business etiquette is shaped by a culture where respect for authority and collective harmony run deep. Greetings tend to be formal and measured. Handshakes are standard, often gentle rather than firm, and may be accompanied by a slight bow of the head when addressing someone senior. Rushing through introductions or skipping pleasantries can feel abrupt. Taking the time to greet each person individually, and asking after their well-being, shows that you understand the relationship comes before the transaction.

Dress codes lean conservative, especially in government or formal business settings. Men typically wear suits, while women opt for modest, professional attire that avoids drawing unnecessary attention. Presentation reflects both personal seriousness and respect for the institution or individual you are meeting. In a context where collective identity and social cohesion hold considerable weight, what you wear is read as a statement about how you view the encounter.

Gift-giving is not a routine feature of Eritrean business life, but a thoughtful gesture at the right moment can strengthen rapport. If you bring a gift, keep it modest and functional. Avoid anything overly personal or expensive, which could be misread as an attempt to influence. Offering a gift with both hands, or with the right hand supported by the left, reinforces the gesture's sincerity. Gifts are typically set aside rather than opened immediately, so do not expect an on-the-spot reaction.

Business cards are exchanged, but the practice feels more functional than ceremonial. There is no elaborate protocol, though handing over your card with care rather than casually signals professionalism. A bilingual card in English and one of the local languages can be appreciated, but is not expected.
