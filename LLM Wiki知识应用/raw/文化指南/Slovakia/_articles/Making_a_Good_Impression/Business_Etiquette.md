# Business Etiquette

## Making a Good Impression

Respectful realism sets the right tone in Slovak business life. Warmth matters, but it shows up as sincerity and steadiness rather than visible enthusiasm or charm. Slovaks tend to value people who are direct about what they know and realistic about what they don't, and who build rapport through consistency rather than grand gestures. A handshake is standard, small talk is light, and personal distance is maintained during conversation. Folk traditions and regional identity run deep here, so if you reference Slovak culture, keep it specific and grounded. Treating folk heritage as novelty or tourist curiosity will undercut credibility. Recognizing a name-day (meniny) or holiday shows you've done more than gloss a guidebook, and that attention tends to be noticed.

Communication often starts cautiously, especially with people from cultures where relationships form fast or decisions happen quickly. Slovaks may give answers that feel indirect or vague at first, particularly if relationships are new or if a straightforward "no" might risk offense. This tendency reflects a preference for avoiding confrontation, not a lack of clarity. As relationships deepen, communication becomes more direct, but early-stage ambiguity is common. If you're managing a Slovak team, ask open questions and give people time to respond honestly rather than pushing for a quick yes or no.

Historical context tends to help conversations land better. Slovaks carry strong civic memory around the 1989 Velvet Revolution and the peaceful 1993 dissolution of Czechoslovakia, both reference points for valuing order, pragmatism, and change without chaos. These are meaningful touchstones, not trivia. If you frame a project or partnership with a bit of background before jumping to the ask, you're working with how people here process information, not against it. Education and institutional longevity also command respect. Including advanced degrees on a business card or noting how long a company has been operating reflects what people value when sizing up a counterpart.
