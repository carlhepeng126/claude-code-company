# Feedback and Coaching

## Managing Relationships

Giving feedback in Cabo Verde requires attention to dignity, relationship, and setting. Direct criticism, especially in front of others, risks damaging rapport in ways that are hard to repair. Managers typically frame feedback gently and personally, often beginning with affirmation before raising concerns. The goal is to guide without shaming. Even when performance issues are serious, the conversation will be softened with care for the person's standing and the trust between you. A blunt assessment, no matter how honest, can feel like a public slight even when delivered privately.

Negative feedback should always be given one-to-one, away from colleagues. Public correction embarrasses not just the individual but the manager as well, signaling a failure to manage relationships with tact. If you need to address a recurring issue, frame it as a shared challenge or an opportunity to improve together rather than a personal failing. Positive feedback, by contrast, can be shared more openly and helps reinforce team morale, though even here, singling someone out too forcefully can create discomfort.

Coaching conversations often unfold gradually. Cabo Verdeans may not respond immediately to feedback, preferring to reflect or consult trusted friends and family before acting. If change seems slow, it may reflect the relational process through which advice is weighed, rather than resistance. Allow time for the conversation to settle, and follow up with warmth rather than impatience.
