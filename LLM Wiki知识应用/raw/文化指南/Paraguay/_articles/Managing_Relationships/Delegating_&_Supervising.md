# Delegating & Supervising

## Managing Relationships

In Paraguay, delegation unfolds within a strongly hierarchical culture shaped by centuries of centralised authority. Spanish colonial rule imposed rigid social hierarchies, and the 35-year Stroessner dictatorship embedded deep habits of deference and caution. Employees learned that questioning authority or surfacing problems could be dangerous. The result is a workplace where managers are authority figures whose decisions are followed, and where employees wait for clear direction rather than take initiative. This means that if you delegate without first building personal trust, you will likely receive polite compliance with little proactive engagement or problem-solving.

*Personalismo* (personal loyalty) runs through all professional relationships in Paraguay. Loyalty attaches to the individual manager, not the institution. Before you can supervise effectively, you need to invest time in personal connection. Informal conversations, showing genuine interest in family or personal circumstances, and demonstrating reliability over time build the foundation for real collaboration. Without this relationship, directives will be followed to the letter, but obstacles will go unreported and flexibility will not appear. Managers from cultures where professional roles are kept separate from personal rapport may read this as inefficiency. Paraguayan colleagues, in turn, may interpret a manager's reluctance to build personal connection as coldness or lack of commitment to the team.

Employees will rarely volunteer concerns or challenge a plan openly. Decades of authoritarian governance made public disagreement risky, and that wariness persists. Create private, one-on-one check-ins where people can raise issues without direct confrontation. Ask specific, supportive questions rather than waiting for problems to be offered. Any criticism must be delivered privately and framed constructively. Public correction risks serious damage to trust and to the person's standing within the group.

Deadlines are treated flexibly, and schedules shift to accommodate family obligations, social commitments, or unforeseen circumstances. The relationship and the person come before the timetable. This reflects both the Guaraní cultural influence, with its relaxed relationship with time, and a broader cultural priority that values people over process.
