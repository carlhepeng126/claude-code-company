# Decision-Making

## Working Together

Decisions in Paraguay tend to stabilise through relationships rather than formal processes alone. The goal is often that key people feel respected and included, because exclusion can create resistance across dense social networks. This approach reflects a history in which institutional trust became uneven. The long dictatorship period shaped how people relate to formal structures, and personal credibility often became a more reliable pathway than official channels. In professional settings today, this means that decisions may move through informal consultation before they are finalised, and that relational alignment can hold more weight than documented approval.

Language choice can also influence how a decision is received. Framing a decision in Spanish can lend it institutional legitimacy and formal defensibility. Framing it in Guaraní or jopará can root it in shared community logic and relational belonging. Managers who move between these registers are often making a decision feel both officially sound and socially accepted. For colleagues from cultures where decisions are documented and closed through formal procedures, this can read as ambiguity. From a Paraguayan perspective, a decision that has been formally approved but has not been relationally socialised may still lack the support it needs to be implemented.

Regional variation also matters. In the eastern frontier, where cross-border commerce and mobility are more common, decisions may move faster and respond to practical pressures. In interior settings with denser social memory, the reputational consequences of a decision tend to weigh more heavily, encouraging more careful handling. In major partnership contexts, fairness and reciprocity can become particularly visible. The Itaipú hydroelectric project and the ongoing sovereignty debates around it have reinforced a cultural sensitivity to leverage and national dignity, even when discussions are framed as technical.
