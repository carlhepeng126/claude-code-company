# Business Lunch & Dinner

## Meetings & Presentations

Business meals in Paraguay often function as relationship infrastructure, where the atmosphere and ritual of sharing can hold as much weight as the conversation. Tereré, the chilled yerba mate infusion passed hand to hand in a shared guampa (cup), often appears at or around meals and acts as an informal inclusion technology. The rhythm of passing, drinking, and conversing builds familiarity without formal agenda. This is not peripheral to the work. In settings where trust is built through consistent presence rather than transactional exchange, the tereré circle can offer the relational foundation that later makes business conversation possible.

Warmth in conversation does not always mean openness about content. Paraguay's history under authoritarian rule reinforced selective disclosure as a social skill in some circles, so professionals may be genuinely friendly while remaining careful about what becomes widely known. This combination can be misread by colleagues from cultures where warmth implies transparency. For Paraguayans, the reverse can also feel jarring: directness without relational groundwork may come across as transactional or dismissive, especially where emotion is treated as a legitimate part of collective life and meals are expected to carry relational tone, not just function.

Food choices carry cultural memory. Chipa, a cassava-and-cheese bread tied to precolonial Guaraní traditions and later colonial fusion, is widely associated with national identity and religious seasons like Holy Week. Recognizing its significance, or simply noting its presence, shows cultural literacy. Regional variation also matters. Dining norms in Asunción may feel more cosmopolitan and institutional, while interior towns may operate with denser social networks where visibility shifts the stakes of every interaction, and frontier zones may reflect faster rhythms and mixed cultural codes. What feels appropriate in one setting may not translate seamlessly to another.
