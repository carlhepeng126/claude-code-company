# Business Meeting Culture

## Meetings & Presentations

Meetings in Paraguay typically open with greetings and social exchange before any substantive agenda. This is relational groundwork, rooted in a culture where trust is built through personal familiarity rather than job titles alone. In a country where "who is connected to whom" functions as working infrastructure, especially outside the formal bubble of Asunción, establishing warmth first can determine how smoothly information flows later. Expect conversation about family, shared acquaintances, or local matters. If you move to business content too quickly, you risk signaling that the relationship itself is unimportant, which can create distance that is difficult to close afterward.

Communication during meetings tends to be indirect and measured. Political memory from the Stroessner dictatorship trained caution about what is said in front of whom, and this discretion persists, especially in institutional or hierarchical settings. Disagreement is often softened or implied rather than stated bluntly, and consensus is built through gradual consultation rather than open debate. For a colleague accustomed to rapid back-and-forth or explicit yes-or-no answers, this can feel opaque or noncommittal. From a Paraguayan perspective, pushing for clarity too soon can feel confrontational or dismissive of the relational context that makes a decision safe to voice.

Paraguay's bilingualism shapes how meetings feel. Spanish dominates formal and corporate environments, while Guaraní and the blended register jopará carry intimacy and moral warmth. The same idea expressed in Spanish may feel correct but emotionally distant; in Guaraní, it may feel relationally appropriate but less suited to official distance. Reading which register is in play helps you gauge the social temperature of the room and adjust tone accordingly.

Regional variation also matters. In the eastern frontier zones around Ciudad del Este, cross-border trade and cultural mixing create a faster, more transactional tempo than in interior towns, where social ties run deeper and decisions take longer to emerge.
