# Business Lunch & Dinner

## Meetings & Presentations

Business meals in Comoros tend to be informal and relationship-focused rather than structured around a formal agenda. The slower pace reflects a wider cultural preference for building trust before moving into transactional discussion. Eating together is seen as a way to demonstrate goodwill and establish common ground, so rushing through courses or steering conversation quickly toward business outcomes can feel abrupt and may weaken the connection you're trying to build.

Comorian cuisine draws on Swahili, Arab, and French influences. Rice often forms the base of a meal, served alongside grilled fish, coconut-based curries, or stews flavored with vanilla, cloves, or cardamom. These spices reflect the islands' agricultural history and trade connections. Meals are frequently shared from communal dishes placed at the center of the table, and offering food to others is a routine gesture of hospitality. Declining what's offered, especially more than once, can feel like a rejection of the relationship itself, so it's worth accepting at least a small portion.

Alcohol is uncommon, particularly outside international hotels, given the country's Islamic majority. Fruit juices, coconut water, or spiced tea are more typical. If you have dietary restrictions, mention them ahead of time rather than at the table, as last-minute adjustments can be difficult to accommodate and may cause embarrassment for your host.

Meals often end with strong coffee or sweet tea. This final stage is not rushed, and leaving immediately afterward can feel dismissive. Allow time for the conversation to wind down naturally before suggesting next steps.
