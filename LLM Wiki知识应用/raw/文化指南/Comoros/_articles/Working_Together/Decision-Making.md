# Decision-Making

## Working Together

Decisions in Comoros move through family networks and trusted intermediaries before they are finalized. Formal titles exist, but authority is deeply relational. A manager or director will often appear to hold decision-making power, yet in practice they consult widely with relatives, elders, and colleagues before committing to a course of action. This reflects the country's strong clan-based social structure, where decisions that affect a group are expected to be shaped by the group. Moving ahead without this informal consultation can create quiet resistance or withdrawal, even if no objection is voiced in the room.

This means that the person chairing a meeting may not be the person who ultimately determines the outcome. Decisions are often made outside the formal setting, in conversations over tea or in family gatherings, where trust and social cohesion take precedence over speed. A colleague from a culture where decisions are reached quickly in structured meetings may interpret this as indecision or avoidance. From a Comorian perspective, the reverse can feel reckless: choosing speed over relationships risks making a decision that will not hold once it reaches implementation.

If you are waiting for a final answer, expect the process to take longer than the meeting itself suggests. Follow up through trusted contacts rather than pressing the original decision-maker directly. Patience here is not passive waiting but active relationship-building, which is what makes a decision durable in the first place.
