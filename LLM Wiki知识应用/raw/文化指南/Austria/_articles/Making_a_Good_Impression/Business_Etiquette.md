# Business Etiquette

## Making a Good Impression

Austrian professional culture runs on correct form. Protocol, procedure, and propriety are how you show that you take the relationship seriously. Following the rules here is a sign of respect, and getting the small things right — titles, tone, timing — tells your counterpart that you understand how things work. Skipping over formalities or rushing through procedure can undermine credibility before any real conversation begins. An American colleague accustomed to quick first-name introductions and informal check-ins may read this precision as stiffness, while an Austrian counterpart might view informality as carelessness or a lack of professionalism. Both are applying different standards of what constitutes respect in a business setting.

Communication is direct but restrained. Austrians say what they mean and expect the same in return, but they will not raise their voices or show emotion to make a point. Hyperbole, excessive enthusiasm, and promises that sound too polished are viewed with suspicion. Claims should be backed by facts. Understatement is a virtue, so what sounds like mild approval may be genuine enthusiasm. A colleague from a culture where praise is generous and frequent may wonder whether their contribution registered at all, while an Austrian counterpart may find overt warmth distracting or unconvincing. Learning to read understatement, and respond in kind, is a meaningful calibration.

Compliments are rare and therefore carry weight. They are not used to smooth interactions or build rapport quickly, so receiving one should be taken seriously rather than deflected with modesty. Humor exists but it runs subtle and dry, especially in Vienna, where the local "Schmäh" — a self-deprecating, ironic wit — is a real cultural register. The joke doesn't announce itself. A high-energy sales style or loud humor is likely to create distance rather than warmth. If an Austrian makes a dry remark, take a beat before responding. The humor is there, it just doesn't signal its own presence.
