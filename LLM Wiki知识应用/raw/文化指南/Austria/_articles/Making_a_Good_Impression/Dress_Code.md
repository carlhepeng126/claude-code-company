# Dress Code

## Making a Good Impression

Austrians dress conservatively and well, and the care they put into their appearance reflects more than personal preference. Presentation is a form of respect — for the occasion, for the people present, and for oneself. Even in less formal settings, what people wear tends to be neat and considered. This approach has historical roots in the Habsburg tradition of formal court culture, where there was a correct way to dress for every occasion. That legacy still influences professional and social life. Attending to dress codes and dressing appropriately for the setting demonstrates cultural fluency, and Austrians will notice when you do or when you don't.

In professional contexts, overdressing is far less of a risk than underdressing. Austrians are unlikely to comment if you arrive too smartly dressed, but they will register if you are not smart enough. Conservative, quality clothing communicates that you understand and respect Austrian professional norms. If you're unsure, choose the more formal option. A well-tailored jacket or suit is rarely out of place.

Business entertaining often includes theatre, opera, or formal dinners, and the dress codes for these events are real and enforced. If your counterpart invites you to the opera, assume formal attire unless told otherwise. These occasions are not symbolic — they are part of how business relationships unfold. Arriving unprepared can create a poor first impression that lingers longer than the evening itself.

For international colleagues from cultures where business dress has become more casual or where formality varies widely by sector, the consistency of Austrian expectations can feel strict. From the Austrian perspective, a relaxed approach to dress in professional settings may suggest a lack of seriousness or preparation. The dress code is not arbitrary. It reflects an expectation that attention to form will extend to attention to detail in the work itself.
