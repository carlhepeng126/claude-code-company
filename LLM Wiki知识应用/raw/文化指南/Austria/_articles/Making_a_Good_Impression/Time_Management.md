# Time Management

## Making a Good Impression

Punctuality in Austria has a moral dimension. Being late does not simply delay a meeting — it signals disrespect for the other person's time and a failure to organize yourself properly, both serious lapses in a culture built around *Ordnung* (order). This is not a flexible norm. Arriving on time, or slightly early, reflects competence and seriousness. The reputational cost of lateness lingers far longer than the delay itself.

Austria follows what is often called a controlled-time approach. Schedules are planned with precision, deadlines are taken at face value, and missing them has consequences that extend beyond the immediate project. Tasks are completed one at a time, with each given full attention. Interruptions and multi-tasking are discouraged, as they undermine the quality and integrity of the work. A colleague from a culture where deadlines shift according to relationship or circumstance may see this rigidity as inflexible. An Austrian colleague, in turn, may interpret missed deadlines or late starts as unprofessional or even disrespectful.

Work and personal time are kept strictly separate. Reaching someone after 5pm on a weekday or at the weekend is generally neither expected nor welcomed. This boundary reflects a broader commitment to structure and order in daily life. Respecting it signals awareness and consideration. Pushing into personal time without clear justification will be noticed, and not positively.
