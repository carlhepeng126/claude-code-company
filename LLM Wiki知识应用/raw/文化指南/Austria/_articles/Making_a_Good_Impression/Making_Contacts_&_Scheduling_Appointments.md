# Making Contacts & Scheduling Appointments

## Making a Good Impression

In Austria, making initial contact works best through written channels that allow your counterpart time to assess the request and prepare a considered response. Email is widely preferred, followed by a formal letter if the relationship is new or the organization particularly traditional. A personal introduction from someone already known to your Austrian contact smooths the way considerably. Social and professional networks matter here, rooted in a culture where trust develops through reliable patterns over time rather than quick rapport. If you lack a direct connection, a clearly worded email that states your purpose, credentials, and the reason for contact will be taken seriously, provided it arrives with enough lead time.

Appointments should be scheduled several weeks ahead. Austrians plan their calendars carefully and value the predictability this creates. Once an appointment is set, confirm it in writing and avoid changing it unless absolutely necessary. Last-minute cancellations or rescheduling, even with a reasonable excuse, risk being read as disorganized or unreliable. A colleague from a context where schedules shift fluidly around emerging priorities may find this rigidity frustrating. From the Austrian side, however, frequent changes suggest a lack of respect for their time and preparation. If you must cancel, apologize clearly and give as much notice as possible.

Punctuality is expected without exception. Arriving late, even by a few minutes, undermines your credibility from the start. This extends beyond business meetings to social occasions as well. Unannounced visits are unwelcome, as they place your Austrian counterpart in the awkward position of being unable to offer the attention and preparation they consider appropriate. Always schedule in advance.

Avoid planning meetings in July and August, when many people take extended holidays, or around national holidays when long weekends are common. Friday afternoons are also best avoided, as people often leave early for the weekend.
