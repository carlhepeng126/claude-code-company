# Feedback and Coaching

## Managing Relationships

Feedback in Austria is formal, private, and factual. Public criticism, even when phrased gently, is read as disrespectful and embarrassing. The right setting is a scheduled conversation, away from colleagues, where issues can be addressed calmly and clearly. A manager from a culture where feedback is given spontaneously in team settings may find Austrian colleagues visibly uncomfortable or resistant. From the Austrian perspective, feedback shared openly feels like an attempt to shame rather than support, and undermines the trust needed for the relationship to work.

When Austrians give feedback, they are direct but measured. The tone is neutral, the language precise. They describe what happened, what the impact was, and what should change. There is no emotional framing, no exaggeration for emphasis. A manager who wraps critical feedback in layers of diplomatic softening may find the core message gets lost entirely. The expectation is that the point will be made clearly, supported by evidence, and delivered without drama.

Positive feedback is equally restrained. A single clear acknowledgement of good work is valued. Repeated praise, especially when effusive, is seen as insincere or manipulative. An Austrian colleague receiving enthusiastic compliments from a manager accustomed to frequent affirmation may grow suspicious of the motive rather than motivated by the recognition. One genuine, specific comment does more than a stream of general encouragement ever will.
