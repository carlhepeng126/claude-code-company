# Making Contacts & Scheduling Appointments

## Making a Good Impression

Access in Hong Kong runs through networks forged in classrooms and early-career cubicles, not extended family ties. People quietly track which secondary school you attended (La Salle, DBS, DGS, St Paul's, Wah Yan) or which firm shaped your first years (Big 4 accounting, bulge-bracket banks). These old boys' and girls' networks operate discreetly but powerfully, opening doors through shared reference points and mutual trust built over decades. When mapping your stakeholders, note school and firm affiliations. A warm introduction from someone inside the right alumni circle can shift a cold approach into a genuine conversation.

Two relationship webs coexist in the city, and they don't fully overlap. One is rooted in Cantonese-speaking, common-law Hong Kong, shaped by Lion Rock-era families and local institutions. The other connects through mainland professionals, state-owned enterprises, and ties to Beijing or Shenzhen. Both matter, but not everyone moves comfortably between them. Identifying someone who can bridge the two circuits gives you reach that a single network cannot.

Scheduling happens on WhatsApp or WeChat first, with calendar invites serving as confirmation rather than the opening move. People expect near-instant replies during working hours, even for minor adjustments or location clarifications. Specify exactly which MTR exit, which tower in a complex, which floor. Micro-precision prevents confusion in a city where "Central" can mean twenty different buildings within a five-minute radius.

Punctuality here reflects reliability. The city runs on MTR time, and "I'm on my way" usually means three stops out, not still at the office. Arriving ten minutes late, even with an easy manner, registers as carelessness. Chronic lateness will quietly close doors you may not realize were open.
