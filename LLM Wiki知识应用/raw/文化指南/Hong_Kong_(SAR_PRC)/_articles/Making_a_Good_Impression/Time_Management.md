# Time Management

## Making a Good Impression

Hong Kong professionals schedule their days with the precision that keeps the MTR running, and they expect the same from others. In a city where back-to-back 30-minute slots fill calendars and meetings span Admiralty to Kowloon and back before lunch, a 15-minute overrun doesn't just delay a conversation, it forces someone to choose between you and their next commitment. Finish five minutes early, not five minutes late. Time slots are treated as hard edges because the density of the working day demands it.

Messages arrive at all hours. WhatsApp notifications continue well past midnight, and people see them, but replies are triaged. Being reachable doesn't mean being instantly responsive. If you need an answer today to submit a filing or close a deal, mark the urgency clearly. Without that signal, your message joins the queue and a reply may come only when the recipient has worked through higher priorities.

Hong Kong teams routinely juggle London, New York, Shanghai, and Shenzhen time zones. Lunchtime meetings and 7 p.m. calls become the only common windows. Ask about global call schedules up front and propose options that acknowledge the juggle rather than assume your own time zone should anchor the conversation.

Typhoon signals and black rain warnings can flip the entire city to work-from-home mode within hours. Locals build "if there's a T8" contingencies into big plans as a matter of course. Rescheduling for a weather signal reflects preparation, not flakiness, and teams are practiced at structured improvisation around sudden disruptions.
