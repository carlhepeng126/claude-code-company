# Motivating Others

## Managing Relationships

For many professionals in Hong Kong, motivation begins with tangible realities: paying the mortgage in one of the world's most expensive property markets, saving for a first flat, funding children's tutoring and overseas education, or supporting aging parents. These are not background concerns. They shape daily priorities and make stability, clear increments, and transparent reward structures more compelling than abstract mission statements or team slogans.

High performers expect to move if growth stalls. Lifetime loyalty to a single employer is rare. People look for projects that build portable value on their CV: China exposure, regional responsibilities, new product lines. If staying feels like stagnation, they will leave. This means offering visible development paths matters as much as salary. Rotations, cross-border work, and stretch roles show that staying is a career move, not just continuity.

In a culture of long hours, how leaders manage time says something about how they value people. Cutting pointless meetings, protecting some evenings, and honoring leave stand out. People notice who burns them out and who manages workload. Streamlining processes and avoiding performative busyness demonstrates respect in a way that rhetoric alone cannot.

Ambiguity around bonuses or promotions demotivates quickly. Make reward and progression rules transparent, and follow through on what you promise. Recognition works best when it is specific and timely, not saved for annual reviews. A manager who acknowledges a colleague's contribution to a difficult project in the moment, or who invites someone into work that will raise their profile internally, shows that effort is seen and valued.
