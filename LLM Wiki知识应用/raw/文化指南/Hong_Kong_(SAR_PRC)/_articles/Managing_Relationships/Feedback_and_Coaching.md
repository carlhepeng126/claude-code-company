# Feedback and Coaching

## Managing Relationships

Many Hong Kong professionals grew up in exam-driven education systems where only mistakes were marked and top scores were expected without celebration. Generic praise can feel hollow in this context, but well-aimed positive feedback on specific behaviors or outputs stands out. People want to know what they did well and why it worked, not just what went wrong. Balancing critique with targeted reinforcement helps colleagues understand what to repeat, not only what to avoid.

Being criticized in front of others, especially cross-border peers, threatens face in ways that can rupture trust. A colleague may nod politely in a group setting, say very little, and then quietly withdraw or begin looking elsewhere for work. Delivering tough feedback privately, quickly, and with concrete examples protects dignity and keeps the conversation focused on improvement. A manager from a setting where open, group-based feedback is the norm may read silence as agreement, while the Hong Kong colleague may be processing humiliation and planning an exit.

Vague, feelings-heavy coaching conversations tend to unsettle people in a fast-paced environment where clarity and efficiency are prized. Structured support works better: here's what went wrong, here's what good looks like, here are two ways to get there. Using frameworks, samples, and checklists as coaching tools often delivers more traction than open-ended reflection. Show, don't just tell.
