# Being a Host & Guest

## Meetings & Presentations

Uruguayan hospitality favors steadiness over spectacle. Care is shown through timing, attentiveness to comfort, and a lack of fuss. If you are invited to dinner, expect the stated time to be loosely interpreted. An invitation for 9 pm typically means arrival around 10 pm, especially at someone's home, and weekend dinners often start closer to 10 pm. This flexibility reflects a broader comfort with social time unfolding at its own pace. The person who extends the invitation usually offers to pay, and reciprocation is expected at a later date, including partners if the original event was an evening occasion.

What happens after the meal often holds more relational weight than the meal itself. Sobremesa, the lingering conversation that follows eating, is where people become more candid and personally warm. The shift is not announced; it simply happens as people settle in. Moving on to a bar or club afterward is common on weekends, and nothing typically starts before 1 am. If you are likely to continue the evening, dress accordingly. Getting drunk is rare in business contexts; the focus is more on extended conversation and perhaps dancing, with a few drinks rather than heavy consumption.

Mate, the shared herbal infusion passed in a circle, often serves as a quiet ritual of inclusion. Being offered mate is a small but meaningful gesture of belonging. If you can accept, do so. If not, decline simply and warmly. The thermos habit makes this portable and frequent, and the rhythm of sharing reinforces connection without formality.

If you are welcomed into community or cultural settings, particularly those connected to candombe heritage, recognize that these spaces hold collective meaning. Respect includes observing how locals frame what the space represents, rather than treating it as a backdrop.
