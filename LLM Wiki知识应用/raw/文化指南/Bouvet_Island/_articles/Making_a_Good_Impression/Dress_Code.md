# Dress Code

## Making a Good Impression

Bouvet Island is an uninhabited Norwegian territory in the South Atlantic Ocean. It has no permanent population, no commercial activity, and no workplace culture. The island is covered almost entirely by glaciers and is accessible only by ship or helicopter in rare weather windows, typically for scientific expeditions or governmental surveys.

There is no dress code to observe because there are no offices, no business meetings, and no social occasions that involve local counterparts. The only people who set foot on Bouvet Island are researchers, meteorologists, or occasional expedition crews, and their clothing decisions are dictated entirely by sub-Antarctic conditions: thermal layers, waterproof outer shells, and boots suited to ice and rock.

If you are working with Norwegian institutions that oversee the territory, or collaborating with scientists who have visited the island, standard Norwegian professional norms apply. In those settings, dress tends to be practical and unstuffy. Suits are rare outside formal government or corporate contexts. Business casual is common, and there is little emphasis on hierarchy expressed through clothing. Appearing put-together and functional is enough.

For fieldwork on Bouvet Island itself, the environment is the only consideration. Expect expedition-grade cold-weather gear, not business attire. There is no symbolic meaning to decode and no cultural expectation to navigate, only the practical demands of one of the most remote and inhospitable places on earth.
