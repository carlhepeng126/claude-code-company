# Business Etiquette

## Making a Good Impression

Business etiquette in the Faroe Islands reflects the values of a small, tight-knit society where relationships are personal and professional boundaries are softer than in many larger or more formal cultures. The islands' population of around 55,000 means that most Faroese professionals will know each other, or know someone who does. This shapes how people interact. Introductions tend to be informal and friendly, often on a first-name basis from the start. Titles are rarely used unless someone holds a formal role where it's relevant, such as a doctor or minister. A firm handshake and direct eye contact signal openness and sincerity, and warmth is assumed rather than built up gradually through protocol.

Dress codes lean toward the practical. Even in professional settings, formality is understated. A colleague attending a business meeting might arrive in smart casual clothing rather than a full suit, especially outside the capital. The climate and the culture both favor practicality over polish. That said, neatness and appropriateness still count. Showing up well-groomed and dressed thoughtfully for the context demonstrates respect without needing to lean into corporate formality.

Punctuality is valued, but it operates within a rhythm shaped by geography and scale. Meetings start on time, but if weather disrupts a ferry or a road, flexibility is expected. The Faroese understand that plans sometimes shift, and they adjust without fuss. A colleague from a culture where delays require detailed explanation might find this adaptability reassuring. From the Faroese perspective, someone who becomes visibly frustrated over a weather-related delay may seem rigid or disconnected from the realities of island life.

Gift-giving is not part of standard business practice. A small gesture, such as bringing something from your own country during a first meeting, can be well-received, but it should feel personal rather than transactional. Hospitality, when offered, is genuine and often extends beyond the office.
