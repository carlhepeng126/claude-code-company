# Forms of Address

## Making a Good Impression

Faroese professional culture favors informality rooted in a small, egalitarian society where many people are connected through family, community, or shared networks. First names are used almost immediately in the workplace, even with senior colleagues or clients, and this directness reflects a cultural preference for openness and lack of pretension. Formal titles such as "Mr." or "Ms." are rare and can make interactions feel awkward or overly stiff. Using a first name is less about familiarity and more about treating someone as an equal participant in the conversation, regardless of their role or experience.

In email and written communication, the same informality applies. Greetings are simple and direct, often just a first name followed by the message itself. There is no expectation of elaborate openings or closings, and maintaining formality in writing can come across as distant or out of step with local norms. For colleagues from cultures where professional distance is shown through titles and structured greetings, this can initially feel too casual. From the Faroese side, insistence on formality may be read as a reluctance to engage on equal terms or as a signal that hierarchy is being invoked unnecessarily.

The shift to first names happens quickly, but it does not mean that respect is absent. Tone, attentiveness, and follow-through are the ways respect is expressed, not through forms of address. If you are unsure, follow the lead of your Faroese counterpart. If they sign an email with their first name, respond in kind.
