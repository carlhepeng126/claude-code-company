# Leadership Styles

## Working Together

Leadership in the Faroe Islands tends to be low on formality and high on accessibility. Managers are generally approachable and often work alongside their teams rather than from behind closed doors. This reflects a broader cultural preference for equality and pragmatism rooted in the islands' small scale and strong communal traditions. In a society where everyone knows everyone, hierarchical distance becomes harder to maintain and less socially useful. Leaders are expected to earn respect through competence and fairness, not through title alone.

Decision-making often involves consultation, though the degree of participation can vary by sector and company size. In smaller or family-run businesses, which still make up a significant part of the economy, input may be welcomed but final decisions rest clearly with the owner or senior figure. In newer sectors like aquaculture or tech, there is often more collaborative discussion and shared ownership of outcomes. A colleague from a culture where leaders decide quickly and communicate downward may find this inclusive style slow or indirect. From a Faroese perspective, rushing a decision without hearing people out can seem careless and risks damaging group trust.

Managers are also expected to be hands-on and involved in daily operations. A leader who delegates extensively without staying visibly engaged may be seen as out of touch. This expectation reflects the Faroese value of contributing practically to collective effort, not just directing it from above.
