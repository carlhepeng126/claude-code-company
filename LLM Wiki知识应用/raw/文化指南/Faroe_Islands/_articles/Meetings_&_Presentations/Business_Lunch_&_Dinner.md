# Business Lunch & Dinner

## Meetings & Presentations

Business meals in the Faroe Islands tend to be low-key affairs. Conversations flow naturally between work and personal topics, often touching on family, fishing, or local news. The boundary between professional and personal life is less rigid than in many larger business cultures, and sharing a meal is as much about building trust through familiarity as it is about sealing deals. Meetings often blend seamlessly into meals, with decisions emerging from the discussion rather than being formally announced over dessert.

Faroese cuisine centers on what the islands produce: lamb, fish, and root vegetables. Traditional dishes like ræst kjøt (wind-dried mutton) or fermented fish may appear at more informal gatherings, though international visitors are more likely to encounter milder preparations of fresh lamb or cod. Portions are hearty, and meals are designed to be filling rather than elaborate. The focus is on quality and simplicity, not presentation or variety.

Alcohol is available but not central to business dining. Beer or wine may be served, though not always. If your host doesn't order alcohol, it's worth following their lead. Tea or coffee usually closes the meal, but the pace is relaxed and there's no rush to leave. Lingering at the table is normal, and ending a meal abruptly can feel jarring to local colleagues who view the conversation after eating as part of the relationship itself.

Dining etiquette is informal. Don't expect elaborate toasts or rigid seating plans. Hospitality is expressed through inclusion rather than ceremony, and making an effort to engage in conversation matters more than knowing formal protocol.
