# Feedback and Coaching

## Managing Relationships

Hungarians grow up in a system where marks are harsh, professors are blunt, and parents rarely soften criticism. This background means many Hungarian professionals can absorb direct feedback about their work without drama, as long as it stays factual, stays private, and treats them as capable adults. What provokes real resentment is being criticized in front of the team or being spoken to as if they lack basic competence. Keep critical feedback detailed and delivered one-to-one, especially when there is a significant age or seniority gap. Public reprimands trigger defensiveness quickly.

Coaching is taken seriously when it comes from a real *szakértő* (expert), someone who clearly knows the field and can teach from lived experience. Generic soft-skills trainers without local knowledge or technical depth will struggle to gain traction. Anchor your coaching in real expertise and show you understand how Hungarian banks, municipalities, or factories actually function. If you are coaching on change or process, referencing how similar situations played out locally will make your guidance credible.

Older colleagues often use stories from past crises as teaching tools. References to the privatizations of the 1990s, the 2008 financial shock, or shifting EU regulations serve as parables that help younger staff recognize patterns and anticipate risks. Inviting senior Hungarians to share these narratives during coaching or onboarding sessions respects their experience and makes abstract change feel concrete and survivable.
