# Teamwork

## Working Together

Hungarian teams often contain a quiet inner circle that rarely appears on any org chart. Colleagues use the phrase *szűk kör* or *bizalmi kör* (inner circle of trust) to describe the handful of people with whom they share unfiltered views on politics, managers, or even personal finances. Outside that circle, conversation remains courteous but more guarded, and pessimism is edited before it reaches senior ears. This division shapes information flow more than formal reporting lines. A manager who expects open discussion in a kickoff meeting may hear only cautious consensus until trust shifts someone across that invisible boundary. Building credibility takes time, but once you are inside the circle, the quality of insight and willingness to problem-solve together changes sharply.

Teams that span Budapest and regional branches often carry a quiet geographic tension. The capital is seen by many outside it as liberal, arrogant, and detached from the rest of the country. Smaller cities are sometimes dismissed by Budapest colleagues as parochial or predictable. These perceptions, rarely voiced in plenary sessions, influence whose strategic input is taken seriously. A colleague from a culture where hierarchy determines voice may assume the Budapest office leads simply because it is headquarters. A Hungarian colleague from Debrecen or Miskolc may assume the same, but with resentment rather than deference. Actively asking how a decision looks from each location, by name, can surface concerns that would otherwise stay regional.

Among older colleagues, especially those who worked during the Kádár-era *gulyáskommunizmus* (goulash communism), there is sometimes a reflex to solve problems quietly rather than claim credit publicly. Initiative happens, but in the shadows. If a project risks becoming politically awkward or attracting blame, these team members will step back from formal ownership even if they contributed the solution. A colleague from a culture where volunteering ideas in meetings is expected may read this as passivity. From the Hungarian side, the colleague's visibility may look naive or self-promoting. Protecting people who take initiative, and making that protection explicit, helps shift the calculus.
