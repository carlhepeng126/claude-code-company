# Teamwork

## Working Together

Teamwork in Lebanon is shaped by the everyday practice of navigating plural belonging. Most Lebanese professionals grow up fluent in what might be called mixed-room calibration: adjusting tone, humor, and topics depending on who is present. This reflects a society long organized through communal balance rather than majority rule, where sensitivity around religious and political representation runs through daily life. In practical terms, this means that trust and alignment in a team often depend on whether the group feels socially safe and balanced, not just whether the plan is sound. A manager assembling a project team should pay attention to how the group composition reads informally, since people may hesitate to speak freely if the room feels communally uneven.

Cooperation flows more through networks than through formal structure. In a context where institutions have repeatedly faltered, people rely on family, neighborhood, school, and community ties to get things done. This reliance has intensified since 2019, during an economic crisis the World Bank has described as among the most severe globally since the 19th century. In the workplace, this means informal channels often shape how work moves. A colleague may solve a problem through a contact outside the org chart, or defer to someone whose formal title does not reflect their actual influence. Recognizing these networks without romanticizing them helps avoid misreading how decisions are actually reached.

Many Lebanese professionals also live with what might be described as diaspora presence inside the team, even when no one is abroad. Remittances represent a significant share of GDP, and they bring not just money but emotional obligations and transnational family rhythms. A team member may be managing urgent family decisions across time zones, or may be planning to leave soon themselves. This can affect availability, risk appetite, and long-term commitment in ways that are rarely stated outright but shape how people engage with collective goals.
