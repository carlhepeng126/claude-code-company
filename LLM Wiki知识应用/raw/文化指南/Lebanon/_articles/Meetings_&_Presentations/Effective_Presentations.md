# Effective Presentations

## Meetings & Presentations

Lebanese audiences value oral fluency and persuasive skill rooted in a long tradition of verbal virtuosity. Zajal, a form of recited poetry recognized by UNESCO, is performed as call-and-response jousts at social gatherings—quick thinking, wit, and rhythm valued as cultural art. This appreciation for spoken agility shows up in professional settings. A strong presenter blends structured content with story, analogy, and emotional intelligence. People listen for whether you understand the human context behind the facts, not only the facts themselves. A purely linear argument may feel clinical. Grounding your message in examples that reflect lived constraints builds resonance.

Years of severe economic crisis have sharpened audience radar for overpromising and performative optimism. Credibility rises when claims feel realistic and acknowledge local realities rather than glossing over them. Avoid the hard sell or sweeping guarantees. Instead, introduce yourself clearly—your role, your company's history, who will be involved—and explain how collaboration benefits both sides. Keep your tone people-focused. Trust often outweighs polish.

Language choice shapes authority and connection. Presenting in English or French can signal global professionalism or technical precision. Arabic communicates closeness, shared reality, and cultural fluency. Switching between languages thoughtfully shows you belong in more than one Lebanese public. Choose your register intentionally, and translate slides into the language your audience expects.

Expect phones to ring and people to come and go. Movement during a presentation reflects the layered nature of professional life, especially in smaller or family-run firms. Allow time after your presentation for one-to-one questions. Some attendees prefer to ask clarifying or sensitive questions privately to avoid the risk of losing face in a group setting.
