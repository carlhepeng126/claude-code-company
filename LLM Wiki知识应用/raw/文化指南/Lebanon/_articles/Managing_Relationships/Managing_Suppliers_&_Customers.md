# Managing Suppliers & Customers

## Managing Relationships

Lebanon's long history as a coastal trading hub has left a lasting mark on how commercial relationships are built and maintained. Persuasion, hospitality, and the cultivation of long-term ties are treated as professional skills, and relationships themselves are understood as assets rather than conveniences. This means that suppliers and customers expect more than timely orders and payments. They expect presence, repeated contact, and the kind of social investment that signals commitment. Visiting regularly, sharing meals, and communicating by phone or in person rather than through email alone helps anchor a relationship in ways that written exchanges cannot. A foreign manager who treats supplier communication as a series of logistical exchanges may find responsiveness drops, not because terms are unclear but because the relationship has not been built.

Access and responsiveness often move through introductions and established ties rather than formal queues alone. In an environment where institutions can be fragile or slow, knowing someone who knows someone can unlock practical support. This does not mean contracts are irrelevant, but it does mean that a strong relationship can reduce friction when something goes wrong or needs adjusting. For a business partner accustomed to systems where recourse is institutional, this reliance on interpersonal access may feel opaque. From a Lebanese perspective, a purely procedural approach with no relational foundation may seem brittle or impersonal.

The post-crisis economy has shaped expectations around flexibility and payment. Electricity shortages, banking restrictions, and the growth of cash and remittance channels mean that many suppliers and customers now operate within parallel systems. A delivery timeline may shift because a generator failed, or a payment method may need to change because a bank account is inaccessible. Managers who assume stable infrastructure or rigid timelines will struggle. Building in contingency time and discussing payment options early, without treating improvisation as a failure, will make collaboration more realistic. What looks like inconsistency is often adaptive capacity under constrained conditions.
