# Forms of Address

## Making a Good Impression

Anguilla's approach to forms of address reflects a blend of British-influenced formality and the relational warmth typical of small Caribbean societies. In professional settings, titles such as Mr., Mrs., Miss, or Dr. paired with a surname are standard, particularly during first meetings or when addressing someone senior in age or position. This formality reflects a cultural expectation that respect must be established before familiarity, and that hierarchy, while gently expressed, should be acknowledged. Jumping to first names too quickly can read as presumptuous, especially if the relationship has not yet been built.

The shift to first names typically happens once someone invites it, often after initial meetings or as the working relationship develops. In a small island community where many people are connected through family or long-standing networks, this transition can happen more quickly among locals than it might with outsiders. Visiting colleagues should wait for the cue rather than initiating informality themselves, as doing so can feel like skipping steps in a relationship that Anguillans value.

Greetings combine verbal courtesy with warmth. A handshake is common in business contexts, but depending on familiarity and setting, you may also encounter a light kiss on the cheek or a nod. What remains consistent is the expectation of acknowledgment. Passing someone without greeting, even in a professional corridor, can come across as dismissive in a culture where visibility and recognition within the community matter.
