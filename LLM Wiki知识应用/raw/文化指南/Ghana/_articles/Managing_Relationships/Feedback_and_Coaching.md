# Feedback and Coaching

## Managing Relationships

In Ghana, senior managers are often seen as both protector and judge, like a father or mother figure whose opinion goes beyond professional evaluation. The fear attached to criticism is less about immediate dismissal and more about public disgrace, the sense that colleagues will talk and reputation will suffer. This means feedback is filtered through a lens of personal dignity and social standing, not just performance metrics.

Good managers in Ghana tend to praise openly and correct quietly. Recognition often happens at team gatherings or durbars, where contributions are named in front of others. Difficult feedback, by contrast, is saved for one-to-one conversations in an office or even a car park, away from witnesses. This separation protects the individual from embarrassment while still allowing for honest critique. Public shaming, even unintentional, can damage trust and make future coaching nearly impossible.

Coaching in Ghana often takes the form of advice from an elder. A manager might say, "My son, in this life you don't do X," blending personal story, moral guidance, and career direction. This style is relational rather than procedural. It frames development as part of a longer journey, not as a checklist of competencies. For outsiders, adopting a "let me share what I've seen" approach works better than clinical HR language. It shows care while still being clear about what needs to improve.

When giving feedback, frame critique as a path to growth and dignity, not as character judgment. Highlight strengths in public settings, especially for those who rarely get recognition, and save heavier conversations for private moments.
