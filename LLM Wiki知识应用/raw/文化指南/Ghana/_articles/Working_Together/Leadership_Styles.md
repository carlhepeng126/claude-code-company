# Leadership Styles

## Working Together

A respected leader in Ghana is expected to be both commanding and caring, a combination often described through the "big man" or "big woman" role. This means providing opportunities, offering protection when staff face personal difficulties, and showing visible generosity at weddings, funerals, and family crises. At the same time, the same leader is expected to be approachable enough to greet cleaners and drivers by name and to listen when people need help. Authority is real and visible, but it must be exercised with warmth and attention to people's lives outside the office. A manager who makes firm decisions but never shows up at a team member's funeral or contributes to a family emergency will lose trust, no matter how technically capable they are.

Leadership also happens under scrutiny. Staff, church members, extended family, political networks, and increasingly social media all watch how a leader behaves in public and private moments. How you handle funeral contributions, respond to a staff crisis, or speak during election season all feed into whether you are seen as having a "good heart." This means that your off-duty conduct, your generosity, and even your religious participation shape how your authority is received at work. For leaders from cultures where professional and personal life are more separated, this can feel intrusive. From a Ghanaian perspective, a leader who remains distant or invisible outside the office may seem untrustworthy or cold, no matter how fair their decisions.

In some cases, traditional authority and corporate roles overlap. Executives may also be chiefs, queenmothers, or members of royal families, and their leadership style will reflect palace norms as much as management training. This can mean specific protocols around timing, forms of address, and certain taboos or obligations that affect scheduling and decision-making. Respecting this dimension of their identity means adjusting your expectations around availability and interaction, not treating it as a distraction from "real" work.
