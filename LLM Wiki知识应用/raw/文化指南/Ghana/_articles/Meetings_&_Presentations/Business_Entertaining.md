# Business Entertaining

## Meetings & Presentations

Where you entertain tells a story before anyone sits down. Hosting fufu at a respected chop bar (a local eatery serving traditional food), grilled tilapia at a roadside spot, or cocktails on an Accra rooftop each conveys a different social message: one says "I'm with the people," another "I know the real city," and the last "we're playing in the global league." The choice reflects how the host wants to position the relationship and themselves. For visitors planning to host, this means matching the venue to who your counterpart is in their community, not just their job title. A chief, a youth activist, a banker and a creative director will each have different expectations about what feels appropriate.

Many senior contacts navigate a careful divide between their public and private selves. The same person who relaxes in a quiet East Legon or Cantonments venue (affluent residential neighborhoods in Accra) may avoid being seen in flashy settings back home, where family and community expect a certain public respectability. Suggesting a high-profile or visibly indulgent outing without checking how visible they want to be can put someone in an uncomfortable position. Ask where they prefer to meet, or offer options that range in tone.

Conversation over drinks or a meal builds the relational ground that formal meetings need. Football, especially the Black Stars or English Premier League, party politics, and gospel or highlife music are shared reference points that open up talk more naturally than abstract small talk. A Chelsea versus Hearts of Oak joke or a comment about the latest political debate usually lands better than leading with the weather. Just don't assume everyone supports the government of the day or follows the same club.

Alcohol is widely available and acceptable in most professional settings, though northern regions and devout Muslim contacts may abstain. Wait to see what your host orders, and keep your own composure regardless. The gathering itself strengthens the relationship. Business may come up late or not at all, and that's often the point.
