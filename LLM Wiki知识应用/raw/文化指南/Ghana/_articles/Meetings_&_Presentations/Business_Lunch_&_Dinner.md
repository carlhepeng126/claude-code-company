# Business Lunch & Dinner

## Meetings & Presentations

Sharing food in Ghana is built around warmth and respect, and the table reflects the same hierarchies that structure professional life. Seniors are typically served first and may be given the best portion — the head of the chicken, the largest piece of fish. If you try to refuse your portion or redistribute it without reading the room, you risk unsettling the moment. The gesture behind the offering is at least as important as the food itself.

Many traditional staples like fufu (pounded cassava or plantain served with soup), banku (fermented maize dough), and tuo zaafi are heavy and filling. Some people prefer them at lunch if they can rest afterward; others keep them for dinner. A colleague scheduling intensive work straight after a big fufu lunch may notice attention drifting. If you're organizing meetings around a meal, consider placing high-concentration sessions earlier in the day or after a lighter option.

Hand-washing stations and communal bowls are common, especially with dishes eaten by hand. Fufu is typically torn and dipped; cutlery is less usual in informal settings. People will notice if you avoid touching food entirely or act uncomfortable. If eating by hand is difficult for you, ask quietly for cutlery and frame it as habit, not aversion. Better still, try a small portion by hand if you can — the effort registers more than the technique.

Let your host guide the pace and serving order. Accept what you're given, then adjust your portion discreetly if needed. Ghanaian meals are rarely rushed, and the time spent together builds the trust that formal meetings alone may not.
