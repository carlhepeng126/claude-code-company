# Decision-Making

## Working Together

Decisions in Pakistan are typically made at senior levels, most often by those who hold formal authority through seniority, age, or organizational position. This reflects a broader cultural expectation that leadership includes the responsibility to decide, and that hierarchy exists to provide clarity and accountability. But authority alone does not guarantee acceptance. A decision also needs to feel socially legitimate, meaning it respects relationships, protects the dignity of those involved, and aligns with what is seen as morally or socially appropriate. This is why consultation happens, often through trusted circles, before a formal decision is announced. The consultation is less about seeking input than about building legitimacy and ensuring the decision will be supported once made.

In some regional contexts, particularly in parts of the northwest, there is a tradition of community-based decision-making through forums like jirgas, where elders deliberate collectively and decisions gain strength through communal legitimacy. Even in settings where this model is not formally present, the idea that decisions should be socially grounded rather than imposed remains influential. This can mean that a technically sound proposal may struggle if it has not been aligned informally first, or if it feels disconnected from the relational fabric of the group.

Public commitment can be cautious. In environments where reputation and face are closely guarded, people may avoid committing too early in visible settings. They prefer to align privately, test the direction of the group, and then commit once it feels socially safe to do so. A colleague from a setting where decisions are made quickly in open forums may read this as evasion or lack of clarity, while a Pakistani colleague may read the push for immediate public commitment as socially insensitive or premature. Neither is delaying or rushing, both are working within different systems of legitimacy.

Many people navigate two decision worlds. One follows institutional logic, where credentials, process, and formal roles guide decisions. The other follows network legitimacy, where decisions depend on who can mobilize support, whose backing matters, and what relationships are in play. In practice, both often operate at once. If you are seeking approval or alignment, identify which system is active and who holds influence within it, whether that is a senior title-holder or a respected connector who operates outside the formal chart.
