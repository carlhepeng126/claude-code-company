# Dress Code

## Making a Good Impression

Clothing in Pakistan communicates dignity, moral seriousness, and respect for the occasion. Looking polished and put-together tells the people you are meeting that you take the relationship seriously. The care you show in your appearance reflects your sense of self-possession and your understanding of the social situation. A colleague stepping into a formal meeting in an outfit that feels too casual or careless may find it harder to establish credibility, regardless of their expertise or rank.

What counts as appropriate varies widely depending on setting, region, and social world. English-medium elite offices in Karachi may expect Western business attire or tailored versions of local dress, while public-sector or small-town commercial settings may read that same outfit as a sign of arrogance or distance. Someone used to more casual professional environments may arrive in smart casual and find themselves underdressed for the tone of the room, while a colleague from Pakistan visiting a less formal workplace abroad might initially read the relaxed dress code as a lack of seriousness. Reading the baseline for the specific environment you are entering becomes essential, and asking a trusted local contact what is expected will usually yield clear guidance.

Modesty is a public expectation shaped by Pakistan's overwhelmingly Muslim context, but how it shows up depends on region, gender, and the formality of the setting. Women typically cover their arms and legs to the knee, and often wear a shawl or dupatta that can be adjusted to cover the head when visiting religious sites or more conservative environments. Men wear suits for business meetings, and avoid shorts outside of private or sporting contexts. In mixed urban spaces like Karachi or Lahore, professional dress can lean more cosmopolitan. In family-linked or smaller-town settings, more conservative norms tend to apply. The goal is to signal respect for the local tone rather than to stand apart from it.
