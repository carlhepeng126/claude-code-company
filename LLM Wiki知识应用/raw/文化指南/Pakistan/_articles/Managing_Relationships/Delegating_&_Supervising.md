# Delegating & Supervising

## Managing Relationships

Pakistan's workplace hierarchy has deep roots in both colonial administration and Islamic tradition. Nearly two centuries of British rule left a formal, top-down bureaucratic system where authority was concentrated in an administrative elite, and Pakistan inherited this structure virtually intact at independence in 1947. The colonial-era *sahab* (master) culture, in which deference to those above was expected, remains visible today in how many employees relate to managers. Islamic values reinforce this through respect for authority and the principle that leaders hold genuine responsibility for those beneath them. The result is a paternalistic workplace where managers are expected to provide for and protect their teams, and employees give loyalty in return.

Delegation in this context means giving clear, specific direction. Employees will wait for instruction and are unlikely to act outside their defined scope or take initiative without being invited to do so. Close supervision of projects is expected, and managers who remain involved throughout are seen as fulfilling their role responsibly. If you assign a task assuming someone will interpret broad goals and fill in the gaps, you may find that work stalls or waits for further guidance. Building in regular check-ins and clarifying each stage explicitly will keep things moving.

Deadlines are interpreted flexibly. Pakistan is a relationship-first culture where time bends around people and process, and decisions often move slowly through multiple layers of approval. If a deadline is fixed, state that clearly, monitor progress with regular updates, and build in contingency time. A manager from a culture where deadlines anchor the work schedule may read this flexibility as disorganization. A Pakistani colleague may read rigid insistence on timing, without room for adjustment, as disregard for the human or relational factors that shape how work actually gets done.

Critical feedback belongs in private, framed with care. Honor runs deep, and public correction can damage both the individual relationship and your standing as a manager. Employees respond more fully to leaders who show genuine personal interest in their lives, not just their output. That relational investment earns a different quality of commitment.
