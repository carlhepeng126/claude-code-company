# Forms of Address

## Making a Good Impression

In Turkish professional settings, using first name plus Bey (for men) or Hanım (for women) offers a respectful middle ground between formality and approachability. You might address a colleague as Ertan Bey or Elif Hanım rather than using first names alone or full surnames with titles. This reflects Turkey's relationship-oriented culture, where showing respect through form does not require rigid distance. It also avoids early informality that might feel presumptuous before a working relationship has been established.

Professional titles often appear on their own or alongside names. You may hear Doktor, Avukat (lawyer), or Mühendis (engineer) used to address colleagues, and senior staff might be called by their role, such as Müdür Bey (Mr. Manager). This use of titles acknowledges both expertise and hierarchy, two anchors of workplace credibility in Turkey. Watch how colleagues address one another and match that level. In multinational or younger teams, formality may soften quickly, but in more traditional sectors or with senior figures, titles remain in place longer.

When speaking English, begin with title and family name, then move to first names if your counterpart invites it. Many Turkish professionals shift toward informality once rapport has been built, but the invitation usually comes from the senior person or host. Greetings follow a similar logic. A handshake is standard in business, though among established colleagues, a light cheek kiss or brief embrace may replace it. Across genders or in first meetings, let the other person signal their preference rather than assuming physical contact.

You may also hear efendim (a polite "yes?", "pardon?", or "how can I help?") used in response to a question or to invite clarification. It is a mark of attentiveness, not deference, and appears across hierarchy levels.
