# Time Management

## Making a Good Impression

Turkish professionals typically arrive on time for scheduled meetings, yet the way time unfolds once everyone gathers often surprises those from cultures where agendas proceed point by point. Conversations may shift direction as senior colleagues join late or introduce new priorities mid-discussion. Phone calls interrupt without apology, and side conversations emerge and dissolve. This polychronic rhythm reflects a relational culture where preserving connection outweighs locking people into a rigid sequence. In relationship-driven firms especially, the flow responds to who is present and what they need, not simply what was written in the calendar invite. If you anchor discussions with occasional check-ins on where things stand, you maintain forward motion without imposing a structure that feels mechanical.

Meeting formats vary by company type. International firms tend to follow clear agendas and documented action points, while local businesses shape discussions more organically around senior input and emerging concerns. This flexibility grows from Turkey's hierarchical decision-making, where authority concentrates at the top and priorities shift as leaders weigh in. A formal agenda may serve as a starting point rather than a binding script. Ask early what the meeting aims to achieve, and if the conversation drifts, guide it gently back rather than insisting on strict sequencing. That restraint registers as respect for the relational process.

Timelines stretch more than schedules do. Deadlines shift when approvals stack up, stakeholders enter late, or relationships require tending before decisions can move. This reflects a collectivist orientation where consensus and senior alignment precede closure. Rather than treating a deadline as fixed, confirm next steps in writing with named owners and build review buffers into your planning. Decision-making can feel slow as trust forms and hierarchies align, but once clarity emerges and relationships solidify, implementation often accelerates sharply. If progress stalls, ask what needs to happen next. That question reveals pathways more reliably than pressure, and it reinforces the shared accountability that underpins long-term partnership in Turkish business.
