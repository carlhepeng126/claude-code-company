# Business Etiquette

## Making a Good Impression

Business cards are exchanged early, often at the start of a meeting, and a clear job title helps. Turkey's status-conscious culture means seniority and role shape how people relate from the first introduction. English-only cards work fine, but adding Turkish on the reverse is increasingly appreciated, especially in a context where language pride has grown stronger in recent years. The gesture recognizes identity alongside efficiency.

Personal space tends to be closer than in cultures where physical distance equals professionalism. Direct eye contact, expressive hand gestures, and standing nearer during conversation all signal engagement and warmth. This reflects Turkey's relationship-first approach, where trust grows through human connection before formal process. Styles vary widely by region and person, so mirror your counterpart's comfort level rather than imposing a fixed standard. Folded arms or stepping back can read as cold or closed.

A few gestures carry specific meaning. A slight downward nod means yes, while no is shown with an upward head tilt or a quick "tsk" sound, both rooted in older Ottoman-era communication. Colleagues from cultures where yes and no are spoken aloud may miss these cues entirely, while someone from Turkey might find repeated verbal confirmation oddly emphatic. Avoid pointing the sole of your shoe toward anyone, as it's considered unclean, and never use the fig gesture (thumb between fingers), which is a sexual insult.

Communication early in a relationship leans toward warmth and indirectness. Refusals are softened into hesitation, "we'll revisit," or polite deferrals to protect face and preserve harmony. Summarize agreements clearly in writing after meetings to stay aligned without forcing premature commitments. Shared meals or coffee invitations build rapport more effectively than formal gifts, reflecting Turkey's tradition of generous hospitality. If invited to someone's home, bring sweets or chocolates. Alcohol is fine if you know your host drinks, but the gesture itself reinforces reciprocity more than the price tag.
