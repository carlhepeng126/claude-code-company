# Feedback and Coaching

## Managing Relationships

In Turkish workplaces, feedback is rarely delivered in isolation. It tends to be woven into conversation, softened by relationship, and shaped by awareness of hierarchy and face. Direct criticism, especially in front of others, can damage trust quickly. Even constructive feedback needs careful framing if it's to be heard as intended rather than as personal rejection. Managers often prefer to give corrective input in private, using indirect language or hypothetical scenarios to guide thinking without causing embarrassment. A phrase like "perhaps we could consider another approach" leaves room for adjustment without pointing to fault.

Praise, by contrast, can be more openly expressed, though it still tends to be relational rather than transactional. Public recognition works well when it acknowledges effort and contribution to the group, rather than singling someone out in a way that might set them apart from colleagues. Positive feedback given warmly and in person tends to resonate more deeply than anything written into a formal system.

Turkish professionals may also take time to reflect on feedback before responding or acting on it. This delay reflects thoughtfulness, and often a need to understand how the input fits within broader relationships and expectations. For colleagues from cultures where feedback is expected to prompt immediate response or visible action, this reflective pause can feel ambiguous. From the Turkish perspective, a colleague who responds instantly to critique without pausing to consider context or consult with trusted others may come across as reactive or overly individualistic.

Coaching conversations are most effective when they begin with rapport and acknowledge the person's broader context. Feedback that feels impersonal or purely procedural may not land, no matter how clearly structured.
