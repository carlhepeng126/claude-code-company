# Negotiating

## Working Together

Negotiations in Turkey draw energy from personal connection. Discussions move more smoothly when there's a foundation of trust between parties, which means early exchanges often explore credibility, shared interests, and whether the relationship feels sustainable before settling on final terms. This relational grounding reflects a broader preference for partnerships that endure beyond a single transaction. Leading with your best offer can leave little room for the give-and-take that builds rapport. Expect some back-and-forth, and be prepared to explain the reasoning behind any concessions you make. Framing adjustments in terms of the wider partnership rather than as tactical moves keeps the tone cooperative.

Decision-making can follow a layered path, especially in organizations with clear hierarchies. The person leading the negotiation may need to consult upward before committing, and senior stakeholders often step in once the proposal has enough shape to evaluate. Asking early who holds decision rights and what internal approvals are needed can help you gauge timing and avoid mistaking engagement for final authority.

Non-verbal cues add texture to verbal exchanges. A slight downward nod typically signals agreement, while a small upward chin movement paired with raised eyebrows or a soft "tsk" sound often means no. These gestures are subtle and not always consistent, so when uncertainty surfaces, confirming meaning aloud keeps both sides aligned without forcing interpretation where clarity would work better.
