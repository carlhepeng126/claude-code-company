# Business Meeting Culture

## Meetings & Presentations

Punctuality matters in Turkish business settings, especially in larger cities like Istanbul. Arriving on time shows respect, though traffic can be unpredictable, so building in a buffer is wise. If you're delayed, a quick message helps preserve the tone. In more traditional or observant organizations, Friday midday prayer times may affect scheduling, so it's worth checking availability when confirming the meeting.

First meetings often begin with mid-level colleagues who act as gatekeepers to senior decision-makers. This reflects the concentrated nature of authority in many Turkish organizations, where access to those who can approve decisions is earned through relationship and seniority. Greetings are polite and formal. Handshakes are common, but watch for cues, particularly around gender. Business cards are exchanged early, as titles and roles help frame how the relationship will unfold. Tea or coffee is usually offered, and accepting it eases the opening. The conversation may start informally before moving to the agenda, giving both sides a chance to read the other's character.

Interruptions such as phone calls or brief visits can happen, especially in relationship-driven environments. These are generally seen as part of maintaining wider networks, rather than signs of disrespect. Staying flexible and occasionally recapping key points keeps the discussion on track without appearing rigid. Senior figures tend to set the tone and direction, and addressing them with appropriate deference supports a productive exchange.

Meetings often close with a return to informal conversation, sometimes accompanied by refreshments. Saying goodbye individually, and acknowledging the most senior person present, leaves a positive impression. A short written follow-up summarizing what was agreed and outlining next steps helps clarify commitments without undermining the relational warmth that opened the door.
