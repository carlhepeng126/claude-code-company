# Business Lunch & Dinner

## Meetings & Presentations

Business dining in Turkey unfolds slowly, and that pace serves a purpose. Meals, especially dinners, create room for connection before business gets serious. Lunch may stay more functional, but even then, the rhythm is relaxed. Meze arrives first: small shared plates of humus, eggplant salad, stuffed vine leaves, or fava. These dishes invite easy offers and light comments on flavour, building warmth without forcing the conversation forward. The sharing itself creates openness, allowing people to read each other's tone and responsiveness before moving into negotiations or decisions.

Regional pride often shows up through food. The Aegean brings herbs and olive oil, the southeast favours spice and rich kebabs, Istanbul offers a mix from fresh fish to baklava. Noticing what's local or asking about a dish signals genuine interest and makes the conversation easier to enter. Food becomes a low-pressure bridge into rapport, especially when hierarchy or formality might otherwise slow things down.

When alcohol appears, rakı or wine may be served, and "şerefe" (cheers) often marks a moment of acknowledgment or friendship. Non-drinkers can join with tea or ayran without discomfort. The gesture of raising a glass together holds more weight than what's inside it. Turkish coffee usually closes the meal, ordered by sweetness level: sade (none), az şekerli (light), orta şekerli (medium), or şekerli (sweet). If a host offers seconds, a small taste shows appreciation more than a firm refusal does.
