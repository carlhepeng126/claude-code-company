# Effective Presentations

## Meetings & Presentations

Turkish audiences often listen for credibility before content. Opening with a brief personal introduction or a shared connection can help establish the trust that technical details alone rarely build. If someone in the room knows your work or has introduced you, referencing that relationship reinforces your legitimacy. In a culture where reliability over time holds real weight, people want to understand who you are and whether you will follow through, not just what you are offering today.

Present your proposal as a partnership, not a transaction. Link your points to how the relationship will actually function—who will be responsible for what, how decisions will be made, and what will happen when something needs adjusting. Turkish colleagues often respond better to a long-term frame that includes concrete support than to a pitch focused only on quarterly results. Show commitment through specifics: continuity of key people, local responsiveness, or service levels that go beyond the contract.

Expect the presentation to unfold as a conversation. Questions may come midway through, sometimes from different directions, and the discussion may move sideways before returning to your main point. Build pauses into your structure and be ready to explain the reasoning behind your offer, not just the headline figures. If the thread wanders, gently guide it back by summarizing what has been agreed and what still needs a decision.

You may notice phones being checked or brief side conversations, especially in offices where people are managing multiple priorities. Keep your points clear, return to the thread without commentary, and use short checkpoints to stay aligned. Close with a written recap and a simple action list—some colleagues may prefer to clarify details one-to-one after the meeting rather than in front of the group.
