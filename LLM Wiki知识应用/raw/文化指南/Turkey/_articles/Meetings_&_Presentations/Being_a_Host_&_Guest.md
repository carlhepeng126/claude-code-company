# Being a Host & Guest

## Meetings & Presentations

Turkish hospitality reflects the belief that guests are *misafir Allah'ın misafiri* (God's guests), bringing fortune to the home. This view shapes how hosts approach meals, whether in restaurants or at home. A host will prepare or choose offerings that reflect regional heritage—Aegean meze platters, Cappadocia's *testi kebab* (a meat dish slow-cooked and served in a sealed clay pot), or layered Istanbul baklava. The effort invested reflects the guest's value, so accepting offers warmly and showing appreciation for what has been prepared builds trust. Repeated servings are a sign of esteem, and reciprocating later with your own invitation signals that the relationship has a future.

Business meals usually take place in restaurants, where the inviter will insist on paying. A brief and courteous attempt to contribute—offering once—honors the gesture without undermining it. Pushing too hard risks diminishing the host's role. These exchanges test reciprocity and reliability, both central to enduring partnerships in Turkey. Follow up with your own invitation soon after to keep the balance visible.

Meals unfold progressively. Meze arrive first, then mains. Start as dishes come and help yourself from shared plates, pacing unhurriedly since second helpings affirm the host's effort. Crossing cutlery signals a pause; leaving traces on communal dishes shows abundance rather than waste. Asking about preparation—"Is this a family recipe?"—opens deeper conversation and shows genuine interest.

Home invitations are rarer in urban business settings but mark real warmth when they happen. Arrive on time with modest sweets or chocolates, and remove shoes at the door unless the host indicates otherwise. Evenings stretch late with rounds of tea, and departing early can feel abrupt. Mirroring the host's energy and staying engaged throughout shows respect for the intimacy of the gesture.
