# Business Entertaining

## Meetings & Presentations

Hospitality in Turkey reflects the deeply held belief that guests bring blessing. When Turkish colleagues invite you to dinner, they often extend care beyond the meal itself: arranging transport, recommending hotels, or personally guiding you through unfamiliar parts of the city. This generosity is relational investment, a visible commitment to the partnership you're building together. Accepting this attentiveness with warmth and genuine appreciation is part of the exchange. It confirms you're serious about the relationship, not just the transaction.

Meals unfold slowly, often over shared plates of meze, grilled meats, and seasonal vegetables. Regional pride runs deep. An Ankara host may order testi kebab, an Izmir colleague boyoz pastries, an Istanbul counterpart baklava from a trusted supplier. Showing curiosity about how a dish is prepared locally or accepting a second helping builds connection in ways that formal conversation cannot. Business may not be discussed at all during the meal, or only lightly, usually after tea or dessert. The host decides when, and sometimes whether, to raise it. Starting too early can feel transactional. The gathering itself deepens trust, and once rapport solidifies, the table becomes a natural setting for testing ideas or finding alignment.

Alcohol is common in secular and urban circles. Rakı, wine, and beer may be poured freely among those who drink. But tea and ayran, a salted yogurt drink, ensure everyone can participate comfortably. Non-drinkers face no pressure, and the warmth of the occasion lies in shared time rather than what's in the glass. When hosting Turkish colleagues in return, lead with non-alcoholic options and let others choose from there.

The person who invites traditionally covers the bill, often after a brief, polite tussle. Reciprocating later with coffee or lunch, rather than insisting on splitting the check in the moment, shows goodwill and keeps the relationship balanced over time.
