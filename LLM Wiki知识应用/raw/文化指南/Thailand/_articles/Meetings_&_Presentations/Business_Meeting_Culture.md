# Business Meeting Culture

## Meetings & Presentations

Meetings in Thailand often feel warm and conversational, but beneath the friendly surface runs a quiet awareness of hierarchy and face. The room's social stability depends on everyone knowing their place without needing to assert it. Junior participants are there to observe and learn, rarely speaking unless invited. Even across peer levels, rank, age, and organizational position shape who speaks first, whose view settles a point, and whose silence signals caution rather than agreement.

The concept of *sia naa* (loss of face) acts as the meeting's invisible agenda. Disagreement is rarely voiced head-on. Instead, people soften language, redirect topics, or delay resolution to avoid putting anyone in an uncomfortable position. This protects both their own dignity and yours. A colleague from a culture where decisions are resolved through open debate may read this as evasion or a lack of clarity. From a Thai perspective, pushing for immediate resolution in front of others can feel aggressive and destabilizing, creating embarrassment where none was necessary.

*Kreng jai*, the reluctance to impose or burden others, further shapes participation. People may hold back questions, offer indirect pushback, or wait for a senior person to create permission for a different view. Silence often means the issue needs more safety to discuss, not that no one has concerns. Watch for vague phrasing, requests to "check with the team," or repeated confirmations that sound agreeable but carry hesitation. These are signs that consensus hasn't yet formed.

Decisions often mature outside the meeting itself. Side conversations, follow-up calls, and informal soundings build alignment in ways that preserve harmony. The meeting confirms shared understanding; the real commitment comes later, once it can be managed without friction.
