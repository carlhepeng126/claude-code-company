# Dress Code

## Making a Good Impression

The British Indian Ocean Territory has no permanent civilian population and no local business culture. The territory is administered by the British government and hosts a joint UK-US military facility on Diego Garcia, the largest atoll. Professional norms on the island reflect British and American military and contractor workplace standards rather than a distinct local tradition.

In practice, dress on Diego Garcia is shaped by function and setting. Military personnel follow the dress codes of their respective services. Civilian contractors and administrative staff typically dress in practical business casual or casual attire suited to the tropical climate and operational environment. Lightweight fabrics, closed-toe footwear for safety reasons, and sun protection are common across roles. Formal business dress is rare and generally reserved for official visits or ceremonial occasions involving senior military or government officials.

The isolation of the facility and the absence of a host culture mean that workplace appearance is guided more by institutional policy and practicality than by local expectation or the need to signal respect for partnership in the way you might in a country with its own professional norms. If you are visiting or working temporarily, check in advance with your host organization about any specific requirements. What you wear will depend more on the nature of your work and the protocols of the institution you are engaging with than on any territory-wide dress convention.
