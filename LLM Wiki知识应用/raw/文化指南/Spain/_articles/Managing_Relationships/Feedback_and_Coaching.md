# Feedback and Coaching

## Managing Relationships

In Spain, feedback works best when it respects dignity and invites dialogue. Public criticism, especially if delivered bluntly, risks causing embarrassment and damaging the relationship. Spanish professionals value being treated with respect, and a challenge to their competence in front of others can feel like a personal affront. Developmental conversations should happen privately, framed as collaborative exchanges rather than one-way evaluations.

Feedback is typically delivered through conversation, not formal critique. A manager might explain the context, offer constructive suggestions, and ask for the other person's view. This approach reflects Spain's preference for explicit communication tempered by warmth and relational awareness. The goal is clarity without harshness. Encouragement paired with honest guidance builds trust and makes the message easier to receive. A colleague from a culture where feedback is more direct and task-focused might read this style as indirect or overly cautious, while a Spanish professional might perceive unfiltered critique as rude or dismissive of their contribution.

Generational differences shape expectations. Younger professionals, particularly in urban centers and international firms, often prefer more straightforward feedback and may be comfortable with structured performance reviews. Older colleagues, especially in more traditional sectors, typically expect greater tact and may view blunt assessments as disrespectful. Building the relationship first makes feedback more effective across generations. When trust exists, even difficult conversations can be framed as shared problem-solving rather than judgment.

Use concrete examples to support your points, but choose them carefully to avoid singling someone out or creating discomfort. Asking for the person's perspective turns feedback into a two-way exchange and reinforces the sense that the conversation is about growth, not fault-finding.
