# Delegating & Supervising

## Managing Relationships

Spanish management blends centralized authority with deeply relational expectations. The supervisor is expected to be the most knowledgeable person in the room, and asking subordinates for input on decisions can be read as uncertainty rather than shared ownership. Authority flows downward, and employees expect clear direction on both what needs to be done and how to approach it. This doesn't mean micromanagement, but it does mean being willing to provide structure. Delegation without detailed guidance often leaves team members waiting for further instruction rather than taking initiative beyond their brief.

At the same time, Spain is personalista: professional trust is inseparable from personal connection. Employees give more of themselves to a manager they genuinely feel cares about them as a person. Asking about family, showing interest in life outside work, spending time in conversation that has nothing to do with the task at hand — these are not intrusions. They are what build the foundation on which performance rests. A manager who stays distant, no matter how fair or competent, will struggle to win the loyalty that unlocks discretionary effort.

The concept of dignidad (dignity) shapes how feedback must be delivered. Public correction, however mild, is experienced as a social wound and will quietly damage the relationship. Keep all corrective conversations private, and frame them with personal warmth and respect for the individual. Positive recognition, by contrast, can and should be shared openly.

Younger professionals and those in multinational environments are shifting toward more collaborative styles, but the relational logic remains. Authority still flows down, and loyalty still flows from genuine personal regard.
