# Business Etiquette

## Making a Good Impression

Spanish business culture blends formal courtesy with relational warmth, shaped by centuries of monarchy, bureaucracy, and Catholic tradition. Professionalism here includes respect for hierarchy and seniority, particularly in traditional industries, alongside a clear expectation of personal connection. The result is a style that favors measured politeness over bluntness, and steady relationship-building over transactional speed. Greetings may include small talk about family or mutual acquaintances, and exchanges often move through multiple topics before settling on a decision. This reflects a philosophical conversational style rooted in testing trustworthiness and establishing common ground before business deepens.

Criticism requires careful handling. Spanish colleagues place high value on personal dignity, known as *pundonor* (a sense of honor and self-respect), and negative feedback delivered too directly will often be taken personally. Frame concerns with logic and balance them with positive observations where possible. A colleague from a culture where feedback is expected to be plainly stated may read this diplomacy as evasive, while a Spanish counterpart may interpret blunt critique as a personal slight rather than professional candor.

Physical greetings vary by familiarity and gender. Handshakes are standard at first meetings, but close male colleagues may exchange an *abrazo* (a brief hug with a shoulder pat) or use a two-handed shake. Women often greet each other with a light embrace and a kiss on each cheek, left first. Regional styles also differ: Castilians tend toward clarity, Catalans toward measured analysis, Andalusians toward expressive warmth, and Basques toward initial reserve with deeper loyalty once trust forms.

Gift-giving is generally reserved for the close of a deal. If offering a gift, choose quality over extravagance and present it well-wrapped. Spanish colleagues typically open gifts immediately, so expect the same if you receive one.
