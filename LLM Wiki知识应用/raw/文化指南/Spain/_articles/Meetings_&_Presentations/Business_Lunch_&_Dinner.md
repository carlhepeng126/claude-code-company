# Business Lunch & Dinner

## Meetings & Presentations

In Spain, meals are more than professional courtesy. Food culture connects to regional and national identity, from *paella* (a rice dish with saffron and seafood or meat, originally from Valencia) to *pintxos* (small bites served on bread, especially in the Basque Country), *jamón ibérico* (cured ham from acorn-fed pigs), and local wines. These aren't just menu choices. They reflect where people are from and what they take pride in. Business meals offer a chance to build trust and enjoy conversation, not just to close a deal. A colleague who treats the meal as a tick-box exercise risks seeming transactional in a context where relationship-building happens through shared time and good humour.

Timing runs late by the standards of many northern European or North American schedules. Lunch may start at 2pm and run until 4pm. Dinner often begins at 9pm or later. A colleague from a culture where dinners start at 6pm may find the rhythm unfamiliar, while Spanish colleagues might read an early departure as disinterest. The meal itself unfolds slowly. Small talk comes first. Business topics, if raised at all, usually wait until the end. Rushing to the agenda or leaving quickly can interrupt the relational rhythm the meal is meant to create.

Wine is common but never compulsory. Offer choice and ask about dietary needs in advance. Lingering after the meal finishes is part of the rhythm, a sign that the time together has value beyond the transaction.
