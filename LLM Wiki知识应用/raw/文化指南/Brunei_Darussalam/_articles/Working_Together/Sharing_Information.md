# Sharing Information

## Working Together

In Brunei, information flows through established relationships and clear hierarchies rather than being widely distributed across teams. Authority structures matter here. Senior figures control what is shared and with whom, and people generally wait for decisions to be communicated from above rather than volunteering information across levels. This reflects the country's monarchical system and the deep respect for rank embedded in Malay Islamic Monarchy, which shapes how workplace roles and communication channels are understood.

Expecting open-door access to senior leaders or assuming information will be shared freely in meetings can misread the situation. A colleague from a culture where flat communication is the norm might interpret this reserve as withholding or inefficiency. From the Bruneian perspective, sharing information too quickly or bypassing someone in the chain can feel disrespectful and disruptive. The caution reflects care for hierarchy and social harmony, not reluctance to collaborate.

Building personal trust is the practical route to clearer information flow. Bruneians share more openly once they know you, respect the structure, and demonstrate discretion. Informal moments, tea breaks, and respectful one-on-one conversations often reveal more than formal channels. If you need information, approach the right person at the right level and allow time for the relationship to develop. Direct requests to someone you have not built rapport with are less likely to succeed.

Younger professionals and those with international exposure may communicate more directly, especially in multinational firms or technical sectors. Still, deference to seniority and a preference for careful, relationship-based sharing remain consistent across most workplaces.
