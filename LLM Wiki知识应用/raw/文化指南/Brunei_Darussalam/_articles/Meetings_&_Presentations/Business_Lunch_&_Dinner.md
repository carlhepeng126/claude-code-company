# Business Lunch & Dinner

## Meetings & Presentations

Business meals in Brunei Darussalam offer a window into the country's cultural fabric, shaped by Malay Islamic traditions and the sultanate's deep respect for social harmony. These occasions are less about closing deals and more about building trust through unhurried conversation and shared hospitality. The meal itself provides a neutral, respectful setting where relationships can develop without the directness of formal negotiation.

Alcohol is not served, reflecting Brunei's adherence to Islamic principles. Meals typically feature Malay cuisine such as *nasi katok* (rice, fried chicken, and sambal), seafood curries, and *ambuyat* (a sticky sago dish eaten with bamboo forks). If you have dietary needs, mention them quietly to your host in advance rather than at the table, which allows them to accommodate you without drawing attention to the request.

Expect a slower rhythm than in cultures where meals are scheduled tightly or followed immediately by business discussion. Rushing through courses or steering conversation toward contracts too early can feel transactional and may undermine the relational foundation you're meant to be building. A colleague from a culture where decisions are made quickly may read this pace as evasive, while a Bruneian counterpart may interpret abrupt pivots to business as disrespectful or premature.

Status differences are observed with care. Senior guests are served first, and it's common to wait for the most senior person to begin eating before others do. If you're hosting, show similar deference to rank, and allow the conversation to unfold naturally rather than using the meal as a platform for persuasion.
