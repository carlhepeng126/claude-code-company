# Effective Presentations

## Meetings & Presentations

Presentations in Brunei are shaped by the country's strong respect for hierarchy and the cultural principle of maintaining harmony. If you are delivering a proposal or briefing, your tone should be calm, measured, and respectful. Avoid language that could be read as pushing for an immediate decision or putting pressure on senior figures in the room. Bruneians value humility and collaboration over assertiveness, and a hard sell may be perceived as culturally out of step. Lead with your intent to support shared goals rather than framing your ideas as the only solution.

Credibility is built through relationships, not through self-promotion. Highlight your organization's track record or relevant examples, but do so without overstating your role or making comparisons that diminish local expertise. Audiences in Brunei respond well to presentations that acknowledge existing efforts and position your proposal as complementary rather than corrective. A colleague from a culture where confidence is signaled through directness might interpret this approach as underselling, while a Bruneian presenter might view overt self-promotion in return as lacking grace.

Allow time for questions, but don't expect them to come immediately or in front of the group. Bruneians may prefer to raise concerns privately after the session, particularly if the question could be seen as challenging authority or exposing a gap in understanding. Follow up individually with key stakeholders rather than assuming silence means agreement.

Keep slides simple and visually clear. Supporting materials in Malay are appreciated, especially if your audience includes civil servants or public sector partners. Dense text or overly technical language can create barriers, particularly if interpretation is involved.
