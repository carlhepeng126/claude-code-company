# Business Etiquette

## Making a Good Impression

In Brunei's business environment, composure and courtesy shape every professional exchange. The country's Islamic monarchy influences daily conduct, and hierarchy runs through workplace interactions in ways that may feel subtle but are closely observed. Deference to seniority is expected, and titles matter. Address people formally unless invited otherwise, and use titles with care. A direct challenge to someone senior, even if unintended, risks causing visible discomfort and may quietly damage the relationship.

Physical greetings between men and women follow Islamic norms of modesty. A handshake is usually acceptable between men, but wait for a woman to offer her hand first. If she doesn't, a smile and a slight nod are enough. Many local businesswomen will not shake hands with men outside their family. What might feel like hesitation to a colleague from a more physically demonstrative culture reflects religious observance, while someone from Brunei meeting a colleague who reaches for a handshake without pause may read it as culturally unaware rather than simply friendly.

Meetings begin with pleasantries and build toward the business topic gradually. Jumping straight to your agenda can seem transactional and may cool the tone before discussion even starts. Time moves differently here, shaped by Islamic prayer schedules and a cultural preference for deliberation over haste. Meetings may pause for prayer, and decisions rarely happen in the room. Give space for consultation and reflection rather than pressing for a conclusion on the spot.

Dress conservatively. Men should wear long trousers and long sleeves. Women should ensure their clothing covers shoulders, arms, and knees. This expectation holds year-round and reflects both religious values and respect for the monarchy.
