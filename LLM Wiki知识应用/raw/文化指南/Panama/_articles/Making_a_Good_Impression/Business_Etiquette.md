# Business Etiquette

## Making a Good Impression

Panama's business etiquette reflects a society skilled at reading difference and calibrating tone. As a transit hub shaped by migration and external presence, Panamanians often switch registers depending on the context. Warmth and openness can coexist with careful boundaries. This agility is practical, not performative. Knowing when to lean in and when to hold back is valued, and missteps in tone can feel like a failure to notice where you are.

Communication is polite and often indirect. A straight "no" is uncommon, especially where maintaining face is concerned. Non-committal answers like "maybe" or "we will look into it" soften refusal without creating confrontation. Listen for what's left unsaid. If someone begins telling a story at length, it's rarely filler. Panamanians enjoy narrative, and the story may reveal context, relationships, or intent. Cutting in or visibly checking the time risks signaling disrespect. For a colleague from a culture where decisions are made quickly, this can feel like avoidance. From a Panamanian perspective, rushing through the exchange may suggest the relationship doesn't count.

Panamanians pay close attention to how you show up. Eye contact signals trust. A handshake both opens and closes an interaction, confirming the relationship. Beyond that, physical contact is minimal. Don't grab an arm to emphasise a point or pat someone on the back. Avoid the okay sign, which is read as obscene. Business cards are common and should include your title and qualifications, ideally with Spanish on one side. Study a card when you receive it rather than pocketing it immediately.

Panama's history of external control has left a sensitivity to being treated as a route rather than a place. Avoid framing the country as a corridor or a convenience. Lead with respect for sovereignty, and your etiquette will land better.
