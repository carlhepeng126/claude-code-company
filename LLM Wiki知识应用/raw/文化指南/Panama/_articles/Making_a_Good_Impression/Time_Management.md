# Time Management

## Making a Good Impression

Panama's approach to time is shaped by geography and infrastructure. The canal corridor runs on schedules and operational discipline, where the global logistics economy demands that ships transit on time and that systems align across borders. In this environment, professionals often expect clarity around timing and tend to work with tighter deadlines than elsewhere in the region. When working with colleagues based in Panama City or the canal zone, treat schedules seriously and confirm dependencies early.

In the interior, time bends toward community rhythm. In towns like Las Tablas, carnival is not a long weekend but a months-long season of preparation, rivalry, and public life. During these cycles, availability can drop and priorities shift toward shared obligations. The same applies around major festivals or patron saint days. When planning across Panama, ask about festival seasons and build around them rather than through them.

Climate now shapes urgency in ways that formal schedules cannot always accommodate. Drought affects canal operations, and when water levels fall, national conversations and operational priorities recalibrate quickly. What seemed routine last month may become urgent this month, or may be delayed while attention goes elsewhere. Build buffers for environmental disruption and treat water and infrastructure constraints as real planning factors, not exceptions.

A colleague accustomed to fixed-schedule cultures may read this flexibility as disorganization. From Panama, the reverse may also hold: the refusal to adjust a meeting when a genuine disruption occurs can read as rigid or disconnected from context. The difference lies in how tightly time is held when conditions change.
