# Dress Code

## Making a Good Impression

In Panama City and the canal corridor, professional polish functions as a social signal. The capital sits at the crossroads of global finance, logistics, and legal services, and in these outward-facing sectors, appearance communicates seriousness and intent. Men typically wear business suits with ties, even in heat and humidity. Women opt for tailored suits or dresses, paired with careful attention to hair, nails, and grooming. This expectation holds across settings, from office meetings to formal dinners. Looking polished reads as readiness to engage professionally, while appearing careless can undermine credibility before a conversation even begins.

Heat shapes what people wear, but it does not override the expectation of effort. Lightweight fabrics and breathable cuts are practical, but the presentation remains deliberate. Some businessmen wear a guayabera, a lightweight cotton tunic worn untucked, though this tends to be less common among younger professionals and is rarely adopted by foreigners. In most formal contexts, default to a full suit until you understand the specific norm of that sector or group.

Panama's interior carries a strong tradition of visible cultural pride, most notably through the pollera, an intricately crafted traditional dress used in festivals and national celebrations. This garment reflects hours of specialised artisanal work and reinforces the cultural idea that clothing can communicate respect for community and occasion. When attending events tied to cultural pride or public visibility, treat your choice of dress as a reflection of your regard for the setting.

In Indigenous comarca contexts, clothing is read through local norms and protocol expectations. Respectful practicality is key. Avoid anything that might feel intrusive or tone-deaf in a community-rooted environment where territoriality and respect for local authority shape interaction.
