# Delegating & Supervising

## Managing Relationships

Panama's workplace hierarchy has deep roots in three centuries of Spanish colonial rule, which created a rigidly stratified social order reinforced by the Catholic Church's emphasis on deference and patron-client relationships. The expectation that the powerful protect the less powerful in exchange for loyalty still shapes how authority works in Panamanian organizations. Managers are understood to hold their position because of superior knowledge and skill, and consulting those lower in the hierarchy can feel inappropriate to the supervisor themselves. Openness that looks like uncertainty will undermine credibility in a setting where leaders are expected to provide clear direction. If you want input or participation from your team, frame it as explicitly invited rather than assuming people will volunteer ideas.

Employees will rarely act beyond their defined role without explicit permission, though they will often step in to help solve a problem when asked. Many prefer to avoid positions where they might be blamed for things going wrong and lose face. This means giving clear instructions and checking in on progress toward deadlines, which may fall lower on the priority list than they would in cultures with a more linear approach to time. Loyalty flows both ways: teams will support their manager and each other if the company shows loyalty in return.

Panama's position as a global transit economy has introduced more participative styles in multinationals and tech companies, but traditional or family-run businesses often maintain the paternalistic model. Personal relationships remain the foundation of business trust across all sectors. People do business with those they know, so investing time in personal connection alongside task management is essential. Never correct or criticize in front of others. Public embarrassment damages both the individual and the relationship, and dignity is not easily restored once lost.
