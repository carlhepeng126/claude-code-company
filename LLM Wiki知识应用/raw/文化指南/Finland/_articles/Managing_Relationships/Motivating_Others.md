# Motivating Others

## Managing Relationships

Finnish professionals draw energy from honest treatment and real autonomy. In a welfare-state context shaped by the 1990s recession, job security still registers as valuable, but overpromising feels suspicious. A manager who says "this role is secure for at least three years barring major disruption, and here's why" will be taken more seriously than one who offers vague assurance or permanent guarantees. Finns have learned to trust candor over hype, and this applies equally to how roles are framed and how risk is discussed.

Autonomy functions as a baseline expression of respect. Micromanagement reads not as diligence but as doubt in someone's competence, and it demotivates quickly. People respond well when given clear objectives but freedom to choose their tools, sequence their tasks, and shape how their day unfolds. Decision latitude within known boundaries is more energizing than detailed instruction, and it builds the kind of commitment that lasts beyond a single project.

Meaning registers most clearly when tied to concrete, pro-social outcomes. Grandiose mission statements generate less traction than being able to point to a safer machine, a more reliable system, lower emissions, or work that makes a teacher's or engineer's life measurably easier. Finns value craft and quality, and noticing that effort, rather than celebrating speed alone, deepens engagement. Showing how a project contributes in practical ways, without rhetoric, speaks more clearly than abstract purpose.

Time for life outside work is not negotiable. Band practice, coaching youth teams, association work, time at the summer cabin with family — these are part of what it means to live well. A leader who treats evenings and weekends as default extensions of the workday will lose credibility. In Finland, protecting that boundary is seen as good structure, not indulgence, and honoring it builds trust and sustained performance.
