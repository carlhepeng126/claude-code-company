# Negotiating

## Working Together

Finnish commercial discussions rest on a broader cultural logic shaped by decades of consensus-building in labor relations. The term *sopimusyhteiskunta* (agreement society) describes a system where unions, employers, and the state negotiate national frameworks that set the tone for entire sectors. This collective bargaining tradition influences how Finns approach business negotiations: the assumption is that there is a fair middle ground to be found within known parameters, not a contest where the most aggressive party wins. People expect you to say roughly what you mean from the start. Inflated opening positions or artificial time pressure feel manipulative rather than strategic. A Finnish counterpart may sit quietly through the performance and then simply say the proposal does not work for them.

When preparing your offer, bring realistic numbers and a modest margin for adjustment. Coming in with an extreme anchor and planning to "meet in the middle" will undermine your credibility before technical discussion begins. Finnish buyers and procurement teams want to understand the reasoning behind your price, not just the headline figure. Expect questions about unit costs, lifecycle ownership, energy use, and how a product or system will perform in year fifteen. This is partly shaped by climate and infrastructure realities, where long-term durability outweighs short-term savings. Have specialists or engineers ready to discuss detail. Technical depth builds trust more effectively than polished sales language.

In negotiations that touch working conditions, salaries, or contracts involving staff, you are negotiating in the shadow of collective agreements and the role of shop stewards (*luottamusmies*). Trying to bypass or downplay that system will be read as either naive or hostile. Involve union representatives early, be transparent about constraints, and recognize their role as part of the process rather than an obstacle to be managed.
