# Business Entertaining

## Meetings & Presentations

An after work in Finland typically means one or two drinks, maybe something small to eat, then home. The term itself has become a Finnish workplace fixture, but the practice runs short. Most colleagues have early starts, hobbies, or families waiting, and stretching a weeknight social into multiple rounds can feel pushy or out of step with how people structure their evenings. If you're organizing something, plan for it to be low-key and time-bounded. Let people leave when they're ready without needing an excuse.

The sauna is a different kind of occasion altogether. Being invited to sauna with colleagues or business partners is a gesture of trust. Shared nudity, heat, silence, and often a lake dip flatten the usual markers of rank or formality. This is relationship-building space, not pitching space. The serious conversation, if it happens at all, usually takes place in the changing room afterward or over the meal that follows. A colleague from a culture where entertaining means showcasing hospitality or demonstrating closeness through conversation may find the quiet unsettling. For Finns, that same quiet is what makes the setting meaningful.

Splitting the bill is common, even when a manager is present. Insisting on picking up the tab every time can feel uncomfortably close to hierarchy-play or bribery, especially in a culture where power distance is low. If you're hosting, it's fine to offer, but don't assume generosity requires paying for everyone. Sometimes the more Finnish approach is a reasonable split.
