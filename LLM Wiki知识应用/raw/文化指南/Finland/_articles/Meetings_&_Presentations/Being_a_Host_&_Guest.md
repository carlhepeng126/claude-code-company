# Being a Host & Guest

## Meetings & Presentations

Being invited to someone's cottage, or *mökki*, by a lake marks a shift from professional acquaintance to inner circle. You will see your Finnish colleague in rubber boots and a wool hat rather than city clothes, and the weekend will involve shared tasks like heating the sauna, chopping wood, or rowing to fetch water. This is high trust hospitality, and the appropriate response is a simple, thoughtful gift like good coffee, chocolate, or a small local specialty rather than anything lavish.

At home, shoes come off at the door without ceremony. Food may be laid out buffet-style, and hosts will downplay the effort even if they have spent hours preparing. You might hear "it's nothing special" after a carefully made meal. This modesty is a social script, and the expected response is acceptance rather than elaborate praise. Offering to help with dishes or other tasks is part of fitting in, not an imposition.

Sauna etiquette varies by household. In many homes, men and women sauna separately, though some families mix. People may be nude or use towels. Conversation is fine, but silence is equally social. Sitting quietly and staring at the stove, or *kiuas*, without talking is not awkwardness but companionable ease. A colleague from a culture where hospitality centers on constant dialogue might read this silence as discomfort, while Finns may find continuous talking in the sauna intrusive. Topics often surface naturally afterwards over food or beer rather than during the heat itself.
