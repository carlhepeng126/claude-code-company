# Negotiating

## Working Together

People judge a potential partner by the respect and trustworthiness they show before weighing the fine print. In a culture shaped by patronage and hierarchy, how you listen, how you acknowledge constraints, and whether you seem interested in the other side's position reveal more than your opening offer. If early conversations feel focused on rapport rather than clause-by-clause comparison, that is the groundwork that allows a deal to move forward.

Saying no directly, especially to a senior counterpart or guest, creates discomfort. A proposal may drift instead of being refused outright. Delays, silence, and vague replies can all indicate that the other side has stepped back. If you sense the conversation losing momentum, invite a private discussion about whether the deal still makes sense. This allows the other party to exit without public loss of face. For a partner from a culture accustomed to faster clarity, this can read as indecision. From the Cambodian side, a blunt refusal or tight deadline can feel dismissive of the relationship.

Awareness runs deep of how foreign firms, traders, or local elites have sometimes profited disproportionately from land, labor, or contracts. People watch for signs that you operate differently. Show clearly who benefits, who assumes risk, and what happens if things go wrong. Back this with behavior, not just language in a contract. Haggling is expected, so hold some room for concession. Offering your best terms at the start can make you appear either naive or inflexible.
