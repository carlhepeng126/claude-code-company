# Decision-Making

## Working Together

In many Cambodian organizations, especially government ministries and family businesses, final decisions rest with a small circle of senior figures whose authority is rarely questioned openly. This concentration reflects both hierarchy and a lived memory of systems where stepping outside your role could have serious consequences. Mid-level managers often hesitate to sign off on bold choices alone. Instead, they seek cover in joint committees or keep minutes deliberately vague, spreading accountability across the group. The reluctance is rooted in a history of punitive structures where individual visibility brought risk, and it shapes how proposals move through an organization today.

For significant decisions involving land, large investments, or major organizational shifts, it is common to consult monks, fortune-tellers, older relatives, or respected bongs (older siblings or respected figures) alongside formal analysis. These consultations are not decorative. They offer reassurance that the decision aligns with deeper values and timing, and dismissing them as superstition will damage trust. If you are proposing a significant change, allow time for these conversations and focus on providing clear, honest information that can be carried into those consultations.

Understanding who ultimately needs to be comfortable with a decision is more useful than tracking who attended a meeting. Authority may rest with someone not present in the room, and your proposal may need to reach them indirectly through trusted intermediaries. A colleague from a culture where decisions are made quickly in the room may find this process opaque or slow, while a Cambodian counterpart may read that speed as reckless or disconnected from the relationships that will sustain the work. Offering shared-risk mechanisms such as pilots, phased commitments, or trial periods allows individuals to move forward without feeling they are stepping out alone, and it respects the caution that protects both person and group.
