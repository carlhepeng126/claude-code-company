# Motivating Others

## Managing Relationships

Many Cambodian employees are working to pay off debts, support aging parents, fund a sibling's education, or build a family home. The job itself often serves as a means to these ends rather than a source of personal identity or career ambition. A manager from a culture where career progression and individual achievement are strong drivers may find that promotions or abstract corporate goals generate little enthusiasm. From the Cambodian side, that same focus on climbing the ladder can look narrow, even selfish, when family obligations are so pressing. Linking goals and rewards to tangible benefits — bonuses, stable contracts, health coverage — makes motivation visible and real.

Generosity holds moral weight. Helping a colleague, contributing to a funeral fund, or donating to a pagoda are all seen as building merit and good reputation. A workplace that treats time off for ceremonies as an inconvenience, or that discourages collective donations, clashes with this moral economy. Building in visible generosity — small hardship funds, flexibility for family events, or group contributions to community causes — helps people feel the organization itself behaves well.

Education and certification carry emotional resonance. Because the educated were targeted during the Khmer Rouge period, learning and formal recognition now hold deep social value. Training that leads to recognized skills, certificates, or titles can be highly motivating, honoring both ambition and the rebuilding of knowledge as something worthy and safe to pursue.
