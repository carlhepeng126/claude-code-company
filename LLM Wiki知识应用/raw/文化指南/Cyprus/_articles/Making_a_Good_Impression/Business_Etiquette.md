# Business Etiquette

## Making a Good Impression

Cyprus operates within layers of cultural influence that have left their mark on professional manners. Greek Cypriot norms reflect Orthodox Christian traditions and wider Mediterranean values around hospitality and respect. Turkish Cypriot workplace culture carries Ottoman and Turkish influence, emphasizing deference to seniority and collective harmony. Both communities share a preference for warmth and personal connection before formal business begins, though they express it differently.

Greetings tend to be warm but not overly effusive. A firm handshake, direct eye contact, and a genuine smile go a long way. Address people by their professional title and surname until invited to do otherwise, especially in more traditional settings or when dealing with senior figures. This shows respect for hierarchy, which remains influential even in modern, privately owned firms. Younger professionals and those working in tech or international companies may move to first names more quickly, but it's safer to wait for their lead.

Dress codes lean conservative, particularly in government offices, banking, and legal sectors. Men typically wear suits or business casual; women opt for smart, modest clothing. Appearing well-groomed signals professionalism and respect for the occasion. In creative industries or startups, especially in Nicosia or Limassol, expectations relax, but when meeting new contacts or attending formal discussions, err on the side of formality until the tone is set.

Small gestures of courtesy add up. Offering coffee or a light refreshment at the start of a meeting is common, and declining outright can feel abrupt. Accepting, even if you take just a sip, acknowledges the gesture and helps ease into conversation.
