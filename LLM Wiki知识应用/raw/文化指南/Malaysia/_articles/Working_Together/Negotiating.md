# Negotiating

## Working Together

Negotiation in Malaysia draws on musyawarah, a deliberation tradition that frames agreement-making as a shared process aimed at preserving cohesion rather than producing clear winners and losers. The goal is to find a settlement everyone can live with, and that shapes how discussions unfold. Conversations may circle back to earlier points, or pause to allow consultation with family, religious advisors, or senior figures not in the room. This rhythm reflects the need to confirm that the proposed terms feel socially and morally legitimate to multiple audiences, including ethnic, religious, or community networks. A colleague from a culture where decisions are made quickly may read this as avoidance or delay, while a Malaysian counterpart may see rapid closure as socially reckless or dismissive of the relationships at stake.

Malu, a form of social shame or embarrassment tied to loss of face, makes blunt confrontation costly. Proposals that back someone into a yes-or-no corner risk triggering discomfort that can stall or derail the entire discussion. Instead, offer options, phased arrangements, or room for soft disagreement. This allows your counterpart to move forward without losing dignity. The concept of budi, which centers on reading intention and generosity behind an offer, means people assess whether a proposal feels respectful or coercive. A technically strong deal presented without attention to how it lands socially may be declined on grounds that have little to do with price.

Build in time for relationship-building at the front end. Early meetings often establish tone and trust rather than resolve terms. Chinese and Indian Malaysian businesspeople may negotiate more directly and focus on commercial advantage, particularly in transactional settings, but even in these contexts, preserving face remains socially significant. Expect bargaining, and leave room for concessions that strengthen the sense of mutual benefit. Agreements move faster once there is a shared feeling of fair settlement, not when one side visibly wins.
