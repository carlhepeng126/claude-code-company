# Teamwork

## Working Together

Collaboration in Malaysia often begins long before the work itself. The concept of *gotong-royong* (mutual assistance undertaken together for shared benefit) shapes how teams function. Originally a term for communal work such as repairing a road or preparing for a festival, it describes a social grammar in which people share the load collectively, with value placed on the act of doing things together as much as on the outcome. In professional settings, this translates into an expectation that team effort is collective rather than divided into isolated contributions. A manager who assigns tasks without also creating opportunities for shared deliberation or joint problem-solving may find the group waits for more explicit coordination before moving forward.

Alignment is built through *musyawarah* (deliberation), a process of consultation designed to maintain group cohesion and dignity throughout decision-making. This is social alignment work, and it can take time. A colleague from a culture where decisions are made quickly may read this as hesitation or inefficiency, while a Malaysian colleague may read rapid decision-making as disregard for group consensus and an invitation to fracture. The goal of *musyawarah* is to ensure the team remains intact and that individuals feel included, so skipping this phase in pursuit of speed can leave team members uncertain or uncommitted, even if they have agreed aloud.

How someone works within the team is read as a reflection of character. *Budi bahasa* (refined conduct expressed through language and gesture) is how good intent and reliability become visible. Team members who show restraint, protect others from embarrassment, and speak tactfully are understood to have *budi*, and this earns trust. The concept of *malu* (a sense of shame or social exposure) functions as a regulator within teams. Calling someone out in front of others, even for a legitimate mistake, risks triggering *malu* and can damage the relationship and the group's cohesion. Feedback is more effective when given privately and framed in a way that preserves dignity.

Malaysia's ethnic and regional diversity means there is no single team culture. Malay-majority environments may center concepts like *adat* (customary norms) and *adab* (proper conduct), while Chinese Malaysian teams may draw on association-based networks, and Indian Malaysian groups on community or temple structures. Teams in East Malaysia often operate within indigenous customary frameworks distinct from Peninsular norms. Read the room's cultural center before assuming a uniform approach.
