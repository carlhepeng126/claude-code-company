# Delegating & Supervising

## Managing Relationships

American managers tend to delegate both tasks and decision-making authority, not just execution. This stems from a workplace culture that values individual autonomy and sees control over one's work as motivating. Delegating responsibility, not just assignments, is how you signal trust and give team members a stake in the outcome. When someone is given ownership of a piece of the project, they expect the room to shape how it gets done and to be recognized for the result.

The coaching model of leadership runs deep. Managers are expected to push for high performance while staying accessible and open to input. Even when you hold formal authority, the relationship functions better when subordinates feel they can question your reasoning or suggest a different approach. This does not undermine your position. It reflects a shared assumption that good ideas can come from anywhere and that being challenged helps refine the work. Regular one-on-one meetings and feedback loops support this, reinforcing that listening and responding are part of the manager's role.

For colleagues from cultures where delegation means detailed instruction and close oversight, this level of autonomy can feel like under-management. From the other side, an American team member may interpret closer supervision as micromanagement or a lack of confidence in their ability. Clarifying expectations early around both the outcome and the degree of freedom helps bridge this gap. When you delegate, be clear about what you are handing over and what you are not.
