# Leadership Styles

## Working Together

Leadership in the United States reflects the cultural ideal of upward mobility through hard work, ambition, and merit. The concept of the American Dream has shaped how people rise to positions of influence: roles are typically earned through visible achievement such as innovation or commercial impact, rather than granted by status or seniority. Titles alone hold limited sway. What counts is the value a person delivers and the results they produce. For colleagues from cultures where seniority or formal credentials define authority, this can feel disorienting. A manager from a culture where leadership is granted through years of service may find it difficult to accept direction from someone younger or less experienced, while an American leader may interpret deference to seniority as reluctance to challenge or contribute.

American leaders are expected to hit the ground running. Leadership is highly action-oriented, with indecision or delay often read as weakness. Strategic thinking is valued, but execution is essential. Phrases like "fail fast" or "course-correct" reflect a mindset that treats calculated risk as strength. Leadership transitions and new initiatives are frequent, and adaptability is seen as central to success. If you are working with an American leader, expect decisions to move quickly and be prepared to act without prolonged consultation or comprehensive planning.

Despite the emphasis on individual achievement, lasting impact depends on a leader's ability to unlock performance in others. Empowering teams and granting autonomy are core to the leadership approach. Employees are encouraged to solve problems independently, often leaning on peers rather than seeking help from a manager, which may be read as unpreparedness. Trust is extended early but depends on performance and can be withdrawn if expectations are not met. In cultures where employees expect clear instruction and regular oversight, this can create uncertainty. An American manager may assume silence means progress, while a team member from a culture where guidance is expected may feel unsupported.

More recently, emotional intelligence and sensitivity to individual working styles have gained emphasis, particularly with the rise of hybrid work and generationally diverse teams. These evolving expectations broaden the definition of effective leadership without replacing the core traits of decisiveness, autonomy, and performance.
