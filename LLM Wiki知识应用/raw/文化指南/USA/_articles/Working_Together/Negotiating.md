# Negotiating

## Working Together

American negotiators tend to move quickly toward a decision. The focus is often on speed, cost, or efficiency rather than on building a long-term partnership, and the goal is usually a concrete outcome by the end of the meeting. Presentations are polished and data-driven, often delivered through slides that set out a clear case. The relationship is generally understood to last as long as the project does. Once the objective is met, people move on without expecting the connection to continue. This task-focused approach reflects a broader cultural orientation toward results and the next opportunity.

Persistence is common. If an American counterpart hears a "no," they are likely to ask why and then reshape their argument to address the objection. The expectation is that if the reasoning is sound and the objections are answered, agreement will follow. This can feel circular to colleagues from cultures where a refusal is meant to end the discussion, but for Americans it reflects a problem-solving reflex. The conversation loops back not to pressure, but to find a path forward. If you prefer time to reflect or need to consult internally, make that clear early. Silence or hesitation may be read as an invitation to continue.

Flexibility helps. Someone will usually need to adjust their position for a deal to close, and Americans expect that as part of the process. The key is to be direct about what you can and cannot move on, so that compromise happens efficiently and the conversation stays focused on action.
