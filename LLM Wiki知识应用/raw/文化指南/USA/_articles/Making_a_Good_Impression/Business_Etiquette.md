# Business Etiquette

## Making a Good Impression

Americans tend to get down to business quickly, and their directness is rooted in a belief that clarity and efficiency show respect for everyone's time. Small talk happens, but it's usually brief and functional. Meeting time is limited, and the focus is on moving forward. If you come from a context where trust builds slowly through conversation and personal connection, this can feel transactional. An American colleague might read slower, more indirect exchanges as evasive or unfocused. What's happening is a different understanding of how professionalism works: in the United States, being straightforward and fact-oriented is considered trustworthy.

Body language reflects that same openness. Americans are encouraged from childhood to stand tall, make direct eye contact, and project confidence. A firm handshake and steady gaze signal sincerity. Physical distance is maintained at around a meter, and while back-slapping between men is common, any touch in cross-gender professional settings is rare due to a heightened awareness of harassment. The goal is to appear engaged but not overly familiar.

Business cards are exchanged casually, before or after a meeting, and aren't treated with particular ceremony. Carry enough to share, and if your title translates awkwardly, consider how it will read here. "Manager" sounds junior; "Senior Vice President" carries more weight. Don't be surprised if cards are left on the table during a discussion.

Gift giving in a professional setting is sensitive. Corporate policies often restrict receiving anything that could resemble a bribe, and a lavish gift will raise questions. If you bring something, keep it modest: chocolates, a book about your home country, or something related to a known interest. Gifts are opened immediately in front of the giver, so be ready for that response.
