# Dress Code

## Making a Good Impression

American business dress tends toward the practical rather than the polished. In many companies, men wear dress trousers with a sport coat or sweater, and women choose trouser suits or slacks with a blouse. The overall feel is often less formal than in parts of Europe or Asia, where tailoring and finish receive more attention. This reflects a broader cultural bias toward getting things done over standing on ceremony. Looking competent and approachable tends to matter more than looking expensive.

Regional and sector differences are real. The West Coast, especially in technology, skews casual. A chief executive in jeans and a T-shirt is not unusual in Silicon Valley. The East Coast remains more conservative, and certain industries hold firm to traditional formality. Banking, legal, accounting, and financial consulting still expect suits and ties. Many senior managers across sectors continue to dress formally, even where company policy allows otherwise. A colleague from a culture where dress conveys hierarchy may find it hard to read seniority in a room where the most senior person wears sneakers and the junior analyst wears a blazer.

Many companies observe dress-down Fridays, and the line between business casual and truly casual can blur. In large cities, you may see women walking to work in sneakers, switching to dress shoes on arrival. This mix of pragmatism and grooming is particularly visible in New York, where women tend to pay close attention to hair, nails, and makeup. Appearance still gets noticed, especially for women, even in environments that claim to prioritize performance over presentation.

Evening events can be harder to decode. Terms like "resort casual" or "informal" do not always mean what they suggest. If the invitation is unclear, asking directly is the safest move.
