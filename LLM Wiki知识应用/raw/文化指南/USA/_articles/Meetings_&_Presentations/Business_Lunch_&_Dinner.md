# Business Lunch & Dinner

## Meetings & Presentations

Business meals in the United States tend to be informal compared to many cultures, reflecting a broader preference for ease and directness in professional settings. Even at higher-end restaurants or client dinners, the atmosphere is usually relaxed. Mistakes around protocol are rarely held against you. The focus is on getting to know someone and moving work forward, rather than observing strict dining etiquette. That said, the setting itself offers clues. A lunch at a country club or upscale steakhouse suggests a more polished tone, while drinks at a pub or casual chain restaurant signal informality.

Americans typically eat dinner early, often between 5:30 and 8:00 pm, which can feel abrupt to colleagues from cultures where evening meals are social rituals that begin later and unfold slowly. Lunch meetings are common and functional, usually scheduled for an hour or less. Drinking alcohol at lunch is uncommon, especially in corporate environments, and some companies have policies against it. If invited to lunch, wait for your host to order before assuming a glass of wine is appropriate. At dinner, wine or cocktails are more typical, though the choice is always individual.

Service in American restaurants emphasizes speed, customization, and attentiveness. Servers often introduce themselves by name, describe daily specials in detail, and check in frequently throughout the meal. This level of interaction may feel overly familiar to visitors from cultures where restaurant service is more reserved, but it reflects customer expectations around friendliness and personal choice. Drinks are brought quickly, often before the meal is ordered, and menus offer extensive options with the assumption that nearly any dish can be modified. Tipping is standard practice, usually 15 to 20 percent of the total bill, and is considered part of the server's income rather than a discretionary reward.

Dietary preferences are openly discussed and accommodated without awkwardness. Vegetarian, gluten-free, kosher, vegan, or low-carb diets are common, and most restaurants are accustomed to handling them. If you are hosting, it's courteous to ask about dietary needs in advance so the venue can be chosen accordingly.
