# Motivating Others

## Managing Relationships

Motivation in Bolivia is deeply rooted in relationships and the need to support family. Many professionals, especially those with dependents in regions where economic opportunities remain limited, work to send money home or ensure their household can meet basic needs. When managers recognize that a colleague's effort directly supports parents, siblings, or children elsewhere, they understand what truly drives performance. Financial incentives matter, but they resonate more when paired with respect for the person behind the work.

Recognition works best when it is personal and sincere. A manager who takes time to thank someone individually, especially in a way that acknowledges their contribution to the team, creates loyalty. Public praise can feel uncomfortable in some contexts, so a quiet word or a message that highlights someone's role in a successful outcome often lands better than a formal announcement. People respond to being seen as individuals, not just as job titles.

Strict deadlines and pressure tactics tend to backfire. Bolivian workplaces often move at a pace shaped by relationships and the realities of infrastructure, where meetings start late and plans shift. Pushing for rigid timelines without room to adjust can feel disconnected from how things actually get done. Managers who set clear goals but remain flexible about how and when they are reached will see more sustained effort than those who enforce tight schedules that ignore local rhythms.

Younger professionals in urban centers may be drawn to opportunities for learning or advancement, while colleagues in smaller towns or with strong family obligations may prioritize stability and respect. Understanding what someone is working toward, whether it is supporting a family member's education or building a longer-term career, allows managers to offer what actually motivates, rather than what they assume will.
