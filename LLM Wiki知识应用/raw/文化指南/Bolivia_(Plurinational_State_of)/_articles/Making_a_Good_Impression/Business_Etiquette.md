# Business Etiquette

## Making a Good Impression

Business etiquette in Bolivia is shaped by a culture that values personal warmth and respect for social position. Greetings are often unhurried, and it's common to shake hands with everyone present when entering or leaving a meeting. Among people who know each other, a brief hug or a kiss on the cheek may follow, even in professional settings. These gestures build familiarity and show that the relationship extends beyond the purely transactional. Arriving at a meeting without acknowledging each person individually can seem abrupt or dismissive.

Titles matter. Addressing someone as *Doctor*, *Ingeniero* (engineer), or *Licenciado* (a university graduate, often used for professionals) acknowledges their education and standing. In hierarchical environments, especially in more traditional sectors or outside major cities like La Paz or Santa Cruz, using the correct title helps establish mutual respect. First names may be used once a relationship develops, but waiting for your counterpart to suggest it avoids overstepping.

Dress tends to be formal, particularly in government, banking, and established industries. Men typically wear suits; women opt for conservative business attire. In warmer lowland cities like Santa Cruz, dress codes may relax slightly, but erring on the side of formality is safer until you've gauged the office culture. Dressing too casually early on can undermine credibility, especially when building trust with senior colleagues or clients.

Gift-giving is not routine in professional contexts, but a small, thoughtful gift after a relationship has been established can strengthen the bond. Items from your home country or something for the office work well. Avoid anything overtly expensive, which may cause discomfort or be misread.
