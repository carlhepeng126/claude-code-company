# Business Entertaining

## Meetings & Presentations

Being invited to share ouzo or tsipouro with meze is often when the real evaluation begins. How you handle small plates passed around the table, whether you can let conversation drift from pleasantries into politics or family, and whether your phone stays in your pocket—all of this tells your Greek counterparts whether they want to work with you beyond the contract. Meals are not just relationship-building. They are a way of observing character and deciding if someone can be trusted over the long term.

The setting also reveals where you stand. There is a clear difference between the taverna on the tourist strip and the quieter place locals visit year-round, where the owner greets regulars by name. Being taken to the latter signals that you have moved inside the circle. Quality and atmosphere count more than formality or price. Greeks value expansive, personal hospitality, and the table is where that shows. Rushing the meal or steering too quickly toward business can feel transactional. Let your host decide when or whether to discuss work. Often, the meal itself is the point.

Over time, business entertaining may begin to include family. You might meet spouses, children, even parents. This is not a blurring of boundaries but a sign of deepening commitment. If this happens, respond with reciprocal openness—mentioning your own family or inviting them in turn when appropriate. You are being welcomed not just as a partner but as someone who belongs at a Greek table.

Put your phone away and lean into the slower rhythm. Conversations will meander. That is not wasted time. It is how trust deepens, and how both sides assess whether the relationship will hold.
