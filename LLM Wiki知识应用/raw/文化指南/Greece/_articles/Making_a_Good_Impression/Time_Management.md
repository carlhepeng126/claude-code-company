# Time Management

## Making a Good Impression

Time in Greece moves at two speeds. The phrase *siga siga* (slowly, slowly) reflects a cultural comfort with letting conversations unfold, taking a second coffee, or allowing a process to breathe. This unhurried rhythm serves a purpose: it protects the social fabric of the workday, the detours that keep relationships warm and decisions well-informed. Yet the same people who move slowly through the week will pull off extraordinary last-minute sprints when a real deadline hits, working through the night before an EU tender closes or a client submission is due. Both tempos coexist, and understanding which one applies in a given moment is key to planning effectively.

Meal times anchor the day in ways that cannot be easily moved. Lunch typically falls between 2 and 3 p.m., and evening meals stretch late. These are not simply breaks but defended rituals, moments when people step away from work to reconnect over food. Scheduling a three-hour workshop through lunch or stacking evening meetings night after night will meet quiet resistance or low energy. If a colleague from a culture where meetings are tightly scheduled over midday slots suggests a working lunch, they may read the reluctance as disengagement. From the Greek side, the request itself may feel like an intrusion into one of the few joys still worth protecting.

Greeks treat disruptions as part of the landscape. Strikes, protest marches through central Athens, farmers blocking highways with tractors, ferries canceled due to wind: these are met with a shrug rather than shock. Time plans are held loosely, not because people are careless but because experience has taught that rigidity breaks more often than it holds. When building timelines, name which deadlines are immovable and which phases can flex. Treat unexpected events as shared challenges to navigate together, and you will find more goodwill than if you respond with lectures about better planning.
