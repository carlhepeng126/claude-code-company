# Business Lunch & Dinner

## Meetings & Presentations

Business meals in Benin tend to be relaxed, social occasions where trust is quietly built through shared time rather than formal agendas. Conversations often begin with personal topics—family, health, weekend plans—before turning to work. Jumping straight into business matters can feel abrupt and transactional. Taking time over food and conversation shows respect for the relationship and signals that you value the person, not just the deal.

Meals are typically long and paced around the rhythm of the group, not the individual. Courses arrive gradually, and finishing quickly or checking your phone repeatedly may be read as impatience or disinterest. If you're working with colleagues from cultures where meals are shorter and task-focused, this pace may feel inefficient. From a Beninese perspective, however, rushing through a meal can suggest that the relationship isn't valued. Allow the conversation to unfold naturally and avoid glancing at your watch.

In many professional settings, especially outside international hotels, restaurants do not serve alcohol, reflecting the country's Muslim and Christian traditions. Soft drinks, fruit juices, and sometimes bissap (a hibiscus drink) or ginger juice are common. If alcohol is served, it's usually beer or wine, but it's wise to let your host take the lead. Asking a local colleague in advance can prevent awkward moments.

Sharing food is customary. Dishes may be placed in the center of the table for everyone to serve themselves, and offering a taste of your dish to others is seen as generous and inclusive. Vegetarian options may be limited outside larger cities, so mention any dietary restrictions ahead of time.
