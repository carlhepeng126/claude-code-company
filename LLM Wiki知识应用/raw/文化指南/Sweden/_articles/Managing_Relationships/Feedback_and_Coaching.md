# Feedback and Coaching

## Managing Relationships

Feedback in Sweden is expected to be honest and delivered without fuss. The ideal is to speak plainly about what needs to change while keeping the tone calm and factual. You might hear "this isn't good enough yet" or "we need to adjust this," offered in a matter-of-fact way rather than through raised voices or emotional language. Public criticism is strongly avoided. This directness comes from a belief that clear, early feedback helps everyone improve, but the flat organizational culture means it must be delivered without humiliation. For a manager from a culture where feedback is layered with indirect signals or softened through relationship-building, this frankness can feel blunt. From the Swedish side, heavily indirect feedback may seem evasive or unclear, leaving the person unsure what actually needs fixing.

Praise tends to be understated. Because of Jantelagen, the cultural norm that discourages standing out or claiming superiority, people are often sparing with compliments. A comment like "this looks good" or "it works" can be stronger approval than it sounds. Feedback is seen as real only if it reflects genuine assessment, so over-the-top enthusiasm registers as insincere. If you expect regular affirmation, the quietness can feel like indifference, but steady work is noticed even when it's not loudly celebrated.

Coaching and development are viewed as ongoing rights, rooted in Sweden's long tradition of folkbildning (popular education) and formal workplace policies. Employees expect regular development talks, training plans, and structured performance dialogues. Coaching that appears only when something goes wrong feels punitive rather than supportive. Build a rhythm of check-ins that cover both tasks and growth, and frame feedback sessions as part of normal professional development, not crisis management.

Employees also expect to be heard. In a flat, unionized environment, questioning workloads, priorities, or work conditions is seen as legitimate, not insubordinate. Managers who respond with "because I said so" or dismiss concerns based on authority alone will meet resistance. Invite feedback in return, listen when people raise concerns, and be ready to explain decisions in terms that show you've considered their input.
