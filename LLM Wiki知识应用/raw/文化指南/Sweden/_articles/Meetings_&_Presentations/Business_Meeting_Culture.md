# Business Meeting Culture

## Meetings & Presentations

Swedish meeting culture rests on the principle that decisions should emerge from informed group discussion, not individual authority. This reflects a broader cultural commitment to shared responsibility and equality at work. Meetings are designed to surface multiple perspectives before a conclusion is reached, and the person convening the meeting will typically guide that process by drawing out contributions rather than steering toward their own preferred outcome. The result is often slower decision-making but smoother implementation, because people leave the room genuinely aligned.

Preparation is taken seriously. Participants are expected to have read all relevant documents in advance, and arriving unprepared is seen as disrespectful and undermines the whole process. Meetings are used for discussion and consensus-building, not for presenting information that could have been shared earlier. If you are joining a meeting in Sweden, send materials well ahead of time and assume they will be read. Do not spend meeting time walking through slides or repeating what has already been circulated.

Meetings are often structured so that everyone has a chance to speak. Silence does not always mean agreement; it can also signal that people are still thinking or not yet ready to commit. If you want input, invite it explicitly by asking for views around the table, rather than waiting for the loudest voice to dominate. A colleague from a culture where decisions are made quickly by senior leaders may find this roundtable style slow or indirect. From a Swedish perspective, rushing to closure without full input risks overlooking concerns that will surface later and slow things down anyway.

Fika, the coffee break before or after a formal meeting, often plays a practical role. This is where hesitations may be voiced more openly or where a decision gets quietly tested. Use these moments to listen and check understanding, not just to socialize.
