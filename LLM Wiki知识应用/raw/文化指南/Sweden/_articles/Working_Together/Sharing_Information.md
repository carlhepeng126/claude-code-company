# Sharing Information

## Working Together

Sweden's transparency reflex runs deep. The principle of public access, known as *offentlighetsprincipen* (the legal right to inspect public documents), shapes expectations far beyond the government sector. The underlying belief is that information should be shareable and traceable, not hoarded. In workplaces, this translates into a habit of making data, decisions, and assumptions visible. Colleagues will share documents proactively, not because they have been asked but because it feels responsible. When someone withholds context or keeps decisions private, it reads as either careless or calculating, neither of which sit well.

After a meeting, expect a written record. Decision notes, action lists, or *mötesprotokoll* (meeting minutes) are standard, especially after consensus-heavy discussions. These documents serve as a shared reference point so people can check what was actually agreed. For colleagues from cultures where decisions are confirmed verbally or live mainly in memory, the Swedish habit of documenting everything can feel slow or bureaucratic. From the Swedish side, a lack of written follow-up suggests the discussion was not serious or that commitments are flexible. Sending a clean, factual recap after a meeting will be read as professionalism, not micromanagement.

Plain language is expected. Sweden has an official *klarspråk* (plain language) movement, and the preference for lean, concrete wording shows up in emails, reports, and presentations. Jargon-heavy prose or marketing language lands badly. People want writing that sounds like educated Swedish conversation: clear, unadorned, and free of theatrics. Colleagues will respond more readily to straightforward explanations than to polished persuasion.

Digital tools form the backbone of information sharing. Email, Teams, Slack, and shared drives are used naturally and asynchronously. People expect to find what they need without a meeting or a direct request. If your work lives only in your inbox, it will seem fragile and unprofessional. Making information findable and version-controlled is how you demonstrate that you take your role seriously.
