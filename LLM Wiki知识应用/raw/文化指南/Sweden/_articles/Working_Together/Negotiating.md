# Negotiating

## Working Together

Swedes walk into negotiations with homework done. Expect benchmarks, cost breakdowns, sustainability data and comparative offers already mapped out. The tone will be calm, the questions direct, and the opening position reasonable. Large opening gambits or aggressive "take it or leave it" postures clash with a tradition of collective wage bargaining and labour law built around fairness. Swedish negotiators assume the other side has also prepared, and that both parties are working toward an outcome that makes sense on the merits, rather than through pressure or performance.

Drama has no place at the table. You will not see raised voices, table-thumping or emotional appeals. If frustration surfaces, argue your numbers and principles, but keep the delivery even. Swedes ask direct questions about price, payment terms and timelines without hesitation, and expect the same clarity in return. Talking up how "world-leading" your offer is, or suggesting the other side would be lucky to have you, invites scrutiny and runs straight into Jantelagen, the cultural expectation that you do not elevate yourself above others. Let your data do the persuading.

Environmental impact, labor standards and long-term social responsibility can weigh as heavily as price, especially in public procurement and with larger brands. Indigenous rights in supply chains, particularly those affecting Sami communities, may also come up. If you have thought through these angles, say so clearly. They can be real differentiators, especially when two offers are otherwise close.

Power to decide may rest with middle managers or even working-level staff, reflecting Sweden's distributed decision-making structure. If you need a firm answer, ask who will make the final call and what their timeline looks like. A clear, respectful "no" with explained constraints is preferred over hedging or drawn-out evasion.
