# Business Etiquette

## Making a Good Impression

Swedish business culture rests on a principle called *lagom* (a Swedish term meaning "just the right amount"), and it shapes how people present themselves and interact at work. Moderation guides behavior: you're expected to contribute without dominating, to speak clearly without overstating, to be engaged but not pushy. Over-selling an idea or interrupting to make your point registers as tone-deaf. Instead of leading with why you're the best in the world, present your evidence and let others draw their own conclusions. This restraint flows from *Jantelagen*, an informal cultural rule that discourages standing above the group. Overt self-praise invites quiet skepticism, while understatement paired with solid work earns credibility.

Hierarchy exists in Swedish workplaces, but it sits lightly. People call their bosses by first name and expect to be treated as equals in discussion. Being overly deferential can seem unnecessary, while domineering behavior stands out immediately. If you're used to adapting your tone sharply depending on rank, that shift won't land well here. A colleague from a culture where visible deference signals professionalism might find the lack of formality confusing, while a Swede encountering elaborate deference may wonder whether the relationship can be genuine. The practical implication is to treat everyone in the room as worthy of being listened to, not just the most senior person present.

Directness is valued, but it comes with softness. Swedes say what they mean, gently and without excessive flattery. If something doesn't work, you'll usually hear so, framed calmly and with reasons. A phrase like "I see three risks here" works far better than "This is completely wrong." The goal is honesty that preserves respect. If you need to disagree, do it with evidence and show you've considered their perspective. That combination of clarity and care reflects the culture's emphasis on both equality and collaboration.
