# Forms of Address

## Making a Good Impression

Sweden is strongly first-name based. Managers, professors, and senior officials are typically addressed by their first name from the start, and there's little attachment to formal titles or honorifics. Using "Mr Andersson" or "Ms Lindqvist" can sound stiff or even ironic in many professional settings. The cultural logic behind this lies in Sweden's egalitarian workplace culture, where hierarchy is downplayed in favor of functional roles. The move to first names reflects a broader distrust of status displays and a preference for flat, accessible interaction. 

In practice, begin with first name and surname in your opening email, then shift to first name only as soon as your Swedish counterpart signs that way. The transition usually happens quickly. If you continue using surnames or titles after this point, it can create unnecessary distance or signal that you're unfamiliar with local norms.

What matters in Sweden is role clarity, not formal address. People refer to each other by function: project lead, safety delegate, union representative, HR partner. These labels help clarify who has responsibility or input in a decision, which is central to how Swedish workplaces operate. Understanding someone's role tells you more about how to collaborate with them than their seniority ever would.

For colleagues from cultures where titles signal respect and where seniority shapes interaction, the casual tone can feel unexpectedly informal or even dismissive. From a Swedish perspective, formality tied to titles can feel like an unnecessary barrier or an attempt to assert hierarchy. The first-name habit coexists comfortably with high expectations and serious accountability, so the informal address shouldn't be mistaken for a relaxed approach to work itself.
