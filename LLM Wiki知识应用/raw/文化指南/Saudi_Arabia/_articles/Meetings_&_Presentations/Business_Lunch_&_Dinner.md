# Business Lunch & Dinner

## Meetings & Presentations

Business meals in Saudi Arabia centre on making guests feel received. The host's role is to offer generously and ensure everyone at the table feels comfortable. This reflects a broader cultural understanding that shared meals build cohesion, and that the quality of reception reflects directly on personal and professional standing. If you're invited, expect your counterpart to lead the ordering, suggest dishes, and actively encourage you to eat more. Accepting these offerings, even if you pace yourself, shows you value the relationship being built.

Timing shifts noticeably during Ramadan, when the rhythm of the day reorients around sunset. Business dinners often take place after *iftar* (the meal that breaks the fast), meaning they start later and extend well into the evening. Energy and availability change accordingly. Scheduling a lunch meeting during Ramadan may not be practical, and proposing an early dinner will likely miss the mark. Working within this rhythm, rather than around it, signals cultural awareness.

Regional variation shows up in what's served and how meals unfold. In some settings, you may encounter traditional dishes like *kabsa* (spiced rice with meat) or *mandi* (slow-cooked lamb or chicken), often eaten communally from a shared platter. In others, especially in larger cities or with internationally oriented counterparts, you're more likely to be taken to Lebanese or international restaurants. The choice of venue often reflects the host's sense of what will make the guest most comfortable, so follow their lead rather than suggesting alternatives unless asked.

Meals are rarely just about food. Conversations stay broad and relationally focused. Bringing up contentious topics or pressing for decisions during dinner can feel jarring. The aim is to deepen trust, not to resolve tension or advance negotiation. Let the conversation move at its own pace, and leave space for your counterpart to guide it.
