# Feedback and Coaching

## Managing Relationships

In Saudi Arabia, feedback is given with close attention to honour and social standing. Criticism in front of others can be read as an attack on character, disrupting the social harmony that workplace relationships depend on. The traditional majlis setting, where elders guide privately to protect dignity, shapes how correction is delivered. Feedback is seen as an act of care, not exposure, and the goal is to support improvement while keeping reputation intact.

The practical implication is clear: deliver feedback privately, in a calm and measured tone. Frame it as stewardship or shared responsibility rather than personal fault. Use specific examples rather than broad character judgments, and allow space for the person to respond without feeling cornered. A manager might say, "I've noticed the reports have been coming in late this month. Let's talk about what's happening and how I can help," rather than stating, "You're not meeting deadlines."

Vision 2030 has introduced more direct communication styles, particularly in multinational firms and among younger Saudi professionals exposed to international management practices. This means feedback preferences are now mixed. Some colleagues will expect and respond well to clear, direct input. Others, especially those in more traditional sectors or older generations, will still prefer indirect approaches that preserve face. The safest approach is to read the individual and adapt, asking how they prefer to receive feedback if the relationship allows it.
