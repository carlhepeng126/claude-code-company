# Time Management

## Making a Good Impression

In Saudi Arabia, time follows religious and social rhythms before it follows a calendar grid. The workweek runs Friday and Saturday, established by royal order in 2013, and Friday holds particular centrality as the day of congregational prayer. Meetings are often scheduled around prayer times, not the other way around, and the daily rhythm pauses five times for prayer. For colleagues accustomed to uninterrupted workdays, this can create the sense that time is fragmented. From a Saudi perspective, a schedule that ignores prayer is simply not viable.

Ramadan reshapes the daily clock entirely. Working hours shift and contract, activity moves later into the evening, and the daytime pace slows as people fast from dawn to sunset. A colleague from a culture where schedules are fixed year-round might read this as disruption. A Saudi colleague might see a rigid insistence on normal hours during Ramadan as tone-deaf. If you are planning launches, audits, or high-intensity work, avoid Ramadan or plan for reduced bandwidth. 

The Hajj and Umrah seasons create their own tempo, particularly in the western region around Jeddah, Makkah, and Madinah. Availability tightens, logistics become more complex, and public life adjusts to the influx of millions of pilgrims. If your project involves stakeholders or travel to these cities, confirm availability well ahead and expect that some weeks are simply not workable.

Vision 2030 has introduced a faster-moving public calendar of events, entertainment, and industry activity, but many families still organize life around religious observance and family commitments. The result is two time cultures operating side by side. One person may work to a packed professional schedule while another prioritizes ritual and kinship time. Don't assume uniformity, and don't interpret flexibility as lack of seriousness.
