# Forms of Address

## Making a Good Impression

Names in Saudi Arabia reflect lineage and paternal ancestry, and women traditionally retain their own family name after marriage. Getting a colleague's full name right, and pronouncing it carefully, shows that you recognize not just the individual but their family background. When names are misspelled or shortened without permission, it can feel like a dismissal of identity rather than a simple administrative error.

Formality is expected at the outset. Use titles such as "Your Excellency" for ministers and "Your Highness" for members of the royal family. Professional titles like "Doctor," "Mohandas" (engineer), or "Ustadh" (professor) are used with the person's first name, so Dr. Ahmed bin Abdullah is addressed as Dr. Ahmed. The word "bin" means "son of" and appears in genealogical chains; avoid shortening names that include it. "Sheikh" is an honorary title and also pairs with the first name only. Wait to be invited before moving to less formal address.

In practice, this means checking the spelling and structure of names before a meeting, greeting the most senior person first when entering a room, and listening for how colleagues introduce themselves. In group settings, tone and sequence communicate respect. A handshake is standard among men, though it may be light and prolonged rather than firm. If meeting a Saudi woman in a professional context, do not initiate physical contact; if she extends her hand, keep the handshake brief.

Family-based forms of address such as "father of" or "mother of" a child's name may emerge once relationships deepen. These are invitations, not defaults. Mirror them when offered, but do not improvise them early on.
