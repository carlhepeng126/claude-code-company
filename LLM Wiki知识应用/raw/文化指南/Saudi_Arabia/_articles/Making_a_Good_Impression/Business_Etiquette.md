# Business Etiquette

## Making a Good Impression

Business meetings in Saudi Arabia begin with hospitality because the initial exchange of coffee, dates, and conversation is where trust takes root. This is the choreography of honouring the guest, and it functions as a relationship ritual that both signals and creates safety for more sensitive exchanges later. Serving Arabic coffee in a particular order (elders and guests first, always with the right hand) anchors people in a shared etiquette that has remained stable even as public life has changed quickly. A colleague from a culture where meetings move straight to business might read this opening as evasive or inefficient. From a Saudi perspective, someone who skips the social opening or treats it as filler may seem transactional or dismissive. Give the ritual its time. Accept what is offered, mirror the host's pace, and let the tone settle before turning to outcomes.

Communication in Saudi business settings relies heavily on what is not said aloud. A pause, a shift in tone, or a gentle deflection often holds more meaning than the words themselves. Body language, especially between men, tends to be warm and tactile. A colleague might touch your arm to emphasize a point or stand closer than North Europeans or East Asians typically would. Drawing back can come across as a rejection of connection. Eye contact, posture, and even small gestures like raising an eyebrow contribute to the message being exchanged. Watch for these cues and respond in kind.

Handle potentially awkward topics with care. Many Saudis expect delicate issues to be raised in ways that preserve dignity, often in private or through an intermediary rather than in a group. Open disagreement or critique, especially in front of others, can damage the relationship. If something needs addressing, frame it discreetly and give the other person room to respond without losing face. This is less about indirectness and more about protecting the relational foundation on which the work depends.
