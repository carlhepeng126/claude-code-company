# Business Lunch & Dinner

## Meetings & Presentations

Business meals in Guinea-Bissau are shaped by the country's legacy as a Portuguese colony and its deep Islamic and West African roots. The pace is slower than in many other settings, and the meal itself often becomes a space where trust is built through shared time rather than structured agenda points. If you arrive expecting to move quickly through courses and back to business, your hosts may read this as impatience or disinterest in the relationship itself.

Meals often feature rice-based dishes, grilled fish from the coast, and chicken prepared with palm oil, onions, and peppers. *Caldo* (a rich, slow-cooked stew) and *jollof rice* are common. Portions are typically generous, and food may be served communally. Sharing from a central plate is normal in some settings, especially outside the capital, and reflects a broader cultural emphasis on collective participation. Refusing food or eating very little can be taken as a sign of disrespect or discomfort.

Alcohol is rarely present at business meals, particularly when dining with Muslim colleagues or in local restaurants. International hotels may offer wine or beer, but it's safer to follow your host's lead. Strong coffee or sweet tea usually closes the meal, and this final stage is not rushed. Lingering over drinks allows space for relationship deepening that formal discussion may not provide.

Dietary restrictions should be mentioned ahead of time and framed respectfully. Vegetarian options exist but may not be widely understood as a category, so offering a brief explanation helps.
