# Making Contacts & Scheduling Appointments

## Making a Good Impression

Building professional relationships in Guinea-Bissau begins with personal connection, rooted in a culture where trust develops through face-to-face interaction rather than formal credentials or cold correspondence. The country's history of political instability and its small, interconnected professional networks mean that people rely on established relationships to navigate uncertainty. A warm introduction from a mutual contact opens doors far more effectively than an unsolicited email or phone call. If you're trying to arrange a meeting, work through someone who already knows both parties. That shared link provides reassurance and speeds up what would otherwise be a slow process of building confidence from scratch.

Once an introduction is made, expect the first meeting to focus on getting to know one another rather than advancing business goals. Conversations may touch on family, shared acquaintances, or your experience in the country. This isn't a formality to move past quickly. It's how people assess whether you're someone they want to work with. Rushing toward outcomes before this foundation is in place can come across as transactional and may stall the relationship before it starts.

Scheduling works best when kept informal and flexible. Appointments may be arranged days or even hours in advance, and confirmations closer to the time are common. Meetings can begin later than planned or be interrupted by other visitors, especially if those visitors hold social or professional standing. A colleague from a culture where schedules are fixed and time is allocated precisely may find this difficult to read, interpreting it as disorganization. From a Guinea-Bissau perspective, holding rigidly to a timetable when a respected elder or official arrives unannounced would signal a lack of respect for relationships and hierarchy. Allow extra time between commitments and treat shifts in timing as part of how business gets done.
