# Business Meeting Culture

## Meetings & Presentations

The formal meeting in Croatia often tells only part of the story. Coffee before or after the scheduled session is where colleagues drop their guard and share what they really think. The official agenda may be covered around the table, but the real alignment happens at the kafić around the corner. If you treat the meeting room as the only arena, you will miss where decisions actually get tested and refined. Plan time for those unofficial conversations and use them to ask what people truly believe will work.

Regional style varies in ways that affect how the same topic gets discussed. In Zagreb, meetings tend to be more structured, time-boxed and document-heavy. In Dalmatia, the same agenda may unfold with more storytelling, digressions and humor, especially in the afternoon when *fjaka* (a drowsy midday slowness) sets in. Inland and Slavonian meetings often start more formally but warm up once rapport is built. A colleague accustomed to tight time management may read the coastal pace as unfocused, while a Dalmatian counterpart may find the Zagreb style overly rigid. Adjusting your expectations by region means allowing more flexibility on the coast and tighter scheduling in the capital.

Croats can be quite direct about problems. You may hear "ovo ne valja" (this doesn't work) or "ovako neće ići" (it won't go this way) stated plainly, without softening. That directness reflects a preference for addressing issues openly rather than letting them linger. But it should stay controlled. Theatrical outbursts or raised voices are seen as unprofessional, even when disagreement is sharp. Saying "we disagree" or "this won't work" calmly and clearly earns more respect than polite vagueness or aggression. If you come from a culture where critique is wrapped in positive framing, the Croatian style may feel blunt, but colleagues here may in turn read overly cushioned feedback as evasive or unclear.

War memory and political alignments sometimes surface unexpectedly. References to the Homeland War, Vukovar, Operation Storm or World War II can freeze a conversation, especially if someone feels their sacrifice or narrative is being dismissed. These moments are not invitations to debate history in the meeting. Listen respectfully, acknowledge what has been said without trying to resolve it on the spot, and steer the discussion back to practical collaboration.
