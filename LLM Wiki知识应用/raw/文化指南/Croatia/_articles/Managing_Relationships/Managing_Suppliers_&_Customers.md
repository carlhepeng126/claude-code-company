# Managing Suppliers & Customers

## Managing Relationships

Many Croatian suppliers and customers operate with economic realities that shape their negotiating position in ways that aren't always visible. A coastal supplier may earn their entire annual income during three summer months; a small producer in Slavonia might depend on one large buyer plus EU subsidies to survive the year. These are not inefficiencies to smooth over. They reflect the survival calculations behind business decisions, and foreign partners who design contracts without accounting for seasonal cash flow or harvest cycles will find their terms feel disconnected from how the business actually works.

Trust develops over repeated cycles, especially through crises. Croats tend to value long-term supplier relationships with people who have stayed through difficult periods over lower-cost alternatives that appear without a track record. If a supplier fails to deliver during peak season, word travels quickly through local networks, and the damage to reputation is hard to repair. Showing up consistently, honouring warranties, and fixing mistakes visibly is how a foreign partner moves from transactional status to "our" supplier. In-person visits reinforce this continuity far more effectively than email alone.

Croatian suppliers and customers are alert to whether foreign partners treat the market seriously or as a short-term experiment. The distinction between being seen as a committed partner versus a cost-driven buyer looking for a "cheap Balkan back office" affects not just pricing but willingness to stretch during tight windows. Being explicit about long-term intent and backing that with predictable behaviour and fair pricing builds loyalty that carries through when flexibility becomes necessary. Setting deadlines with buffer room and having a local representative who can monitor progress in real time helps ensure commitments are met without the blame-shifting that can arise when things slip.
