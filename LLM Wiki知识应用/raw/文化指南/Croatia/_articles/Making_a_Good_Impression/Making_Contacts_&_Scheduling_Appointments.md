# Making Contacts & Scheduling Appointments

## Making a Good Impression

Scheduling a meeting in Croatia means planning ahead, but the real work begins over coffee. "Ajmo na kavu" ("let's go for a coffee") is the default opening, and what might look like a social courtesy is where the early relationship-building and informal due diligence actually happen. One coffee can stretch to an hour or more, and the tone, credibility and trustworthiness of a potential partner are tested long before any formal agenda is opened. For colleagues from cultures where first meetings move quickly to business substance, this can feel indirect or slow. From a Croatian perspective, a foreign contact who skips the coffee and leads with a slide deck may come across as transactional or impatient, someone who hasn't grasped that the relationship underpins everything that follows.

Regional differences in approach are noticeable. A contact from Dalmatia may be more relaxed and conversational upfront, taking time to feel you out. In Slavonia, warmth tends to come alongside a certain guardedness, especially around money and commitment. In Zagreb, you're more likely to encounter formality, credentials mentioned early, and a more structured tone. Asking local colleagues how coastal, inland and capital-city contacts prefer to be approached will help you mirror their pacing and avoid missteps.

In smaller cities and war-affected regions, influence often sits with figures who don't appear on an org chart. Homeland War veterans, local politicians, church figures or football-club presidents can act as quiet gatekeepers. A recommendation from one of these networks can open doors that a polished cold email will not. Many Croatian businesses also have relatives or partners abroad, especially in Germany, Austria or Ireland, and are used to foreign interest. But they're equally familiar with promises that don't translate into concrete benefit at home. Signaling early that you're committed to an ongoing partnership, not just closing a deal and disappearing, will shape how seriously your approach is received.
