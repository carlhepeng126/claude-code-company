# Forms of Address

## Making a Good Impression

Croatian professional settings operate with a formality that shifts with context, generation, and setting. The default is respectful distance: gospodin (Mr) or gospođa (Mrs) followed by the surname. This holds especially true in public institutions, with older colleagues, and in rural or more conservative areas, where moving too quickly to first names can come across as poorly raised or overly American. Younger professionals in Zagreb and in international firms may shorten the distance faster, but the initial tone still leans formal.

Academic and professional titles such as doktor, inženjer, or profesor are used in Croatia, particularly in universities, state organizations, and among older generations. Acknowledging them when they appear shows respect for earned status. At the same time, overplaying titles where they are not expected reads as self-important. Watch who gets addressed with a title and follow the pattern rather than bringing in your own assumptions about hierarchy.

The shift from formal to informal is signaled, not assumed. Let Croatian colleagues introduce first names or suggest dropping the formality. In mixed-age settings, pitch your tone to the oldest or most senior person present and relax only when they do. A colleague from a culture where informality builds rapport quickly may feel the formality slows connection, while Croatian professionals may read an early switch to first names as a lack of tact or awareness of social nuance.

In practice, this means starting with gospodin or gospođa and surname, using titles where they are offered or visibly expected, and waiting for the invitation to shift. That pause gives space for trust to form on Croatian terms.
