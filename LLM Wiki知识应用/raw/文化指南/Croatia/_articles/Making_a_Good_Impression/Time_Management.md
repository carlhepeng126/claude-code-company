# Time Management

## Making a Good Impression

How time is handled in Croatia varies by region and context. In Zagreb and many inland firms, people generally aim to arrive on time for meetings and expect others to do the same. On the Dalmatian coast, the culture of *fjaka* (a relaxed state of being, especially in warm weather) makes timelines more elastic, particularly during summer afternoons. This reflects a long-standing coastal rhythm where heat and pace of life slow the clock. If you schedule a meeting with coastal partners in midday July, expect delays or distraction. Early mornings or shoulder seasons work better for non-urgent discussions.

Tourist season intensifies this divide. Between July and August, hospitality professionals along the coast work long, irregular hours and rarely have time for meetings that are not directly tied to the season's demands. Off-season, they have more availability but tighter cash flow, which can shift priorities. If your project involves coastal stakeholders, plan major milestones around this cycle rather than against it.

Bureaucracy adds another layer. Public offices, permit systems, and pension administration can be slow, and most Croatians are used to waiting or relying on personal connections to speed things up. Veterans and elderly populations navigate these systems visibly, and queues are expected. If your work depends on state paperwork or permits, ask locals how long processes realistically take, not just what official timelines say. Building in lead time is standard practice.

Deadlines are often treated as flexible, with a last-minute push to meet what is seen as the real cutoff. Setting artificial deadlines or building in buffer time can help, especially if the work involves teams outside Zagreb or EU-facing sectors where punctuality expectations are stricter.
