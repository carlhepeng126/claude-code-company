# Sharing Information

## Working Together

Many Croats instinctively assume that what is written down and what is really happening are two different things. Formal emails and policy documents serve their purpose, but they are rarely treated as the full picture. People expect a gap between the official version and lived reality, and they look for that gap through corridor conversations, coffee breaks, and WhatsApp exchanges. This expectation comes from a long history of navigating systems where official messages often served political or bureaucratic purposes rather than transparency. The practical result is that sending a clear memo will not, on its own, mean everyone has understood or accepted the message. Building in time for follow-up conversations, where colleagues can ask questions informally and test how a new process will actually work, helps close that gap.

Complaints that surface repeatedly in informal settings, known as *kukanje* (grumbling), are not just background noise. When the same concern appears across different coffee chats or group messages, it usually points to something unclear, impractical, or perceived as unfair in the official message. A colleague from a culture where information is shared once and assumed to be understood may read this ongoing commentary as resistance or negativity. From a Croatian perspective, a colleague who expects full clarity from a single announcement and then moves on may seem either overly trusting of formal channels or uninterested in how the change will actually play out.

Generational differences shape how information is received and processed. Younger team members often expect updates via Teams, Slack, or WhatsApp and are comfortable piecing together information from multiple digital threads. Older colleagues tend to prefer face-to-face briefings and written summaries they can review in their own time. Using a mix of channels, rather than relying on one, increases the chance that key information reaches everyone and gets translated into everyday practice.
