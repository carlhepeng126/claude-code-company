# Decision-Making

## Working Together

Decisions in Jordan gain authority when they move through the right people. Even if analysis happens collaboratively, the final step often requires endorsement from a senior figure or a network that lends legitimacy. This reflects a society where hierarchy is respected and where approval from a recognized authority makes a decision feel grounded and credible. If you move too fast or expect closure in the room, you may find yourself out of step. What looks like delay to a colleague from a culture where decisions are made quickly is often strategic consultation. From a Jordanian perspective, locking in a decision without the right sponsorship or relational alignment can feel reckless, not efficient.

Consultation extends beyond the office. In a society where kinship and reputation structures run deep, many people seek input from trusted circles before committing. This is social due diligence, not indecision. Consequences travel relationally, and a choice that affects one person may ripple through family or professional networks. A colleague from Jordan working with someone who expects individual accountability may see that approach as isolating or incomplete. In return, someone used to fast individual decisions may misread consultation as avoidance, when it is actually a way of ensuring alignment and reducing relational risk.

Cooling time is culturally normal, especially in sensitive situations. Pausing protects dignity and allows decisions to settle after private discussion rather than being forced into the open. This echoes customary practices like *ʿaṭwa* (a traditional cooling-off period in dispute resolution), where time creates space for reflection and face-saving. If you push for an immediate answer, you risk locking someone into a position they cannot walk back from gracefully. Build room for follow-up after the meeting and confirm commitment gently rather than assuming a nod in the room means the decision is final.
