# Sharing Information

## Working Together

In Jordan, openness is relational. People share more freely when they know who introduced you, what your reputation suggests, and whether you've shown you can be trusted with discretion. In a networked society where connections reach across sectors and families, information can have social consequences. A colleague may hold back initially because something said in the wrong setting, or to the wrong person, could cause embarrassment or damage a relationship. This caution grows with trust, not despite it.

Public settings raise the stakes. Sharing a problem or delay in a group meeting can expose someone to blame or threaten dignity, so sensitive updates often shift to private channels or are softened in tone. A manager from a culture where problems are flagged openly in team meetings may read this as evasiveness. A Jordanian colleague, in turn, may see blunt public reporting as careless or disrespectful. Asking for difficult information one-to-one, and framing it without blame, makes clarity more likely.

Intermediaries help messages move safely. Just as wasta (connections and influence) can open doors, a trusted third party can communicate updates or concerns that might provoke defensiveness if delivered directly. If a message is not landing, revisiting it through a respected mutual contact often achieves more than repeating it louder.

Reciprocal exchanges shape the flow of information. People share what they know with those who have helped them or whom they expect to work with again. Building that exchange means showing reliability over time and respecting what is told to you in confidence.
