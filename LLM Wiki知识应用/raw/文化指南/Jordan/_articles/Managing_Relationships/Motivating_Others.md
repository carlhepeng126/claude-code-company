# Motivating Others

## Managing Relationships

Money matters in Jordan, especially when the cost of living is high and salaries often trail behind. Job security runs a close second. But what sparks real loyalty and sustained effort is the feeling that you belong and that your dignity will be protected. In a socially networked environment where reputation travels through family, friends, and colleagues, being looked after by your manager, not publicly shamed or left alone when things go wrong, can generate powerful commitment.

Recognition works best when it strengthens someone's standing rather than putting them in an awkward spotlight. People are motivated by being seen as respectable, capable, and reliable in the eyes of peers and elders. Specific praise that acknowledges contribution without theatricality, or entrusting someone with credible responsibility, raises their standing in ways that feel meaningful. A manager who remembers someone's birthday or asks after their family signals that the person is valued beyond their output, and that attention is often remembered longer than a formal bonus.

Fairness is watched closely, and perceptions of favoritism can spread fast through interconnected communities. When people believe access, opportunities, or praise are distributed by closeness rather than merit, motivation drops sharply. Decisions that affect status or opportunity need to be explained, not assumed to be understood. A manager from a context where performance speaks for itself might not realize how much explanation is expected here, while a colleague in Jordan might read silence around a promotion decision as deliberate concealment or unfair preference.

Multinationals in Amman often bring more structured incentive schemes and clearer performance frameworks, but even there, the sense of being protected and treated fairly carries more motivational energy than any formal system alone.
