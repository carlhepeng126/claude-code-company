# Managing Suppliers & Customers

## Managing Relationships

In Jordan, reliability is judged as much by how you handle difficulty as by what you deliver. When something goes wrong, people remember whether you stayed calm, spoke respectfully, and allowed the other side room to fix the issue without losing face. Public blame or a sharp escalation can shut down cooperation quickly, because it creates a social injury that outlasts the original problem. Quiet, direct repair, by contrast, often restores performance faster than formal complaints, because it lets the supplier or customer act without defending their standing.

Access and responsiveness can shift depending on who introduces you and whether you are speaking to the right person in the hierarchy. This reflects a culture where credibility is relational, not automatic. Using the right introduction, or going through the appropriate channel, reduces friction and speeds up responses in ways that repeated emails or direct cold calls rarely will. Calling or meeting in person tends to work better than written communication when you need movement or clarity.

The relationship you build is with the individual, not the institution. If that person leaves, the trust and access often need to be rebuilt from the beginning. This means that showing up regularly, accepting invitations, and investing time in the connection are part of how business continuity is maintained. Treat respect and tone as part of the operating system, not just the social layer. How you repair a problem will shape whether the next one gets solved.
