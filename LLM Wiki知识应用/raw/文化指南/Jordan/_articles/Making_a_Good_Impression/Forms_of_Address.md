# Forms of Address

## Making a Good Impression

In Jordan, how you address someone signals whether you recognise their dignity, age, and social standing. Names come with context, and people take note when forms of address acknowledge or ignore those markers. Starting formal and letting the other person guide the shift toward warmth is the safer approach, as moving too quickly to first names can be read as a lack of respect.

Professional titles such as Doctor, Engineer, or Professor are used routinely and hold real weight. They reflect education and achievement, and using them acknowledges what someone has earned. The title Sheikh addresses senior men, family heads, or figures of communal influence. For older people or those who have completed the pilgrimage to Mecca, Hajj (for men) or Hajjeh (for women) is a respectful honorific that signals moral and religious standing more than job rank. If you know someone's first name, you can pair the title with it.

Another culturally meaningful form is the kunya, addressing someone as Abu (father of) or Um (mother of) followed by the name of their eldest child. This offers warmth and respect without the over-familiarity of first names alone. It is especially common once rapport has been established. In more informal or long-standing relationships, a colleague from a culture that defaults to first names quickly might find this indirectness slow to navigate, while a Jordanian colleague may read an immediate switch to first names as premature or tone-deaf to the social placement being negotiated.

Handshakes are customary among men, typically starting with the most senior person in the room. Women should wait to see if a Jordanian woman extends her hand first. Between women who know each other, a cheek kiss is usual.
