# Making Contacts & Scheduling Appointments

## Making a Good Impression

In Jordan, making contact through someone already known is not just polite networking; it's a form of credibility transfer rooted in a kinship society. Wasta, often described as a family and tribal connection practice, helps a new person become legible and trustworthy. The introduction signals that you come with backing, which shifts how you're received. A warm email from a mutual colleague or a phone call setting up an informal coffee can open doors that a cold approach rarely will. This isn't about cutting corners; it's how relationships are built when social capital sits in personal networks rather than institutional credentials alone.

Jordan's majlis tradition, where people gather in sitting spaces to exchange news, resolve disputes, and host guests, still shapes how many understand professional relationships. These spaces encode the idea that meaningful exchanges happen through presence, not only email threads. When scheduling a meeting, expect the opening to include coffee and settling-in time. This isn't filler; accepting coffee signals that the relationship channel is open. Treating this ritual as dead time or trying to fast-forward to business can read as transactional, especially outside international or NGO circles where the pace may be quicker.

Amman holds many social worlds at once: government ministries, family businesses, international organizations, neighborhood scenes. The same person may move between them in a single week, and how contacts are made can shift depending on which setting you're in. Younger professionals in global firms may connect quickly online and move to substance faster, while older or more traditional contacts may prefer a slower build with intermediaries. Reconfirming appointments a day ahead is standard practice, and it's wise to leave buffer time. Meetings may start late, expand to include others, or shift direction mid-conversation as priorities realign.
