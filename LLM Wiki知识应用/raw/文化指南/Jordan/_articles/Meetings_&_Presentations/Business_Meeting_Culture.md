# Business Meeting Culture

## Meetings & Presentations

Meetings in Jordan operate in the shadow of the *majlis*, the traditional sitting space where communities have long received guests, resolved disputes, and built legitimacy through presence. The logic still holds: a business meeting is not just a container for an agenda but a place where relationships are established and dignity is managed. This means the opening exchange of courtesies and the careful observation of who sits where and who is served coffee first are not delays to the real work. They are the real work. Coffee itself follows rule-bound etiquette rooted in reconciliation and hospitality practices: served with the right hand, offered to elders and guests first, and reciprocated as an act of goodwill. If you finish and hand the cup back with a gentle shake, it signals you have had enough. No shake, and your host will assume you want more. These cues quietly structure status and flow, and they often tell you where a decision can safely land.

When a topic becomes tense, expect de-escalation to be part of the process rather than a sign that progress has stalled. Jordanian dispute-settlement traditions recognize mechanisms like *aṭwa* (a cooling-off truce) and *ṣulḥ* (reconciliation) as culturally normal, not last resorts. That means intermediaries, private follow-ups, and time to restore face are valued tools, not complications. A colleague from a culture where confrontation is expected to be direct and resolved in the room may read this as avoidance. From the Jordanian side, forcing a public confrontation risks breaking the relationship entirely when careful mediation could have preserved it.

The meeting door may remain literally open, with people arriving or leaving, phone calls interrupting, or conversations branching into side exchanges. This reflects the organic, relational nature of Jordanian professional life. Pushing for immediate closure or dominating the floor to keep things on track undermines the equality and respect that underpin a credible partnership. Build cooling time into your calendar, avoid back-to-back scheduling, and recognize that the lunch or dinner afterward is often where the real relationship settles.
