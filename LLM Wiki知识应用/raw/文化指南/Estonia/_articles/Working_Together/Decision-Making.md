# Decision-Making

## Working Together

Decisions in Estonia move quickly once the right people are in the room. The expectation is that a meeting should produce a clear outcome, and time spent revisiting decisions already made is seen as inefficient. Leaders are expected to arrive prepared, present their case clearly, and stand by their reasoning. Once consensus emerges or the responsible person makes the call, the group moves forward. Reopening the discussion without new information can raise questions about whether the decision was properly thought through in the first place.

Hierarchy exists but does not dominate the process. Senior leaders set direction and hold final accountability, but input is expected from those with relevant expertise or responsibility for implementation. A manager who makes decisions without consulting the people who will execute them risks both practical missteps and quiet disengagement. Estonians value competence and direct communication, so if you disagree, you are expected to say so during the discussion, not afterward. Silence in the room is often read as agreement.

Colleagues from cultures where decisions are revisited as new concerns arise may find this pace inflexible or the finality too abrupt. From an Estonian perspective, repeated discussion of settled issues can look like indecision or a lack of preparation. If conditions change, the decision can be revisited, but the change needs to be named and justified. Otherwise, the assumption is that everyone committed and the work continues.
