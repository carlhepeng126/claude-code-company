# Teamwork

## Working Together

Estonian teams tend to operate with little hierarchy and a strong expectation of individual contribution. Each person is expected to be competent in their own area, and collaboration usually means working independently on separate parts of a shared goal rather than constant coordination. This reflects a cultural preference for self-reliance and a distrust of inefficiency. Team meetings are often short and task-focused, used to align on what needs to be done rather than to build consensus or strengthen relationships. If someone needs to discuss an idea, they prepare it beforehand and present it clearly. 

This can feel ungenerous to a colleague from a culture where teamwork involves frequent exchange, brainstorming together, or thinking aloud as a group. From an Estonian perspective, that style may seem inefficient or unfocused. Equally, an Estonian colleague working in a more collaborative setting may come across as distant or unwilling to engage when in fact they are simply waiting for a clear task or decision point.

Seniority exists but does not dominate team interaction. Junior colleagues are expected to speak up if they have relevant expertise, and silence is usually interpreted as agreement or lack of input rather than deference. If you join an Estonian team, prepare your contributions in advance, keep updates concise, and expect others to do the same. Trust is built through reliability and competence, not through socializing or team-building exercises.
