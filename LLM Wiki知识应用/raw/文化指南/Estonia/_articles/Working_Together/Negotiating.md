# Negotiating

## Working Together

Negotiations in Estonia move quickly once both sides are at the table. Estonians prepare thoroughly in advance and arrive expecting the other party to have done the same. The focus is on facts, timelines, and getting to a workable agreement without extended back-and-forth. Long preambles or relationship-building conversations before discussing terms can feel inefficient. The assumption is that if you are meeting, the groundwork has already been done.

Silence during negotiation is common and functional. It gives people time to think through what has been said before responding. An Estonian counterpart may pause for several seconds after you present a point, not because they are uncomfortable or uncertain, but because they are considering the details. Filling that silence with further explanation or softening your position can be read as a lack of confidence in what you have just proposed.

Trust is earned through consistency and follow-through, not through personal warmth or extended dialogue. Estonians expect commitments made during negotiation to be honored exactly as stated. If you agree to a deadline or deliverable, it will be taken as binding. Vague assurances or over-promising to keep the mood positive can damage credibility more than a direct acknowledgment of what you cannot deliver. A colleague from a culture where flexibility in timelines is common may find this rigidity frustrating, while an Estonian counterpart may view requests to revisit agreed terms as unreliable unless backed by clear reasoning.

Hierarchies are relatively flat, and the person negotiating often has the authority to decide. Needing to consult upward repeatedly can slow momentum and raise questions about whether the right person is in the room.
