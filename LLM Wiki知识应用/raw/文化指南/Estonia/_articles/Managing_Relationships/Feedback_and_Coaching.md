# Feedback and Coaching

## Managing Relationships

Estonian professionals expect feedback to be direct, factual, and grounded in observable performance. Long preambles or personal framing can feel evasive. If something needs to change, say so clearly and explain why it matters to the work. This directness reflects a broader cultural preference for efficiency and honesty in professional exchange. Estonians tend to view feedback as a practical tool for improvement, separate from personal regard, and expect colleagues to receive it in that spirit.

That separation can create friction in cross-cultural teams. A colleague from a culture where feedback is softened through relationship-building or indirect language may read Estonian directness as harsh or dismissive. From the Estonian side, elaborate cushioning or circling around the point can come across as unclear or even disrespectful of someone's time. The key difference lies in how criticism is framed: Estonians assume goodwill is implicit, while others may need it stated.

Positive feedback is given, but sparingly. Praise tends to be specific and tied to tangible outcomes rather than general encouragement. Over-praising can reduce the value of recognition or feel insincere. Coaching conversations often focus on solving problems together rather than delivering judgment. Managers may ask what support is needed or suggest adjustments without extensive justification. The tone remains low-key, and follow-through is expected without repeated reminders.

Feedback works best when it is timely, concrete, and actionable. Avoid saving observations for formal reviews if the issue affects current work. Estonians appreciate when performance discussions stay focused on results and next steps, not personality or effort alone.
