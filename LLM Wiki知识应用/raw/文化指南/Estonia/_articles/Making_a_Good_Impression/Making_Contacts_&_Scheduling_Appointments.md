# Making Contacts & Scheduling Appointments

## Making a Good Impression

In Estonia, initial business contact is typically made through formal channels such as email or professional networking platforms, rather than unannounced phone calls or walk-ins. Estonians expect clarity and efficiency in these exchanges. When reaching out, state your purpose directly and provide enough context for the recipient to assess whether and how they can help. Vague introductions or overly broad requests may go unanswered, not out of rudeness but because busy professionals expect you to have done your homework. If you are seeking a meeting, propose two or three specific times rather than asking when someone is available.

Scheduling tends to move quickly once interest is established. Estonians value punctuality and clear commitments, so confirm details in writing and arrive on time. Late changes to agreed appointments are generally viewed as disrespectful unless genuinely unavoidable, in which case notify the other person as soon as possible with a brief explanation. A colleague accustomed to more fluid scheduling norms may find this level of precision rigid, while an Estonian counterpart may interpret frequent rescheduling as a lack of seriousness or organizational skill.

Personal introductions through a mutual contact can smooth the way, particularly in smaller sectors or outside the capital, but they are not required. Estonia's relatively small professional community means reputation spreads quickly, so professionalism in early interactions can open doors beyond the immediate contact.
