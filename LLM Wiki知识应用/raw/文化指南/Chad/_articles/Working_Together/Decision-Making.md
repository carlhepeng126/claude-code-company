# Decision-Making

## Working Together

In Chad, decisions rest firmly at the top. Senior leaders, government officials, and those with recognized authority make final choices, often without consulting widely. This concentration of decision-making reflects deeply hierarchical social structures rooted in clan-based governance, where age, lineage, and position define who speaks and whose voice counts. To consult too broadly risks appearing weak or indecisive, and can invite unnecessary complications in environments where loyalty and control are closely guarded.

That said, decisions are rarely made in isolation. Leaders typically consult a small, trusted circle before acting, often drawn from family, ethnic kin, or longstanding personal networks. These conversations happen informally and away from formal meetings. The goal is to test the ground, ensure alignment among key allies, and avoid moves that might fracture fragile coalitions. Consensus here does not mean agreement across the organization. It means ensuring the people whose backing matters most are on board.

For colleagues from cultures where decisions are debated openly or delegated down the line, this can feel opaque or exclusionary. A decision may appear sudden because the groundwork happened out of sight. From a Chadian perspective, a manager who involves too many voices or defers to the team may seem unsure of their standing or unable to lead.

Decisions can also shift after they are announced, particularly when social or political realities change. What looks like inconsistency is often adaptation. In practice, this means confirming decisions directly with the person who holds authority, not assuming that what was discussed in a meeting will automatically proceed.
