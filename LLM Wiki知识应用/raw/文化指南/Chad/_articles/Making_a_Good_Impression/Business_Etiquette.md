# Business Etiquette

## Making a Good Impression

Business interactions in Chad are shaped by formality, care with hierarchy, and the central place of relationships in professional life. Greetings are structured and attentive. Handshakes are common, though between men and women they may be offered only if the woman initiates. When meeting a group, greet each person individually, starting with the most senior. Colleagues from cultures where introductions are faster may read this as time-consuming, while a Chadian counterpart may read a quick group greeting as dismissive or insufficiently respectful. Using formal titles when addressing senior figures or elders reflects the value placed on age and position, and omitting them can feel abrupt or overly casual.

Dress expectations lean toward conservatism, especially outside the capital. Men typically wear long trousers and collared shirts, while women dress modestly, often covering shoulders and knees. In N'Djamena and within international organizations, standards may relax slightly, but erring on the side of formality is rarely misjudged. What you wear signals how seriously you take the meeting and the people in it.

Punctuality is valued differently depending on who holds the meeting. Arriving on time for appointments with senior officials or formal business meetings is expected from visitors. Local colleagues may arrive later, particularly if managing competing demands or navigating unreliable infrastructure. Building in flexibility and not expressing visible frustration when schedules shift will support the relationship more than rigid timekeeping.

Gift-giving is not standard practice in early business meetings and may be misread if introduced too soon. Once trust is established, a thoughtful gift from your home country or something practical can strengthen the connection. Present and receive with your right hand, as the left is culturally associated with impurity.
