# Negotiating

## Working Together

Negotiations in Gabon move slowly because they are designed to do more than finalize terms. They establish whether the relationship will hold over time. Early conversations may circle around topics unrelated to the contract itself, testing rapport and checking for alignment beyond the deal on the table. If you move too quickly toward signing, you may be seen as interested only in the transaction, which makes Gabonese counterparts less willing to commit.

The structure of talks is rarely linear. A point you thought was settled may resurface later, or the discussion may shift to something apparently unrelated before returning to the original issue. This allows both sides to explore flexibility and verify that all angles have been considered. Reopening a topic is not a sign of confusion or delay, it is part of making sure the agreement will last. Expect the conversation to loop back, and use those moments to clarify rather than push forward.

Slowing down can also be strategic. Your counterpart may be consulting someone who holds decision-making authority, or they may be signaling that your offer could improve. For this reason, do not present your best terms at the outset. Leave room for visible concessions that show you are willing to meet them partway. Bargaining is expected and understood as a way to reach something both sides can stand behind.

Building trust with the person who has authority, or close access to it, helps talks move from discussion to action. Personal gestures, such as an introduction or help with an unrelated request, are often part of the process and are expected to be returned over time. These exchanges affirm the relationship and make the agreement more durable.
