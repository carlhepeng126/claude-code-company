# Effective Presentations

## Meetings & Presentations

Presentations in Gabon work best when they balance technical substance with relational warmth. The environment is hierarchical, and people pay close attention to who is speaking and how they frame their message. Your credibility comes partly from your content, but also from the respect you show toward seniority and the care you take in building connection. A confident but respectful tone tends to resonate. Avoid coming across as rushed or transactional. Allow time for your message to settle, especially if you're working through an interpreter.

French is the primary language of business, so prepare your slides and supporting materials accordingly. Keep visuals simple and avoid text-heavy slides. If you're using an interpreter, brief them in advance on technical terms or sector-specific language. Pacing becomes especially important in interpreted settings. Give each point time to be understood fully before moving on. Silence after a question or statement reflects consideration, and filling it too quickly can undercut the sense that you value input.

Storytelling strengthens your message. Rather than leading with data alone, frame your points around scenarios or real examples that feel relevant to your audience's experience. This approach aligns with a broader oral tradition across Central Africa and makes abstract ideas more tangible. A colleague from a culture where presentations are expected to be concise and data-driven may find this slower and more narrative style indirect, while someone from Gabon might read a tightly packed, fact-only presentation as impersonal or lacking depth.

Expect that the most senior person in the room may not speak much during the presentation itself. Questions may come from others gathering input to share upward. Don't mistake quiet engagement for disinterest, and be prepared for follow-up conversations afterward where clarifications happen more freely.
