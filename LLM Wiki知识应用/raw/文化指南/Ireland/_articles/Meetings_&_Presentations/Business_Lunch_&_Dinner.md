# Business Lunch & Dinner

## Meetings & Presentations

Business meals in Ireland blur social and professional lines in ways that can feel unfamiliar to colleagues from cultures where the two are kept separate. Food and drink serve as the setting for conversations that meander between personal anecdotes, humor, and eventually business, often without clear transitions. The meal itself is rarely the point. Building rapport is. That means storytelling, sharing experiences, and finding common ground before work topics surface, if they surface at all.

This approach reflects a broader Irish preference for relationship before task. A colleague from a culture where business gets discussed early in a meal may find the initial small talk extended or purposeless. An Irish counterpart might read a quick pivot to business as overly transactional or uncomfortable. Following the host's lead on timing helps. If business stays at a high level or doesn't come up until dessert, that's by design. Pressing for decisions or detail too soon can undercut the trust the meal is meant to establish.

Pubs often double as venues for business meals, especially outside major cities. They're informal but not unserious. You might discuss a contract over fish and chips and a pint, and the lack of formality doesn't diminish the professional value of the conversation. Asking about dietary preferences beforehand shows respect, and increasingly so as vegetarian, vegan, and religious considerations become more visible. Alcohol remains common but optional. Choosing a soft drink is unlikely to stand out.

Being open to trying local dishes or following your host's recommendations is read as curiosity and respect. Traditional fare like stews, seafood, and roasts still appear frequently, though international cuisine is widely available in urban areas. The meal may stretch late into the evening, especially if the conversation takes off. That endurance is part of the connection.
