# Making Contacts & Scheduling Appointments

## Making a Good Impression

Scheduling a meeting in Ireland is usually straightforward. People at most levels are approachable and willing to connect. What stands out is the warmth around first contact. A purely transactional email can feel abrupt. Opening with something personable, mentioning a shared connection or a mutual colleague, or acknowledging something you have in common will get a warmer response. This reflects the culture's preference for conversation and relational ease, rooted in centuries of village life and clan networks where reputation and relationships mattered. Business hours are typically 9:00 am to 5:30 pm, Monday to Friday. Some smaller companies close entirely for lunch between 1:00 and 2:00 pm, so calling during that window may go unanswered.

Shared connections can speed things up significantly. School ties, county links, or diaspora networks often open doors faster than a formal introduction alone. If you know someone who knows your contact, or if you have a mutual friend in common, mention it early. This isn't name-dropping. It's how trust forms, especially outside the largest cities where community identity remains strong.

Family and local commitments shape availability in ways that international colleagues may not expect. Christmas and Easter are major family occasions, and scheduling around them is wise. Summer months, especially June through August, can be busy with holidays and family travel. September through May is generally better for arranging visits. Outside Dublin and other large urban centers, weekends may be devoted to local GAA matches or community events, and these can influence when people are free or focused.

Initial meetings often begin with light conversation and humor before turning to the agenda. Someone working in a culture where meetings start immediately with a review of objectives may read this as unfocused or inefficient. From an Irish perspective, the opening chat builds rapport and signals that the relationship will be more than just transactional. Arriving a few minutes late is generally overlooked, though repeated lateness may raise questions about reliability.
