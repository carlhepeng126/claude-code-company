# Business Etiquette

## Making a Good Impression

Irish business etiquette draws on deep cultural roots. Bardic and monastic traditions once prized eloquent conversation, listening, and respectful exchange, and that preference still shapes professional life. Catholic and Protestant moral frameworks reinforced values of humility, fairness, and hospitality, while discouraging arrogance or talking down to others. A history of marginalization under colonial rule created lasting sensitivity to condescension or dismissiveness. Together, these influences produce a workplace culture that values warmth, modesty, and the ability to engage with good humor without cruelty.

Being polite and approachable builds trust more reliably than credentials or titles. Irish colleagues often assess character through conversation and informal interaction, not just formal presentations. Showing genuine interest in people, not only the task, helps establish rapport. A colleague from a culture that emphasizes individual achievement and direct self-promotion may find this low-key style frustrating or unclear, while Irish counterparts may read overt confidence as boastful or off-putting. Neither response is wrong, each reflects a different understanding of how credibility is earned.

Public confrontation or blunt criticism is typically avoided. Disagreement is often signaled indirectly, through humor, hedging, or a shift in tone. When delivering unwelcome news, many Irish professionals will soften the message to protect the relationship, which means listening for what is left unsaid becomes important. Light-hearted teasing is common and accepted as a sign of ease, but it should never be mean-spirited. Humor that mocks rather than includes will close doors quickly.

Business cards are exchanged without ceremony, often at the end of a meeting. Personal space is respected, and physical contact such as a hand on the arm may feel intrusive. Eye contact shows openness, but the real work of impression-making happens in conversation, not gesture.
