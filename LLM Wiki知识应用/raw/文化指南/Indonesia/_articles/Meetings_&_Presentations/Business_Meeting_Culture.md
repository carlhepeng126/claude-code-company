# Business Meeting Culture

## Meetings & Presentations

Meetings in Indonesia draw on the principle of *musyawarah-mufakat*, a process of talking things through until a consensus emerges that everyone can accept. This is rooted in the idea that decisions should reflect collective agreement rather than a single voice or a quick vote. The result is meetings that can feel incremental to someone from a culture where decisions are made quickly. Phrasing is measured, ideas are circled back to, and the pace allows space for everyone to signal their position without confrontation. If you find yourself chairing a meeting and wondering why no one is voicing concerns, that silence may in fact be dissent. Asking "Adakah hal yang masih menjadi kekhawatiran?" (Are there any concerns still on your mind?) gives people permission to raise doubts without breaking the face-saving norm.

Disagreement is rarely stated openly, especially toward someone senior. You are more likely to hear "mungkin kita bisa pertimbangkan opsi lain" (maybe we can consider another option) than a direct rejection. This allows the group to maintain harmony while still adjusting course. For someone accustomed to explicit debate, this can feel evasive. From the Indonesian side, however, a colleague who challenges a senior figure publicly may appear disrespectful or unaware of group sensitivities. Design your meetings to accommodate indirect feedback by using open-ended questions, small breakouts, or follow-up one-on-ones where people can speak more freely.

Formality shifts depending on setting. In Jakarta, especially with ministries and state-owned enterprises, expect layers of protocol: seating order, formal greetings, and photo moments that reflect institutional rank. In regional meetings, you may encounter *adat* or religious opening remarks, local coffee, and long introductions establishing who is from which clan or institution. These rituals are not delays. They establish context, honor relationships, and affirm that the right people are in the room. Respecting protocol means learning who should speak first, where people sit, and how long these openings run before the agenda begins.
