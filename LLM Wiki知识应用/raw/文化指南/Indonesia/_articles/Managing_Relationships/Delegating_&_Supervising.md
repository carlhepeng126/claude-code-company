# Delegating & Supervising

## Managing Relationships

Indonesian managers operate within a paternalistic model known as bapakism, from bapak (father), which shapes how authority works. A manager is expected to provide not just direction but protection and genuine concern for their team's wellbeing. Employees in return offer loyalty and compliance. This is not a soft metaphor. It structures daily expectations. A manager who only engages at the level of task completion will be seen as a poor leader, someone who has misunderstood the job. The relationship extends beyond the work itself.

This model sits within a broader Javanese cultural template that dominates professional life across much of Indonesia, particularly in Jakarta and other urban centers. Central to that template is the value placed on harmony (rukun) and the avoidance of open conflict. Disagreement is rarely surfaced directly, and the manager's authority is preserved by a social obligation not to challenge or embarrass superiors in front of others. Public correction causes malu (loss of face), a serious social wound that damages the relationship and silences the team. Feedback must be delivered privately and framed collaboratively, such as suggesting a different approach rather than naming what went wrong.

"Yes" in this context is an unreliable signal. It may mean agreement, acknowledgment, or simply a polite refusal to cause discomfort. Managers who take yes at face value risk managing on incomplete information. After delegating, ask the person to walk through their approach or explain the next steps. This allows uncertainty to surface indirectly, without putting anyone on the spot. Build in follow-up checkpoints and watch for hesitation, vague replies, or shifts in tone. These are often the only signs that something is unclear or unworkable.

Role boundaries also matter. Managers who perform tasks considered below their station lose credibility. Hierarchy is not incidental; it is a social signal that the structure is intact.
