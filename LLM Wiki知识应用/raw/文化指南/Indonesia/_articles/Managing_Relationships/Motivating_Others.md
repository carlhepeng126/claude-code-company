# Motivating Others

## Managing Relationships

For many Indonesian employees, the real driver is what work enables them to do for parents, siblings, spouse, and children. A role that clearly improves their ability to pay for education, healthcare, or maintain dignity in front of relatives taps into something deeper than corporate incentives. When a manager connects a project or performance goal to tangible benefits for families, not just shareholder value, engagement rises. Motivation here is rarely about individual achievement alone. It is about what that achievement makes possible at home.

Pride also comes from being part of something that visibly helps communities. Companies that support disaster relief, fund scholarships, or improve local infrastructure give employees something they can point to in their hometowns. This reflects the principle of *gotong royong* (mutual assistance), and when staff can see their organization contributing in this way, their sense of purpose strengthens. Community-facing projects that employees can join or witness firsthand build motivation in ways that internal targets often do not.

Recognition works best when it lifts the team while honoring individuals. Reward schemes that single out one person too prominently can trigger *iri* (envy) and fracture harmony. Team-based recognition, or rotating opportunities for training, travel, or high-profile assignments, distributes both the reward and the goodwill. Over time, this approach reduces resentment and sustains collective commitment more effectively than purely individualistic systems.

A supportive boss and friendly colleagues often count as much as salary. Loyalty flows toward managers who are kind, understanding, and effective within the organization, because their success reflects well on the team. Keeping that relationship strong is itself a form of motivation.
