# Managing Suppliers & Customers

## Managing Relationships

Many Indonesian supply chains run through family firms that have traded with each other across generations, often along ethnic lines: Javanese, Minang, Bugis, Chinese-Indonesian. Trust in these networks grows from track record, paying on time, and showing up to family events, not just from signed contracts. A supplier who has known your distributor's family for decades will move faster on your behalf if you invest in that relationship too. Visit their premises, understand their family story, and maintain contact when business is slow, not only when orders are high.

Local government officials often function as both gatekeepers and customers, especially at the district and municipal level. They control permits, land access, and market entry, but they also expect the courtesy and service you would extend to a commercial partner. Treating them purely as bureaucratic obstacles will slow things down. Regular courtesy visits, clarity about your intentions, and respect for protocol help build the goodwill that keeps projects moving.

Chinese-Indonesian suppliers and customers tend to value precision, reliability, and discretion. Many families carry memories of the 1998 violence and earlier periods of discrimination, and they prefer partners who honour commitments, avoid drama, and don't pull them into politically exposed positions. Clarity in your agreements and consistency in delivery build confidence. Springing last-minute changes or involving them in contentious local issues will strain the relationship quickly.

Regular face-to-face contact strengthens all these ties. Customers and suppliers respond better when they can put a face to the request, and senior decision-makers expect direct engagement, not only email updates from junior staff.
