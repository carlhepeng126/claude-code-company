# Sharing Information

## Working Together

In Indonesian workplaces, information often travels along two tracks. The formal meeting version, delivered in polite *rapat* (meeting) language, tends to be optimistic and carefully worded to preserve harmony and protect face. The more candid account surfaces later, over afternoon coffee or in a quiet corner, where people feel safer speaking frankly. Sensitive topics, whether workplace tensions, political issues, or serious project risks, are often coded or avoided entirely in official channels. Written communication follows the same logic. Emails and reports stay polite and hopeful even when problems loom, because a permanent record might harm someone's reputation or relationship later. The harshest truths are delivered verbally, one-on-one, where there's room for nuance and no paper trail.

This dual approach can puzzle colleagues from cultures where transparency means putting everything on the table immediately. They may read the meeting minutes and assume they have the full picture, only to discover later that key concerns were never written down. From an Indonesian perspective, a colleague who raises every problem in writing or speaks bluntly in mixed company may seem insensitive or even reckless, failing to consider how their words affect others. To get the real story, ask a trusted colleague after the formal discussion how an issue is actually being talked about outside the room. Move sensitive conversations into smaller, in-person settings where people can speak more freely, then follow up with carefully framed written summaries that acknowledge what was shared without exposing anyone.

Information also moves fast and informally through WhatsApp groups organized around family, neighborhood, alumni ties, or work teams. A policy announcement, rumor, or piece of news can spread nationally within hours, often layered with commentary and reinterpreted along the way. When you share information, consider how it will read as a screenshot in someone's family group chat. Frame announcements with that secondary audience in mind, because the informal version will reach far beyond the people in the room.
