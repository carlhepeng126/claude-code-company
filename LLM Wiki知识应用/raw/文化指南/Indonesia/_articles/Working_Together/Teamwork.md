# Teamwork

## Working Together

The teammate who arrives early to set up chairs, chips in for a colleague's family emergency, or stays to tidy up after the event without being asked is living *gotong royong*, the communal self-help principle that underpins Indonesian collaboration. Teams remember who consistently shows up in these ways and who fades when the work becomes invisible. Recognizing these contributions out loud ensures that the people who quietly hold the team together feel valued, and that collaboration doesn't drift into a pattern where only headline achievements get noticed.

Many Indonesian teams describe themselves as *keluarga besar* (big family), and colleagues often use kinship terms like *Mas*, *Mbak*, *Kak*, *Abang*, or *Adik* rather than formal titles. This language creates warmth and mutual care, but it also brings expectations: tolerance of each other's quirks, willingness to cover for one another, and a reluctance to escalate problems to outsiders. The family frame can protect cohesion, but it can also shield behavior that would otherwise need to be addressed. Leaders who lean into care and fairness while still holding people accountable will find the framing more sustainable.

Much of the team's daily coordination happens through WhatsApp groups, one for the project, another for social chat, sometimes a third for the core group. Quick decisions, emotional check-ins, and informal feedback often appear there before being formalized in email. Engaging through these channels keeps you connected to the flow of information, but without agreed boundaries, team chat can become a 24-hour expectation.

In some settings, women and junior staff are still informally expected to handle minutes, snacks, or logistics while senior men focus on strategy. This quiet division affects who gains visibility and whose contributions are recognized. Rotating these tasks and actively assigning high-value work across the team can shift the pattern.
