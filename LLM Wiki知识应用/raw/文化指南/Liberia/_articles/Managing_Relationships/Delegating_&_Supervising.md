# Delegating & Supervising

## Managing Relationships

Delegation in Liberia depends far more on the strength of personal connection than on formal authority. Liberians have lived through a century and a half of institutions that promised fairness but delivered patronage, from the Americo-Liberian elite's racialised governance through to the collapse of state structures during two civil wars. The result is a workplace culture where genuine human trust generates commitment in ways that job titles alone cannot. If you delegate without first building a visible, consistent relationship with the person receiving the task, follow-through becomes unpredictable. Investment in one-to-one time, regular informal conversation, and demonstrated fairness across ethnic lines will shape whether your delegation succeeds far more than the clarity of your instructions.

Communication styles blend directness with indirectness in ways that can be hard to read initially. A colleague may confirm understanding verbally even when uncertainty remains, because protecting the relationship feels safer than admitting confusion in front of others. Asking "Is that clear?" will reliably produce agreement. Instead, ask open questions that invite explanation: "Can you walk me through how you'll approach this?" or "What do you see as the first step?" This creates room for honest clarification without loss of face and surfaces gaps before they derail the task.

Many employees support extended families on a single income, and women frequently manage work alongside single motherhood with limited or no childcare infrastructure. Rigid timelines that ignore these realities will undermine trust faster than they improve performance. Flexibility around timing, paired with clear expectations on outcomes, demonstrates respect for the whole person and strengthens the relationship that makes delegation work. Oversight should be regular and visible, framed as mentoring rather than policing, and used to build capability over time rather than simply monitor compliance.
