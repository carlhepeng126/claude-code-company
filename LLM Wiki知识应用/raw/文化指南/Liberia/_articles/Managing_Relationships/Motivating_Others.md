# Motivating Others

## Managing Relationships

In Liberia, people respond most strongly when recognition affirms their respectability. Being seen as a serious person—reliable, decent, thoughtful—reflects not only on the individual but on their family and how they are spoken about in their wider network. Acknowledging someone's trustworthiness or offering them responsibility tells them their character has been noticed, and that can motivate more deeply than praise for output alone. A manager who publicly affirms someone's steady presence or invites them to represent the team on a decision reinforces their standing in a way that resonates beyond the task itself.

Financial security remains essential. Many Liberians are the sole earners in extended households, and the pressure to provide shapes what people prioritize at work. But money alone rarely sustains effort over time. What strengthens commitment is the combination of fair pay and an environment where people feel cared for through action: managers who check in during difficult times, who show up when it matters, or who share knowledge that helps someone grow. This ethic of mutual support, rooted in Liberia's recent history of collective survival, is what makes belonging feel real.

Recognition that feels genuine tends to be specific, spoken naturally, and delivered in the moment. Hype or blanket cheerleading can ring hollow. What lands is moral weight: being trusted with something that affects others, being included in decisions, or being seen as someone the group can count on. Framing a stretch assignment as a sign of confidence in someone's judgment, rather than just an urgent deliverable, can shift how the work is received.

Church life, central to public culture, has shaped a familiar vocabulary of encouragement and uplift. Many Liberians are used to environments where dignity is reinforced through visible respect, testimony, and affirming language. Managers who borrow from that register—acknowledging effort, speaking with warmth, lifting people up rather than only pushing them forward—often find stronger engagement than those who rely on targets and timelines alone.
