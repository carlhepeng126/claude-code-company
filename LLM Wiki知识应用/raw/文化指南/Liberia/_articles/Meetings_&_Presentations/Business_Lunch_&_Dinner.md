# Business Lunch & Dinner

## Meetings & Presentations

Meals in Liberia open access to warmth and trust in ways that formal meetings often do not. Sharing food is where people decide whether you feel safe, and whether the relationship has room to grow. Dishes like cassava leaf stew (known locally as *gbassajama*, a cassava-leaf dish) and palava sauce belong to a wider West African comfort logic: greens, palm oil, spice, and protein served over rice to create something sustaining. The cultural grammar of rice "dry" versus rice "with soup" shapes how hosting is understood. Serving a proper meal means abundance and care, and colleagues will notice whether you grasp that or approach the meal as something to move through quickly.

In many settings, especially outside Monrovia or in less formal contexts, sharing plates and communal eating styles can create intimacy. In institutional or more internationally oriented venues, the style may shift toward individual plating and cutlery. The skill is reading which context you are in, and adapting without drawing attention to the shift. If you are unsure, follow the host's lead and watch how others begin.

Christianity and Islam are both significant in Liberia, and food and drink choices can reflect personal or community norms, even when those choices are not announced. Beer is common, but not universal. Offering a range of non-alcoholic options and avoiding assumptions about what someone might want to drink or eat shows respect without requiring intrusive questions. If you are hosting, choose venues that allow flexibility.

Pacing the meal slowly and allowing conversation to move at its own rhythm signals that you value the relationship, not just the outcome. Rushing to finish or steering the conversation too quickly toward business can read as transactional, and may close down the very connection the meal was meant to build.
