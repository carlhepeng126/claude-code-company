# Business Meeting Culture

## Meetings & Presentations

Meetings in Liberia follow a rhythm that prioritizes keeping the room intact and workable over forcing quick resolution. National experience of fragmentation during the civil war has left a social intelligence that avoids escalation. People often use circular discussion, storytelling, and revisiting points as a way to allow all voices to be heard and to let agreement emerge without anyone losing face. This approach draws on the Palava Hut tradition, a widely recognized format for handling conflict that aims for reintegration rather than a clear winner or loser. The structure is legible across the country, so much so that Liberia's Truth and Reconciliation Commission recommended Palava Hut committees as a national repair tool. When a colleague from a culture where decisions are made quickly pushes for closure, Liberians may read this as disregarding the relational groundwork that makes implementation possible. Conversely, someone accustomed to faster decisions may misread the process as avoidance, when it is actually building the legitimacy needed for the outcome to hold.

Hierarchy shapes who speaks and when. Liberians show deference to elders and senior figures, and expect those with moral or communal standing to be recognized in the room. Even where formal structures exist, legitimacy is often felt through people who hold respect locally, chiefs, elders, or respected community figures. Seating may reflect seniority, and it is common to avoid prolonged direct eye contact with the most senior person present as a sign of respect. Meetings typically begin with relational exchange, checking in on families and establishing tone before the agenda begins. If you are hosting, offer tea or water. Do not be surprised if someone opens with a prayer or includes religious references. These moments set the emotional temperature and signal that the meeting is being approached seriously and with shared goodwill.

Language register also signals emotional temperature. Shifting between formal English and Liberian Kreyol (or Kolokwa) is a culturally normal way to move between official and familiar, or between serious and at ease. Pay attention to these shifts. They often mark whether a moment is being framed as formal or personal, delicate or open. Interruptions, whether from visitors or phone calls, are common and should be met with patience. Humor and narrative may weave through discussion, reflecting a communication style that values texture and connection over linear efficiency. If someone agrees to a deadline that seems unrealistic, follow up after the meeting to confirm understanding. Agreement in the room may prioritize maintaining rapport, with practical alignment worked out later through continued conversation.
