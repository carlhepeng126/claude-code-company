# Making Contacts & Scheduling Appointments

## Making a Good Impression

Making contact in Liberia starts with knowing who someone is through trusted links. This has roots in the country's layered history: civil war disrupted institutional continuity, and the 1980 coup shifted ideas of who held legitimacy and representation. People learned to rely on networks they could verify through consistent presence and shared connections. When you approach someone cold, you remain an abstraction. When a mutual contact introduces you, you gain social clarity. In practice, this means an intermediary who can vouch for you opens doors far faster than a direct email or phone call. If you're already in Monrovia, attend networking events and expatriate gatherings. The professional community is tightly connected, and face-to-face presence builds the recognition that written contact alone cannot.

Scheduling works best when you allow flexibility. Meetings can be arranged by phone or email, though internet access remains patchy outside the capital and power cuts affect office connectivity. Confirm appointments a day or two ahead, and don't pack your calendar tightly. Transport across Monrovia takes longer than maps suggest, and priorities can shift mid-day, especially in government or senior-level settings. What might read to a colleague from a tightly scheduled culture as disorganization reflects a different rhythm: relationships and immediate needs often take precedence over pre-set agendas. Someone from Liberia working in a culture where calendars are held firm may find that same structure impersonal or rigid.

Questions about where you're from or who you know are identity-mapping, not small talk. Responding with ease and specificity builds comfort quickly. Listen for shifts in language register too: formal English in official settings, Liberian Kolokwa (Liberian Kreyol) in more relaxed exchanges. Matching the social temperature shows you understand the context you've entered.
