# Business Lunch & Dinner

## Meetings & Presentations

In many British workplaces, lunch is a functional pause rather than a social event. Desk sandwiches, meal deals from chain cafés, or a quick trip to a nearby shop are the norm. This reflects a workday shaped by efficiency and packed schedules, not cultural indifference to food. The long, wine-accompanied lunch exists in certain sectors, but it's far from typical. When organizing a working lunch, assume shorter timing and simpler settings unless you're explicitly told otherwise.

Evening dinners carry different weight. They're used more selectively, often reserved for deeper relationship building or marking milestones. But they also compete with long commutes and limited childcare, particularly outside central London. Many colleagues quietly calculate how often they can manage a late return home. Asking preferences early and choosing venues near good public transport acknowledges this reality and builds goodwill.

Restaurant choices tend toward the cosmopolitan. "Going for a curry" is almost a cliché of British business entertaining, rooted in post-colonial history and now part of everyday professional life. Italian, pan-Asian, and gastropubs also feature heavily. Dietary preferences have become more complex and visible, especially in cities. Vegetarian, vegan, halal, kosher, gluten-free, and low-alcohol requests are increasingly common and discussed openly. Checking dietary needs in advance is expected and taken as a sign of attentiveness.

Conversation during the meal often ranges beyond immediate work topics. Jumping straight into business at the start can feel abrupt. Let the discussion find its rhythm.
