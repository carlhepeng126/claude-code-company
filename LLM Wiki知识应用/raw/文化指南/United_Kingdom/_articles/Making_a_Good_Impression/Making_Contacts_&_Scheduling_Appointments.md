# Making Contacts & Scheduling Appointments

## Making a Good Impression

The path to a meeting in the UK often runs through professional networks, mutual contacts, and alumni associations. While London clubs and old-boy societies have faded as gatekeepers, the principle remains: who introduces you still shapes how easily doors open. A credible referral from a colleague, client, or professional body lowers resistance and makes it easier for someone to justify the time. Cold outreach is possible, but a warm introduction from a trusted contact will typically move things forward faster.

British politeness means that declining a meeting rarely involves a direct refusal. Instead, you'll hear "now's not a great time" or "let's touch base later." These phrases allow the person to deflect without committing or closing the door entirely. If your request goes unanswered or receives a vague response, treat it as a polite no rather than an oversight. Personal assistants and inbox filters can be formidable, and persistence beyond a courteous follow-up risks irritating the very person you're trying to reach.

Diaries fill quickly, especially in cities where long commutes and full workdays leave little breathing room. People appreciate a specific, well-structured request: what you want to discuss, why them, and how long you need. This makes it easier to slot you in. Rescheduling happens often, so build in some flexibility. A vague "we must catch up sometime" is usually just a polite placeholder. If you want the meeting to happen, follow up with concrete dates and a calendar invitation.

Email remains the default for first contact, with calendar tools used to formalize appointments. Phone calls without prior notice are often unwelcome. Once a working relationship develops, younger or tech-savvy colleagues may shift to WhatsApp or text for quicker coordination, but it's worth agreeing on preferred channels early to avoid losing follow-up in the gap between British courtesy and actual commitment.
