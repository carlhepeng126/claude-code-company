# Dress Code

## Making a Good Impression

Outside a few traditional corners such as City law, finance, or certain government boardrooms, the UK leans firmly toward smart-casual. Neat shirts, chinos, blazers, dresses, and even trainers are everyday professional wear in tech, creative industries, and much of the public sector. This preference reflects a cultural comfort with understated professionalism over formal display. People value being put together without appearing to try too hard, and the line between appropriately casual and too relaxed can be subtle. Arriving overdressed in a full suit to a start-up meeting can create as much distance as showing up in jeans to a law firm.

Sector and setting shape what works. London finance or the Bar still expect suits, while a charity or a tech company might find cardigans and rolled-up sleeves entirely normal. If you are visiting for the first time, aim for the upper end of smart-casual and adjust once you see what others are wearing. Matching the band of formality helps you blend in and be heard, rather than standing out for the wrong reasons.

The UK's changeable weather also shapes how people read preparedness. Arriving drenched because you did not bring a coat or wearing impractical shoes when rain was forecast can come across as poor planning. Outerwear and sensible footwear are part of looking professional, especially if you will be walking between sites or standing outside between meetings.

Flashy logos or overt displays of wealth can trigger quiet skepticism. There is cultural preference for quiet quality, even a bit of shabbiness, over aggressive show. In joint teams, understated dress tends to foster easier rapport, while status-driven clothing can create unnecessary distance.
