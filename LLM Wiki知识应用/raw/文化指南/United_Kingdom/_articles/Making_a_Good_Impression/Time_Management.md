# Time Management

## Making a Good Impression

The UK's approach to punctuality carries the moral echo of the railway age, when timetables shaped national life and lateness suggested unreliability. Arriving late to formal meetings, especially with clients or new contacts, still registers as a minor breach of trust. That threshold tightens further in London and the South East, where pace is faster and schedules are packed. For external appointments, assume strict punctuality is the baseline expectation.

Inside established teams, the rules relax. A five-minute delay explained by trains or childcare draws a nod rather than a frown, particularly when everyone knows the strain of fragile commuting and squeezed public services. Yet this elasticity often masks deeper pressure. Many people are overstretched but under-dramatic, offering "bit busy but fine" while quietly firefighting behind polite faces. If you're collaborating over time, check real capacity instead of accepting reassurance at face value. A colleague from a culture where workload is voiced more directly might assume all is well, while a British partner quietly manages overload to stay collegial.

Regional rhythms also vary. Outside the capital, local patterns such as school runs, ferry timetables, or farming seasons subtly shape availability and pace. When working across UK offices, ask how these rhythms affect shared milestones rather than assuming uniform tempo.

If you slip on time, apologize clearly, give brief context, and propose a new plan. Owning the delay and offering a solution restores trust more quickly than downplaying it.
