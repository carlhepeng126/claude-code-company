# Forms of Address

## Making a Good Impression

How you address someone in Algeria reflects whether you understand nif, the shared code of preserving dignity and not making anyone look small. Tone, pacing, and greeting style communicate as much as the words you choose, because respect is tied to upbringing and honour, not just politeness. Getting this wrong can close doors before a conversation has even begun.

Seniority and age are treated with formal respect. Titles such as *Docteur*, *Professeur*, *Monsieur*, or *Madame* should be used alongside surnames, especially in early meetings. But outward deference does not mean automatic acceptance. Algeria holds a deep cultural memory that authority should be credible and fair, so while seniors are addressed formally, their legitimacy is being quietly evaluated. Drop the formality only when your counterpart invites it. Skipping titles too soon can feel presumptuous, particularly with older colleagues.

Language choice shifts the register. Arabic connects to national and religious life, while Amazigh languages signal belonging in regions like Kabylie, Aurès, and parts of the Sahara. French often marks education and institutional credibility. Switching between them can change the perceived seriousness or intimacy of an exchange. In business, French is the most common professional language, but opening with a greeting in Arabic can show cultural awareness. If you are unsure, ask in advance.

Formality also depends on setting. Algerians often use more formal address in mixed company to protect dignity, then shift to warmer, more relaxed language once trust is established. This reflects a broader pattern of separating the public self from the trusted-circle self. Expect regional variation too: Kabyle and Aurès contexts may hold stronger elder-respect cues, while urban coastal settings may relax formality more quickly once rapport is built.
