# Business Etiquette

## Making a Good Impression

Hospitality in Algeria functions as a social contract of honour, and it shapes the first minutes of almost every professional interaction. Offering tea, food, or attentive hosting demonstrates generosity and moral standing, and refusing these gestures too quickly can read as ungracious. The host protects the guest's comfort and status, and the guest reciprocates through gratitude and visible restraint. Accept what is offered, even if you only take a small amount, and express appreciation clearly. This exchange is widely practised across regions and generations, even where lifestyles have modernised, because it anchors trust and signals that you understand dignity-based reciprocity.

Discretion protects dignity and prevents long-term damage, so what is said publicly is carefully managed. Speaking too bluntly, exposing someone in a meeting, or creating public embarrassment will be interpreted as poor judgement rather than honesty. A colleague from a culture where directness is valued as transparency may see this indirectness as evasive or unclear, while an Algerian counterpart may read blunt feedback as a failure to respect relationships and reputation. Give critical input privately, frame concerns gently, and listen for what is implied in pauses, deflections, or shifts in tone. What is left unsaid often holds as much meaning as what is spoken.

Authority is performed through formality, but trust is earned through fairness and consistency. A long historical rupture in leadership legitimacy contributes to a cultural split between role-respect and credibility-trust. People may show deference to your position while privately judging whether you are just, competent, and protective of others' dignity. Younger professionals and those in urban settings may be less deferential to hierarchy alone, expecting evidence of competence alongside title. Restorative approaches to conflict, rooted in customary law traditions like azref and council mediation, remain culturally intelligible. Solutions that allow dignity-preserving repair, rather than public defeat, are more likely to be accepted and sustained.

Respectability is read through small behavioural cues. How someone greets elders, shows composure, and manages impatience all influence whether they are seen as someone "well brought up." Moral baselines vary across religious and regional worlds, especially around mixed-gender interaction, alcohol, and food, so read the context carefully and follow your counterpart's lead. Etiquette here is less about checklists and more about whether your behaviour protects dignity and shows moral seriousness.
