# Business Lunch & Dinner

## Meetings & Presentations

In Algeria, sharing food is a way of entering relationship territory. A business meal does more than feed people. It communicates welcome, seriousness, and a willingness to invest time in connection. Table manners and pacing reflect upbringing and self-control, and both are closely tied to reputation. Eating quickly or steering conversation back to business too soon can read as transactional and undermine trust. The meal unfolds across several courses, with mint tea or Turkish-style coffee marking the close. This rhythm is intentional, allowing relationship to deepen before decisions are expected.

Seating, serving order, and attention distribution often reflect seniority and respect for elders. These cues are rarely spelled out, but they are culturally legible. Paying attention to who is acknowledged first or given a seat of prominence helps signal your own regard. In internationalised urban settings, these courtesies may be lighter. In more traditional environments, they tend to be more formal and carry greater weight.

Halal is the widespread baseline, and many restaurants outside international hotels do not serve alcohol. Religious and household comfort zones around drinking and gender mixing vary, and people usually manage this through quiet cues rather than direct explanation. Asking in advance about venue suitability, or checking with a local colleague, helps avoid awkwardness. Vegetarian dishes like vegetable couscous or tagines are often available, but it is best to mention dietary needs early.

Regional dishes can carry pride and identity, especially where Amazigh heritage is salient. Serving local food is sometimes a way of saying "this is our place." Receiving it with interest and appreciation strengthens connection, particularly outside the capital.
