# Business Entertaining

## Meetings & Presentations

Inviting a colleague to a meal is more than a courtesy in Algeria. Hospitality functions as a moral act tied to honour and reputation. The host demonstrates generosity and protects the guest's dignity, while the guest responds with gratitude and restraint. This is not a social extra. It's a serious relationship signal, rooted in values of good upbringing and reciprocal obligation. If you receive an invitation, understand that you are being included in a way that reflects how you are viewed.

Shared time over meals allows observation of character: how you respect elders, whether you can keep the atmosphere dignified, and how discreetly you handle sensitive topics. Trust is built through repeated, embodied participation rather than quick declarations. Business may come up late or not at all. The host usually decides when to raise it. Bringing it up early, especially before the meal has settled into its rhythm, can read as transactional. The gathering itself does the work.

Comfort with alcohol, mixed-gender socialising, and the degree of formality vary sharply by household, region, and generation. Halal norms are widespread, but conservatism levels differ. In Algiers and other coastal cities, some circles adopt more globalised styles. In smaller cities or more traditional environments, hosting may be more family-centred, with stronger gender and elder norms. These baselines are often managed through implicit cues rather than stated rules. If you are hosting, avoid offering alcohol unless you are certain it will be welcome, and follow your counterpart's lead on setting and tone.

Reciprocating an invitation, whether during the visit or later, shows you understand the relationship as mutual.
