# Business Etiquette

## Making a Good Impression

Formality in Turkmen business settings reflects a broader orientation toward authority and respect for position. Meetings begin with courteous greetings, often accompanied by handshakes, and titles are used consistently throughout. Address people by their professional title and surname unless invited to do otherwise. The hierarchical framing of the workplace means that those in senior positions expect deference, and junior colleagues will rarely challenge or contradict them openly. If you're working with a counterpart from a culture where informality develops quickly, be aware that your Turkmen colleagues may interpret first-name use or casual tone as a lack of seriousness or respect, while you may read their formality as distance.

Punctuality is expected from foreign visitors, though meetings may begin later than scheduled. This reflects a relationship-driven approach where flexibility is often extended to those with greater authority or influence. Arriving on time demonstrates professionalism, but be prepared for the meeting itself to unfold at a measured pace. Business discussions often begin with tea and pleasantries, and rushing toward the agenda can seem abrupt or transactional.

Dress conservatively. Men typically wear dark suits, and women should opt for modest, professional attire that covers arms and legs. Appearance conveys respect for the occasion and the people you are meeting, and a polished presentation will be noticed and appreciated.
