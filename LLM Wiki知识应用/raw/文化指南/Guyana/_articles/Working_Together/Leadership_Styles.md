# Leadership Styles

## Working Together

Leadership in Guyana tends to be directive and centered at the top, though this often coexists with consultation that may or may not shape the final decision. Authority is respected, and managers are expected to provide clear direction rather than invite open challenge. This reflects a workplace culture where hierarchy offers structure and clarity, and where questioning a superior's decision too openly can be seen as undermining their position. Leaders are expected to know, to decide, and to guide.

At the same time, many Guyanese managers do consult their teams before making decisions. This consultation is rarely a vote or an invitation to override the manager's judgment. Instead, it serves to show respect, gather useful information, and allow people to feel heard. A manager who makes a unilateral decision without asking for input may be seen as dismissive or disconnected, even if the outcome would have been the same. The act of asking reinforces the relationship and the manager's credibility.

For colleagues from cultures where consultation implies shared decision-making, this can feel unclear. They may assume that voicing concern will change the outcome, then feel frustrated when it doesn't. From the other side, a Guyanese manager may view a colleague who pushes back after being consulted as overstepping, especially if the pushback happens publicly or bypasses the manager's authority. The key difference is that consultation in Guyana often affirms the decision rather than opens it up for revision.

Understanding this distinction helps avoid misreading tone or intent. If you're leading in Guyana, consult genuinely and explain your reasoning, but be clear about what is settled and what is still open. If you're working with a Guyanese leader, offer input when asked, but recognize that the final call remains with the person accountable.
