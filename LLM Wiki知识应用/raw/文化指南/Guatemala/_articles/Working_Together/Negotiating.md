# Negotiating

## Working Together

In many parts of Guatemala, particularly the highlands, a business proposal can land on layers of historical memory that never appear in the paperwork. In Quiché, Alta Verapaz, or Huehuetenango, the land under discussion may lie near sites of 1980s massacres or burnt villages. For communities there, the word "development" does not arrive neutrally. It comes freighted with questions about who benefits, who leaves, and what gets taken. If you open negotiations without acknowledging this context, your counterpart may listen politely while mentally filing you alongside the long list of outsiders who promised roads, schools, or fair coffee prices and then disappeared. That collective memory of broken commitments shapes how trust is earned.

This history explains why Guatemalan negotiators often move slowly and test intentions before committing. A cooperative that has been let down by exporters may insist on starting with a single container for one season, waiting to see if you pay on time before scaling up, regardless of your growth targets. Small, visible wins that shift lived experience outweigh verbal assurances. Pressing for speed can read as an attempt to move past people rather than with them. For land, infrastructure, or extraction projects, include truthful acknowledgment of the past and symbolic acts of respect, such as listening sessions or commemoration, as part of the negotiation itself.

Communication during these discussions tends to be indirect. Guatemalan counterparts are unlikely to say no outright, preferring to preserve face on both sides. Listen closely to what is left unsaid and watch for hesitation or conditional phrasing. Decisions are often made by consensus and signed off higher up the chain, which can extend timelines. Make sure you are negotiating with someone senior enough to influence that outcome. Be prepared to make concessions and to ask for them in return. The process is relational, and each round of give-and-take tests whether the partnership will hold over time.
