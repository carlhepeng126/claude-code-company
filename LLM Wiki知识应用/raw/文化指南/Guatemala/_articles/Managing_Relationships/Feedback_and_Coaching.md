# Feedback and Coaching

## Managing Relationships

Delivering feedback in Guatemala means working with a history that has made public criticism dangerous. Public shaming, especially of indigenous staff by a ladino boss or foreigner, can replay older patterns of racist humiliation and military contempt. The person may smile and say nothing and simply never fully trust you again. To preserve both dignity and the relationship, give tough feedback in private and frame it around specific behaviors and processes, not worth or identity.

Traditional Guatemalan mentors often share guidance through stories rather than direct assessments. A more experienced colleague might say, "When I started, I also did this and mira lo que pasó" (look what happened). The lesson arrives through narrative, not through a list of competencies that need work. This approach allows someone to reflect and adjust without feeling judged. If you're coaching a team, ask people to tell their version of what happened, share yours, and build new approaches together rather than simply delivering a verdict.

Written warnings can feel threatening rather than developmental. Many Guatemalans have seen paperwork weaponized to justify firings or to criminalize activists, so a formal letter in someone's file may trigger anxiety rather than improvement. If documentation is necessary, explain it transparently as protection for both sides and always pair it with a humane, face-to-face conversation that makes clear your intent is growth, not punishment.
