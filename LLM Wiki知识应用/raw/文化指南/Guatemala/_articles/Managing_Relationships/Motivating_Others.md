# Motivating Others

## Managing Relationships

Many Guatemalans are working first and foremost so their children can finish secundaria or reach university, opportunities they themselves were denied. This reality, often summarized in the phrase *que mis hijos estudien* (so my children can study), shapes what feels rewarding. A small scholarship fund, flexible hours for school events, or access to training can resonate more deeply than a one-off cash bonus. When benefits connect to education goals, they speak directly to what drives people beyond the paycheck.

For younger Guatemalans especially, there is a constant question running underneath every job conversation: *me voy o me quedo?* Do I migrate or do I stay? Roles that offer clear growth, safety, and a sense of impact at home speak directly to that tension. If the path forward is vague or the environment feels unstable, the Northbound option starts to look more compelling. Make it clear how someone can grow over time, and what staying makes possible.

Recognition lands best when it honors not just the individual but their team, their community, or their roots. Being singled out as an exceptional individual, particularly when framed around resilience or indigenous identity, can feel more like being turned into a symbol than being truly seen. When you acknowledge a contribution, name both the person and the people or place behind them. Success here feels shared, not extracted.

A nurturing, human environment combined with fair pay inspires more sustained engagement than a formal incentive scheme alone. Guatemalans are mentally tough and bring a strong work ethic, but most are more interested in supporting their families and preserving dignity than in feeding corporate ambition. Tight deadlines, late nights, and high-pressure targets rarely spark creativity. What does is the sense that they belong, that their well-being matters, and that their work connects to something meaningful beyond the bottom line.
