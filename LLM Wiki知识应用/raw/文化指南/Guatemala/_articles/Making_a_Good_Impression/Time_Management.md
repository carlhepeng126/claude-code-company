# Time Management

## Making a Good Impression

When a colleague in Guatemala says "ahorita llego," they might mean five minutes or sometime in the afternoon. The phrase buys room to maneuver in a context where road blockades, sudden downpours, or extortion calls can upend any timetable. Promising precision risks making a commitment you cannot keep, so language flexes instead. For a visiting colleague from a culture where schedules are fixed early and closely followed, this can feel evasive. From a Guatemalan perspective, the refusal to acknowledge reality and insist on rigid timelines can seem both naive and disrespectful. When you hear "ahorita," ask gently for a range: "¿Antes de almuerzo o por la tarde?" Frame it as clarification, not correction.

The calendar itself runs on multiple tracks. Donor budgets may dictate an August training rollout, but August might also be maize weeding season or the week before a town's *feria patronal* (patron saint festival), when most people are preparing food, organizing processions, or fulfilling community obligations. Semana Santa, coffee harvests, teacher strikes, and highway blockades each claim their own weeks, and planning around them requires knowing which rhythms take precedence. A deadline that ignores the agricultural or ritual calendar often becomes a deadline that no one meets.

Guatemalans value punctuality for appointments and expect the same in return. Arriving late to a scheduled meeting signals disrespect. But meeting a project deadline two weeks out operates under different rules. Without visible buy-in or a clear collective stake, urgency does not automatically transfer. Progress happens when the goal becomes shared, not when the timeline is declared. Check in often, map no-go periods with local partners at the outset, and distinguish between your own hard deadlines and areas where you can align with the community's calendar. That distinction makes the difference between a plan that holds and one that quietly unravels.
