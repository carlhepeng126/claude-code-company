# Forms of Address

## Making a Good Impression

In Guatemala, forms of address speak directly to questions of earned achievement and social standing. Calling someone *don* Pedro or *doña* Tomasa (don or doña followed by their first name) signals that you recognise their place, whether that's a market vendor, a cooperative elder, or a truck driver with decades on the road. The term applies across class lines, but it always acknowledges seniority or respect. Skipping it with older people, especially in rural or community settings, can sound abrupt or dismissive.

Professional titles function differently. In a country where university remains beyond reach for most families, addressing someone as *licenciada* (a university graduate, often in law or administration), *ingeniero* (engineer), *arquitecto* (architect), or *doctor* (physician or PhD holder) acknowledges a path that required real sacrifice and determination. Dropping these titles too soon, or defaulting to first names without invitation, can read as though you're erasing what someone worked years to achieve. Use the title until the person themselves invites you to do otherwise.

The choice between *usted*, *tú*, and *vos* also marks relational distance. *Usted* is the default respectful form with older people, clients, mayors, and community authorities. In urban circles and among close colleagues, you may hear *vos*, the informal Guatemalan second-person singular. Shifting to *tú* or *vos* before the other person does can feel overfamiliar or even patronising. Wait for them to move the register. If someone begins using *vos* with you, it usually means you've crossed from formal outsider to trusted colleague or friend.

In written correspondence, formality runs deeper still. Academic and professional titles replace courtesy forms like *señor* or *señora* in both speech and letters. Mirror the forms of address you hear Guatemalans using with one another, particularly with elders and authority figures, and let your counterpart set the pace for any shift toward informality.
