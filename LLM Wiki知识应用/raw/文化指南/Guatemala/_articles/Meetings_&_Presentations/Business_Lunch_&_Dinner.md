# Business Lunch & Dinner

## Meetings & Presentations

In many Guatemalan workplaces, almuerzo (lunch) is when colleagues decompress, swap stories about rent and remittances, and shift between Spanish, Spanglish, and sometimes K'iche' or Kaqchikel. The meal is a social anchor, a moment when people breathe and reconnect after a tense morning. Skipping it to squeeze in another meeting can signal that you don't understand how people cope with the weight of daily life. Protecting lunchtime and eating with colleagues, rather than staying at your laptop, builds the kind of trust that opens doors later.

Regional dishes quietly declare identity. When a Kaqchikel manager orders pepián instead of generic grilled chicken, or a Q'eqchi' colleague suggests kak'ik (turkey soup), they are placing their roots and home region on the table. Asking about these dishes by name, rather than defaulting to familiar options, shows you are interested in their story beyond their work. Jocón, hilachas, and rellenitos are not just food; they are gestures of pride and continuity.

Alcohol expectations vary sharply. Some evangelical circles and rural communities avoid beer and rum entirely, shaped by painful histories with alcoholism and violence. Others see a few drinks as part of a normal convivio (gathering). The same word can mean radically different atmospheres. Watch how the group behaves and match the most moderate person present. When in doubt, stay one notch more restrained than the quietest drinker at the table.
