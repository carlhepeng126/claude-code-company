# Motivating Others

## Managing Relationships

In Afghanistan, motivation grows from belonging and the protection of family honor. People give their best when they feel part of a trusted group and when their contribution enhances the standing of their household. Work is rarely just a personal pursuit. How someone is seen at work ripples outward to their family's reputation, and that connection fuels sustained effort far more reliably than targets or formal incentives alone.

Money helps, but it rarely drives engagement on its own. A manager who acknowledges effort openly, who invites someone into a visible project, or who thanks the team after a difficult stretch sends a clear message: you are valued here. Recognition works best when it is immediate, personal, and rooted in real contribution. Warmth and respect within the team, combined with fair pay, inspire more than a bonus structure applied from a distance.

Rigid deadlines and pressure tactics, however, rarely work. Afghan professional life follows a more fluid sense of time, shaped by family obligations and a belief that outcomes rest ultimately with God. People outside senior roles will not routinely sacrifice family time to meet an arbitrary cutoff, especially if the reason for urgency is not explained or shared. Managers who can adjust timelines while keeping the team engaged will see better results than those who insist on fixed schedules without room to adapt.

What motivates someone can shift with context. Younger professionals in Kabul may seek training, development, or exposure to international work. In smaller towns or more traditional settings, stability and a respected position may hold more appeal. Across all contexts, the desire to protect and elevate family standing remains central.
