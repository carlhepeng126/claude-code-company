# Dress Code

## Making a Good Impression

Conservative dress in Afghanistan reflects the interplay of Islamic values, tribal custom, and political history. Clothing choices have long been shaped by religious interpretation, regional identity, and the expectation that modesty, particularly for women, protects both personal and family honor. These norms extend into professional life, where appearance is inseparable from credibility and respect.

For men in government offices or formal organizations, a suit or traditional Afghan clothing such as the shalwar kameez (long tunic and loose trousers) is standard. Foreign businessmen are expected to wear conservative suits, long-sleeved shirts, and ties. In more relaxed professional settings or NGO offices, business casual with long sleeves is acceptable, though appearance should remain formal and covered. Shorts, open-toed shoes, or casual wear undermine professional standing.

Women's dress is governed by strict norms. Under current Taliban rule, most professional contexts require full-body covering including the headscarf, and many women wear the chadri (a full-body veil, commonly called a burqa). In international organizations or among diaspora professionals, expectations may be slightly more flexible, but long sleeves, full-length skirts or trousers, and a headscarf are still the baseline. Choosing modest, high-quality fabrics and avoiding bright colors or fitted clothing demonstrates understanding of local norms and facilitates smoother collaboration.

Regional and generational differences exist. Urban professionals in Kabul, particularly those educated abroad, may dress more formally in Western-style suits while still observing modesty. In rural areas or more conservative provinces, traditional dress and stricter covering are expected. If hosting a meeting or visiting a counterpart's office, ask in advance what is appropriate.
