# Negotiating

## Working Together

Negotiation in Afghanistan centers on relationships and trust more than transactions. Before discussing terms or contracts, expect time spent on personal connection. Conversations may cover family, background, or mutual acquaintances. This exchange is how credibility is built and intentions are read. Moving too quickly to business points can suggest a shallow commitment to the partnership, and may slow the process rather than speed it up.

The path toward agreement is rarely linear. A point discussed and seemingly settled may be revisited later, or the conversation may shift to something unrelated before circling back. This allows each party to test the flexibility and sincerity of the other, and to ensure all angles have been considered. A colleague from a culture where agreements are made sequentially may read this as backtracking or indecision, while an Afghan counterpart may find a refusal to revisit terms rigid or distrustful. Allow room for this back-and-forth. It often surfaces unspoken concerns that would otherwise undermine the deal.

Pace can also serve a strategic purpose. Slowing down may reflect a need to consult family, elders, or influential figures whose approval is required. It can also indicate that your offer has not yet met expectations. For this reason, it is wise to hold back your best terms early and leave space for movement. Bargaining is customary and viewed as a collaborative process, one rooted in Afghan tradition and understood as a way to reach a fair outcome.

Building rapport with decision-makers or those close to them is essential. Authority is concentrated, and real progress often depends on access to the person whose word will hold.
