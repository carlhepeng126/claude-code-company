# Business Lunch & Dinner

## Meetings & Presentations

Business meals in Afghanistan are rarely about efficiency. They exist to build trust in a context where long-term relationships determine whether agreements hold. Invitations to lunch or dinner reflect a willingness to invest time and social proximity before commitments are made. Meals often unfold over several hours, with conversation moving slowly between personal and professional topics. Rushing the pace or turning discussion quickly toward contracts can undermine the relational foundation the meal is meant to create.

Afghan hospitality runs deep, rooted in Pashtunwali traditions of honor and guest protection that extend across ethnic groups. Hosts will often order generously and insist that guests eat more, even when they decline. Accepting food and expressing gratitude reinforces respect. Meals are typically shared from communal platters, with rice dishes like kabuli palaw (rice with raisins, carrots, and lamb) or shorwa (meat stew) served at the center of the table. Bread, usually naan, accompanies every meal and is torn by hand to scoop food. Using the right hand only is expected.

Alcohol is not served. Tea, often green tea with cardamom, appears throughout and at the close of the meal. Colleagues from cultures where meals conclude quickly after the last course may feel the rhythm is stretched. For Afghan hosts, the extended close offers a final space for connection before returning to work. Leaving immediately after finishing your plate can feel abrupt and signal disengagement.
