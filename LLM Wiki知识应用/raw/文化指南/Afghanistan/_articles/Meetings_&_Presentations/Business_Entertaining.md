# Business Entertaining

## Meetings & Presentations

Business meals in Afghanistan are rare and carry serious weight. Security concerns, limited infrastructure, and the absence of a restaurant culture in many areas mean that most business stays firmly within the office. When dining does happen, it's usually at a very late stage of the relationship, often in someone's home, and only after significant trust has been established.

If you are invited, the setting will likely be private and the occasion formal. Gender separation is common, particularly in more conservative or rural contexts, and alcohol is not part of the picture. Afghanistan is an Islamic country, and serving or consuming alcohol would cause deep offense and likely end the relationship. Tea, soft drinks, and elaborate multi-course meals are the norm. The food itself reflects hospitality, and refusing what is offered can seem disrespectful. Eating with your right hand is expected if bread is served without utensils.

Business is almost never discussed during the meal. The gathering exists to deepen the relationship, not to advance an agenda. A colleague from a culture where meals double as working sessions may find the lack of explicit progress frustrating and may be tempted to steer conversation back toward the project. From an Afghan perspective, that same behavior reads as transactional and disrespectful of the honor being extended. The meal is the relationship work.

Reciprocating can be complicated. Inviting an Afghan counterpart to a restaurant abroad may work, but suggesting a meal in-country without understanding the context or the venue can feel inappropriate or unsafe. A thoughtful gift or gesture of thanks is often more practical than a return invitation.
