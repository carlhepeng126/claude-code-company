# Business Lunch & Dinner

## Meetings & Presentations

The Cocos (Keeling) Islands have no restaurants in the conventional sense and limited commercial infrastructure. Most business interaction happens informally, often in private homes or through community gatherings on Home Island or West Island. If a business meal does take place, it typically reflects the Malay culture of the Home Island community, where hospitality runs deep and the act of sharing food strengthens trust and signals goodwill.

Meals tend to be communal. Dishes are placed in the centre and everyone helps themselves. Rice, fish, and curries flavoured with coconut, lime, and chili are common. The pace is unhurried. Eating quickly or steering the conversation too directly toward business can feel transactional and may undermine the relationship you're there to build. For a colleague from a culture where deals are closed over lunch, this may feel inefficient. From a Cocos perspective, a colleague who shifts straight to outcomes without time to connect may seem impersonal or overly focused on the transaction rather than the person.

Alcohol is not served, reflecting the Islamic values of the Malay community. Sweet tea or coffee usually follows the meal, offering a natural pause before discussion resumes. If you have dietary restrictions, mention them in advance. Flexibility is valued, but preparation relies on what's available locally, which can be limited. Expect informality, warmth, and an emphasis on connection over formality or agenda.
