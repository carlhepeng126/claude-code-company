# Forms of Address

## Making a Good Impression

Botswana's professional culture values respect and courtesy, and forms of address reflect both hierarchy and relational warmth. Titles are used consistently in formal and business settings, especially when addressing senior colleagues, government officials, or anyone older. "Mr." and "Mrs." paired with the surname are standard until you are invited to move to first names. Professional titles such as "Doctor" or "Professor" are acknowledged and used, particularly in sectors like education, health, and public administration. This formality is rooted in a broader cultural expectation that age and position deserve visible recognition.

The shift to first names happens more readily among peers or once a working relationship has been established, but it is rarely immediate. Waiting for the other person to suggest the change shows awareness of the relational boundary. In meetings with mixed seniority, defaulting to titles for senior participants even when juniors use first names with each other helps avoid misreading the tone of the room.

Greetings typically begin with a handshake, often accompanied by a brief verbal exchange asking after the person's well-being. Eye contact and a warm demeanor are expected, as they demonstrate sincerity. Among colleagues who know each other well, greetings may be more relaxed, but the initial formality sets the tone for new or cross-organizational interactions.

In written communication, maintaining formal address is especially important. Emails to senior colleagues or external contacts should open with the appropriate title and surname, and closings should remain courteous. Moving too quickly to casual language in writing can come across as presumptuous, even if the face-to-face relationship feels comfortable.
