# Leadership Styles

## Working Together

Leadership in Botswana is shaped by the cultural value of *botho* (respect, dignity, and shared humanity), which calls for decisions to be made with others, not imposed from above. Leaders are expected to consult widely before deciding, creating room for input from the team and often from broader stakeholders. This reflects a legacy of traditional governance structures such as the *kgotla*, a village council where everyone has the right to speak and where decisions emerge only after all voices have been heard. Authority is respected, but it must be earned through humility and demonstrated care for the group, not declared through title alone.

A manager from a culture where decisions are made quickly and individually may read this process as slow or risk-averse. But in Botswana, a decision reached without consultation can undermine trust and be quietly resisted, even if compliance appears outward. From the Motswana perspective, a leader who decides alone may seem disconnected or dismissive, regardless of how competent the decision itself is. When leading or working with Batswana colleagues, allow time for input before finalizing direction. This is how legitimacy is built.

Feedback is typically given indirectly and framed gently to preserve dignity, and public challenge of a leader is rare. Leaders reciprocate by staying approachable and refraining from overtly authoritarian behavior. Titles exist and hierarchy is real, but using rank to shut down conversation will damage long-term credibility more than it accelerates the decision.
