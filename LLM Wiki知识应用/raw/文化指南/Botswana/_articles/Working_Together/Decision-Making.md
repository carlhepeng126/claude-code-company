# Decision-Making

## Working Together

Decisions in Botswana tend to move through consultation before they settle. Leaders recognize that choices affect relationships and group cohesion, so they take time to gauge opinion and build alignment before committing publicly. This means that what looks like delay to a colleague expecting a quick call from the top may actually be careful coordination designed to ensure the decision holds once it's made.

Senior figures hold the final say, and their approval often anchors the process. But that authority is rarely exercised in isolation. Leaders consult informally with trusted colleagues, often outside formal meetings, to test ideas and understand where resistance or support might lie. This blending of hierarchy and consultation reflects a practical reality: a decision that ignores key voices or disrupts harmony is harder to implement, no matter how technically sound it may be.

Flexibility often enters the picture once a decision is announced. If circumstances shift or if family, community, or logistical realities require adjustment, implementation may adapt accordingly. A rigid adherence to the original plan can be seen as ignoring context. For colleagues from cultures where decisions are expected to be fixed once agreed, this can read as inconsistency. From a Botswanan perspective, adjusting to what's workable is a sign of good judgment, not weak follow-through.

If you're working across this difference, clarify early what aspects of a decision are firm and which can bend. Make space in timelines for the consultation that underpins buy-in, and recognize that a slower decision process often means smoother execution later.
