# Sharing Information

## Working Together

In Botswana, information moves through established relationships rather than formal channels alone. The cultural principle of *botho* (a relational ethic emphasizing respect, interdependence, and care for others) shapes how knowledge is shared at work. People prefer to give sensitive or detailed information to those they know and trust, particularly when the content could affect someone's standing or create tension within a team. Building rapport through consistent, respectful interaction opens doors that email requests or org charts cannot. A colleague might withhold fuller detail in early meetings but share more candidly once they sense you value the relationship and will use what you hear with care.

Hierarchical respect also influences the flow of information. Junior staff often wait for senior colleagues to invite their input rather than offering updates unsolicited. This reflects deference to authority and a concern not to overstep, especially in public settings where correcting a senior figure could cause embarrassment. Managers working with Batswana teams may need to ask direct, specific questions in one-on-one conversations to surface what people know. Open-door policies help, but they work best when paired with active outreach rather than waiting for staff to come forward on their own.

Generational differences are visible. Younger professionals, particularly those educated abroad or working in multinational firms, may share information more readily across hierarchies and departments. Yet even in these settings, the instinct to protect relationships and avoid public disagreement remains strong. A colleague from a culture where transparency is expected regardless of rank might read this reserve as withholding. A Motswana colleague, in turn, might see that directness as disrespectful or careless with group harmony. Recognizing both perspectives helps prevent misreading discretion as disengagement or openness as disregard.
