# Delegating & Supervising

## Managing Relationships

South African workplaces are formally hierarchical, with final decisions resting at the top, but the indigenous philosophy of Ubuntu — roughly "I am because we are" — shapes a strong expectation that consultation happens before those decisions land. Moving fast by skipping this process will often generate resistance rather than efficiency. Involving people in the conversation before a decision is finalized generates commitment. The consultation itself is part of how authority is legitimized, and when it is rushed or bypassed, the decision may be followed on paper but resisted in practice.

Ubuntu also means that personal relationships are central to working trust. Employees give more to managers who take a genuine interest in them as people. A purely task-focused, transactional management style will feel cold, and it will limit the quality of the working relationship. This does not mean formality is absent, but it does mean that performance and loyalty are built on a foundation of personal connection. Correcting someone publicly damages dignity and the relationship in equal measure. Feedback should always be given privately.

South Africa's diversity across race, ethnicity, language, and history means there is no single management culture. Afrikaner colleagues may expect a more direct, structured style. Black African colleagues are more likely to respond to a relational, Ubuntu-influenced approach. Both are legitimate, and calibrating to the individual rather than assuming one style fits all is the practical response.

The country's history of inequality means trust between groups can be fragile. Consistency and visible fairness matter more here than in many other contexts. Perceived favoritism, even when unintentional, can erode authority quickly.
