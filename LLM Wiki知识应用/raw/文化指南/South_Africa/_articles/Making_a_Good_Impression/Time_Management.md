# Time Management

## Making a Good Impression

Time management in South Africa is shaped by infrastructure realities that affect people unevenly. Commute times in Gauteng, the economic hub, vary widely depending on where someone lives and what transport options are available. The minibus taxi system is a vital part of how millions of people move through cities, particularly where formal public transport is limited, but it also brings unpredictability. The same meeting time can feel straightforward for one colleague and require hours of planning for another. These differences reflect spatial inequality, not personal preference.

Energy disruption has become a routine part of daily life. Load shedding, South Africa's ongoing electricity rationing, has shaped how people schedule tasks and manage reliability. Work that depends on connectivity or power cannot always follow a fixed timeline. Teams learn to build backup plans into critical deliverables and avoid assuming that systems will hold. This context means that flexibility is often a practical necessity, not a cultural preference for informality.

Punctuality expectations depend on sector and setting. Corporate environments, particularly those linked to global markets, tend to emphasize timeliness and structured schedules. But even in these contexts, colleagues often allow for buffer time when coordinating across locations or communities. The phrase "now-now" signals high priority, while "just now" means it will happen later. Both reflect a system where urgency is communicated through language, not just the clock.

If you are coordinating across teams, confirm transport assumptions and offer time windows rather than fixed slots. Start meetings with a settling-in period and keep agendas realistic. Where the same group is repeatedly traveling long distances, consider rotating locations or offering hybrid options.
