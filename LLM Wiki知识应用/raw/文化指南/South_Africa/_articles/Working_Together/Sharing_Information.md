# Sharing Information

## Working Together

South Africa's multilingual, historically divided landscape has made information sharing a question of both access and fairness. With 11 official languages and uneven infrastructure, who can follow what is being said is never just a logistical matter. It touches questions of inclusion and respect. Many organizations now recognize that withholding information can replay old patterns of exclusion, and there is genuine effort to share more openly across organizational levels. But trust still travels most reliably through people rather than systems. Family networks, church communities, union channels, and WhatsApp groups often move information faster and more credibly than formal announcements, especially where official channels feel distant or unreliable.

Transparency counts. The legacy of the Truth and Reconciliation Commission, however contested its outcomes, left a public expectation that disclosure and accountability matter. In the workplace, that means colleagues respond better to straight talk about constraints than to spin or evasion. If something has gone wrong or a decision has changed, correcting the record clearly builds more trust than defending the original version. At the same time, infrastructure realities such as load shedding and patchy connectivity mean that a single email rarely reaches everyone. Teams often build in redundancy, sharing key updates through multiple formats or checking in verbally to confirm understanding.

Language differences also shape how information lands. In meetings where English is the common language but not everyone's first, some colleagues may hold back questions to avoid drawing attention to their accent or comprehension. Inviting clarification without singling anyone out makes it easier for the whole group to engage. Plain summaries, follow-up messages, and documents that work offline all help ensure that information genuinely reaches the people who need it.
