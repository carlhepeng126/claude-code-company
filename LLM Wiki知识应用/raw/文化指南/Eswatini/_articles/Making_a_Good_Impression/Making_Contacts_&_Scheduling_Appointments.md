# Making Contacts & Scheduling Appointments

## Making a Good Impression

Building a business contact in Eswatini begins with respect for hierarchy and protocol. The monarchy remains central to public life, and this reverence for structure extends into professional settings. Cold approaches rarely lead anywhere. A credible introduction from someone already known to your prospective contact opens the door. That person vouches for you, and their standing lends you credibility before you've said a word. Without that link, you may find polite acknowledgment but little forward movement.

Informal channels often prove more effective than formal ones. A conversation at a community event, a connection made through a church group, or a mutual acquaintance at a social gathering can lead to a meeting where a direct email might not. Urban centers like Mbabane may see some openness to direct professional outreach, particularly among younger professionals or those working in multinational firms, but even there, a warm introduction shortens the process considerably.

When scheduling meetings, allow more lead time than you might expect. Book appointments at least a week or two in advance, and confirm the day before. Flexibility matters. Meetings may start late, run longer than planned, or be rescheduled at short notice. This reflects competing priorities and a relational rather than strictly time-driven approach to the day. Building in buffer time between appointments acknowledges this reality and avoids the frustration of a disrupted schedule.

A colleague accustomed to precise timing may read delays as disorganization. From the Swazi perspective, however, a colleague who rushes from one meeting to the next without allowing time for conversation may seem transactional or disinterested in the relationship itself.
