# Business Meeting Culture

## Meetings & Presentations

Meetings in Eswatini move at the pace of relationship building, and that relationship often begins with the most senior person in the room. Hierarchy shapes who speaks, when they speak, and how decisions are made. The eldest or most senior figure will typically lead the conversation and expect deference from junior colleagues. If you arrive with a team, ensure your most senior representative is present and visible. A mismatch in seniority can stall progress or suggest the partnership lacks real commitment.

Expect meetings to open with greetings that acknowledge respect and status. Rushing straight into business can come across as dismissive. Allow time for introductions and some initial exchange before the agenda begins. Once underway, communication tends to be polite and indirect, especially when disagreement or difficulty arises. Colleagues may avoid open contradiction of a senior person or a guest, preferring instead to signal concern through silence, hesitation, or requests to revisit a point later. If you sense a shift in tone or a sudden quietness, pause and invite clarification rather than pressing ahead.

Decisions are rarely made in the meeting itself. Senior figures may need time to consult others or reflect privately before committing. Pressing for an immediate answer can feel disrespectful and may result in a polite deflection rather than a genuine response. Follow up with patience, and confirm next steps in writing without assuming verbal agreement constitutes finalized commitment.
