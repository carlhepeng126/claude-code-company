# Sharing Information

## Working Together

Information flows carefully in Eswatini, shaped by hierarchies rooted in the monarchy and by the strength of personal networks. Formal structures command deep respect, and sharing sensitive information outside trusted circles can feel risky. People rely on relationships they know well, especially when uncertainty or potential criticism is involved. In workplaces, this often means that details stay within small groups until trust has been clearly established.

Hierarchy influences who speaks and when. Junior colleagues may hold back information in settings where senior figures are present, waiting to be invited or deferring to those with authority. This reflects cultural norms around respect and position, not reluctance to contribute. A manager from a context where information is shared freely across levels might read this as passive or disengaged. In return, a Swazi colleague might view that same openness as premature or even disrespectful if the relationship has not yet been built.

Creating conditions for open exchange takes time and intentional relationship-building. Informal conversations outside formal meetings, consistent follow-through on commitments, and showing genuine interest in colleagues as people all help. Once trust is in place, information becomes more accessible and collaboration deepens. Pushing for transparency too early, or bypassing established reporting lines, can have the opposite effect and close channels that might otherwise have opened naturally.
