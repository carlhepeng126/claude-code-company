# Delegating & Supervising

## Managing Relationships

Authority in Oman flows from the top, shaped by a dual inheritance: Islam, which entered in the seventh century and runs through every layer of life, and tribal tradition, which defines identity and respect. The Ibadhi school of Islam dominant here teaches that leaders earn their standing through wisdom and conduct, not title alone. Yet Sultan Qaboos's transformation of the country from 1970 onward set a parallel template: power concentrated at the apex, decisions made there, and modernization directed downward. This blend means that while personal integrity and earned respect still matter, the gap between manager and subordinate is structural and carefully maintained.

In practice, major decisions remain with the most senior figure. Middle managers rarely hold decision-making power, and employees lower down expect none. Delegation tends to cover execution rather than judgment, and subordinates will not question direction or act without instruction. This reflects both deference and the understanding that relationships underpin business. Trust is built person to person, often drawing on tribal or personal connections, and the formal structure follows from that foundation. A manager from a culture where delegation includes authority and initiative may see this as inefficiency. An Omani colleague may read the push for autonomous action as bypassing the relationship and risking disrespect to the leader.

Supervision should be regular and private. Employees will not surface concerns on their own, so framing check-ins as consultation rather than control opens the door. Criticism must always be delivered one to one, with full attention to honor and standing. Public correction, however mild, undermines dignity and ruptures the relationship. Accept hospitality, shared coffee, and extended conversation as part of the professional bond, and plan around prayer times and Islamic observance as structuring realities of the day, not interruptions to it.
