# Feedback and Coaching

## Managing Relationships

In Oman, public embarrassment cuts deep. Criticizing someone sharply in front of others can rupture trust permanently, because the judgment extends beyond their professional competence. It can be read as disrespecting not just the individual but their family, their tribe, and the honor they represent in the workplace. Feedback that might be seen elsewhere as direct or efficient is experienced here as an attack on dignity. This means that difficult conversations need to happen behind closed doors, framed in language that acknowledges the person's experience and good intentions, even when the work itself needs improvement.

The way feedback lands also depends on generation. Younger Omanis, especially those who have studied or worked abroad, may actively seek structured development conversations and clear coaching frameworks. They are often comfortable with formal appraisals and expect regular input on how they are progressing. Their older colleagues, by contrast, may find the same process uncomfortably judgmental or foreign, interpreting it as a sign of mistrust rather than support. If you are managing across this divide, it helps to ask each person how they prefer to receive guidance and agree on development steps that make sense to them, rather than applying one system across the board.

When an expat offers critique, particularly a junior one challenging the work of a senior Omani, the stakes are even higher. Status matters, and seniority is respected. A manager from a culture where feedback flows freely in all directions might frame a correction as a straightforward exchange of ideas. An Omani colleague may experience it as a public slight that undermines their standing. The reverse also applies: when Omani colleagues coach expatriates on local norms or missteps, responding seriously and showing willingness to learn makes your own feedback far easier to accept. It signals that guidance flows both ways, and that respect is mutual.
