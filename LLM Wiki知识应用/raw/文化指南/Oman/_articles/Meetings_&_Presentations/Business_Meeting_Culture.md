# Business Meeting Culture

## Meetings & Presentations

Hierarchy expresses itself quietly in Omani meetings. The person who sits closest to the host usually holds the real authority, and those in supporting roles may sit slightly apart. Junior or younger participants often speak carefully or stay silent until invited, allowing the most senior figure to weigh in last. These patterns come directly from the *majlis* tradition, the customary gathering space where elders speak with deference and decisions are reached through collective listening. That same logic still shapes corporate meetings today, even when the setting is a modern office. Coffee is served in a particular order, attentive silence follows an elder's view, and seniority is observed without anyone announcing it. If you pitch to the person doing most of the talking, you may be addressing the wrong person. Ask a local colleague beforehand who the actual decision-holder is and direct your proposal accordingly.

Indirect language is a form of courtesy, and the softer the phrasing, the clearer the reservation. "We will study this further" or "it may be challenging" can mean the proposal will not move forward. Omanis avoid blunt refusals, especially early in a relationship, so you need to read the tone and the follow-through. Low conversations in Arabic during or after your presentation are not sideline distractions. They are part of the decision process, where Omanis test your idea against local realities related to regulation, reputation, or relationships that may not translate easily into English.

Wide-ranging conversation is how trust and shared understanding are built. Meetings rarely follow a fixed agenda in sequence, and attempts to keep strictly to time or structure can feel impatient or transactional. A colleague accustomed to linear agendas and quick decisions may interpret this as inefficiency, while Omanis may read fast-paced meetings as shallow and overly task-focused. Allow time for ritual elements like greetings and coffee service. Rushing through them undermines the relationship you are there to build.

After the meeting, debrief with an Omani colleague. What felt like agreement may have been polite acknowledgment, and phrases like "inshallah" require careful interpretation. This is where real understanding develops, not in the meeting room itself.
