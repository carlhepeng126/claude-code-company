# Business Lunch & Dinner

## Meetings & Presentations

Abundance is a form of respect in Oman. Hosts order more food than the group will eat because this shows the guest has been properly honored. Traditional meals may involve large shared trays of rice and meat or fish, sometimes eaten by hand or with a spoon, and people are used to sharing food and space without the Western convention of individual plating. If you're unfamiliar with communal eating, watch others and follow their lead rather than stepping back. Refusing to share on hygiene grounds, unless you have a genuine health reason, can suggest discomfort with closeness that quietly undermines rapport.

Trying a little of everything and praising a few dishes openly is more valued than finishing your plate. Asking where a dish comes from, particularly if it has a regional root like Dhofar, affirms both effort and regional identity. Compliments on food are heard as compliments on the person and the place they represent. 

Conversation usually begins with family, travel, weather or local news, and moves gradually toward business. Launching straight into detailed negotiation over the appetizer can feel abrupt and transactional. Let your host set the pace and signal when it's appropriate to shift topics. Be ready with questions or stories that sit comfortably between personal and professional, such as education, youth employment or regional development. These topics build common ground without forcing a premature close.

If you're hosting, arrange high-quality halal food with a mix of local dishes and some simpler, recognizable options. Extremes in either direction, overly unfamiliar or aggressively plain, can leave guests uneasy. Balance shows you've thought about their comfort without flattening the occasion.
