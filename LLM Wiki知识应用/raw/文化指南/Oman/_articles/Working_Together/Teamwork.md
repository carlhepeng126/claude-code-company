# Teamwork

## Working Together

Omani team members often manage layered loyalties that extend well beyond the organization. A colleague may balance commitment to the company, to their manager, to family, to tribe, to region and to nation. An instruction that appears straightforward from an organizational perspective can create quiet inner tension if it conflicts with a tribal or family obligation. When that happens, the conflict rarely surfaces openly. A manager from a culture where professional and personal spheres are more clearly separated may interpret hesitation or delay as lack of engagement, while the Omani colleague may be working to honor competing commitments without causing offense in either direction.

Most Omani workplaces operate on an implicit division of labor between citizens and expatriates. Expats often provide technical depth and global exposure. Omanis are expected to be the long-term stewards of the institution. This division shapes how teams function. When building project teams, ask who will still be in the organization in ten years, and design leadership development and decision-making authority accordingly. Teams that treat expats as the only real experts and Omanis as headcount risk missing the context intelligence and relationship strategy that Omani colleagues bring.

Much of what holds a team together in Oman happens out of view. Omani staff frequently handle relational work that never appears in a job description: smoothing issues with local authorities, calming relatives, interpreting ambiguous directives, aligning with tribal elders. This labor is essential but easy to overlook. If only the colleague who writes the slide deck or closes the deal gets recognized, the work that made those outcomes possible goes unseen. Make that invisible contribution visible in how you plan timelines, allocate credit and promote. Avoid asking Omani colleagues to publicly contradict a relative or respected elder. Find ways to resolve problems without forcing that choice.
