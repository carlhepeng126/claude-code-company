# Teamwork

## Working Together

Teamwork in Iceland draws from a culture shaped by voluntary rescue teams (ICE-SAR), where ordinary people train together, drop everything when the call comes, and trust each other with their lives in storms and avalanches. That reflex to show up when things go wrong, regardless of job title, runs through many workplaces. When a crisis hits or a deadline looms, colleagues step in quietly and get on with it. If you lead a team here, recognize that stepping up when needed is a core local virtue, and acknowledge it explicitly.

Teams are often small and responsibilities overlap widely. A single team in a town might handle tourist safety briefings, IT, logistics and local outreach because there are fewer departments to hand things off to. This means people hold broad knowledge and expect to be consulted widely before processes change. Assume that the person you are speaking to knows more than their job title suggests, and involve them early.

Reflection after intense periods tends to happen informally, over coffee or in swimming pools, rather than only in structured debrief sessions. People process collectively but casually. A manager from a culture that relies on formal review meetings might wait for the scheduled session to gather feedback, while Icelandic colleagues have already worked through what happened in the kitchen or the hot tub. Build in time and space for these informal moments alongside any formal review, and listen to what surfaces there.
