# Decision-Making

## Working Together

In Iceland, decisions happen within a culture where social proximity shapes accountability. Leaders know their choices may be questioned casually by neighbours in the local pool, at the store, or at a school event. This visibility means decision-makers think carefully about how a call will sound if explained face-to-face in everyday language. A decision that cannot be defended simply and honestly to someone outside the company is harder to stand behind. If you are leading a project, frame your reasoning in a way that would hold up in a brief, direct conversation, not just in a boardroom presentation.

Leaders draw on both data and local instinct, particularly the sense of whether something fits the place. Evidence matters, but so does gut feeling about whether a project belongs in a particular valley, bay, or community. If locals sense a mismatch, formal approval may not translate into social acceptance, which can slow or derail implementation. Engage stakeholders early to test whether your plan feels right for the context, not just whether it meets technical criteria.

Iceland's geology and global exposure mean that waiting for perfect stability is unrealistic. Leaders balance eruptions, earthquakes, shifting markets, and changing political conditions by making clear decisions and preparing fallback options. Present not only your preferred route but also credible alternatives for when conditions shift. A colleague from a culture where decisions are made quickly and definitively may view this as hedging or indecision. From an Icelandic perspective, offering a single path with no backup in an unpredictable environment can look rigid or naive.

Consensus building is standard, and decisions can shift if new evidence emerges or if someone raises a point that was overlooked. Once a decision is finalized, however, action follows quickly. Do not mistake the time spent gathering input for slowness in execution.
