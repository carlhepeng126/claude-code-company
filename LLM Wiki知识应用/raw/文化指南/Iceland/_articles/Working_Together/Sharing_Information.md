# Sharing Information

## Working Together

Information flows freely in Icelandic workplaces because flat hierarchies and a small, tightly networked society make secrecy impractical and culturally suspect. Managers communicate directly with whoever holds the most relevant knowledge, bypassing formal reporting lines without hesitation. Withholding information to gain leverage or protect status will not make you look strategic. It will make you look dishonest and unsuitable as a partner, because you have wasted everyone's time. Decisions rest on hard facts, so colleagues expect you to share what you know early and completely. If you are negotiating, a strong argument and clear data will move things forward. Holding back details to reveal later will undermine trust more than it strengthens your position.

Iceland's civil protection culture sets the standard. When storms or volcanic eruptions threaten, authorities issue clear, timely alerts that tell people exactly what is happening and what to do next. That same directness transfers into workplace communication. When trouble is brewing inside an organization, people expect leaders to communicate early, plainly, and without spin, much like a weather warning. Colleagues from settings where problems are softened or withheld until solutions are ready may find this bluntness unsettling. For Icelanders, vague reassurances without practical detail feel evasive and untrustworthy. They want to know what you know, when you know it.

During major national events, the national broadcaster and shared online platforms create a sense that everyone is following the same storyline. Internal communications that ignore these external realities can feel disconnected or tone-deaf. Acknowledge the eruption, the storm, or the closures in your messages, and explain how the organization is adapting. Pretending nothing is happening outside the office will not help you maintain focus. It will make you seem out of touch.

Some workplace topics carry nuance that English cannot fully capture. Local politics, village relationships, and clan-like histories often make more sense in Icelandic. Even in international firms, staff may switch languages to clarify what a message really means. If your communications go out only in English, expect informal translation and interpretation to happen afterward. Make room for that discussion rather than assuming your message has landed as written.
