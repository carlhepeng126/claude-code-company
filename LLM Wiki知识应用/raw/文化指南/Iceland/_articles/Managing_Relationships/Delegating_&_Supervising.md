# Delegating & Supervising

## Managing Relationships

Iceland's exceptionally flat hierarchy reflects a cultural logic rooted in its founding: the Althing, established in 930 AD, gave every free man an equal voice. That democratic instinct endures in the workplace today. Managers are addressed by first name, sit among their teams, and earn respect through competence and contribution rather than through title. Visible status markers — separate offices, formal registers, deference rituals — are largely absent. What earns credibility is whether you know what you are talking about. For managers from cultures where authority is more clearly defined by position, this can feel ambiguous. It helps to establish your expertise early through what you contribute, not what your title permits.

Autonomy is both expected and genuine. Icelanders value innovation and have little tolerance for rigid procedure applied for its own sake. Employees expect to tackle problems their own way, and micromanagement will be read as a lack of trust in their competence. Managers delegate quickly and often assign tasks on the spot during meetings that include representatives from across departments. Set direction clearly, then step back. If you need frequent check-ins or detailed progress reports, explain the business reason rather than treating it as standard supervision. Otherwise, it can feel like oversight where none is needed.

The informal, collegial tone does not mean accountability is soft. Iceland has more of a hire-and-fire mentality than other Nordic countries, and individuals are expected to take responsibility for bad decisions. Supervisors are also expected to give constructive criticism directly, and to receive it in return. Icelanders appreciate honesty and will critique one another's work in ways that can sound blunt to those unused to it. The criticism is not meant to be personal, and it is usually delivered without aggression, but it is direct. A colleague from a culture where feedback is softened or delivered privately may hear this as unusually sharp. From the Icelandic side, cushioning criticism or avoiding it altogether can seem evasive or unclear.

The small scale of Icelandic society means that reputations travel quickly and relationships extend well beyond any single project. How you behave, how you treat people, and how you handle disagreement are noticed and remembered across professional networks. Be direct, but also remain collegial. The flat structure can also mask real decision-making authority, so take time to understand who actually holds accountability, as this may not be obvious from titles alone.
