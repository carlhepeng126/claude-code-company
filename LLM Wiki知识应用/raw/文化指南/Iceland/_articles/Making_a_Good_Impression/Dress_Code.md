# Dress Code

## Making a Good Impression

Function always wins in Iceland, and the country's weather ensures it. A business day might begin with a harbour visit in horizontal rain and end in a downtown meeting room, which is why senior leaders own high-quality waterproof shells and good boots alongside their smarter clothes. A neat sweater, clean jeans and weather-appropriate footwear often feel more appropriate than a suit that won't survive a five-minute walk outdoors. The question is not whether you look polished, but whether you look prepared.

This practicality reflects a deeper cultural logic rooted in Nordic egalitarianism. Quiet quality registers more than visible branding. Clean, well-fitted, functional clothes make a better impression than luxury labels or flashy accessories, which tend to jar against the culture's preference for understatement. A good Gore-Tex jacket carries more credibility than a delicate coat that signals unfamiliarity with the environment. What someone wears is read as a statement about competence and belonging, not status.

That said, casual does not mean careless. Smart casual is the baseline in most professional settings, and the expectation is that you arrive looking intentional. For formal occasions, dress accordingly, but even here the emphasis is on being well put-together rather than overdone. Pack layers, a decent outer shell, sturdy footwear, and one smarter outfit for evening events or presentations. Your wardrobe should accommodate meeting rooms and lava fields in the same day, and colleagues will notice if it can't.
