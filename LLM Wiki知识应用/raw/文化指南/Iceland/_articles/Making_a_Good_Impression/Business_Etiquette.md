# Business Etiquette

## Making a Good Impression

Icelandic business culture has a relaxed surface but a watchful core. First names come immediately, dress codes lean casual, and small talk about the weather is standard. But this informality should not be mistaken for a lack of rigor. Competence, follow-through, and realistic plans matter far more than polished presentation. If you promise something you cannot deliver, people will not challenge you in the moment, but they will remember. That memory outlasts the meeting.

Communication follows what Icelanders sometimes call a saga style: understated, dry, and stripped of decoration. A calm "this might be difficult" can mean the plan will not work at all. Disagreement rarely arrives as a speech or confrontation. Instead, it shows up as a raised eyebrow, a short silence, or a plain statement of fact. A colleague from a culture where concerns are expressed through lengthy justification might read this brevity as coldness or lack of engagement. An Icelander, in turn, might find extended explanations unnecessary or even a sign that the speaker lacks confidence in their own point.

Emotional restraint runs deep. Meetings stay level in tone. Passionate appeals or visible frustration can feel out of step in a culture shaped by long winters and unpredictable weather. Conviction is more effectively shown through preparation and calm realism than through displays of enthusiasm. Listen closely to what is not said and to pauses, which often hold as much weight as the words around them.

Gift giving plays a modest role. Small, tasteful items are appreciated at first meetings, but anything expensive may be read as inappropriate. If invited to someone's home, bring imported wine or flowers, but do not expect elaborate thanks. Icelanders open gifts quietly and gesture with restraint.
