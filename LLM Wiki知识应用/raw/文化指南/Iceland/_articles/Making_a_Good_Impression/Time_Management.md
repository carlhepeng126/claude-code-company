# Time Management

## Making a Good Impression

Iceland runs on two clocks. The first is Nordic punctuality: turning up when you said you would, replying when promised, and treating meeting times as fixed points. The second is the weather forecast. People check vedur.is and road alerts constantly, and leaving early to beat a storm is seen as responsible planning, not unreliability. Both clocks run at once, and managing them well earns trust.

When a red weather warning hits, schools close, buses stop, and flights cancel. Everyone from government to small startups accepts that the storm is the boss. What people remember later is how you handled the disruption: whether you communicated early, put safety first, and rescheduled smoothly. If you treat bad weather as a freak event or express frustration at delays, you will be seen as someone who does not understand the environment. Build weather contingencies into timelines upfront, and Icelanders will take you more seriously.

Seasonal rhythms also shape the working day. In deep winter, people guard scarce daylight for travel, site visits, and outdoor tasks, shifting admin and online meetings into the dark hours. In summer, the long light encourages extended field days and late-evening work on farms, boats, or projects. If you are suggesting schedules from outside Iceland, ask directly how a timeline works with daylight and weather where they are. That question alone shows real cultural understanding and will be met with respect.
