# Business Meeting Culture

## Meetings & Presentations

Meetings in Iceland often feel like a modern echo of the Althing at Þingvellir, where farmers, fishers and scholars gathered in a circle and every voice counted. Today, people still prefer to sit around a single table, use first names from the start, and give each participant a turn to speak. Long presentations by one person jar against that instinct. Expect colleagues to jump in with questions or challenges regardless of seniority, and build time for everyone to contribute rather than controlling the floor yourself.

Many of the people in the room wear multiple hats. A manager may also serve on a municipal committee, volunteer with the local rescue team, and help organize a festival. When they weigh your proposal, they think through all those roles at once: business case, yes, but also community impact, safety, and reputation. Address those layers directly. A pitch that covers ROI but ignores local or environmental implications will feel incomplete, even if no one says so out loud.

Icelanders make decisions with incomplete information and adjust as conditions shift. A meeting may close with "we go with option B, and if the river floods we pivot to C" rather than waiting for perfect data. If you frame your recommendation with clear contingency paths built in, you signal that you understand the island's realities. Flexibility here is competence, and a willingness to decide and adapt beats hesitation every time.
