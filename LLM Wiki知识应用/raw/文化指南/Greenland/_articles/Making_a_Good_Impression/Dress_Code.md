# Dress Code

## Making a Good Impression

Professional dress in Greenland reflects practical realities shaped by climate, economy, and a small, interconnected society where most people will encounter each other repeatedly. In public sector roles and meetings with government officials, business casual is the norm: neat trousers or jeans, shirts or blouses, and practical footwear. Suits are rare and can feel mismatched to the setting. The emphasis is on being clean, well-maintained, and ready for a day that might include both office work and moving between buildings in unpredictable weather. Dressing too formally can create distance rather than professionalism, particularly in communities outside Nuuk.

In sectors tied to natural resources, fishing, or outdoor operations, clothing becomes more functional. Workwear, layered outerwear, and weather-appropriate gear are standard and respected. What reads as casual in a corporate setting elsewhere is simply sensible here. A colleague from a culture where formal business attire signals seriousness might interpret this as informality, while a Greenlandic counterpart may see overdressing as a lack of awareness about how work actually gets done in the local environment.

Warmth and practicality take priority across settings. Offices and meeting rooms can be drafty, and outdoor transitions are frequent. Layering is common, and no one expects you to remove your sweater for the sake of appearance. What works is what keeps you comfortable and functional, and that approach is quietly appreciated.
