# Effective Presentations

## Meetings & Presentations

Presentations in Greenland succeed when they feel like conversations rather than performances. The cultural preference for consensus and collective input means that your audience will be listening for openness, not certainty. People expect to contribute, refine, and question, so leave room for that exchange. A polished, tightly scripted delivery can feel closed off. If your presentation style assumes buy-in follows from clear logic and confident messaging, you may find that the discussion afterward matters more than the content you prepared.

Pace yourself. Greenlandic communication often moves at a slower rhythm than in cultures where efficiency is prized above all else. Silence between points reflects thought, not disengagement. If you rush to fill pauses or move quickly through slides, you may unintentionally cut off the very dialogue your audience needs to reach shared understanding. A colleague from a culture where decisions are made quickly may read this slower tempo as indecision or lack of urgency. From a Greenlandic perspective, that same rapid pace may feel pushy or dismissive of the group's need to think together.

Keep materials simple and visual. Dense slides or technical jargon can create distance, especially in smaller communities where English may not be everyone's first working language. If you're presenting in a mixed setting, brief any interpreter in advance and check in as you go. People may step out, take calls, or join partway through. This reflects the informal, community-oriented nature of many professional settings, not a lack of interest.

Follow-up conversations often reveal more than the formal session. Questions that might feel too direct or personal in front of the group may surface privately afterward. Treat these as part of the process, not as signs that something went wrong.
