# Feedback and Coaching

## Managing Relationships

Greenland's small, interconnected communities mean that feedback is rarely delivered in isolation. Relationships are layered and long-term, and people are often related by blood, marriage, or shared history. A critical comment made too directly can ripple beyond the workplace, affecting family ties and community standing. Managers tend to soften feedback through indirect language or by raising concerns in a way that allows the other person to reflect without losing face. This approach protects the relationship while still opening the door to change.

Coaching conversations often happen informally, outside structured performance reviews. A manager might raise an issue during a shared coffee break or through a casual conversation after hours, creating a setting where the exchange feels less confrontational. Greenlandic communication style values understatement, so what might sound vague or incomplete to someone from a culture that prefers explicit feedback is often understood as a respectful way to guide behavior without imposing hierarchy.

Public praise is welcomed, especially when it acknowledges the team or group effort rather than singling out one person. Individual recognition can feel uncomfortable in a culture where collective harmony is prized. Negative feedback, by contrast, should always be delivered privately and framed gently. A colleague from a culture that values directness may interpret this indirectness as avoidance, while a Greenlandic colleague may view direct criticism as unnecessarily harsh and damaging to trust.

Younger professionals and those working in larger towns or international organizations may be more accustomed to structured feedback systems, but the preference for relational, low-key communication remains strong across generations.
