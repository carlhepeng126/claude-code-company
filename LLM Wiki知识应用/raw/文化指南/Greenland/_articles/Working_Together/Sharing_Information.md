# Sharing Information

## Working Together

In Greenland, information sharing tends to happen through established relationships rather than formal channels. The small population means that most people know each other directly or through a few connections, and trust develops through sustained personal contact. Someone new to a workplace or project should expect that full transparency will come once they have shown themselves to be reliable and genuinely interested in collaborative outcomes. Greenlanders are generally warm and welcoming, but they take time to assess whether someone will stay engaged or disappear after a short assignment, a pattern that has shaped expectations over decades of transient outsiders.

Within organizations, knowledge often stays with individuals or small teams rather than being documented or broadcast widely. This reflects both the oral tradition that remains strong in Greenlandic culture and a practical reality where informal conversation is often more efficient than written systems. A colleague from a culture where processes are heavily documented may find this frustrating and wonder why information is not proactively shared. From a Greenlandic perspective, the same colleague's reliance on emails and procedures may seem impersonal or overly rigid. Building direct relationships with key people, asking questions in person, and participating in informal settings like coffee breaks or community events will open access to the information you need far more effectively than expecting it to arrive through official updates.

Younger professionals and those working in larger public or international organizations are increasingly comfortable with structured knowledge-sharing tools, but the instinct to rely on trusted contacts remains strong. If you need information, invest time in the relationship first. Show up consistently, follow through on what you promise, and demonstrate that you value local knowledge rather than assuming outside expertise is superior.
