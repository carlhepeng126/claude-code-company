# Decision-Making

## Working Together

Decisions in Greenland often unfold slowly, shaped by a deep preference for consultation and consensus. Authority is recognized, but it does not operate in isolation. Leaders consult widely across personal and professional networks before finalizing a decision, seeking input from those with relevant knowledge or those whose support will be needed during implementation. This reflects a belief that decisions work better when people affected by them have been heard, and when social cohesion is maintained alongside operational goals.

This consultative approach means that moving from discussion to conclusion can take longer than colleagues from cultures with faster decision cycles may expect. A manager from a setting where leaders are expected to decide quickly and independently might read the extended consultation as indecisiveness or lack of leadership. From a Greenlandic perspective, the same manager's speed might appear rushed or disconnected, as though key voices were bypassed in favor of efficiency.

Authority still holds. Senior figures and those with experience have greater influence, and their approval is often necessary before action proceeds. But that authority is exercised alongside, not instead of, consultation. Leaders balance their formal role with a relational one, using trusted networks to inform and legitimize decisions while retaining the final say.

Flexibility is also present. Plans and timelines may shift as new information emerges or as social and family considerations come into view. What looks like inconsistency is often adaptation, adjusting the decision to fit both the task and the people involved.
