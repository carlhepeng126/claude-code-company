# Effective Presentations

## Meetings & Presentations

A well-reasoned argument earns respect in the Republic of Congo. Brazzaville served for decades as the educational capital of French Equatorial Africa, and that legacy shows up in professional life today. Congolese colleagues, especially in the capital, bring intellectual confidence to the table. Slick visuals alone won't land if the underlying logic is weak. Structure your presentation clearly, support claims with evidence, and demonstrate expertise without overselling. Coming across as experienced and thoughtful counts for more than rehearsed delivery.

Oral tradition runs deep. Bakongo and other Bantu cultures value proverbs, storytelling, and indirect analogy as sophisticated ways of making a point. If your counterpart uses a proverb or tells a story during discussion, treat it as a considered argument, not a digression. It often frames a position while maintaining harmony. In return, colleagues may find presentations built entirely on bullet points and rapid-fire data to lack warmth and cultural register. Framing your proposal around long-term mutual benefit, job creation, and local investment fits the cultural rhythm far better than a short-term pitch focused solely on profit.

Hierarchy shapes who speaks and when. Acknowledge the most senior person in the room early, whether in your opening remarks or by briefly noting their presence and role. This sets the right tone and shows cultural awareness. Questions may come mostly at the end, and some colleagues may prefer to speak privately afterward rather than challenge or probe in front of others.

All materials should be in French. Prepare hard copies for the audience, not just memory sticks.
