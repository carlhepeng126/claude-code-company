# Teamwork

## Working Together

Teamwork in the Republic of Congo is rooted in the Bantu communal worldview, where collective wellbeing and community obligation run deep. This is genuine cultural logic, reflected in the phrase "it takes a village." Teams function best when there is shared purpose and when leaders demonstrate both authority and care. Hierarchy is taken seriously, and teams expect direction from someone they respect. A manager who sets clear, achievable targets and monitors progress closely will see better follow-through than one who offers broad autonomy or vague incentives. Congolese colleagues may agree to ambitious goals to preserve face or avoid confrontation, so learning to read between affirmation and genuine commitment becomes essential. Asking follow-up questions and creating space for quiet clarification helps surface any misalignment early.

Beneath the surface, ethnic and regional networks shape trust and cohesion in ways that may not be immediately visible to outsiders. Colleagues from the same clan or region often share pre-existing bonds that influence how information flows and where loyalty sits. The civil wars deepened tensions between northern Mbochi and southern Lari and Bakongo communities, particularly in Brazzaville, and those divisions can linger within mixed teams even when outward behavior appears professional. A team that looks unified on an org chart may have fault lines that only reveal themselves under pressure or when resources are limited.

Building genuine cohesion across these lines requires investing in personal relationships, not just shared deliverables. Managers who take time to understand individual backgrounds, show interest in people's lives beyond work, and remain alert to inter-group dynamics will be better positioned to address tension before it undermines performance. Foreign managers are often seen as valuable sources of knowledge and experience, so mentoring is welcomed, provided it is offered with respect and without condescension. Promoting someone or giving them additional responsibility works best when the whole team understands and supports the decision, reinforcing fairness and avoiding the perception of favoritism along ethnic lines.
