# Decision-Making

## Working Together

Decisions in the Republic of Congo are made at the top, but rarely in isolation. The most senior person holds the final say, but will often consult a trusted circle first. This group might include family members, close advisors, or colleagues whose judgment has been proven over time. The goal is less about gathering data and more about confirming relational fit and managing risk. Religious belief and personal loyalty can weigh as heavily as operational logic. A proposal that benefits a contact or strengthens a key relationship may move forward faster than one that doesn't.

The pace is measured, and pushing for speed can backfire. Many senior leaders came of age under a one-party system where decisions were handed down without explanation. The shift toward more consultative approaches has made the process slower, but also more relationally grounded. Risk aversion runs deep. If your proposal involves significant change, expect the timeline to stretch. Going around someone to escalate a decision will cause them to lose face and weaken your own position in the process.

Junior staff are not expected to show initiative or make decisions independently. They will look to their manager for direction, even on matters that might be delegated elsewhere. For a colleague from a culture where junior staff are encouraged to act autonomously, this can feel overly cautious. From a Congolese perspective, a junior employee making decisions without checking upward may appear reckless or disrespectful of hierarchy.

Once a decision is made, get it in writing. Agree on prices, timescales, and responsibilities, and ensure the outcome serves both parties. A one-sided result will not hold.
