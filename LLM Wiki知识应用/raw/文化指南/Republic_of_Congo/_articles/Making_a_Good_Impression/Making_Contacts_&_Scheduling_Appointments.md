# Making Contacts & Scheduling Appointments

## Making a Good Impression

In the Republic of Congo, business relationships begin with trust, and trust begins with who introduces you. Congolese professionals need to know they can rely on someone before committing time or resources. That's why a personal introduction through a shared contact opens doors far more effectively than a cold email or phone call. Your embassy, local agents, or chambers of commerce can make initial introductions that position you as credible from the outset. Without that endorsement, expect polite distance rather than engagement.

Geography also shapes how you make contact. The oil industry centers on Pointe-Noire, while government offices and embassies operate from Brazzaville. Email alone rarely secures a meeting. Instead, work with a local partner who can reach people by phone or in person and arrange appointments on your behalf. Once an appointment is set, reconfirm it by phone when you arrive in country. Schedules shift frequently, and meetings may be changed or cancelled as other priorities emerge. Building in flexibility shows you understand how things work.

Time in the Republic of Congo follows social rhythms, especially in government settings. Arrive on time yourself, but expect that officials may be delayed by unannounced visitors or more pressing matters. Meetings begin when the person is ready, and that can mean waiting. A colleague from a culture where appointments run to the minute may read this as disorganization or disrespect. From the Congolese perspective, rigid scheduling can seem mechanical and disconnected from the relational context that makes collaboration worthwhile.

Plan to travel with your local agent. Roads in Brazzaville are poorly signposted and taxis can be unreliable, so allow extra time and avoid public holidays when scheduling.
