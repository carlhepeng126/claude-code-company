# Business Etiquette

## Making a Good Impression

Greeting each person individually — by name, with questions about family and health — reflects a Bantu communal worldview in which personhood is relational. Acknowledging someone fully means recognizing that they exist through their connections, not in isolation. Rushing through this exchange to get to business communicates that the relationship doesn't matter. In meetings, allow time for each person to be seen. If you're joining a group, greet everyone present, not just the most senior figure. This recognition is foundational, and skipping it damages your standing before any formal discussion begins.

Indirectness is a social safeguard in a context shaped by civil conflict and ethnic tension. A direct refusal can carry relational and even physical risk. So a vague answer, a story that shifts responsibility, or a polite deferral are not evasiveness — they are ways of preserving dignity and avoiding confrontation. Listen for what isn't said directly. A long explanation, a deflection, or silence where you expected agreement often means no. Pressing for clarity in the moment can make things worse. Create space for follow-up in private, where a clearer answer may emerge.

Behavior is observed not just by colleagues but, in traditional Kongo belief, by ancestors. Integrity has cosmological significance. This shapes how people present themselves in professional settings: maintaining composure, avoiding open displays of error, and protecting face are not just social preferences but moral acts. A colleague may go to great lengths to avoid admitting a mistake, not out of dishonesty but out of a deeper sense that certain behaviors reflect on more than the individual. If you need to address a problem, do so privately and in a way that allows the person to maintain their dignity.

Looking away or down, especially with elders or business superiors, is a sign of deference and respect. Sustained eye contact — standard in many direct communication cultures — can read as confrontational or disrespectful here. If a colleague avoids your gaze during a difficult conversation, they are showing restraint, not discomfort or dishonesty.
