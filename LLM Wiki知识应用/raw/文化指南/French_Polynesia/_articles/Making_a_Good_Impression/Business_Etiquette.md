# Business Etiquette

## Making a Good Impression

French Polynesia's business etiquette reflects the islands' deeply relational culture, where status, respect, and collective harmony take precedence over speed or directness. Hierarchy is observed carefully. Senior figures, whether in a family business, government office, or cooperative, are greeted first and afforded visible deference. Titles are used, voices are modulated, and decisions flow through established channels. Bypassing seniority or addressing a junior colleague over the head of their manager can feel abrupt and disrespectful. This structure comes from Polynesian traditions of chiefly authority and French administrative influence, both of which continue to shape how respect is shown in professional settings.

Greetings are unhurried and personal. A handshake is common, often accompanied by direct eye contact and a warm exchange of pleasantries. Asking after family or commenting on shared acquaintances demonstrates genuine engagement, and helps establish the relational foundation needed before substantive business begins. Rushing through introductions or moving straight to an agenda can come across as impersonal. A colleague from a culture where meetings start promptly with minimal preamble might read this as inefficiency, while a Polynesian counterpart may interpret a brisk opening as coldness or a lack of investment in the relationship.

Dress reflects context and occasion. In Papeete's formal offices or when meeting government officials, business attire is expected. In outer islands or community-based organizations, smart casual clothing is more common and appropriate. Overdressing can feel out of step with the setting, while underdressing may undermine credibility. Observing what local colleagues wear offers reliable guidance.

Physical proximity and expression are relaxed. Conversations may involve light touch on the shoulder or arm, smiles come easily, and laughter is a natural part of interaction. This warmth should not be mistaken for informality in decision-making or hierarchy, which remain carefully observed.
