# Negotiating

## Working Together

Negotiations in Albania are rarely a quick, transactional exchange. Building trust with the person across the table comes before finalizing terms, and that trust develops through relationship, not just logic or price. Early meetings may spend more time on conversation and rapport than on contract details, and this groundwork shapes what becomes possible later. Moving too quickly to close a deal can leave Albanian counterparts uncertain whether the partnership will hold, particularly if they don't yet feel they know who they're working with.

The process itself may circle back on points you thought were already agreed. A concession made in one meeting might be revisited in the next, or the conversation may shift to an unrelated issue before returning to the original topic. This is not a sign of confusion or poor planning. It reflects a way of testing flexibility and ensuring that all angles have been considered before commitment. Colleagues from cultures where agreements move in a straight line may read this as inefficiency or hesitation, while Albanian negotiators may see a rigid, linear approach as overly hasty and unresponsive to new information.

Slowing down can also be strategic. It may indicate that your counterpart is consulting someone with decision-making authority, or it may signal that they are waiting for a better offer. For this reason, avoid presenting your strongest terms at the outset. Leave room for concessions that allow both sides to feel they have shaped the outcome. Bargaining is expected and understood as a normal part of reaching fair terms.

Personal gestures, such as introductions or small acts of support unrelated to the deal itself, may also appear during negotiations. These are expressions of goodwill that strengthen the relationship and are often reciprocated over time, reinforcing the partnership beyond the contract.
