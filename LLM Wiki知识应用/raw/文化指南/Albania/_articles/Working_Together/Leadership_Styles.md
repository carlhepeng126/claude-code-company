# Leadership Styles

## Working Together

Albanian leaders often combine formal authority with personal rapport. Historically, social cohesion in Albania has depended on interpersonal bonds and face-to-face trust, particularly in settings where formal institutions were weak or unreliable. This shapes how authority works today. Managers are expected to be decisive and clearly in charge, but that authority is most credible when paired with warmth, accessibility, and visible personal concern for the team. A leader who keeps their distance may struggle to build the loyalty that makes decisions stick.

Consultation happens, but often informally and with people the leader already trusts. Rather than structured team input, managers are more likely to test ideas in private conversation with one or two key colleagues. This allows for flexibility while still preserving the leader's final say. For someone used to group decision-making or transparent feedback rounds, this can feel opaque. From the other side, a manager in Albania may view broad consultation as unnecessary exposure, or as undermining the clarity people expect from leadership.

Younger managers and those with international experience may adopt more participative styles, especially in Tirana or in companies with foreign ownership. But even in these settings, the shift tends to be gradual. Leadership that feels too flat or indirect can be read as indecisive. What changes is the tone and the frequency of consultation, not the underlying expectation that someone will ultimately take responsibility and make the call.
