# Sharing Information

## Working Together

Information in Albania flows more readily through personal channels than formal systems. The legacy of communism created a lasting wariness of official structures, and trust networks built through family, school, or long-standing friendships remain the most reliable ways to access what you need to know. Colleagues often hold back in early interactions, gauging whether you can be counted on before sharing openly. This hesitation reflects lived experience, not unfriendliness. When someone senses you respect confidentiality, follow through on commitments, and treat them with genuine regard, the flow of information changes. Conversations become more direct, and details that were previously withheld come forward without prompting.

Within organizations, knowledge often stays concentrated within tight-knit teams rather than circulating across departments. This tendency reflects a cultural preference for discretion and a strong awareness of how mistakes or vulnerabilities might be used against someone. Face and dignity remain central concerns, and exposing problems publicly can feel like a personal risk. A colleague from a workplace culture where transparency is routine and information is shared widely by default may read this as secrecy or inefficiency. From the Albanian side, the expectation that everything be laid out openly, especially early on, can seem naive or intrusive, particularly if relationships have not yet had time to solidify.

Having a trusted intermediary who can vouch for you makes a tangible difference. Personal recommendations open doors that formal credentials alone do not. Building that credibility takes time, but once established, it anchors access to the informal networks where much of the real coordination happens. Demonstrating consistency, showing respect in small exchanges, and being willing to reciprocate information when appropriate all help shift the dynamic from cautious to collaborative.
