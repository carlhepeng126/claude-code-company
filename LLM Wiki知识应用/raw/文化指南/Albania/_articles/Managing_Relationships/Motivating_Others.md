# Motivating Others

## Managing Relationships

Motivation in Albania is personal and relational before it is transactional. People respond when they feel trusted by the person they work for, not just the institution behind them. A manager who takes time to understand someone's situation, who recognizes contribution in a direct and human way, will see far more engagement than one who relies on formal incentive structures alone. A word of thanks after a difficult week, or acknowledging someone's role in a successful outcome in front of colleagues, builds commitment in ways that targets and bonuses often do not.

Money matters, but rarely on its own. Financial reward works best when combined with respect, visibility, and the sense that what someone does is valued. Public recognition can be motivating, but it should feel genuine and tied to real achievement. Overly structured praise, or recognition that feels scripted, can come across as empty. Managers from cultures where performance metrics drive motivation may find that pushing hard on deadlines or outcome-based goals produces frustration rather than results. Albanians may see this as missing the point: effort should be recognized, not just output.

Albanians are often motivated by what strengthens their standing within their family and wider network. A promotion or new responsibility reflects well beyond the workplace. At the same time, family commitments take priority. Expecting someone to stay late regularly or work through a family event will erode trust, not build it. Flexibility around timing, especially when personal obligations arise, signals respect and generates loyalty in return.

Younger professionals in urban centers or international firms may be drawn to development opportunities, training, or roles that offer career progression. In smaller towns or more traditional sectors, stability and a respected position within a known organization may motivate more strongly than mobility or ambition.
