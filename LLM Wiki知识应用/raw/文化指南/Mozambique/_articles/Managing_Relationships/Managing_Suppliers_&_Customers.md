# Managing Suppliers & Customers

## Managing Relationships

In Mozambique, many commercial exchanges run through informal and semi-formal networks where reputation, repeated contact, and personal accountability take the place of full institutional frameworks. These systems are livelihood architecture, not temporary fixes. When suppliers or customers operate within these networks, continuity of relationship and proof of reliability over time often count more than a single contract or specification sheet. For outsiders working with local partners, this means investing in visible, repeated engagement. Face-to-face meetings build the trust that makes coordination possible, and distance maintained only by email or phone can quietly erode a business relationship that seemed secure on paper.

Scheduling and delivery expectations need to account for real mobility conditions. In Maputo, for example, chapas form the backbone of daily movement, and their unpredictability shapes how people think about timing and reliability. A supplier delayed by transport realities is not necessarily unreliable, they are navigating a system where delays are routine. Partners from contexts where punctuality is tightly held may read this as poor planning, while Mozambican counterparts may see rigid timelines as disconnected from how daily life actually works. Building in flexibility and checking in regularly helps both sides stay aligned without misreading intent.

In the north, the Cabo Delgado crisis has displaced communities and reshaped where suppliers, labour, and customers are located. Supply chains that depend on northern networks may experience shifts in who is available and where they are based. Stability signals matter in these contexts: showing up, following through, and adapting without withdrawing can strengthen partnerships during disruption. Along the coast, long histories of Indian Ocean trade mean that working across difference and distance is culturally familiar, and trust pathways often reflect this experience of cross-world exchange.
