# Forms of Address

## Making a Good Impression

Greeting someone properly in Mozambique often involves more than a quick exchange of names. In many communities, especially in rural areas and tightly connected urban neighborhoods, a greeting functions as a short ritual that confirms the relationship and the moment. Skipping or rushing through this can read as dismissive, because the labor of greeting is how respect becomes visible. A colleague from a context where greetings are brief may interpret this as inefficient, while a Mozambican colleague may read abbreviated greetings as a sign that the relationship has been devalued. Allow time for this exchange, and greet the most senior person in the room first.

Portuguese is the official language but not the first language for many people, which means the choice of language affects how formality and belonging are understood. Portuguese often functions as the voice of institutions and formal relationships, while closeness and everyday connection are expressed in local languages. Address people by their title and surname when speaking Portuguese, using *Senhor* (Mr.) or *Senhora* (Mrs.), or professional titles like *Doutor* if applicable. Wait to be invited before switching to first names.

Age carries moral authority in most Mozambican settings. Older people are often seen as custodians of continuity and family legitimacy, so deference reflects a commitment to social order, not individual hierarchy. In some regions, especially central and rural areas, traditional leaders such as *régulos* (local chiefs who mediate land, conflict, and community protection) hold real influence. Acknowledging these figures where relevant can shape cooperation and acceptance. In Muslim coastal communities, mosque hierarchies matter. In many Christian settings, especially Pentecostal and Zionist-style churches, pastors and prophets hold significant moral standing. Understanding who holds authority in a given context helps you address people in ways that align with how respect is structured locally.
