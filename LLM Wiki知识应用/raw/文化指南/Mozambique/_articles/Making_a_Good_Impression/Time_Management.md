# Time Management

## Making a Good Impression

Timekeeping in Mozambique follows more than one rhythm. In rural areas, agricultural cycles and seasonal weather shape when and how people commit to plans. Rainy season realities and harvest schedules can structure daily life more than the clock, so timelines that assume year-round availability may not align with lived priorities. In cities, infrastructure often constrains punctuality more than intention. The chapa system operates on passenger volume rather than fixed departure times, and urban congestion makes journey duration unpredictable. When colleagues arrive late, it may reflect genuine transport barriers rather than disregard for the meeting.

Climate events can override carefully planned schedules without warning. Cyclone Idai's landfall near Beira in 2019 demonstrates how quickly routines can collapse under extreme weather, with prolonged knock-on effects for mobility, communication, and basic services. In northern areas affected by the Cabo Delgado insurgency, insecurity has shortened planning horizons and made long-term commitments harder to sustain. These are not hypothetical risks but recurring realities that shape how people approach commitments and contingency.

Kinship and ceremonial obligations can reorganize priorities suddenly. Funerals, weddings, and initiation events often require presence and labor, particularly in communities where kin networks function as core safety systems. These responsibilities hold moral weight that makes them difficult to defer. A colleague from a setting where professional deadlines routinely override personal commitments may read this as lack of discipline. A Mozambican colleague may read rigid adherence to timelines, in the face of kinship duty or climate disruption, as tone-deaf to context. Building realistic timelines means accounting for seasonal patterns, transport constraints, and the fact that some obligations genuinely cannot be postponed.
