# Business Etiquette

## Making a Good Impression

Professional behavior in Mozambique is shaped by a history of rupture and displacement, including a civil war that tested social bonds across generations. Trust is not assumed. It builds through steadiness and follow-through, not eloquence or speed. Where social memory includes repeated breaks in continuity, what someone does over time speaks more loudly than what they promise in a first meeting. This means that reliability and repeated contact often build credibility more effectively than a polished pitch or early enthusiasm.

Tone and relational awareness also shape how people are perceived. Mozambique has long-standing cultural traditions like Chopi timbila, where mastery is collective, learned through apprenticeship, and demonstrated publicly with precision and social intelligence. These performance lineages reinforce the idea that presence, attunement to others, and relational skill are forms of competence. In professional settings, how you engage, the respect you show in conversation, and your ability to read a room can influence how seriously you are taken. A colleague who listens carefully and adjusts their approach based on what others signal is often judged more favorably than one who focuses only on technical capability.

Respectability concerns can shape what people are willing to say openly, particularly where initiation-linked moral codes remain socially influential, especially in parts of the north. These systems historically transmitted norms around adulthood, restraint, and moral reputation. Even where practices are debated or evolving, the legacy lingers in what is considered appropriate to discuss and what remains private. Business conversations may be more guarded than expected, and directness that works elsewhere can feel socially risky here.

Regional differences also affect interaction. The southern urban corridor, the central Zambezi provinces, and the northern coastal and plateau areas differ in language, religious texture, and social rhythm. What reads as warmth, deference, or distance can vary. Engaging only formal institutions may miss key legitimacy pathways in regions where traditional leadership, including régulos, still mediates social order and moral-spiritual concerns. People often navigate both formal and local authority without treating them as contradictory, and recognizing this plural system can shape how partnerships are built.
