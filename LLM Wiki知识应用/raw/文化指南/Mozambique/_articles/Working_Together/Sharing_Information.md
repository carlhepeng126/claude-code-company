# Sharing Information

## Working Together

Language choice can shape who feels included in the message. Portuguese is the language of institutions and formal authority, but many people use local Bantu languages in daily life and among trusted contacts. Switching between them is often also switching between what is perceived as official voice and belonging voice. If you are working multilingually, delivering updates or training in Portuguese alone can unintentionally reinforce distance, while creating space for local language discussion can signal relational inclusion. In urban offices where Portuguese fluency is common, this may be less pronounced, but in mixed or rural settings it shapes how information is received.

Knowledge is often most trusted when learned through demonstration and repetition. Apprenticeship-style transmission, where skills are taught through watching, doing, and listening rather than reading written instructions, remains a culturally familiar and high-status pathway. If you are introducing new procedures or systems, walk through examples and invite hands-on practice alongside written documents. People may ask to see how something works more than once before applying it themselves, reflecting a preference for layered learning rather than one-off briefings.

Some information is understood to belong at certain stages or within certain circles. In communities where initiation structures remain meaningful, there are strong cultural instincts about what should be shared with whom and when. This can show up in workplace settings as a preference for staged disclosure, particularly around sensitive matters or decision-making processes. A colleague from a culture where transparency means sharing early and widely may perceive this as withholding, while a Mozambican counterpart may view premature sharing as inappropriate exposure. Building clarity about who needs what information, and when, helps both sides align without misreading intent.

In conflict-affected areas such as Cabo Delgado, selective disclosure can be a learned safety practice. Information habits are shaped by insecurity, displacement, and the real consequences of being too visible. If someone is cautious about what they share or reluctant to commit details to writing, this often reflects lived risk rather than disengagement.
