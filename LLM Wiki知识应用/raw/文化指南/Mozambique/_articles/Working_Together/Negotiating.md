# Negotiating

## Working Together

Negotiations in Mozambique unfold through relationship and reputation as much as through terms. Because kin networks and moral communities serve as the primary safety net, value is often measured by the reliability of future partnership and likely support, not only the immediate exchange. This history comes in part from Indian Ocean coastal trade, where negotiation became a normalized way of coordinating across difference. Building credibility early opens more room later.

Expect discussions to revisit points repeatedly and move in circles rather than straight lines. This allows everyone to voice their view, confirm alignment, and test flexibility before final commitment. Decision-making authority is concentrated, so while many people will speak, sign-off will rest with a senior figure. If a negotiation stalls, a private conversation with that senior counterpart can help unstick it without public pressure.

The civil war and later instability have left a sensitivity to risk that can make commitments feel heavier than they appear on paper. Security and unpredictability still shape how people weigh long-term deals, even in routine contexts. Giving time to that caution, and demonstrating consistency over multiple interactions, reduces the perceived exposure on the other side.

Regional variation also affects tone and formality. Northern coastal settings influenced by Islamic traditions may carry different expectations around consultation and warmth than southern urban corridors. Listening for those differences and adjusting your level of formality or breadth of engagement accordingly keeps the process aligned with local legitimacy norms.
