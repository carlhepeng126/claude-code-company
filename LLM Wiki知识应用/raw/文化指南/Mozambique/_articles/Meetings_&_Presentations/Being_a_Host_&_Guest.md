# Being a Host & Guest

## Meetings & Presentations

Hosting in Mozambique reflects respect and moral standing in ways that shape professional relationships well beyond the visit itself. The care shown to a guest, through food, attention, and inclusion, affects how the host is regarded in the community and how trust develops between colleagues. A smooth and generous welcome builds the host's reputation, and accepting an invitation to someone's home acknowledges that effort and the friendship it represents. Turning down food or leaving too quickly can undermine the connection, because the gesture itself matters more than the convenience of timing.

In settings where elders or local leaders hold authority, the order in which guests are acknowledged and served can affect legitimacy. A foreign colleague who greets the most senior person first, or who waits to be directed to a seat, signals awareness of those hierarchies. Someone from a culture where informality is valued may read this sequencing as overly formal, while a Mozambican counterpart may see informal greetings that bypass local authority as dismissive or careless.

Coastal Islamic communities often align hospitality with the religious calendar, so festival periods may bring different hosting expectations and availability. In displacement-affected areas, hosting takes on additional weight as a way to rebuild kinship and recreate stability through familiar rituals. These contexts make the act of welcoming someone less about protocol and more about restoring a sense of normality and shared belonging.

When planning gatherings, accept that written invitations may receive many acceptances, not all of which will result in attendance. Saying yes is often the polite response, regardless of actual intent, which makes final numbers difficult to predict and catering a practical challenge.
