# Business Meeting Culture

## Meetings & Presentations

Mozambican meetings often involve more than one layer of authority in the room at the same time. Public and organizational life has long included overlapping systems: formal state or corporate positions, moral communities linked to church and mosque, and locally grounded leaders who hold legitimacy through lineage, spiritual protection, or community arbitration. This means that who can speak with weight may shift depending on whether the meeting feels institutional, relational, or community-linked. A colleague entering from a culture where organizational hierarchy is singular and fixed may read this plurality as ambiguity or slowness, while a Mozambican participant may read the demand for single-line authority as tone-deaf to how respect and legitimacy actually work on the ground. If you are leading or convening a meeting, clarify your own role, acknowledge seniority where visible, and leave room for contributions that come from sources of legitimacy outside the org chart.

Caution around commitment and open challenge often reflects learned risk management rather than disengagement. The civil war's long shadow, and the settlement culture that followed the Rome General Peace Accords in 1992, left many communities practiced in caution around alignment, blame, and being too exposed too quickly. Social memory of insecurity can shape how openly people disagree, commit, or reveal uncertainty. If someone agrees in the meeting but qualifies or reframes later in writing, or if dissent emerges indirectly through questions rather than opposition, these are adaptive strategies grounded in post-war experience. Following up in writing after the meeting allows people to clarify or adjust commitments without the social exposure of a live reversal.

Language choice can quietly shift who feels inside the conversation. Portuguese functions as the official and school-linked register, while many people live primarily in local Bantu languages. Switching between them, or choosing one over the other, can signal whether the meeting feels institutional or relational, and whether the participants share education pathways, urban mobility, or local groundedness. If meetings default to Portuguese when some participants are more fluent in local languages, contributions may become more guarded or simply quieter. Asking in advance about language preference, or acknowledging when interpretation may help, can widen the circle of participation without forcing it.
