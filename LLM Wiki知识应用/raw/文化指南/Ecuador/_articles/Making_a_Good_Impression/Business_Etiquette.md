# Business Etiquette

## Making a Good Impression

Ecuadorian business etiquette reflects a culture that prioritizes personal connection over procedural formality. Meetings rarely begin with the stated agenda. Expect time spent on warm greetings, inquiries about family, perhaps a joke or shared story about recent events. This opening is doing real work. It establishes whether you see the other person as a human being, not just a transaction point, and whether the relationship feels trustworthy enough to proceed. Diving straight into business can read as dismissive or abrupt, signaling that you value efficiency over respect.

Formality runs through how people address each other, especially early on. Use professional titles alongside surnames until someone invites informality. Even as relationships warm, a degree of courtesy remains. Dress codes lean conservative, particularly in the private sector and outside the capital. Men typically wear suits; women wear professional attire with modest hemlines. In coastal regions or more relaxed industries, standards may soften slightly, but it's safer to start formal and adjust than to underdress and risk appearing careless.

Physical greetings vary by gender and familiarity. Men usually shake hands, often adding a pat on the shoulder once rapport is established. Women may greet each other with a single cheek kiss, and men and women sometimes do the same if they know each other well. If you're unsure, follow the other person's lead. Handshakes should be warm and direct, not rushed.

Ecuadorians tend to soften bad news or disagreement to preserve harmony and avoid confrontation. If a colleague responds to a request with "we'll see" or "maybe later," that often means no. Pressing for clarity can strain the relationship. Watch for indirect cues and give space for concerns to surface gradually, usually in one-on-one settings rather than in front of a group.
