# Decision-Making

## Working Together

In Ecuador, decisions are shaped by the need to preserve relationships and avoid open conflict. Authority rests with senior figures, but that authority is rarely used to impose outcomes quickly. Instead, leaders consult informally with trusted colleagues and take time to sense how a decision will be received. This caution reflects a social logic in which disrupting harmony or bypassing key voices can undermine both the decision and the person making it.

Formal meetings may be where decisions are announced, but the real work often happens beforehand through one-on-one conversations or smaller gatherings. Managers seek alignment before presenting a choice publicly, ensuring that no one is surprised or left out. If you propose a decision without this groundwork, you may find quiet resistance or polite agreement that doesn't translate into action. Building informal consensus first allows the formal process to proceed smoothly.

This approach can feel slow to colleagues from cultures where decisions are made quickly by those in charge and then communicated downward. From an Ecuadorian perspective, moving too fast without consultation can look careless or dismissive. What appears as delay is often preparation, reducing the risk that a decision will fail because it lacked social buy-in. If you need a decision within a tight timeframe, start the informal conversations early and make space for input along the way, rather than presenting a finished plan and expecting immediate agreement.
