# Delegating & Supervising

## Managing Relationships

Managers in Malawi need to understand that authority alone does not command respect or honest communication. Three decades of one-party rule under President Hastings Banda created a professional culture in which openly disagreeing with those in power was genuinely dangerous, and that institutional memory still shapes how employees interact with managers today, especially in public-sector and government-adjacent roles. The Chewa concept of *umunthu* (which places the individual within the community as both their source of identity and their primary obligation) adds another layer: harmony within the group often takes precedence over individual candor. The result is that team members are unlikely to volunteer concerns, flag problems, or challenge instructions in group settings. A manager who mistakes quiet agreement for genuine alignment will only discover gaps in understanding or execution when deadlines pass. If you need honest input, create one-on-one conversations where employees feel safe to speak openly.

Delegation requires more hands-on involvement than managers from cultures that expect greater individual initiative might anticipate. Malawian education does not typically emphasize independent problem-solving or taking on responsibility without clear guidance, so tasks that seem straightforward may need explicit instruction and regular check-ins. Groups often work together effectively to solve problems, so framing delegated work as a collective responsibility rather than an individual assignment can yield better results. Younger managers leading older team members should build credibility carefully, since age-based respect does not disappear simply because formal authority exists.

Trust and loyalty are built through personal care that goes beyond the professional relationship. Faith-based expectations of integrity and genuine concern for others run deep in a predominantly Christian society, and employees notice whether a manager shows interest in their wellbeing outside work. In a strongly communal culture, trust and distrust both move through the group, so losing credibility with one person can affect broader team cohesion more than it might in more individualist settings.
