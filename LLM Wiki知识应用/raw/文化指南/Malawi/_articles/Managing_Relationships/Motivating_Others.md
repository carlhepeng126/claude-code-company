# Motivating Others

## Managing Relationships

Motivation in Malawi flows from feeling valued as part of the human circle, a reflection of *umunthu*, the cultural principle that frames contribution as relational and communal. Recognition that protects dignity and includes people in decision-making and shared purpose often energizes more sustainably than pressure or individual reward alone. A manager who takes time to acknowledge someone's role in a team effort, or who invites their input before finalizing a decision, sends a clear message: you belong here. That sense of being seen and included can strengthen commitment in ways that a purely transactional approach does not.

Material security also drives motivation in a context where livelihoods are often precarious. The Chichewa maxim *Chimanga ndi moyo* (maize is life) reflects how food stability and predictability are emotionally central. Motivation rises when people feel that their work brings stability, whether through fair pay, predictable schedules, or benefits that ease daily pressures. In Lilongwe, where most residents live in informal settlements, practical enablers like reliable transport arrangements, advance notice of schedules, or removing logistical bottlenecks can make work feel more doable. When daily life is logistics-heavy, support that reduces friction is experienced as genuine care.

Younger professionals, especially in multinational firms or urban centers, may also be motivated by training and career progression, which offer both skill development and social prestige. But across contexts, financial security, a supportive environment, and being treated with dignity remain foundational. A colleague from a culture where autonomy and stretch goals are seen as motivating might interpret this as risk-averse or overly dependent on structure. From a Malawian perspective, a manager who pushes ambitious targets without ensuring stability or inclusion may appear disconnected from the real conditions in which people live and work.
