# Business Entertaining

## Meetings & Presentations

Malawi's national identity as "the Warm Heart of Africa" shapes expectations around hospitality. Warmth is communicated through genuine attentiveness—how thoroughly you greet people, how you check that everyone is comfortable, and how you include them in the flow of conversation. This is especially true in entertaining, where welcome is expressed through care, not through elaborate settings or extravagance.

Most business entertaining happens over lunch, traditionally the main meal of the day, and usually in hotels or restaurants. Social gatherings often sit within church, neighborhood, and extended family networks, so who you know and where you go can reflect community ties as well as professional ones. Lake Malawi is more than scenery—it's a social and economic corridor. Lakeshore settings and fish-based meals, particularly chambo from the lake, can hold cultural pride. Visitors who try local dishes are appreciated; many local meals center on maize, and fish is often served whole. One saying has it that you haven't truly eaten fish until you've eaten the head.

Socializing outside traditional work hours, especially early mornings or after-hours drinks, is less common. Long commutes and family commitments mean that expectations around late gatherings differ from settings where post-work drinks are routine. Reciprocating invitations is expected. The person who extends the invitation pays, but returning the gesture confirms mutual regard and strengthens the relationship.

Respect for cultural boundaries runs through social exchange. Pushing too quickly into unfamiliar territory—whether topics, jokes, or questions—can feel intrusive. Hospitality works best when curiosity is balanced with restraint, particularly around cultural practices that have clear lines about participation and what can be shared openly.
