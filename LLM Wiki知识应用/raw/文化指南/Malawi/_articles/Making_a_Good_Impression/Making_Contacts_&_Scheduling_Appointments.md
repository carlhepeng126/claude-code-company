# Making Contacts & Scheduling Appointments

## Making a Good Impression

First contact in Malawi begins with a recognition ritual that isn't preliminary to the real conversation; it is the real conversation. Greetings about wellbeing, family, and "how are your people?" establish that the interaction sits within a moral frame of care and community-mindedness. This reflects the national self-image of warmth and welcome, and it builds the relational foundation that allows work to proceed. A colleague from a culture where meetings open with agenda items may read this as inefficiency or avoidance of the task at hand. From a Malawian perspective, jumping straight to business can feel like skipping the human contract entirely. The greeting phase is where trust begins.

Contacts are often oriented through place, lineage, and social belonging. People are instinctively placed through district or village, schooling, church or mosque ties, and family lines. In many Chewa-influenced settings, matrilineal ideas shape how belonging is read, and the maternal uncle may hold particular social authority in some contexts. Along Lake Malawi, connection has historically travelled through routes of exchange, some proud and some painful. The memory of trade networks, including Nkhotakota's role in nineteenth-century slave routes, has left a lakeshore sensibility where intermediaries and reputational chains matter. In Yao-Islamic corridors in the south and along the lake, religious community and learning ties form a distinctive pathway for connection. A warm introduction from someone trusted can open doors that cold contact cannot, because belonging is mediated through these community containers.

Appointments can be scheduled in advance, but practical realities shape when they can actually hold. In Lilongwe, where many residents live in informal settlements and transport infrastructure is uneven, logistics and "today's possibilities" often require real-time negotiation. Reconfirm meetings the day before and build in realistic buffers. Scheduled meetings rarely start on time, because time is understood relationally. If someone is in conversation when you arrive, they will finish that exchange rather than cut the person off. You should still arrive on time yourself. Avoid back-to-back bookings, and don't request meetings too early in the morning, as many people travel long distances to work. In the rainy season, expect delays.
