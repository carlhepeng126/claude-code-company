# Forms of Address

## Making a Good Impression

In Malawi, how you greet and address someone answers a relational question before a functional one: where do we stand? Politeness begins with asking after wellbeing and recognizing the other person's social position. Age, community standing, and family lineage shape who speaks first, who introduces whom, and who is invited to open a conversation. This means formality is not simply about titles but about reading social weight correctly. Use "Sir" or "Mr" and "Mrs" with surnames until explicitly invited to do otherwise, and greet everyone in the room before starting a conversation with one person.

Authority does not always follow the lines outsiders expect. In Chewa-related matrilineal contexts, the maternal uncle may hold moral authority in family-linked matters, including some workplace decisions where kinship and business overlap. A colleague from a culture where authority is assumed to run through the father's line may misread who holds decision-making power or whose endorsement is needed. From a Malawian perspective, ignoring matrilineal structures can look like someone who has not done their homework on how relationships actually work.

Physical greetings carry their own signals. A handshake is standard but may linger slightly longer than in some contexts, often with the left hand placed on the forearm or wrist as a mark of respect. When meeting a chief or very senior figure, a slight bow or bending of the knees is appropriate. In mixed-faith settings, be aware that some Muslim women may not extend their hand to a male colleague. Eye contact is made during the handshake but does not usually hold through extended conversation.

Education and credentials are read through a moral lens shaped by mission-era schooling, where being "educated" fused skill with legitimacy tied to churches and schools. This means professional titles can be heard as markers of seriousness and standing, not just competence.
