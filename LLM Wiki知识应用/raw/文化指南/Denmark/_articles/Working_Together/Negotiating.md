# Negotiating

## Working Together

In Denmark, negotiation happens within a framework, not in a free-for-all. Many sectors rely on *overenskomster* (collective agreements between unions and employer organizations), which set wages, working hours, and notice periods before individual talks begin. Danes expect to negotiate detail and flexibility within these boundaries, so trying to undo what has already been agreed at sector level will make you look either uninformed or unreasonable. Know which lines cannot be moved before you walk into the room.

A deal that holds up is one people can describe as *ordentlig*, meaning decent or proper. This goes beyond the contract itself. If you push too hard, hide clauses in the small print, or chase advantage for its own sake, you may win the immediate point but lose the relationship. Danes talk afterward, and reputations stick. A test you can use internally: would the terms look fair if printed in *Politiken* the next morning? If not, adjust.

Price is rarely the only factor. Increasingly, negotiations include climate impact, ESG commitments, and social clauses, especially in public procurement and larger organizations. Offering the cheapest bid without addressing environmental or social responsibility can make you appear dated rather than competitive. Bring data on CO₂, worker safety, diversity, or long-term partnership value early. These are treated as real variables, and Danes are used to weighing them.

You will not see theatre at the table. No raised voices, no walkouts, no dramatic pauses. If something does not work, your Danish counterpart will say so plainly and expect a reasoned response. This calm delivery should not be mistaken for indifference. Danes win by being very well prepared and by staying rational under pressure. Match that tone, and bring your substance in writing.
