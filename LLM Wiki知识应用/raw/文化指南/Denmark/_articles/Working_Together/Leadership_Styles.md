# Leadership Styles

## Working Together

Danish organisations describe their structures as *flad struktur* (flat structure), and the phrase reflects real social practice. Managers sit in the same open-plan offices, bring their own coffee, and bike to work alongside everyone else. Decision rights still exist, but they are exercised without fanfare or visible status markers. The flatness is not just organisational; it is a moral expectation rooted in equality as a social value. Leaders who exempt themselves from shared routines lose credibility quietly but quickly.

A good Danish leader acts like the chair of a well-run board. They invite input, let people speak, summarise what they have heard, and then decide. The role is to facilitate well and own the outcome, not to dominate the room or hide behind consensus. Overriding others without listening is frowned upon, but so is endless consultation that avoids responsibility. If you lead a team, show that you have listened by explaining how input shaped your thinking, even when the final call differs from what others wanted.

Everyday equality is tested in small moments. Employees watch whether a manager empties the dishwasher in the shared kitchen, takes their turn bringing cake, or sits at the same lunch table. These are not symbolic gestures; they function as quiet checks on whether someone believes they are above the group. Participating in the micro-chores of office life is part of the job, not a gesture of goodwill.

Leaders are also expected to protect work-life balance actively. That means not sending emails late at night, telling people to leave on time, and planning work in a way that avoids chronic overtime. This is seen as part of the manager's responsibility, not a personal favour. If deadlines routinely clash with childcare or evening commitments, the manager is expected to address it.
