# Feedback and Coaching

## Managing Relationships

In Denmark, the formal frame for feedback is the MUS-samtale (medarbejderudviklingssamtale, or employee development talk). This scheduled, structured conversation—usually yearly or semi-annual—is the setting people expect for reflection and planning. The format itself reflects a cultural preference: calm, predictable, and two-way rather than reactive or emotional. A sudden "we need to talk" intervention feels jarring where the rhythm is built around shared preparation and dialogue.

The expectation of dialogue runs deep. From folkeskole onward, Danish children are encouraged to speak up, question their teachers, and call adults by first name. As adults, they bring that habit into the workplace. Feedback is a discussion, and employees expect to offer their own view, question the manager's read, and shape the outcome together. Treating feedback as a one-way verdict will feel outdated. When planning a conversation, invite the employee's perspective first and expect them to nuance or push back on yours.

Tone should stay measured even when the content is direct. It's acceptable to say "this wasn't good enough," but the delivery should be calm, respectful, and focused on behavior and outcomes rather than personal worth. Critique the work, not the person, and keep the register understated rather than dramatic or confrontational.

Arbejdsglæde (joy in work) is taken seriously in these conversations. It's not a feel-good add-on but a real concern tied to retention. If feedback repeatedly drains arbejdsglæde without improving anything, people will leave rather than endure. Include space in coaching conversations to discuss what energizes or exhausts someone, and what could be adjusted within reason.
