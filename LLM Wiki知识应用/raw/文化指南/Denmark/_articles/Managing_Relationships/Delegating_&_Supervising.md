# Delegating & Supervising

## Managing Relationships

Denmark has the flattest workplace hierarchy in the world, and that shows up most clearly in how managers delegate. Structures often have only two or three layers, and the baseline assumption is that everyone, regardless of title, is a professional peer. Janteloven, the cultural norm that no one should consider themselves above others, means Danish managers actively downplay their authority. Emphasising status or giving direct orders tends to erode respect rather than build it. The standard approach is to agree a task, timeline, and budget, then step back entirely. Be clear upfront about goals and constraints, then let go. Your team will expect to own the method completely.

Employees are expected to self-direct. Identifying what needs to be done and getting on with it is the baseline, not optional initiative. Frequent check-ins are often read as a lack of trust rather than good oversight. If you're used to regular updates and structured supervision, recalibrate. The absence of close monitoring signals trust, and trust underpins the whole management relationship here. For a manager from a culture where oversight is a sign of engagement, the Danish approach can feel uncomfortably distant. From the other direction, a Danish employee may experience frequent follow-up as micromanagement, even when it's intended as support.

Feedback flows in both directions, and supervisors should expect the same directness they give. Constructive criticism is seen as useful, and failing to point out someone's mistakes will be seen as an error of judgement on your part. Being made aware of an error is seen as being given an opportunity to put it right. Employees routinely approach the most senior leaders directly, bypassing the chain of command. In Denmark, this is entirely normal. If you're managing here, expect your team to challenge your thinking openly and to reach across the organization without asking permission first.
