# Managing Suppliers & Customers

## Managing Relationships

Danish customers and clients value suppliers who are what they call *ordentlig* (decent, reliable). This means delivering on time, answering questions clearly, admitting problems early, and not changing terms without notice. Reliability often outweighs price. A supplier who consistently meets deadlines and responds quickly to emails can keep a Danish client even when a cheaper competitor appears. If something goes wrong, naming it directly and proposing a solution builds trust more effectively than deflecting or delaying.

Danish organizations, both public and private, expect to co-design solutions rather than simply purchase finished products. Municipalities and companies regularly use pilots, workshops, and user involvement to shape what they buy. A supplier who offers prototypes or a phased rollout will often be preferred over one that insists on a fixed package. This reflects a broader cultural preference for shared decision-making and testing before commitment. If you propose a tender without room for collaboration, you may be seen as inflexible or out of step with how Danish buyers work.

Corporate social responsibility and climate commitments are treated as entry requirements, not optional extras. Large tenders, especially in the public sector, include CSR, labor, and environmental clauses that can disqualify bids before price is discussed. Danish buyers expect documentation, certifications, and evidence of concrete reductions, not just marketing statements. A supplier who cannot demonstrate these clearly will struggle to compete, even with a lower price.

Danes respond poorly to aggressive sales approaches. They expect to be treated as knowledgeable partners, not targets. Over-selling, repeated follow-up, or ignoring their technical questions will lose business more often than it wins. Answer thoroughly, avoid pressure tactics, and focus on building a relationship that can develop over time rather than closing quickly.
