# Making Contacts & Scheduling Appointments

## Making a Good Impression

Danes plan their time carefully, both at work and outside it. This reflects a culture where boundary-setting and personal autonomy are deeply valued, and where respecting other people's calendars is treated as a basic courtesy. Even an informal coffee is often booked weeks in advance. Diaries fill quickly, and people guard their schedules against last-minute disruption. If you try to arrange a meeting with only a day or two's notice, you will likely find no available slots, and you may also come across as poorly organized rather than important or urgent.

First contact is typically short, factual, and modest in tone. An email or LinkedIn message should state clearly who you are, why the conversation might be relevant, and propose a specific time. Long introductory messages that focus on relationship-building before getting to the point tend to fall flat. Danes prefer directness and efficiency at this stage, and overselling yourself at first contact clashes with the preference for understated professionalism. A colleague from a culture where building personal rapport before business is essential might read this brevity as cold or dismissive. From a Danish perspective, though, launching into warm personal exchanges before establishing shared relevance can feel insincere or inefficient.

Many professional contacts still come through foreninger, the associations, boards, alumni groups, and industry networks that play a quiet but steady role in Danish working life. Being vouched for by someone from a shared network can open doors more smoothly than a cold approach. It's worth asking local colleagues which associations matter in your sector and using them to find warm introductions rather than relying on direct outreach alone.

There are regional differences in pace and formality. In Copenhagen, people are accustomed to international contacts and tightly scheduled days. In smaller cities, scheduling is still taken seriously, but the rhythm may be slightly more relaxed, and a coffee-first approach to getting acquainted is more common. Across the country, though, the same principle holds: propose meetings with a clear purpose and a specific time slot, and accept that people have lives outside work. Aggressive rescheduling or expecting flexibility at short notice suggests disorganization, not importance.
