# Forms of Address

## Making a Good Impression

Danes use first names almost immediately, regardless of rank or seniority. A CEO introducing herself will say "Anna," not "Ms Jensen, Chief Executive Officer." Titles exist, but they stay in the background. Professors, senior managers, and public officials are all addressed by their first names in conversation. This informality reflects a deeply rooted social expectation that no one should position themselves above others, a principle tied to Janteloven (the social code discouraging displays of superiority). Formality in address can make the exchange feel stiff and create unnecessary distance.

For a colleague from a culture where titles and surnames signal respect, this can feel uncomfortably casual, even disrespectful toward authority. A Danish colleague, in turn, may read the continued use of "Mr" or "Ms" as cold or overly hierarchical, a sign that the relationship has not moved forward. The practical implication is clear: once a Dane gives their first name, use it. Holding back suggests discomfort or formality that the Danish side does not expect and may not welcome.

The same informality applies in written communication. Emails open with first names, not titles. Insisting on your own title in introductions can undermine credibility rather than establish it. Danes assess competence through the quality of your contribution, not through what you ask to be called. In Danish, the informal "du" (you) is standard across nearly all professional settings, another marker of the same underlying logic.

One small exception appears in formal written contexts. On forms or in old-style correspondence, professions may still appear before a name (for example, "Lawyer Jesper Olsen"), partly to distinguish between common surnames and partly as a quiet nod to professional qualification. This is a remnant rather than a living expectation, and it does not carry over into spoken interaction.
