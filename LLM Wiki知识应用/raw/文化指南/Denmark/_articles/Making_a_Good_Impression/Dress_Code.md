# Dress Code

## Making a Good Impression

Danes with high incomes often dress much the same as everyone else: dark jeans, a plain knit, simple sneakers, maybe a good coat. The goal is to look put together without drawing attention. Loud luxury — prominent logos, flashy accessories — sits badly with Janteloven, the cultural norm that discourages standing out or claiming superiority. What reads as confidence elsewhere can come across here as insecurity or poor taste. If you arrive wearing obviously expensive designer pieces, colleagues may wonder why you feel the need to broadcast it.

Because people bike and walk in all weather, your outerwear often does more work than your office clothes. A good raincoat, sensible shoes, and a backpack can signal you understand Danish life more clearly than a perfect suit that clearly wasn't made for cycling. The full commute outfit — coat, shoes, bag — shapes first impressions, so think beyond what you wear inside the building.

Different sectors set different baselines. In government ministries, finance, or law, expect sharp but understated business wear: tailored, muted, with little decoration. In tech, design, or public sector roles, the tone shifts toward well-considered creative clothing — monochrome layers, clean trainers, carefully casual. Showing up overdressed in a startup can feel as awkward as underdressed in a law firm. High-quality, low-key materials work across most settings. Let fit and function speak instead of labels.
