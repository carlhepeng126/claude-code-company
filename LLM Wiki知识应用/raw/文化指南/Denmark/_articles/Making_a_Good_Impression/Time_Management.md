# Time Management

## Making a Good Impression

In Denmark, being on time reflects a view of fairness. Five minutes late is late because it implies you value your own time more than someone else's. This isn't about formality or bureaucratic rigour. It flows from the same egalitarian logic that shapes most Danish professional life: no one should have their time wasted by someone who didn't plan properly. Colleagues judge repeated lateness not as a minor habit but as a quiet signal that you see yourself as an exception to shared expectations. If you're working with Danish teams, treat meeting start times as firm commitments. Build in enough buffer that traffic or schedule overruns don't leave others waiting. Arriving early is entirely acceptable.

The working day itself is treated as a protected period for focused output, not performative presence. Many Danes negotiate flexible hours to manage school pickups or childcare, and the unspoken expectation is that work happens efficiently during the hours people are there. Lingering in the office without a clear task to complete raises questions about your ability to organize your workload, not your dedication. Long lunches are rare. A quick sandwich at the desk or a half-hour break is standard. This concentration of effort allows people to leave on time and have a life outside work, which is seen as both reasonable and necessary. When collaborating across borders, design workflows that respect working hours rather than assuming evening or weekend availability. Danes will meet deadlines by working together during the day, not by staying late.

Holidays and leave are non-negotiable parts of the annual rhythm. Many families plan around school breaks, particularly the summer weeks. Expecting last-minute availability in July or pushing urgent projects through the Christmas period signals that you don't understand how Danish professional life is organized. When scheduling multi-country projects, ask early about vacation plans and build timelines around them rather than assuming flexibility.
