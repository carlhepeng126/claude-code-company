# Effective Presentations

## Meetings & Presentations

A Danish audience will test the logic of your argument, not the energy of your delivery. Presentations are treated as working documents, a chance to examine how you arrived at your conclusions. The question "how did you get this number?" reflects genuine interest in your methodology, and if you cannot walk through your assumptions and limitations, your credibility drops quickly. This reflects a culture where intellectual honesty outranks persuasive polish. Danes expect you to show your workings, much as a colleague might in a collaborative analysis session. If your slides skip steps or hide complexity behind headlines, people will assume the substance is missing.

Design should be minimal. Clean layouts with white space, simple charts, and restrained use of color are the norm. Over-decorated slides, busy templates, or clip-art feel amateurish and distract from the argument. This aesthetic preference mirrors broader Danish design values, where function and clarity come before decoration. Detailed handouts that summarize your data and sources will be appreciated and used. Danes often refer back to written material after the session to verify claims or share with colleagues who were not present.

Understatement builds trust. Saying "this looks promising based on X and Y" will be read as more credible than "this is the future." Overstatement or hard-sell tactics raise skepticism. A colleague from a culture where confident, assertive claims demonstrate leadership may feel that Danish presenters lack conviction, while a Danish listener may hear the same confidence as overselling or intellectual carelessness. Both are responding to different expectations about what demonstrates rigor.

Expect to be questioned at length, and prepare for detailed challenges. Danes form opinions based on evidence, but once formed, those opinions can be difficult to shift. Answering factual questions thoroughly and without defensiveness strengthens your position. Leaving time for open discussion is not optional.
