# Business Lunch & Dinner

## Meetings & Presentations

Business meals in Jamaica often follow a "link-up" logic rather than the scripted formality of a boardroom dinner. Especially in Kingston, shared food tends to happen in cookshops, informal spots, or around communal tables where the atmosphere is lively and conversational. The meal itself becomes the social experience, with food at the centre and people moving through stories, laughter, and strong opinions. This is family-meal logic applied to professional life: food as togetherness, conversation as bonding, and the understanding that people will remember how you showed up. If you approach the meal expecting tightly controlled small talk and a timed agenda, you may miss the relational work happening at the table.

Food itself carries pride and history. Jamaican cuisine reflects layers of Taíno, African, and colonial influence, and dishes like jerk are explicitly framed as cultural heritage. Showing genuine interest in what's being served, asking about ingredients or preparation, often deepens rapport quickly. But the reverse also applies: dismissing local food or treating it as exotic performance can read as disrespect. A colleague from a culture where meals are task-focused might interpret the warmth and length of a Jamaican meal as inefficient, while a Jamaican counterpart might read a brisk, business-only dinner as cold or transactional.

Table talk can be playful and spirited, but humour and energy are read closely. Teasing that lands well builds connection; teasing that embarrasses someone or hints at class snobbery can sour the entire evening. People are assessing how you handle social power, and missteps linger. Choose venues that match the relationship you're building: local and casual if trust is still forming, more international if the moment calls for polish. Calibrate your energy to the setting. What feels right in one crowd or location may feel off in another, particularly across Kingston, tourism zones, and the parishes.
