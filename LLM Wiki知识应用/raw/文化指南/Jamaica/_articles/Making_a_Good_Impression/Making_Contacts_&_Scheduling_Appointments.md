# Making Contacts & Scheduling Appointments

## Making a Good Impression

In Jamaica, who knows you often opens more doors than a polished email can. Trust builds through networks: family ties, church circles, alumni links, diaspora connections. Reputation circulates quickly in a place where social worlds overlap, so an introduction from someone credible works as a quiet endorsement. Cold outreach can succeed, especially in Kingston's corporate and creative sectors, but a warm referral from a mutual contact tends to move things faster and with less uncertainty. If you're entering a new market or community, ask who might connect you rather than starting from scratch.

Face-to-face meetings still anchor serious collaboration, and it's worth scheduling them in advance. Confirm the day before or once you're on the island, as plans can shift and people are often pulled in several directions. If you're meeting a senior decision maker, that's a sign of real interest. And if a meeting is set just before lunch or late afternoon, be ready for an invitation to continue over food or drinks. Jamaican hospitality is genuine and warmly extended.

Punctuality from visitors is expected, even if your counterpart arrives a few minutes late. That asymmetry reflects a practical reality: international partners are often working to tighter schedules, and Jamaicans know that. Still, the opening of a meeting usually leaves room for human connection before logistics take over. Jumping straight to business can feel abrupt. Let the conversation breathe.

In smaller parishes outside Kingston, consistency becomes your reputation faster. People remember how you show up, not just what you say. Warmth, humour, and directness are all valued, but so is the ability to read tone and respond in kind. That feel of the exchange, what's sometimes called "vibes," offers as much information as your credentials.
