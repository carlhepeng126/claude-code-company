About This Course &#13; As one course among many in the American Management Association’s curriculum, Presentation Success: How to Plan, Prepare, and Deliver Effective Presentations is designed for anyone who needs to develop effective presentation skills. This course offers an updated, step-by-step approach to planning, preparing, and delivering presentations to both large and small groups. Practical tools include a planning worksheet where you’ll evaluate your audience, define your objectives, choose the right format, select and organize information, and identify where and how to use presentation aids. A preparation worksheet will lead you through each step of your presentation, from preparing your opening and writing transitions to planning your questions and answers (Q&amp;A) and troubleshooting possible problems. You’ll learn strategies for effective delivery, including tips on using body language, communicating and listening successfully, and obtaining feedback. In-text exercises enable you to pull the pieces together and practice the skills you need. One important skill is the ability to make presentations that communicate what you know clearly and persuasively. If you thought something was lacking, chances are that one or more of the following elements was missing or incomplete. &#13; Matching Subject and Audience &#13; No matter how good a sales pitch, it’s not very useful unless you have some interest in, or need for, the product or information. Similarly, although an audience might enjoy an entertaining presentation, it will be useful only if people have some interest in, or need for, the information. &#13; When people leave a presentation thinking, “That was a waste of time!” it’s often because the presenter looked at the topic only from his or her point of view. Experienced presenters know that successful presentations begin with an evaluation of what their audience already knows, needs to know, and wants to know. &#13; Organizing Information Effectively &#13; If a poorly organized journal article is confusing and difficult to read, you can read it over and over again until you get the message. With a presentation, you only have one chance to “get it.” It’s the presenter’s job to organize the information so you can follow and understand the points as they are presented. &#13; Successful presenters create an outline that follows logically from point to point. They include supporting statements or evidence for their main points so that listeners can easily follow the rationale for the main argument the speaker is trying to convey. &#13; Choosing the Right Amount of Information &#13; Presenters who overestimate what can reasonably be covered in the amount of presentation time they have often ruin their presentation by speeding up their delivery or skipping important points. &#13; Successful presenters understand that an audience can take in only so much information. They carefully estimate how much information they can include without “overloading” the audience. They use practice sessions to check their timing and adjust the content as needed. &#13; Starting and Ending on Time &#13; There can be many reasons why a presentation fails to start on time. Two of the most common are that audience members arrive late or that equipment is not working properly. Presentations go into overtime when presenters try to cover too much information, allow discussions or question and answer sessions to ramble, or feel they need to make up for a late start. &#13; But presenters have a responsibility to respect the audience’s need to adhere to a time schedule. Successful presenters know that it’s unfair to ask people to come on time just to sit around and wait, or to expect them to pay attention when the presentation runs too long. &#13; Speaking to Be Heard and Understood &#13; Imagine trying to watch a play in which the actors mumbled and stared at their feet. It would be difficult or impossible to follow. In the same way, an audience needs to be able to hear and understand a presenter’s words. If the presenter speaks indistinctly or too softly, talks to her notes, or faces the flip chart instead of the audience, the message may be lost completely. &#13; Successful presenters practice their presentations aloud. They speak loudly enough to be heard and slowly enough so the audience can follow without falling asleep. They use a conversational tone. They pronounce words clearly and distinctly. &#13; Choosing the Right Presentation Aids &#13; Carefully prepared and carefully used presentation aids, such as overhead transparencies, computerized slides, handouts, and videos, can add interest and help the audience understand the points. &#13; Too many presentation aids, visuals crammed with tiny type, or slides flashing by every 15 seconds are distracting and confusing. Experienced presenters make sure that each presentation aid serves a useful purpose. They keep presentation aids simple and clear. They make sure the audience knows where to look at any given time. They always have backup plans in case equipment malfunctions. &#13; Communicating Confidently &#13; Successful presenters establish rapport right away and maintain that rapport throughout the presentation. They project the sense of confidence that comes from careful planning and preparation. They know how to reduce and control the symptoms of anxiety and nervousness. &#13; B ENEFITS OF L EARNING TO G IVE S UCCESSFUL P RESENTATIONS &#13; The good news is that anyone who is willing to devote the necessary time and attention can reap the benefits of learning to give successful presentations. They are the key actions you will learn in this course and described below. &#13; Planning the Presentation &#13; Careful planning improves your chances for success in most activities. For example, you’re more likely to achieve the goals of a project if you develop and follow an action plan. In the same way, it takes careful planning to ensure that your presentations meet the audience’s needs and achieve your objectives. &#13; To plan a presentation, you need to follow these five steps: &#13; • Evaluate the audience. &#13; • Determine what you want to accomplish. &#13; • Select the best type of presentation for the audience and the situation. &#13; • Decide what information to include and organize it in the most effective way. &#13; • Identify information that can be explained, illustrated, clarified, or reinforced with the help of presentation aids. &#13; It doesn’t matter how long your presentation is, or how complicated—even a five-minute talk needs careful planning. &#13; Preparing for the Presentation &#13; Once you have developed a presentation plan, you need to prepare for the presentation. You’ll expand your presentation outline into a set of notes—or, occasionally, a script—that includes an introduction, transitions, and a closing. You’ll design any activities you plan to use. You’ll prepare materials and presentation aids. &#13; The preparation process also includes setting up or becoming familiar with the room in which you’ll deliver your presentation and deciding how to handle questions. Finally, you increase your chances of success by anticipating problems, taking steps to avoid and reduce them when possible, and creating backup plans. &#13; When you’ve planned and prepared carefully, you can enter the presentation room relaxed and confident. &#13; Delivering the Presentation &#13; Last—but far from least—is the delivery itself. The planning and preparation you’ve done will give you an excellent foundation for delivering an interesting, dynamic presentation. Now you need to focus your attention on communicating clearly and making sure the presentation runs smoothly. &#13; Effective delivery starts with effective practice. You need practice to learn the material and the sequence. During practice sessions you can check the timing and your delivery technique. &#13; When you deliver the presentation, you want to be sure to speak clearly, control your tone, pace, and volume, and use effective body language. Then you’ll examine each step in detail and follow them to plan a presentation of your own. &#13; NOTE: The steps are presented in the sequence that works best for most situations. But following the steps in sequence is less important than doing them all. &#13; • Step 1. Evaluate the audience . Think about the people who will be attending your presentation. How many people do you expect? Who are they? Are they a cohesive or diverse group? What are their reasons for attending? How much do they already know about the subject you’re addressing? &#13; • Step 2. Define the presentation objectives . Do you want to engage your audience in a discussion? Explain how something works? Get help solving a problem? Inform people about an upcoming event? Teach people how to do something? Move people to action? If you have several objectives, which one is the most important? &#13; • Step 3. Determine what presentation methods to use . What levels and types of interaction would be most effective, given the available time, the subject, and the audience? Would the best approach be a speech? A demonstration? With or without a question and answer session? Do you want to encourage group discussion? Would experiential activities help people understand and remember the information? &#13; • Step 4. Select the information to include and organize it effectively . What is the most important message you hope to convey? What questions are people likely to have? If you want to influence people to do something, what information will persuade them? What’s the best order in which to present the key points? &#13; • Step 5. Identify where to use presentation aids . What points can you communicate more quickly, clarify, or reinforce through the use of flip charts, videos, slides, or other presentation aids? Will the audience need handouts to follow or remember certain points? You do not need to select the specific presentation aids at this point, but your plan should indicate where various presentation aids would be useful. &#13; Step 1. Evaluate the Audience &#13; Understanding the characteristics, needs and expectations of the audience is crucial to making a successful presentation. You’ll use a different approach and presentation style for an informal group meeting in a conference room than for a large audience at a formal dinner. You’ll include different details for sales managers than for clients. You’ll use a different method to describe a technical process for engineers than for executives. You’ll have different objectives for a half-hour presentation to managers than for a full-day presentation to line supervisors. &#13; Here’s an example. Imagine that you are giving half-hour presentations on financial planning to two different audiences: a mandatory assembly of 100 college freshmen and an optional meeting of 16 manufacturing supervisors who are five years from retirement. &#13; &#13; Think About It… &#13; Instructions: You can make certain assumptions about these groups from the brief descriptions you have of them. What are some of those assumptions? List them below. &#13; &#13; &#13; Here are some of the assumptions you might have listed: &#13; • Information needs. Each group needs different information about the topic. For example, the college freshmen need to know how to obtain and manage a credit card, the benefits and uses of various savings plans, etc. The supervisors need to know how to build retirement savings quickly and safely and how to manage their retirement income. &#13; • Best presentation method. Each group might need a different presentation method. For example, you can expect and plan for more interaction with the smaller group. &#13; • Reasons for attending. The reasons for attending might differ sharply between, and even within, groups. The students are attending because they have been told to come. The supervisors have chosen to be there. As you’ll see, people’s reasons for attending affect the information you include and the way you present it. &#13; • Level of interest. The levels of interest in the topic will differ between, and even within, groups. Some students might be very interested in the subject, while others might not be interested at all. The supervisors’ level of interest could vary from mild to intense. &#13; • Knowledge base. Within each group, people will have differing levels of knowledge about the subject. Some of the students may already have credit cards, savings accounts, and even investment accounts, while others may never have managed more than a weekly allowance. Some of the supervisors may have put substantial amounts into a retirement plan, while others are only beginning to save for retirement. &#13; It’s important to begin the presentation planning process by thinking about your audience. There are six key questions to ask. Some questions are more relevant for some presentations than for others, but consider each question whenever you begin the planning process. &#13; How Large Is the Audience? &#13; The audience size is an important factor in determining the presentation method to use, the amount of information to include, and how to present information. The size also affects the types of presentation aids you can use and the space and equipment you’ll need—you’ll learn more about those elements in later chapters. &#13; Think about our financial planning presentation example. The small size of the supervisors’ group lends itself to an informal presentation in which people can ask questions and discuss issues. You might plan a “brown-bag” lunch, using the first 15 minutes to present information about retirement planning options and the last 15 minutes to answer questions. &#13; The larger group of students will be more diverse in terms of its needs, interests, and concerns, making it more difficult to determine what information to include. The presentation needs to be carefully structured to engage and hold everyone’s attention, and any discussion or question and answer session will have to be planned and managed carefully. &#13; What Are Audience Members’ Relevant Characteristics? &#13; “Characteristics” refers to such factors as the audience members’ ages and genders; educational and cultural backgrounds; jobs, roles, and positions; and relationships. &#13; Although it’s always important to consider the characteristics of the people who will comprise your audience, certain characteristics are more relevant for some presentations than for others. For example, you would approach the subject of sexual harassment differently for an audience of teenage girls than for a group of male office managers, and you’d probably use a more formal style to make a sales pitch to an audience of overseas clients than to a group of local customers. &#13; When planning the financial planning seminars, you would need to consider the fact that the people in one audience are very young, while those in the other audience are nearing retirement age. The students are less likely to know what information they need and why they need the information, while the supervisors already know the value of learning about financial planning. The supervisors may also be older than you and need assurance that you are an authority on the subject. &#13; Why Are People Attending? &#13; The ideal, of course, is a group of people who can’t wait to hear what you have to say; the phrase, “Attendance is mandatory,” evokes images of sullen faces and crossed arms that all but shout, “You can have my body but not my mind.” &#13; Most presentations fall midway between the two extremes. People usually have some interest in or need for the information, even if they have been required to attend. Some might be mildly curious, others intensely interested. Some might need the information to make a decision or to do their jobs. Some might be attending so they can argue against your position. &#13; Thinking about why people are attending helps you build a successful presentation. Knowing that the students’ attendance at the financial planning presentation is mandatory indicates that you will have to find ways to engage, interest, and involve those who might prefer not to be there. For example, you might begin by illustrating the short-term and long-term benefits of learning to manage money while people are young. Knowing that the supervisors are five years’ from retirement tells you to focus on ways to build retirement savings quickly. &#13; What Are the Audience’s Specific Needs, Interests, and Concerns? &#13; The members of your audience might need the same level of detail about your topic, or the level of detail they need might differ. Some people might already know a lot about the situation you’re discussing, and others may be completely unfamiliar with it. Some might have a stake in a decision that is to be made and some might not care one way or another. &#13; A good understanding of your audience’s needs, interests, and concerns helps you decide not only how to achieve your objectives but even what those objectives are. You can’t expect people to understand a technical explanation of how a new product works if they have limited technical knowledge and little interest in that information; perhaps their primary interest is in the production schedule and the marketing plan. &#13; Think about the groups for the two financial planning presentations. Although they need different information and will use it differently, both groups need the concepts explained in plain English. They need enough information so they can make decisions and take action, but not so much that they feel overwhelmed. &#13; How Much Do People Already Know? &#13; People might know nothing or very little about your subject, or they might be very familiar with the subject but know little or nothing about the specific topics you’ll be addressing. What’s their starting point? Are they all on the same page vis-à-vis the subject? Answering those questions helps you determine how much background information to include and what level of technical language to use. &#13; In the financial planning example, the presenter can assume that many of the students may be unfamiliar with the terminology associated with money management and investing. Even the term “mutual fund” might be unfamiliar to them. The supervisors are probably familiar with mutual funds but they might not know much about the different types of funds available. &#13; How Are People Likely to Respond to Your Message? &#13; It’s important to consider whether anyone is likely to be uncomfortable with your message or disagree with your ideas. If so, you might need to acknowledge the issue right away. For example, you might give people a few minutes at the beginning to express their concerns and record them on a flip chart page, explaining that you will try to address all the concerns during the presentation. &#13; In our financial planning example, you need to consider the fact that some students might prefer not to know the consequences of using a credit card too freely. Keep them amused for an hour? &#13; Primary and Secondary Objectives &#13; For every presentation, you will have one overall, or primary, objective, which is the most important objective you want to accomplish. Determine What Presentation Methods to Use &#13; There are many different ways to communicate information and ideas to a group. You might give a speech, with little or no audience participation, or an informal talk during which people can ask questions. You might facilitate a group discussion or problem-solving session, in which your role is more to introduce, clarify, and summarize than to speak. You might lead a class in which you guide people through experiential activities that help them learn. You might combine presentation types—i.e., follow an informal talk or speech with a facilitated discussion of the concepts presented. &#13; Often, the type of presentation is determined in advance. You might have been asked to make a speech to kick off an annual fund drive or lead a class for new employees or facilitate a monthly managers’ meeting. Even then, you usually have some choices—for example, whether to include question and answer sessions, discussions, or experiential activities. &#13; The type of presentation and specific methods depend on such factors as the amount of presentation time available, the presentation objectives, the subject matter, and the size and composition of the audience. &#13; Here is an overview of the most common types of presentations: &#13; • Speech. A speech has little or no interaction between you and audience members or between audience members themselves. Some speakers use visual aids such as slides to illustrate points; others do not. A speech might or might not include a question and answer session at the end. Speeches are most appropriate for large audiences on formal occasions. &#13; • Informal talk. Informal talks usually include some interaction between the presenter and audience members, as well as among the audience members themselves. For example, you might encourage people to ask questions and discuss points throughout the presentation. Informal talks are most effective for audiences of fewer than 30 people. &#13; • Facilitated discussion. As a facilitator, your role is to present, explain, and clarify points, keep discussions on track, make sure everyone has a chance to participate, and summarize discussion points. Facilitated discussions can be part of another type of presentation, such as a speech or informal talk. &#13; Facilitated discussions are most useful when the group needs to make a decision or solve a problem. They are also excellent ways to encourage understanding and help people learn. You can use facilitated discussions with groups of any size; the larger the group, however, the more carefully you’ll need to plan and manage the discussion. A facilitated discussion also requires more time than a speech or informal talk. &#13; • Demonstration. Sometimes the purpose of a speech or informal talk is to demonstrate the way something works. For example, you might show an audience of corporate managers how your company’s new performance appraisal software can help them write performance evaluations. In that situation, you might provide computers and let people try the software out themselves. &#13; A demonstration might be the focus of the presentation, or it might simply be one part of a speech, talk, or class. Demonstrations can work for audiences of almost any size as long as everyone can see, hear and, perhaps, touch what you’re demonstrating. Most demonstrations include questions and answers. &#13; • Class. Some presentations are designed to do more than communicate information and ideas; they are intended to teach something. In a class you do more than explain and illustrate concepts and processes and answer people’s questions. You include activities that help people learn and apply what they learn: facilitated discussions; experiential activities, such as simulations and role plays; and practice exercises. &#13; Classes can be any size, but groups of fewer than 25 are easier to manage and allow more interaction. You’ll need more time for a class than for almost any other type of presentation. &#13; &#13; Apply What You Learn &#13; Instructions: Now that you’ve read about some different types of presentations, what type will you make for your practice situation? &#13; &#13; Now, complete Step 3 on your Presentation Planning Worksheet. &#13; HINT: Think about your subject and your audience and remember that you only have 10-20 minutes of presentation time available. &#13; &#13; Step 4. Select the Information to Include and Organize It Effectively &#13; Have you ever attended a presentation that went overtime or one in which the presenter speeded up the delivery as the time grew short? One in which you felt overwhelmed by the amount of information or had trouble following the presenter’s points? Those problems occur when presenters include too much information and fail to organize it effectively. &#13; Typically, presenters tend to include too much, rather than too little, information. Perhaps they misjudge the amount of information that can be covered successfully in a given amount of time, or they are so fascinated by the topic that they hate to leave anything out. But too much information will only confuse and frustrate your audience, especially if it’s poorly organized. &#13; Key Factors for Selecting and Organizing Information &#13; To decide what information to include, first consider several key factors: the time you have available, your objectives for the presentation, what your audience already knows, and what people need to know. &#13; Read the following discussion of those key factors. Then you’ll learn a step-by-step process for deciding what information to include and how to organize it effectively. &#13; • How much time do you have available? It would seem to be common sense for a presenter to carefully assess the amount of time available before deciding how much information to include. Unfortunately, many presenters first decide what information they want to cover and then try to squeeze it into the available time. &#13; The effect of time is easy to see if you imagine giving two presentations on the topic, “Buying a House.” The audience for both presentations is essentially the same—first-time home buyers in their late twenties and early thirties, with limited budgets. The first presentation can be no more than 15 minutes long. The second can be as long as an hour. In what ways might the presentation content differ? &#13; Fifteen minutes is only enough time for an overview of home buying process, along with some cautions for first-time home buyers. In an hour, you could include additional details about the process, a more comprehensive list of cautions, and a Q&amp;A session. &#13; Here’s an important point about time: Always calculate the actual presentation time you’ll have available and limit the information you include to what you can realistically cover in that amount of time. Consider how long it will take for people to get settled, whether you’ll need breaks (essential for presentations of two hours or more), and how long you’ll need to answer questions. &#13; If the subject is technical or complicated, you can include fewer key points because you’ll need more time for explanation and illustration. If your objective is to teach people to do something, you can present fewer key points because you’ll need time for discussion and activities. &#13; • What are your objectives? No matter how interesting it might be, information that is not related to your objectives wastes valuable time. It can also distract and confuse your audience. &#13; Suppose your objective in a half-hour presentation is to convince a client’s executive committee to hire your public relations firm to conduct a campaign. In that limited time, you must select only the details most likely to convince the audience that your firm is superbly qualified to meet their needs. Which of the following points would be most convincing: (1) Your firm’s success with similar projects and your ideas for their public relations campaign, or (2) details about your company’s mission statement and how the company was founded? &#13; The audience needs information that will help them decide whether you’re qualified for the job. Thus, they will be interested mostly in point (1), your success with similar projects and your ideas for their campaign. Information about the mission statement and company history can be included in a handout—if at all. &#13; • What does your audience already know, and what do they need to know? Instead of including information because you find it interesting or useful, focus on information people actually need. What information they need depends partly on what they already know and partly on the objective for your presentation. &#13; Suppose your company has recently added new health plans to the employee benefits package. You’re planning two 40-minute presentations to employees about their health plan options. Your objective is to make people aware of their options and guide them through the process of selecting the right option. &#13; You will conduct the presentation for two separate groups: Group A, people who are already covered under one of the company’s health plans, and Group B, new employees who have not yet selected their coverage. How might the information you include differ for the two audiences? &#13; The people in Group A are already covered, so they do not need detailed explanations about the company’s existing health plans. They need information about the new plans and how those plans differ from the one in which they are currently enrolled. The new employees in Group B need information about all the health plans so they can make an informed decision about which option to select. &#13; &#13; Think About It… &#13; Instructions: Keeping in mind your objectives, brainstorm ideas about what your audience already knows and what they need to know. &#13; &#13; &#13; A Step-by-Step Process for Selecting and Organizing Information &#13; To decide what information to include in the body of your presentation and organize that information into a logical sequence, follow these steps. &#13; • Write a statement that expresses your primary message. &#13; • List the facts and ideas that support your primary message. &#13; • Identify the key points. &#13; • Summarize the key points and put them in order. &#13; • List the subpoints that support each key point. &#13; NOTE: The order in which you do the steps is not as important as doing them all. &#13; This process is illustrated in Step 4 on the sample worksheet ( Exhibit 3–1 ). You’ll examine factors to consider about the presentation environment. You’ll learn how to prepare for questions and anticipate problems. &#13; S CRIPT OR N OTES ? &#13; When the President of the United States gives a speech, chances are that he is reading the words from a script or a Teleprompter. He might deviate from the script (to the dismay of his speech writers), but the words and phrases he speaks are usually carefully selected ahead of time. Why do you think the President uses prepared scripts? &#13; The President uses prepared scripts because for most of his speeches, an ill-chosen word can have grave consequences. By following a script, he can use exactly the words he and his advisors believe are the best words to use for the topic and the situation. &#13; Most of us, however, seldom need word-for-word scripts. In fact, a word-for-word script can result in a presentation that sounds “canned.” A presenter who reads from a script can find it hard to maintain real contact with the audience. Scripts also make it harder to recover if you lose your place or are interrupted. &#13; Unless the information you’re delivering is extremely sensitive, you’ll do better with a set of notes to guide your delivery. If you’re very familiar with the topic and comfortable with the situation, you might need only a few words to jog your memory about the points to cover and the sequence to follow. Sometimes you’ll want detailed notes that include a scripted opening, transitions, and closing; lists of points to include; detailed descriptions of activities; and so on. &#13; Exhibit 4–1 shows examples of varying levels of detail. To determine whether you need a script and, if not, how detailed your notes should be, answer the questions below: &#13; • How long is the presentation? For a very short presentation, you might be able to rely on your memory, using only a few note cards listing the key points to include. The longer the presentation, the more notes you will need to keep yourself on track and make sure you cover everything. &#13; • How complex is the material and/or the presentation format? If the subject is highly technical or difficult to explain, you’ll need more details in your notes. You’ll also need more detailed notes if your presentation includes a large number of presentation aids and/or activities. &#13; • How familiar are you with the subject and how comfortable are you with the situation? If you know the subject and audience well, you might need only a few notes to guide you through the presentation. Even then, detailed notes might make you feel more comfortable—even if you rarely refer to them. &#13; • How sensitive is the topic? Like the President, you might need very detailed notes or a word-for-word script for very sensitive topics, where each word you use is crucial. &#13; • How formal is the presentation? Word-for-word scripts can be appropriate for formal speeches of 30 minutes or less. But in more casual situations, such as a conference room presentation to a group of colleagues, reading from a script creates a barrier between you and the audience. Also, people are more likely to interrupt with questions in a less formal situation, and it’s tricky to find your place in a script after you’ve been interrupted. &#13; &#13; xhibit 4-1 Levels of Detail for Script or Notes &#13; Below are excerpts from a script and two sets of notes for the same presentation. &#13; EXAMPLE 1. Here are some points to consider: &#13; • Always prepare your opening. No matter how comfortable you are with the subject matter, the situation, and the audience, always decide ahead of time how you will begin. &#13; At a minimum, write out the first sentence you will speak. Then list the other points to include in the opening. It’s also helpful to write out the sentence you will use to make a transition to the body of the presentation: “Let’s begin with a quick look at the ways in which the reorganization will affect you …” &#13; If you’re using an anecdote in the opening, it can be helpful to write out the beginning, note the key story points, and write out the end. &#13; • Tell people what the presentation is about. An important purpose of the opening is to prepare people for what they’re going to hear or do or learn. In most situations, you’ll deliver the most important point in the opening: “During the next hour and a half, I’ll show you how our new project planning software can help you keep your projects on track and achieve your project goals.” &#13; • Keep the opening short and simple. People haven’t come to hear the opening, they’ve come to hear what you have to say about the topic. Maybe all you need is something like, “Good Morning. I’m Jane Doe from Human Resources, and I’m here to tell you how to apply for retirement benefits.” Just remember that the purpose of the opening is to launch the presentation. &#13; • Only use anecdotes that are relevant. Stories can be interesting and they can make the audience laugh. But those are not good enough reasons to include them. Use anecdotes to introduce, illustrate, or explain your primary message. Even the most interesting—or funniest—story should be left out if it doesn’t serve this function. &#13; • Use caution with personal experiences. Personal experiences can draw an audience in, introduce or clarify points, and help people relate to what you have to say. But avoid revealing anything personal that might make the audience feel uncomfortable—such as an embarrassing situation that still makes you feel embarrassed. &#13; • Stay away from inappropriate humor. Humor is great when it works, and when it doesn’t offend or embarrass anyone. Always make sure humor fits the situation and does not seek laughs at the expense of anyone’s gender, race, ethnic origin, physical characteristics, age, religion, or other personal characteristics. Also keep in mind that not everyone tells jokes well—it’s okay not to be funny. &#13; Attention Getters &#13; It’s important to get a presentation started in a way that engages the audience and makes them want to hear what you’re going to say next. 5 Delivering the Presentation &#13; &#13; Learning Objectives &#13; By the end of this chapter, you should be able to: &#13; • Practice your presentation. &#13; • Gain and hold the audience’s attention. &#13; • Speak so the audience can hear and understand you. &#13; • Use effective gestures and body language. &#13; • Manage the group. &#13; • Conduct useful question and answer sessions. &#13; Careful planning and preparation helps you enter the presentation room with confidence. You know what you want to say, and you have organized the information effectively. You’ve developed an introduction, transitions, and a conclusion. You’ve developed relevant activities and presentation aids, prepared for questions, and taken steps to reduce problems. &#13; Now it’s time to focus on your delivery. In this chapter, you’ll learn practice and delivery techniques that help you make sure the information flows, the timing is right, and the audience can easily hear, follow, and understand your points. You’ll also learn strategies for managing your group and conducting Q&amp;A sessions that are helpful to all participants. &#13; P RACTICE M AKES P ERFECT &#13; Professional performers rehearse for weeks or months before going on stage. Those rehearsals help the performer learn the material, experiment with different presentation methods, and build the confidence essential for a great performance. &#13; Fortunately, it’s not necessary to rehearse for that long to give a good presentation. But practice helps you learn your material so that you can deliver your presentation with confidence. It also gives you a chance to make any necessary adjustments in timing, content, or style. Without sufficient practice, you risk experiencing what happened to Alvina. &#13; Alvina’s small consulting firm was one of five finalists for a lucrative contract. Alvina’s manager selected her to present the company’s proposal partly because she had helped develop the proposal. &#13; Alvina worked hard to plan and prepare her 45-minute presentation. She was ready for questions people might have. She previewed the room and made sure the client knew the room setup she needed. She checked the information packets to make sure they were complete and put all the overhead transparencies in order. &#13; The day before the presentation Alvina spent nearly an hour at her desk reading through her notes. When she finished, she felt confident that she knew her material and could deliver a dynamic presentation. She arrived early so she could make last-minute adjustments to the room setup and make sure the equipment was working. &#13; Unfortunately, Alvina’s presentation did not live up to her expectations. She felt nervous and unsure of herself. She stammered through her opening. She fumbled with her notes and the overhead transparencies. She forgot an important point and then added it out of sequence. Her presentation ran so long that there was no time for questions and she had to rush through her closing. &#13; Alvina took the time to plan and prepare her presentation carefully. Why was her presentation so unsuccessful? What might she have done to make sure she was ready to deliver the presentation effectively? &#13; Alvina’s presentation fell short of her expectations because although she knew her material, she didn’t know her presentation. Reading through her notes was no substitute for practice. What Alvina should have done was practice delivering the material out loud and standing up. That practice would have helped her: &#13; • Become familiar with her opening, transitions, sequence of points, and details &#13; • Fine tune the presentation and iron out glitches &#13; • Experiment with delivery methods &#13; • Incorporate activities and presentation aids smoothly &#13; • Check and adjust the timing &#13; • Feel more confident &#13; How to Practice &#13; Practicing a presentation means becoming familiar with it, not memorizing it word for word. Memorized presentations nearly always seem flat and disconnected, and they lack the conversational tone that helps you communicate with the audience. Most presenters also find that the act of trying to remember memorized words creates a barrier between them and the audience. &#13; Instead of memorizing, use your practice time to learn the opening, the sequence of key ideas, and the closing, and any anecdotes you are using. In the rare instances when it’s important to deliver precise words exactly as they have been written, read a prepared statement. &#13; It is important to incorporate as many real elements as possible into your practice sessions. Here are some things you can do to make the most of your practice time. &#13; Reread Your Notes &#13; Go through your notes two or three times, focusing on the opening, sequence of key ideas, closing, and any anecdotes you’ll be using. Be sure your notes clearly indicate where you will be making transitions, exposing and removing transparencies, moving from one slide to another, introducing an activity, and so on. &#13; Practice the Presentation Out Loud and Standing Up &#13; You are not going to deliver the presentation by thinking it through in your chair. You are going to deliver it on your feet, so that’s the way you need to practice. Use a stopwatch to get a better feel for overall length. &#13; Simulate the Situation &#13; If you can’t practice in the presentation room itself, try for a room of similar size and shape. Practicing in a small office doesn’t prepare you for delivering the presentation in a large meeting room or auditorium. If you’re going to use a microphone or podium in the presentation, use them when you practice. &#13; Try Out the Equipment If You Can &#13; Instead of simply thinking to yourself, “Okay, I turn on the projector here,” actually turn on a projector and set up the transparency. Start and stop the videos. Go through the slides. Place the handouts on the tables. That way, you’ll be less likely to fumble with the presentation aids and you can more realistically check the time needed to set them up and use them. &#13; Practice in the Clothes You’ll Be Wearing &#13; People hold themselves differently and move differently in different kinds of clothes. Decide what you’ll wear, and practice in the same or a similar outfit. For example, if you’ll be wearing a business suit with high heels or dress shoes, practice in a business suit with high heels or dress shoes. If you’re delivering your presentation at a retreat and will be wearing jeans and tennis shoes, wear them for practice. &#13; Practice the Q&amp;A Session &#13; Ask a colleague to sit in on your rehearsal and ask questions she thinks the audience might ask. Pay special attention to this part of the practice if the Q&amp;A session is a particularly important part of your presentation. &#13; Get Feedback &#13; You can give yourself good feedback by practicing in front of a mirror. You can audiotape or videotape a practice session. But the best feedback comes from other people. Ask a colleague to watch your presentation (or a condensed version) and give you feedback, focusing on clarity and completeness of content, usefulness of presentation aids, and delivery style. It begins when you arrive in the room—early enough so you can be ready and relaxed when the first audience member enters. It includes the first crucial minutes when you establish rapport and gain your audience’s attention and continues through the closing where you leave confident that you have clearly communicated your message. &#13; Getting Started &#13; The first few minutes of your presentation are crucial. It’s during those few minutes that you establish rapport, get the audience’s attention, set the tone, and tell people what to expect. &#13; Here are some suggestions for making those first few minutes pay off. &#13; Get Started on Time &#13; You’ll quickly lose your audience’s goodwill if you make people wait. It’s not always easy to start on time—people come late, earlier groups run overtime—but it’s always difficult to make up for a late start. &#13; Although you can’t really begin with only two or three people in the audience, don’t wait for everyone to arrive. Start at or near the start time even if only one-quarter or one-third of your audience is there. &#13; If you are forced to start late, be careful not to rush or drop the opening, key transitions, Q&amp;A, or the closing. Decide ahead of time what parts of your presentation you could leave out if you run short on time. &#13; Establish Rapport &#13; Unless you are making a dramatic entrance from behind a curtain, you are “on stage” as soon as people enter the room. That lets you begin establishing rapport even before you step up to the podium. &#13; Some presenters like to stand at the door and greet people as they arrive. Others remain in the “presenter’s area” at the front of the room but establish nonverbal contact with a nod and a smile as people enter. Others enjoy mingling with the group as people find their seats and settle down. &#13; Whatever methods you prefer, keep in mind that the connection you make with your audience helps people feel comfortable and makes it easier to communicate your message. &#13; Get the Audience’s Attention &#13; In the theater, dimming lights and music signify that the play is about to begin. People stop talking, put down their programs, and turn their attention to the stage. &#13; &#13; xhibit 5-1 Presentation Skills Feedback Sheet &#13; PRESENTER’S NAME:___________________________ &#13; TOPIC:________________________________________ &#13; Check the appropriate boxes . &#13; &#13; &#13; &#13; COMMENTS ___________________________________________________ &#13; &#13; For a presentation, you don’t usually dim the lights and bring up the music. Try to convey the impression that there is nowhere in the world you would rather be (with thanks to Dale Carnegie). &#13; Communicating Clearly &#13; Have you ever attended a presentation in which the speaker spoke too softly? Too quickly? In a monotone? You probably found it difficult to pay attention, and you might have never fully understood the speaker’s message. &#13; To communicate clearly, you need to speak clearly and precisely, at the right pace, and at the right volume. Your body language and gestures should be appropriate and helpful, not distracting. You should pronounce words clearly so they are easy to understand. &#13; Here are the key elements for communicating clearly during a presentation. They are discussed in more detail in the following sections. &#13; • Speak loudly enough to be heard &#13; • Speak slowly enough so people can follow what you say but not so slowly that they fall asleep &#13; • Use a conversational tone &#13; • Use good diction—pronounce the words clearly and distinctly &#13; • Avoid distracting gestures, purposeless movements, and inappropriate facial expressions &#13; Volume, Pace, and Tone &#13; Actors who cannot be heard, speak too rapidly, or speak in a monotone cannot communicate a playwright’s meaning effectively to an audience. That’s why actors spend years learning to use their voices effectively. &#13; Unless you speak professionally to large groups, you probably do not need—or have time for—vocal training. But you do need to be aware of how well your voice carries, whether you speak too quickly or too slowly, and whether your voice is interesting or flat. &#13; • Volume. Do you know how well your voice carries? Can you easily be heard at the back of a large room? Are people always asking you to speak up during a conversation? &#13; If you don’t know, ask a friend or colleague to sit at the back of a large room. Recite a poem, read a newspaper article, or deliver a segment of a presentation at a volume that seems comfortable to you. Ask your friend to tell you whether you need to speak more loudly (or more softly). &#13; To keep your voice level from dropping during a presentation think about “sending” your words to the people in the back row. If you’re not sure whether people can hear you, ask: “Can everyone hear me?” or “Please let me know if you have trouble hearing.” If your voice is too soft to carry easily without shouting, use a microphone. &#13; • Pace and tone. For most presentations, speak at the rate of or slightly slower than the rate of ordinary conversation, and use an ordinary conversational tone. Pause often and leave periods of silence after delivering important points; those pauses let the audience take a breath and think about what’s being said, and they help you stay focused. Avoid speaking in a monotone—a flat voice that is devoid of emphasis and emotion. &#13; Keep in mind that your normal rate of speech might be faster or slower than that of your audience. For example, people from the Southern United States tend to speak more slowly than people from the Northeast. &#13; Generally, presenters err on the side of speaking too quickly. That’s because people often speed up their speech when they’re nervous (as if to get it over with) or when they think they are running out of time. People also feel so uncomfortable with silence so they tend to fill every empty moment with talk. &#13; It’s important not to speak so slowly that you put your audience to sleep. But if you speak too quickly, running words and sentences together with few pauses, your audience will find it hard to follow your points. That requires picking up cues that tell you how people are responding; facilitating discussions and activities; and maintaining control so you can keep the presentation on track. &#13; Experienced presenters know that it’s impossible always to predict the behavior of a group or the way a group will respond to a presentation. One group might follow the presentation eagerly and participate actively in discussions, while the next might be restless and respond to discussion questions with shrugs. A fast-paced presentation might work well for one group, yet leave the next confused. &#13; Here is a summary of guidelines for managing a group. We will discuss these guidelines in more detail. &#13; • Pay attention. Establish contact with the audience before you begin speaking, and maintain that contact throughout the presentation. Keep your attention on the audience, not on your notes or presentation aids. That way, you can “read” the audience and make adjustments to ensure you are communicating clearly and responding to their needs. &#13; • Facilitate and control audience participation. Audience participation should increase the value of the presentation to everyone, but you need to make sure that people participate in a helpful way. For example, you need to encourage discussions and questions while keeping people focused on relevant issues and keeping your eye on the clock. &#13; • Respond quickly to distractions and interruptions. Anything that draws the audience’s attention away from the presentation needs to be handled immediately. &#13; Pay Attention &#13; Have you ever attended or given a presentation where the presenter “lost” the audience, yet kept right on going? Can you describe the way the people in the audience looked and behaved? &#13; Here is some behavior you might have noted: People shifting in their seats, slumping, whispering, wearing glazed expressions, asking inappropriate questions, or even getting up and leaving. &#13; Now think of a presentation in which the audience was very interested in what the presenter had to say. How did people in that audience behave? &#13; Chances are that people in that audience kept their eyes on the presenter. Although they might have shifted in their seats from time to time, mostly they sat still, leaning slightly forward with their eyes wide open. They might have been taking notes. Their questions were appropriate and asked at the appropriate times. &#13; People have many ways of letting you know they are interested and following your points, or they are confused, bored, uncomfortable, or distracted. If you pay attention, you can respond to cues that you might need to adjust your delivery or take some other action. &#13; To get this essential information from your audience, you must maintain eye contact. Eye contact provides the connection that lets you “read” your audience. Without eye contact, you might as well lower a curtain between yourself and the audience. Eye contact not only helps you establish rapport and communicate more clearly, it lets you see whether people are attentive and interested. &#13; You can feel confident that people are interested in and following what you have to say if you see that they are sitting up straight and leaning forward slightly, with their eyes open and following your movements. But if you spot any of the following, you might need to adjust your pace, volume, or delivery style: &#13; • A number of people slumped in their chairs with their arms crossed, with set facial expressions and unfocused or wandering eyes &#13; • A number of people shifting in their seats, doodling, or tapping pencils or feet, or starting side conversations &#13; • A number of people getting up and leaving the room &#13; • People leaning forward with their hands cupped around their ears &#13; • People scowling, frowning, or shaking their heads &#13; • People asking inappropriate questions or making inappropriate comments &#13; • A number of people dozing &#13; Facilitate and Control Audience Participation &#13; Most presenters encourage some audience participation, even if it’s limited to a question and answer session at the end (see the end of this chapter for details about managing question and answer sessions). Managing that participation so the presentation stays on track is one of the presenter’s primary responsibilities. &#13; Encourage Input from Everyone &#13; Try to give as many people as possible a chance to speak during discussions. For example, if one or two people seem to be monopolizing discussions, you might say, “You have a lot of useful things to say. But let’s bring others into this discussion.” &#13; Ask Open-Ended Questions to Encourage Discussion &#13; If you want the group to talk about a topic or issue, pose questions that cannot be answered with a simple “yes” or “no.” For example, instead of saying, “Do you think flextime would help us attract more qualified employees?” Say, “In what ways do you think flextime would benefit the company?” &#13; Let People Participate at Their Own Comfort Level &#13; Some people are uncomfortable speaking up in groups. Gently encourage them to participate by saying something like, “We’ve heard from several people on this question. I’d be interested in knowing what the rest of you think. Who else would like to comment?” &#13; Keep Discussions on Track &#13; One of a facilitator’s most important jobs is to make sure that discussions stay focused on the question, issue, or topic. When people veer off in another direction, say something like, “That’s an interesting point, but let’s stick with [repeat question or topic] for now.” If the new points raised are important or people seem unwilling to abandon them, you might say, “Let’s write this point on a flip chart page and come back to it later.” &#13; Don’t Let Discussions and Activities Go on Too Long &#13; Once the point has been made or the objective of an activity achieved, move on to the next part of the presentation, even if people want to continue. You might say, “I know that several of you haven’t had a chance to speak on this issue, but our time is limited so we have to move on,” or, “We have time for two more questions on this issue, and then we’ll have to move on.” &#13; Use Small Groups &#13; If the group is large, discussions and activities are more manageable in smaller “break-out” groups. Make sure each small group knows what questions it’s addressing or activities it’s completing. Set a time limit. When the time is up, ask each group to report its discussion or experience to the larger group. &#13; Respond Quickly to Distractions and Interruptions &#13; You can’t predict such distractions or interruptions as a group of noisy latecomers, someone moving furniture around next door, a loud conversation at the back of the room, a power failure, or a bomb scare. But it’s important to respond quickly to anything that draws people’s attention for more than a moment or two. The way you respond depends on the nature of the distraction or interruption. &#13; For example: &#13; • You might ignore one or two latecomers who slip in quietly. But you might need to stop for a moment to let a noisy group get settled. Usually the attention on the group will be enough to settle them down quickly. &#13; • Two people whispering briefly at the back of the room won’t be much of a distraction. But two or three people who are talking loudly or telling jokes need to be asked politely to stop. &#13; • A single loud noise outside the room might need only a pause of acknowledgment. A loud television monitor in the next room needs to be turned down. &#13; • You might keep on talking if one or two cell phones ring. But beepers and cell phones that go off regularly need to be turned off. &#13; If you must stop a presentation to handle a distraction or interruption, take a moment when you begin again to recapitulate: “You’ll remember that I said…” And remember that if you lose presentation time, you might need to adjust the content so you won’t feel rushed or go overtime. &#13; Handling Difficult Participants &#13; Occasionally, the behavior of one or more audience members makes it difficult or impossible to deliver the presentation as planned. Disruptive behavior includes making hostile remarks, arguing, telling jokes, continually starting side conversations, and refusing to participate in activities. Other behaviors that can be distracting include dozing, humming, and reading the newspaper. Such behavior sends a clear message: “I don’t want to be here.” &#13; You need to recognize and deal with disrupting and distracting behavior firmly and promptly. Not only does it interfere with the presentation, it makes the other audience members very uncomfortable. &#13; Can you think of a time when someone in the audience of a presentation you attended or delivered behaved in a way that you found disruptive or distracting? Why do you think people engage in that kind of behavior? &#13; Here are some reasons you might have come up with: &#13; • People don’t want to be there. They have been told to come or feel compelled to attend. Or perhaps they feel uncomfortable with the topic or the message, fearing that they’ll be asked to do something they don’t want to do or “shown up” in some way. &#13; • Some people tend to be overly talkative or like to “take the stage.” They have a strong need to socialize and to be noticed. A group of any kind offers such opportunities. &#13; • Something is going on in the situation that you, the presenter, don’t know about. Imagine giving a presentation on project management skills to people who have just learned that their company has been purchased. It would be difficult for them to focus on learning to manage projects when they are worried about what will happen with their jobs. &#13; • People had expected something else. When you buy your ticket for a light comedy, you expect to see a light comedy, not a shoot-’em-up Western. When you expect to learn about health care options, you don’t expect to be intensely marketed by a single health plan. People who expect one thing and find another are likely to be frustrated, disappointed, and upset. &#13; • People perceive the presenter as someone who knows nothing about them and their needs. Suppose you had been teaching for ten years and a business person comes in to tell you how to run your classroom. It would be difficult to take the person seriously; after all, what does he or she know about the real world of teaching? In the same way, people attending a presentation won’t take it seriously if they believe the presenter knows less than they do about the situation. &#13; You need attention and patience to handle difficult behavior—but you must handle it or risk losing control of your presentation. Here are some steps you can take: &#13; • Pay attention to cues that all is not going well . As you learned earlier, maintaining eye contact with your audience allows you to read the body language and pick up other signals that you need to take some kind of action. &#13; • Diagnose the problem . If the entire audience is grumbling or not paying attention, you might need to stop the presentation and focus on the problem: “I came here to talk about … I can see by your questions that you expected … let’s talk about why we’re here and what we want to accomplish.” &#13; • Empathize . “I understand that you’re all very busy with… and this isn’t a great time for a training session. But we’re here, so let’s see what we can do to make it worthwhile.” &#13; • Avoid singling out and embarrassing people . If only one person or a small group is behaving in a difficult way, try to talk with them privately to find out what’s going on. Offer the people the opportunity to leave if they really don’t want to be there. &#13; • Set a ground rule . For example, if a few people are monopolizing discussions, you might say, “To make sure everyone gets a chance to speak, let’s set a ground rule: Once you’ve spoken, let three other people talk before you respond again.” &#13; • Show people you respect what they know . “You people are the experts in… my role is to help you focus on what you know and come up with new approaches to …” &#13; M ANAGING Q UESTIONS AND A NSWERS (Q&amp;A) &#13; Unless your purpose is simply to give a speech and your time is very limited, you should expect and encourage questions. Questions are a wonderful tool for involving people and increasing their interest. Of course, questions should be relevant, and should contribute to, rather than distract from, the presentation. &#13; Q&amp;A—How and When? &#13; Let people know how you plan to handle questions. At the beginning of the presentation, tell people whether you’ll take questions at any time or only at the end. For long presentations, perhaps you prefer to take questions at the end of each segment or after breaks. If the group is large and your time is limited, you might ask people to write their questions down and hand them in. &#13; Use Questions to Elicit Questions &#13; Sometimes people are shy about asking questions or unsure of what questions to ask. Here are some ways to encourage people to ask questions and participate in discussions: &#13; • Pose a hypothetical question (“What if…?”; “What might happen if…?”) &#13; • Ask open-ended questions, which are questions that cannot be answered with yes or no. (Instead of, “Has anyone had experience with …?” ask, “What experience have you had with …?”) &#13; • Ask people to work in small groups to come up with questions &#13; • Pose questions to small groups and let them work together on answers &#13; Make It Easy for People to Ask Questions &#13; Have you ever heard a presenter say, “Any questions?” and then immediately move on to another topic or another page of notes before anyone can respond? People may need time to formulate their questions. &#13; • Wait for ten seconds after asking for questions. &#13; • Make eye contact with people while you wait. &#13; Make the Questions and the Answers Useful to Everyone &#13; It’s frustrating to hear a presenter answer a question when you don’t know what the question was. It’s also frustrating to hear long, rambling answers, or listen while a presenter responses to a question that has nothing to do with the topic. &#13; Here are some ways to make sure the questions and answers contribute useful information: &#13; • If some audience members might not have heard the question, repeat it. You usually need to repeat the question if the group is large or the person asking the question speaks very softly. &#13; • Make sure you understand the question. If you’re not sure, restate the question (“You want to know whether …?”) or ask the person to repeat it (“I’m not sure I understand; can you clarify that question for me?”) &#13; • Extend the answer beyond the questioner. Make eye contact with the questioner as you begin your response. As you continue, make eye contact with others in the audience; if you wish, return to the questioner as you complete your answer. &#13; • Answer only relevant questions. If a question is off track, say, “That’s an interesting issue but it’s not our topic today.” If the questioner persists, suggest that he or she talk to you during a break or after the presentation. &#13; • Avoid answering the same question more than once. Say, “I believe we’ve already covered that.” If the questioner persists, suggest that he or she talk to you during a break or after the presentation. &#13; • Focus your response. Answer the question as concisely as you can, and then move on to the next question. Be careful not to ramble, repeat yourself, or get sidetracked. &#13; • Ask others in the group to answer. One way to increase participation is to turn the question to the group: “Jamie asked… Can anyone here answer her question?” or, “Jamie asked… What do you think about …?” &#13; Avoid Emotional Responses &#13; Sometimes people use questions to challenge the presenter or express anger. Always avoid being drawn into an argument or becoming defensive. Do the following instead: &#13; • Empathize with the questioner (“I understand that this issue concerns you. I wish I could give you the answer you want but the facts are that…”) &#13; • Say clearly when you don’t know an answer; if appropriate, say you will try to get it or suggest where the answer might be found (“That’s an interesting question, and I have no answer for you at the moment. I’ll try to find the answer and get back to you.”) &#13; • Say when you are unable to answer a question (“I’m sorry, but I can’t release that information at this time.”) &#13; • Say clearly when a question is sensitive or makes you uncomfortable (“That’s a very difficult question to answer … it raises an issue that I had not planned to address today… “) &#13; &#13; Apply What You Learn &#13; Instructions: Ask someone to videotape your next presentation. 6 Using Presentation Aids Effectively &#13; &#13; Learning Objectives &#13; By the end of this chapter, you should be able to: &#13; • State the key factors to consider when using presentation aids. &#13; • Determine when and how to use specific presentation aids. &#13; Presentation aids such as slides, flip charts, overhead transparencies, videotapes, props, computers, and handouts can be a valuable part of your presentation. Selected carefully and used in appropriate ways, presentation aids reinforce, clarify, explain, and illustrate your points and concepts. They also add variety to a presentation, helping engage the audience and hold people’s attention. &#13; This chapter provides an overview of the different types of presentation aids and guidelines for using them so you know how they’re used. You’ll also learn how to use presentation aids so they enhance, rather than distract from, your presentation. &#13; W HY U SE P RESENTATION A IDS ? &#13; When you read a report, you can go back and reread anything you didn’t understand the first time through. But in a presentation, you have only one chance to “get it,” so the presenter must make sure you can “get it” the first time around. That’s where presentation aids can help. &#13; There are several ways in which presentation aids help you make more successful presentations. Studies show that audiences remember up to 40 percent more when they are able to see the information as well as hear what is being said. Presentation aids can often communicate key points or complex concepts far more effectively than words alone. You can use presentation aids to supplement or even replace your notes. And presentation aids add variety to your presentation, increasing the audience’s interest. &#13; A presentation aid is anything you use to reinforce, illustrate, clarify, or explain the information in your presentation, such as: &#13; • Visuals projected on a screen, such as slides and overhead transparencies &#13; • Printed flip chart pages, wall charts, banners, and posters &#13; • Blank surfaces on which to write, such as flip chart pages, white boards, chalkboards, and blank overhead transparencies &#13; • Props, including models, prototypes, and product samples &#13; • Videotapes and audiotapes &#13; • Technological aids, such as computers and teleconferencing systems &#13; • Handouts, such as copies of key visuals, workbooks, and informational packets &#13; &#13; Think About It… &#13; Instructions: Check off presentation aids you have used. &#13; &#13; Have you ever been to a presentation that used any of these? Presentation aids can also be distracting and confusing. Worse, the problems that presentation aids can create can ruin an otherwise excellent presentation. Think about what happened to Jean-Jacques. &#13; Jean-Jacques is about to make his first important presentation. His purpose is to introduce a new marketing campaign to a select group of sales managers. He has always admired people who used presentation aids to liven up their presentations, so he’s included a number of presentation aids that he hopes will make his presentation especially dynamic and exciting. &#13; Jean-Jacques prepared thick informational packets that contain details about the marketing campaign, mockups of a proposed brochure, and a summary of his presentation. He developed PowerPoint slides to reinforce all the points he planned to make. He set up two flip charts on which to capture questions or comments from people in the audience. Finally, he used a borrowed camera to prepare a videotape he hoped would illustrate the way the marketing campaign would be conducted. &#13; Unfortunately, the presentation didn’t go as well as Jean-Jacques would have liked. During his opening, he saw that people were leafing through the informational packets instead of listening to him. Several times people asked him to go back and repeat slides they hadn’t had enough time to read. He kept bumping into the flip chart easels, which he never actually got around to using. To his chagrin, the sound quality on the videotape was so poor he had to stop it halfway through. &#13; Jean-Jacques used too many presentation aids and he used them without a clear purpose. He made a common mistake: he assumed that presentation aids always made a presentation more dynamic and interesting. He didn’t realize that they could also get in the way. &#13; If you were advising Jean-Jacques, what are some things you would suggest he do the next time around to make sure his presentation aids are useful, not distracting? &#13; Here are some of the guidelines you might have given Jean-Jacques: &#13; • Have a good reason for using a presentation aid. What do you want the presentation aid to accomplish—clarify a point? Capture information? Reinforce your message? &#13; • Make sure the presentation aid is worth the time and cost. Some presentation aids, such as blank flip chart pads, are inexpensive and require no preparation. But workbooks, information packets, slides, videos, and computer-aided presentations can be time-consuming and expensive to prepare. Ask yourself whether value added by a presentation aid justifies the time and cost. &#13; • Recognize that the risk of problems increases when you use complicated equipment. If a flip chart easel collapses, you can set it up again or tape pages to the wall. But if a slide projector doesn’t work, you can’t use your slides. The more complicated the equipment, the more difficult it is to operate and the more likely it is to malfunction. &#13; • Keep visuals simple and clear. It’s difficult to read more than a few words on a slide or overhead transparency or flip chart page, especially when it goes by quickly. Each visual should express only one point. Words and pictures should be easy to see from the back row. See Exhibit 6–1 for guidelines and examples. &#13; • Direct the audience’s attention. Some presenters use so many presentation aids that the audience doesn’t know whether to look at the presenter, a blank projection screen, a slide, a prop, or a handout. The following are descriptions of common presentation aids and guidelines for their use. &#13; Handouts &#13; Handouts let you give people more information than you can cover during the presentation itself. They also help reinforce your points and give people something tangible to take away. &#13; Whether your handouts are limited to a page or two that summarize key points or are elaborate informational packets, they need to be relevant and useful. Here are some points to keep in mind: &#13; • Determine when and how people will use them. Do people need to refer to the handouts during the presentation? Do the handouts include pages on which people will make notes? Paper-and-pencil exercises? Are they primarily supplementary information for people to take away? &#13; • Decide how and when to distribute handouts. It takes valuable time to distribute single handouts. If you have several pages, consider collating them into sets and handing them all out at once, possibly in a folder or binder. If people need to refer to the handouts during the presentation, give them out at the beginning or before the first use. Otherwise, give them out at the end so people won’t spend their time reading instead of listening. &#13; HINT: If you’re saving handouts for the end, tell people at the beginning what handouts they will receive so they can pay attention to the presentation instead of taking unnecessary notes. &#13; • Tell people what they have and what they’re getting. Say how and when the handouts will be used, and tell people when to refer to a specific handout. If you distribute handouts at the end, describe the handouts and their purpose. &#13; • Keep handouts simple, neat, and clear. For most presentations, handouts don’t have to be fancy or elaborate. But the pages should be easy to read and understand. For example, if you photocopy a photograph, make sure it comes out clearly. &#13; • Credit sources. If you include photographs, quotations, journal articles, or other copyrighted material, be sure to credit it properly. &#13; • Be cautious with sensitive material. Handouts are easy to duplicate. Do not include confidential or sensitive material unless you are certain that your audience will keep it confidential. &#13; White Boards &#13; White boards offer an easy way to record points generated by your audience, highlight key points, explain concepts with key words or graphics, or present instructions for an activity. White boards are excellent for small groups in small rooms where everyone can see what’s written on the board; they are not useful in large auditoriums. &#13; Most white boards are simply surfaces on which to write. But one type of white board has a nifty electronic feature that lets you photocopy and print out any information written on the board, which makes it especially useful for brainstorming sessions. The more high-tech versions of that electronic board connect to a computer so you can e-mail what you write or save it to a disk. &#13; HINT: At the other extreme, you can create a makeshift whiteboard by taping a white plastic garbage bag to the wall. &#13; Keep the following in mind when using white boards: &#13; • Before your presentation begins, completely erase anything left on the board by a previous speaker. “Ghost” words and images are distracting. &#13; • Use only the special dry-erase pens made for white boards. Other marking pens might not erase without special chemicals—or might not erase at all. Throw pens away when they begin to run out of ink. &#13; • Use only dark colors—black, blue, green, purple, brown—to write information or draw visuals. It’s okay to use orange or red to highlight, but those colors are hard for people to see clearly. Never use yellow—it disappears on a white board. &#13; • Be sure that everyone in the audience can see the information on the white board. Before your presentation, write something on the board. Check the sight lines, and make sure the letters are large and clear enough to see from the back of the room. Also check to see whether glare from light hitting the board interferes with anyone’s ability to see. &#13; • Leave information up long enough for people to take notes if they need to. Then erase the board. &#13; HINT: White boards have more or less replaced green and black chalkboards. Chalkboards are messy, the writing is harder to see, and the colors aren’t as bright. If you do use a chalkboard, use white chalk, not yellow. Print in large letters and erase the information when it’s no longer needed. And never let the chalk squeak! &#13; Blank Flip Chart Pages &#13; For small groups, a blank flip chart pad on an easel can be one of your most useful tools, especially when your purpose is to generate information. &#13; When you need to capture audience comments or ideas, blank flip chart pages have three advantages over non-electronic white boards: You can easily refer back to previous pages; tear the pages off and post them; and keep the pages for later use. &#13; The guidelines for using flip chart pages are similar to those for using white boards: &#13; • Make sure the easel is stable and at the right height for you. &#13; • Limit what you write to a few key words per page. It’s helpful to use paper printed with light blue lines or graph squares. &#13; • For larger groups, use several easels around the room and enlist helpers to record information simultaneously. &#13; • Use a wide felt-tip water-based marker in strong colors; reserve red and orange for highlighting and never use yellow; throw pens away when they begin running out of ink; and cap pens and put them down when you’re not using them. &#13; HINT: Avoid carrying around an uncapped pen—it’s all too easy to accidentally write on your face or your clothes. &#13; • Give people enough time to take notes before flipping the page. &#13; • If you plan to post pages as you complete them, tear off strips of tape ahead of time and stick them to the back of the easel so you won’t have to fiddle with the tape during the presentation. &#13; • When you’re finished with a page, flip to a blank page even if you’re not going to be using it for a while. &#13; Prepared Flip Chart Pages &#13; For smaller groups, flip chart pages that you prepare ahead of time can be a good alternative to overhead transparencies or slides. Just as with blank flip chart pages, you can easily refer back to specific pages, and you can also tear off pages and post them. &#13; The same guidelines apply for prepared flip chart pages as for blank pages on which you write during the presentation. In addition: &#13; • Carefully consider the size of your audience and the room. If people in the back rows might not be able to read the information easily, use overhead transparencies or slides instead. &#13; • Put blank pages between sheets to keep the writing on the next page from showing through. &#13; • Use Post-it notes to tab pages if you plan to skip around. &#13; Overhead Projections &#13; With today’s computer technology, overhead transparencies that you project on a screen are at least as easy to prepare as flip chart pages, and you can use them with a group of any size. The disadvantages are that overhead projectors are noisy and need to be checked out ahead of time to be sure they work. &#13; The wide range of graphics and fonts available on most word processing programs makes it easy to liven up your presentation with interesting, colorful transparencies. But keep these guidelines in mind: &#13; • Remember that the purpose of the transparencies is not to entertain but to focus, explain, illustrate, clarify, and reinforce your points. &#13; • Make sure each transparency conveys only one main point or idea. Use only a few words per page—and a big typeface. &#13; • Keep charts or graphs simple. But slides still require noisy, complicated pieces of equipment that are prone to malfunction. Always check out your equipment ahead of time and have a backup plan. &#13; Here are some guidelines for using slides: &#13; • Just as with any visual aid, make sure every slide has a clear purpose. For example, a few words on a slide can help highlight key points as you speak them, but do not use slides as presentation notes or project everything you say on the screen. &#13; • Limit the number of slides. Too many slides can be distracting and boring. Keep in mind the first rule of the vacationer showing slides to friends: Cull ruthlessly. &#13; • Keep slides simple and clear. Avoid elaborate graphics that do not help communicate your message. Slides with words should present only one idea or point, with only simple graphics. Slides with pictures should have a clear focal point, directing the audience’s attention in a meaningful way. &#13; • Number the slides so you can quickly rearrange them if necessary. &#13; • If you’re going to refer to a slide more than once, make a duplicate and put it in sequential order. &#13; • Before your presentation begins, make sure the projector is working and nothing blocks the screen. Also, check the slides to make sure they’re in the right order and right side up. &#13; • You don’t need to turn the lights off but the lights should be dim enough so people can see the slides. If necessary, give people handouts with copies of the slides so they don’t have to take notes. &#13; • Synchronize what you say with what appears on the screen. Speak loudly enough to be heard over the noise of the projector, and avoid walking or standing in front of the screen. &#13; Videos &#13; Videotapes can be very useful to show a process in action, to teach, and to stimulate discussion. A carefully produced and edited videotape can make it easier for people to understand certain concepts or see how something is done. Videotapes can also add interest to long presentations. &#13; NOTE: Videos have almost entirely replaced films. If you do use a film, most of the following guidelines apply: &#13; • Order your videos well ahead of time so you can preview them carefully. Catalog descriptions can be misleading. Take special care with humorous videotapes—they’re not always appropriate for all audiences. &#13; • Only show the part of a videotape you need. If you can make your points with one segment, it’s a waste of valuable time to show the entire video, no matter how entertaining it might be. &#13; • Integrate the videotape into your presentation. Prepare an introduction that tells people why you’re showing the video and what to look for while they watch. Decide whether to stop the tape at certain points for discussion. Prepare follow-up comments or a follow-up activity, such as a discussion, and a transition to the next part of your presentation. &#13; • If you’re not using a professionally produced video, evaluate it carefully to make sure the picture and sound quality is acceptable. &#13; • Arrive early enough to make sure the VCR and monitor(s) are working properly. Cue up your tape and set sound levels. Have backup plans in case of problems. &#13; • Provide enough monitors and dim the lights so everyone can see the video easily. Keep the sound loud enough for everyone to hear, but not so loud that it’s annoying. &#13; • Use a remote so you can operate the VCR from the back or side of the room. &#13; Models and Props &#13; A model or prototype can help you demonstrate how something will look and, perhaps, operate. A prop is any object you use to illustrate points and add interest. &#13; Here are some points to keep in mind: &#13; • Make sure people will be able to see the object. You might, for example, place a model on a table in the back of the room where people can examine it closely during breaks. &#13; • If you’re using a working model or prototype, make sure it actually works! Consider giving people a chance to try it out, if possible. &#13; • Be cautious when using props to add humor. Teleconferencing uses sophisticated, highly technical, expensive equipment to allow people in different locations to view and even participate in a remote presentation. &#13; There are different ways of linking the presentation site to the remote site(s). The most sophisticated systems combine two-way video and two-way audio so participants at both (or all) locations can see and hear the others. Other systems use video to transmit images of the presenter and the live audience to remote sites and audio only to transmit comments and questions from remote sites to the presentation site. &#13; Teleconferencing can be useful when time and budgets make it impossible for key people to travel to the presentation site. It’s most useful for relatively short presentations with a limited amount of interaction among participants. In other situations, the technical requirements and limitations of teleconferencing can create serious problems that make the presentation of less value to everyone. &#13; Here are some guidelines to follow when considering or using teleconferencing: &#13; • Avoid the temptation to use teleconferencing just because the technology is there. Teleconferencing is expensive and complicated, requiring a great deal of preparation and coordination. Also, while two-way teleconferencing provides an opportunity for people to interact and ask questions, it’s no substitute for the dynamics and synergy that result when people are gathered together in a room. &#13; • Educate yourself. Find out what kinds of systems are available to you and learn how to use them properly. Before doing a teleconferenced presentation of your own, participate in a few, both on-site and at remote locations. &#13; • Hire professionals. Unless your company has technical people on staff who can set up and run a teleconferenced presentation, you’ll need to hire professionals. That way, you can concentrate on planning, preparing, and delivering the presentation so it works for all participants, on-site and remote, and leave the technical aspects to experts. &#13; • Prepare carefully. It’s harder to hold people’s attention and communicate clearly when they’re watching on a monitor in a remote location. What adjustments do you need to make in your presentation to make sure you communicate to the people at the other end of the link without neglecting your on-site audience? What changes to presentation aids? Your delivery style? What handouts, models, or other materials do remote audiences need? &#13; • Have backup plans. The amount of technical equipment and coordination teleconferencing requires increases the chance that something will go wrong. What will you do if the link to one or more remote locations fails? If people at a remote location do not have the materials they need? &#13; Computer-Aided Presentations &#13; An increasing number of presenters are using computers not only to prepare presentation aids but to “choreograph” an entire presentation. For example, it’s possible today to project images directly from the computer to a screen or monitor, creating and altering images and text as the presentation proceeds. &#13; This technology is changing so rapidly that it is impractical here to discuss the specific options available in both software and hardware—the information would be out of date before this course is in print. But we can offer some observations and suggestions for using computers efficiently and effectively to improve your presentations. &#13; • Don’t let the technology overwhelm the presentation. Technology can easily become an end in itself, taking the focus away from the primary objective. For example, elaborate computer graphics and animations can be impressive and hold the audience’s attention but they do nothing to guarantee a successful presentation. Keep in mind that your primary purpose is to communicate a specific message to a specific audience. Let the technology serve you, not the other way around. &#13; • Educate yourself. If you plan to use computer technology, learn to do it right. Read about the latest products. Take courses to learn how to use them. Then practice, practice, and practice—make your mistakes with the computer before you take it into the presentation room. &#13; • Set up the equipment before the audience arrives. Load the program and make sure it’s working properly. Check sight lines to be sure everyone can see; if you’re using sound, check the sound levels. &#13; • Make backup plans. Remember what you’ve learned throughout this course: the more complex the technology, the greater the risk of problems. If your computer freezes or behaves erratically, you should be in a position to shut it down and continue smoothly with your presentation. For example, you might have backup overhead transparencies or prepared flip chart pages you could use as substitutes for the malfunctioning computer program. &#13; &#13; Apply What You Learn &#13; Instructions: Think about the presentation aids you will use in your presentation.
