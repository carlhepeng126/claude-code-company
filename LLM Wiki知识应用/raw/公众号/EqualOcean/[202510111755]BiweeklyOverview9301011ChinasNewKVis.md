** 1\.  Chery and Renault Discuss Joint Production in South America, Eyeing
New Plants in Colombia and Argentina  ;  **

【Event】  On October 2, Bloomberg reported that China’s Chery Automobile is in
advanced discussions with Renault SA on a potential joint venture for vehicle
production and sales across South America. The plan reportedly involves using
Renault’s existing plants in Envigado, Colombia, and Córdoba, Argentina, with
Chery contributing capital and vehicle design expertise. Some internal
combustion models would be marketed under the Renault brand, while others
would carry the Chery name.

The two companies are also considering establishing a new plug-in hybrid
pickup truck production line in Argentina, managed under Renault’s
distribution network. Although negotiations remain ongoing, industry experts
believe the deal could help Renault cut costs and expand its footprint in
high-growth markets, while enabling Chery to accelerate localized
manufacturing in the region. Renault already partners with Geely Auto in
Brazil on hybrid and low-emission models, and a successful Chery collaboration
would further solidify a multi-tiered China–Europe alliance in Latin America’s
auto industry. Analysts note that both Colombia and Argentina are entering a
critical stage of industrial transition, with Chinese investment and
technology playing a growing role in revitalizing local manufacturing.

If finalized, the Chery–Renault partnership could emerge as a new model for
cross-border co-production and supply-chain integration between Chinese and
European automakers.

  

** 2\.  China’s New K Visa for Young Global Tech Talent Takes Effect,
Signaling a Strategic Pivot in Talent Policy  ;  **

【Event】  In late September 2025, the Chinese government officially launched
the K Visa, a new immigration category aimed at attracting young global STEM
talent. Taking effect on October 1, the policy allows qualified foreign
graduates, researchers, and entrepreneurs to live and work in China without
employer sponsorship—a major departure from traditional work-visa structures.
Applicants are expected to hold degrees or research experience in science and
technology fields, though no strict age or employment limits have been
outlined.

The initiative is widely seen as China’s strategic response to the United
States’ recent increase in H-1B visa fees, now set at USD 100,000 per year per
application. By lowering administrative and financial barriers, Beijing seeks
to position itself as a new destination for mobile tech talent in an era of
intensified U.S.–China technological competition. Other advanced economies,
such as South Korea, Germany, and New Zealand, have also eased entry
requirements for skilled professionals, signaling a global talent realignment.
However, key questions remain about the K visa’s implementation—such as long-
term residency rights, family accompaniment, tax incentives, and integration
with national research funding. Analysts argue that if effectively linked with
China’s innovation hubs and industrial clusters, the program could help
internationalize the country’s R&D ecosystem. The K visa thus represents not
only an entry policy but a pivotal experiment in China’s broader effort to
reshape its global competitiveness through human capital..

** 3\.  Chinese Fast-Fashion Giant SHEIN Launches Permanent Stores in France,
Stirring Debate Over Jobs and Retail Future  ;  **

【Event】  On October 9, Chinese fast-fashion giant SHEIN announced the launch
of its first permanent physical stores in France, marking a pivotal shift in
its global retail strategy. The company will debut in Paris’s BHV Marais this
November, followed by five additional locations in Dijon, Reims, Grenoble,
Angers, and Limoges, hosted within Galeries Lafayette properties operated by
Société des Grands Magasins (SGM). SHEIN aims to merge online speed and
affordability with in-store experiential retail, creating a new “omnichannel”
model. The move, however, has sparked strong debate in France.

Proponents argue it will revitalize city centers, attract younger shoppers,
and create around 200 jobs; critics warn that ultra-fast fashion threatens
domestic textile producers and undermines environmental commitments. Data from
the Institut Français de la Mode (IFM) show that Chinese platforms SHEIN and
Temu already account for about 5% of France’s apparel market, underscoring
their growing presence in Europe. Industry observers view SHEIN’s expansion as
both a bold globalization push and a test of Europe’s retail resilience. For
France, still grappling with deindustrialization and a 10–15% retail vacancy
rate, the brand’s arrival symbolizes deeper shifts in the continent’s consumer
economy—from production outsourcing to retail disruption. As European
policymakers weigh environmental and labor concerns, SHEIN’s French entry may
become a defining case for how global fast fashion adapts—or clashes—with
sustainability-driven markets.

  

** 4\.  OpenAI and AMD Form $10 Billion Chip Partnership, Planning a 10%
Equity Stake and 6 GW of AI Compute Capacity  ;  **

【Event】  On September 30, OpenAI announced a large-scale strategic partnership
with AMD, involving chip purchases worth several tens of billions of dollars
and a potential equity stake of up to 10% in AMD under the agreement
framework. The collaboration centers on deploying 6 gigawatts (GW) of AMD GPU
computing power to support OpenAI’s next-generation AI infrastructure,
strengthening its position in the global AI compute ecosystem.

  

** 5\.  BYD Enters Argentina’s EV Market, Expanding Its South American
Footprint  ;  **

【Event】  On October 8, Chinese electric vehicle manufacturer BYD announced the
official launch of its EV sales operations in Argentina, marking a new phase
in its South American expansion strategy. The initial lineup includes the Yuan
Pro (all-electric SUV), Song Pro (plug-in hybrid SUV), and Dolphin Mini
(compact EV), all priced below USD 16,000 before tax and targeted at the mid-
market segment. The rollout benefits from Argentina’s new import policy, which
allows up to 50,000 electric and hybrid vehicles to enter the country duty-
free through 2026. Analysts say the policy sharply reduces import costs and
provides a rare opening for foreign automakers, especially cost-efficient
Chinese manufacturers.

According to JATO Dynamics, Argentina sold about 421,000 vehicles from January
to August 2025, of which only 486 were EVs or plug-in hybrids—less than 0.12%
market share. Stephen Deng, General Manager of BYD Argentina, said the company
has government approval to import 7,800 vehicles and expects strong growth in
the coming years. BYD plans to link its logistics hub in Chile and
manufacturing base in Brazil to build a “South American EV corridor.” The move
will test BYD’s local capabilities in charging infrastructure, after-sales
services, and regulatory adaptation. Analysts view Argentina as a critical
testbed for Chinese automakers seeking deeper localization across Latin
America’s fast-evolving automotive landscape.

** 6\.  Unitree Launches G1 Humanoid Robot on Walmart U.S. Platform, Expanding
Its Global Reach  ;  **

【Event】  Chinese robotics company Unitree (宇树科技) has launched its G1 humanoid
robot on Walmart’s U.S. online store at USD 21,600 (about RMB 154,000),
roughly 55% higher than its domestic price, with free shipping and a purchase
limit of six units per order. Standing 130 cm tall and weighing 35 kg, the G1
can move at 2 meters per second and features 23–43 joint motors enabling
human-like dexterity and flexibility. Compared with competing humanoid robots
that cost hundreds of thousands of dollars, the G1 has gained popularity in AI
and robotics research for its strong performance-to-price ratio.
